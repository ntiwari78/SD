🧠 The Feynman Bridge

Your Brain Runs RL

Your brain is not metaphorically running reinforcement learning — it is literally running it. Every time you touch a hot stove and recoil, every time you bite into something delicious and crave it again, every time you avoid a person or place that has hurt you before, your brain is solving an RL problem.

The most striking discovery in neuroeconomics of the last 30 years is this: dopamine neurons fire when you get an unexpected reward. They fire less when you get an expected reward. They fire below baseline when you expect a reward and it doesn't come. This is exactly the temporal-difference (TD) error from reinforcement learning theory.

The Brain's RL Algorithm

Dopamine neurons compute the TD error: the difference between what reward you got and what you expected. The striatum (a region deep in the brain) stores value estimates and learns which actions are good. The prefrontal cortex plans ahead using a learned model. Together, they implement an actor-critic algorithm that has been shaped by 300 million years of evolution.

This is not a loose analogy. The correspondence between dopamine firing and TD error is so precise that it has become a cornerstone of computational neuroscience. We can predict dopamine neuron activity from RL equations.

Understanding how your brain does RL is not just interesting neuroscience — it reveals why RL works at all. The algorithms that researchers developed in isolation turned out to solve the same problem that evolution already solved. This convergence is a powerful hint that we're discovering something true about intelligence itself.

🔬 Neuroscience Basics

Neurons, Synapses, and Dopamine

A neuron is a cell in the brain that fires (generates an electrical spike) when stimulated. Neurons communicate via synapses — connections where one neuron releases chemical messengers (neurotransmitters) that affect another neuron's tendency to fire.

Dopamine is a neurotransmitter. It is released by neurons in the midbrain (the ventral tegmental area, or VTA) and projects to many other brain regions, especially the striatum. Dopamine release makes the receiving neuron more likely to fire and strengthens the synapse between them — this is the basis of synaptic plasticity, the brain's learning mechanism.

The Dopamine System

Presynaptic neuron: The neuron that releases dopamine.

Postsynaptic neuron: The neuron that receives dopamine and (if strongly stimulated) fires.

Synaptic weight: The strength of the connection. Dopamine causes weight increases (long-term potentiation, LTP). Lack of dopamine causes weight decreases (long-term depression, LTD).

Hebbian learning: "Neurons that fire together wire together." If a postsynaptic neuron fires at the same time dopamine arrives, the synapse strengthens even more.

This simple synaptic rule — dopamine release increases synaptic strength, dopamine dip decreases it — is the brain's version of gradient descent. When you learn that an action leads to reward, dopamine spikes, and synapses representing that action strengthen. When you learn that something is disappointing, dopamine dips below baseline, and those synapses weaken.

⚡ Reward Signals

Reward Signals, Reinforcement Signals, Values, and Prediction Errors

In RL theory, we have several key quantities:

1. Reward Signal (Rt)

The immediate scalar feedback from the environment. In the brain, this includes primary reinforcers (food, water, warmth, pain avoidance) and learned reinforcers (money, status, likes).

2. Value Function (V(s) or Q(s,a))

The expected future cumulative reward from a state. The brain stores value estimates in synaptic weights and neural firing patterns, particularly in the striatum.

3. Reinforcement Signal (a signal that modulates learning)

What tells the brain when to adjust synapses? The answer: dopamine. But dopamine doesn't encode raw reward. Instead, dopamine encodes the discrepancy between reward and expectation.

4. Prediction Error (or TD Error, δ)

$$\delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma V(S_{t+1}) - V(S_t)$$

The gap between what you expected and what actually happened. This is what dopamine encodes.

The crucial insight: dopamine is not a "pleasure chemical." It is an error signal. Dopamine neurons fire when reality exceeds expectation. They fall silent when reality matches expectation. They dip below baseline when reality falls short.

🧬 The Reward Prediction Error Hypothesis

Dopamine Codes the TD Error

In the early 1990s, neuroscientists W. Schultz, P. Dayan, and T. Sejnowski made a remarkable discovery. They recorded the electrical activity of dopamine neurons in monkeys performing a task for juice rewards. They found three patterns:

  1. Unexpected reward: When a monkey got a squirt of juice with no warning, dopamine neurons fired vigorously — a burst above baseline.
  2. Expected reward: After learning that a light signal predicted juice, dopamine neurons did NOT fire when the juice arrived (only when the light appeared). The neurons had learned to fire for the cue that predicted the reward, not the reward itself.
  3. Omitted reward: When the expected juice failed to arrive, dopamine neurons dipped below baseline — a pause or suppression.

This three-part pattern is the signature of TD error. It suggests that dopamine encodes \(\delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma V(S_{t+1}) - V(S_t)\), exactly as RL theory predicts.

Why This Matters

The reward prediction error hypothesis showed that evolution had solved the credit assignment problem. Dopamine doesn't carry absolute information about reward — it carries relative information about surprise. This is why your brain can learn from the unexpected and adapt when the world changes.

The hypothesis has been tested in hundreds of experiments since the 1990s. Dopamine neuron activity predicts behavioral choices, and animals' learning curves match RL models. The correspondence is so strong that computational neuroscientists can fit a standard RL algorithm to dopamine data and get nearly perfect predictions.

🧬 Neural Actor-Critic

Actor and Critic Learning Rules in the Basal Ganglia

The basal ganglia — a collection of structures deep in the brain — implement an actor-critic architecture. The two key components:

The Critic: Estimating Value

The ventral striatum (especially the nucleus accumbens) learns to estimate the value of states. It receives dopamine input and uses it to adjust synaptic weights. The critic learns \(V(s)\) by minimizing prediction error:

$$\Delta V(S_t) = \alpha \delta_t$$

where \(\delta_t\) is the TD error encoded by dopamine.

The Actor: Learning Policies

The dorsal striatum (caudate nucleus and putamen) learns which actions are good in which states. It also receives dopamine input and learns a policy \(\pi(a|s)\). The actor learns via:

$$\Delta \pi(a|s) \propto \delta_t \cdot \mathbb{1}(a \text{ was taken})$$

Dopamine spikes increase the likelihood of the action that was taken. Dopamine dips decrease it.

Both the actor and critic use the same dopamine signal — the TD error. They learn in parallel:

  1. Agent takes action \(a\) in state \(s\).
  2. Environment transitions to \(s'\) and delivers reward \(r\).
  3. Dopamine neurons compute: \(\delta = r + \gamma V(s') - V(s)\).
  4. Dopamine neuron fires (or dips) according to \(\delta\).
  5. Critic updates: \(V(s) \leftarrow V(s) + \alpha \delta\).
  6. Actor updates: \(\pi(a|s) \leftarrow \pi(a|s) + \beta \delta\).
Why Actor-Critic in the Brain?

Actor-critic avoids a key problem: if you only learn a policy without learning values, you have to wait until the end of an episode to know if you did well. That is slow learning, which is dangerous for an animal. By learning both values (critic) and policies (actor) simultaneously using the same dopamine signal, the brain can update on every step. This is faster and more robust than pure policy gradient methods.

Location: Nucleus accumbens and surrounding areas.

Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA)
Location: Midbrain, where dopamine neurons live.
Function: Computes and broadcasts the TD error.
Dopamine effect: Fires (spike) for positive TD error. Falls silent for zero. Dips below baseline for negative TD error.
Input: Receives value predictions from the critic, compares to actual reward.
Prefrontal Cortex (Model)
Location: Frontal lobe, "most human" part of the brain.
Function: Builds a model of the world. Plans ahead (model-based RL).
Dopamine effect: Learns to predict reward from context and actions.
Interaction: Can override striatum when planning is important (e.g., resisting immediate temptation for long-term goals).
Dopamine Projections
Mesocorticolimbic pathway: VTA → ventral striatum + prefrontal cortex. Carries TD error, guides both actor-critic and model-based learning.
Nigrostriatal pathway: Substantia nigra → dorsal striatum. Crucial for motor learning and movement. Damaged in Parkinson's disease.
Signal speed: Dopamine travels at ~100 ms, fast enough to gate learning within a trial.

The Loop: Dopamine from VTA travels to striatum. It tells the actor "that was a good move" and the critic "that state was better than expected." Both learn simultaneously on the same signal. This is one of the most elegant algorithms in nature.

💎 Hedonistic Neurons

Learning Values in the Brain

Once you understand that dopamine encodes TD error, a natural question arises: where are the value estimates themselves stored?

The answer is subtle. Values are not stored in a single "value neuron." Instead, value is distributed across populations of neurons in the striatum. Each neuron has a preferred state or state-action pair, and its firing rate represents the (roughly) expected future reward from that state.

Population Coding of Value

Imagine you have 1000 neurons in the striatum. Neuron \(i\) fires strongly when in state \(s_i\), and its baseline firing rate encodes the learned value \(V(s_i)\). When you learn that state \(s_i\) is better than expected (positive TD error), dopamine spikes and strengthens synapses into that neuron, increasing its firing rate. Over time, this neuron's rate becomes a value estimate.

These value-encoding neurons are sometimes called hedonistic neurons — they seem to report how "good" a state is. But they don't actually care about the reward in any conscious sense. They're just neurons whose synaptic weights have been shaped by dopamine to predict expected future reward.

Synaptic Plasticity and Dopamine

The key learning rule is three-factor learning:

  1. Presynaptic activity: The input neuron fires.
  2. Postsynaptic activity: The output neuron fires (or is depolarized).
  3. Dopamine signal: Dopamine release (or absence) modulates the synaptic change.

If all three factors align (presynaptic + postsynaptic + dopamine spike), the synapse strengthens dramatically. This is how the brain "locks in" good decisions.

This view explains why dopamine is so important for learning. Without dopamine, synaptic weights don't change efficiently. Animals with dopamine deficits (e.g., in Parkinson's disease) can still move and perceive, but they struggle to learn new behaviors and become apathetic. They have broken the learning signal.

🌐 Collective RL

Population Coding and Collective Learning

A single dopamine neuron is not responsible for all value learning. Instead, tens of thousands of dopamine neurons broadcast the TD error in parallel, and millions of striatal neurons listen and learn.

This parallel structure has several advantages:

  • Robustness: If one dopamine neuron dies (which happens), others compensate. The learning signal is redundant.
  • Specialization: Different dopamine neurons project to different regions. Some drive motor learning (dorsal striatum), others drive reward prediction (ventral striatum), others drive memory (hippocampus). A single error signal (TD error) drives learning across all these systems.
  • Scalability: As the brain needs to learn about more and more state-action pairs, new neurons can be recruited. Population coding scales naturally.
Collective RL vs. Individual RL

Individual RL: A single agent (or neuron) learns a policy and value function.

Collective RL: A population of neurons collectively represents and learn value functions. No single neuron "knows" the true value; it emerges from the collective activity pattern.

This is actually closer to how deep neural networks work. A single hidden unit in a network trained with backpropagation doesn't "know" what it's computing. But the population of hidden units collectively computes a useful representation. The brain's use of population coding suggests that this is a fundamental principle of learning systems.

🗺 Model-Based Methods in the Brain

Planning and World Models

Not all learning in the brain is model-free (reactive, based on dopamine). Humans and many animals also plan — they imagine future sequences of events and choose actions accordingly. This requires a world model: a learned representation of how the environment works.

Where is the world model stored in the brain? The leading hypothesis points to the hippocampus (for spatial and declarative memories) and the prefrontal cortex (for abstract planning and counterfactual reasoning).

Two Systems of RL in the Brain

System 1 (Model-Free, Fast): Striatum learns cached value estimates via dopamine. Quick but inflexible. Habits.

System 2 (Model-Based, Slow): Prefrontal cortex + hippocampus build a model and plan ahead. Slow but flexible. Goal-directed behavior.

Healthy behavior uses both. You automatically tie your shoes (system 1, fast), but you consciously plan your career (system 2, slow). Addiction arises when system 1 overpowers system 2.

The brain's model-based system solves problems that model-free RL struggles with:

  1. Counterfactual reasoning: "What if I had done that differently?" Planning requires imagining alternative sequences.
  2. One-shot learning: If you learn that a new restaurant is closed, you immediately avoid it. You don't need dozens of extinction trials.
  3. Goal-directed behavior: You can flexibly pursue any goal using your world model, even goals you've never experienced.
Dopamine in Model-Based RL

Even in model-based RL, dopamine plays a role. The prefrontal cortex uses dopamine to learn which plans are good and which are bad. This is more abstract than striatal learning (it's about planning, not actions), but the same dopamine signal carries the error.

Recent research (e.g., by Sam Gershman and colleagues) suggests the brain uses a hybrid approach: model-free for fast, automatic decisions; model-based for slow, deliberate planning. Most behavior is a blend of both.

⚠️ Addiction

When RL Goes Wrong

Addiction is a failure of reinforcement learning. It's not (just) about lack of willpower. It's about a broken learning system.

Here's what happens:

  1. Sensitization: A drug (or compulsive behavior) releases dopamine artificially high. The dopamine signal becomes decoupled from prediction error. It just floods the system.
  2. Value learning goes haywire: Drug-associated cues (environments, people, times of day) become powerfully valued. The striatum learns that these cues predict intense dopamine release. The value of the drug-seeking behavior becomes extremely high.
  3. Habitual control: Over time, the behavior becomes automatic — driven by the dorsal striatum (habit system) rather than goal-directed planning (prefrontal cortex). The person "knows better" (system 2 reasoning) but their habits (system 1) overwhelm.
  4. Tolerance and craving: The brain adapts to chronic dopamine surges by reducing dopamine receptor expression. Baseline dopamine drops, creating a state of anhedonia (inability to experience pleasure from normal rewards). The person needs the drug just to feel normal.
The RL Perspective on Addiction

Addiction is not a moral failing. It's a learning system that has been hijacked by an artificial stimulus that triggers dopamine release at unrealistic levels. The brain's error-correction mechanisms try to adapt, but they overshoot. Standard RL algorithms have similar pathologies — they can get stuck chasing rewarding mirages.

Understanding addiction through RL lens has led to new treatments:

  • Cue extinction: Repeatedly exposing addicts to cues (without the drug) in a safe context helps unlearn the strong value associations. This is model-based relearning.
  • Value retraining: Strengthening alternative rewards (healthy activities, social connection) and their dopamine signals can rebalance the reward system.
  • Pharmacological intervention: Medications that affect dopamine or opioid receptors can reduce craving or blocking the dopamine spike from the drug.
RL and Mental Health

The same principles apply to depression (model-based system says "nothing is rewarding," dopamine system confirms it), anxiety (model-based system learns that stimuli are dangerous, dopamine system reinforces avoidance), and other conditions. Therapy and medication work by adjusting value estimates and dopamine signaling.

❓ Socratic Sandbox

Test Your Understanding: Neuroscience-RL Correspondence

Below are five questions designed to deepen your intuition for how the brain implements RL. Take your time, think through each one, and open the answers only after you've reasoned through it yourself.

1
Why does dopamine fire for a predictive cue (e.g., a light that signals juice) rather than the juice itself, after learning?
▼

Answer: This is a consequence of temporal-difference learning and credit assignment. Initially, dopamine fires for the unexpected juice (positive TD error). But once you learn that the light predicts the juice, the value of the cue increases. When the cue appears, dopamine fires because the cue itself now generates positive TD error — "I expected nothing, but this cue signals future reward!" When the juice arrives, you were already expecting it, so TD error is zero, and dopamine stays flat.

This shift is crucial. It allows the dopamine system to guide learning backward in time. Early actions that set up favorable cues become reinforced, even though the reward comes much later.

2
In the actor-critic architecture, both the actor and critic learn from the same dopamine signal (TD error). Why doesn't the actor overshadow the critic, or vice versa?
▼

Answer: They don't overshadow each other because they learn different representations. The critic learns value estimates \(V(s)\), stored as baseline firing rates of value-coding neurons. The actor learns policy weights \(\pi(a|s)\), stored in synapses from state-coding neurons to action-coding neurons. Even though dopamine modulates both, the two systems learn complementary information.

More importantly, they operate on different timescales. The critic updates slowly, building stable value estimates. The actor updates faster, allowing quick policy adaptation. This temporal separation prevents destructive interference.

In artificial neural networks with actor-critic algorithms (like A3C or PPO), the same principle holds: we can have separate heads for value and policy that learn from the same loss signal without collapsing.

3
Why is dopamine sometimes called "the learning signal" rather than "the pleasure signal," even though dopamine is commonly associated with pleasure?
▼

Answer: Because dopamine's primary function in RL is to encode surprise (prediction error), not pleasure. Yes, dopamine is released when you experience pleasure, but that release is because pleasure is unexpected. If you expect pleasure and get it, dopamine doesn't spike.

The classic evidence: when a monkey learns that a light predicts juice, dopamine stops firing for the juice itself and fires only for the light. The monkey still enjoys the juice, but dopamine isn't encoding that enjoyment — it's already encoded by the cue's dopamine response.

This distinction is crucial for understanding motivation. Dopamine drives wanting (the motivation to pursue something), not liking (the hedonic pleasure). Addiction hijacks the dopamine system, creating intense wanting without necessarily increasing liking.

4
If dopamine encodes TD error, and dopamine dips below baseline when an expected reward fails to arrive, what does the dip below baseline actually do to the brain? How does it change learning?
▼

Answer: The dopamine dip below baseline causes long-term depression (LTD) — synapses weaken. Specifically, synapses that were active just before the disappointing outcome become less likely to be used again. This is the brain's way of saying "that strategy didn't work."

For the critic: synapses encoding value estimates decrease their strength, lowering the estimated value of the state.

For the actor: synapses representing the action that was taken weaken, reducing the probability of taking that action again in that state.

This is negative reinforcement in the RL sense — not punishment, but a signal that the prediction was wrong and needs to be corrected downward. It's the key mechanism for learning to avoid dangerous or unrewarding actions.

5
How would you design a neuroscience experiment to test whether the prefrontal cortex uses dopamine for model-based RL in the same way the striatum uses it for model-free RL?
▼

Answer: Here's a research design that captures the key elements:

  1. Task: Train animals on a two-stage decision task. First choice leads to a state, second choice leads to reward. Occasionally, outcomes are reversed (model resets).
  2. Prediction: Model-free RL (striatum) learns cached policies that stick even after reversal. Model-based RL (prefrontal cortex) flexibly adapts to new contingencies. So animals with intact prefrontal cortex should switch faster than those with damage.
  3. Dopamine recording: Record dopamine neurons while animals perform the task. If prefrontal cortex uses dopamine for planning, dopamine should respond to imagined future states (e.g., it should fire before the second choice is made, reflecting a plan). Striatal dopamine responds to actual outcomes.
  4. Pharmacology: Block dopamine receptors specifically in prefrontal cortex during model-based trials. Predict that this will impair flexible learning but not habit learning.
  5. Lesion studies: Lesion the prefrontal cortex and test whether the animal can still do simple learning (striatal, intact) but fails at planning and flexibility.

This kind of experiment has actually been done (by colleagues like Mark Walton and others), and the results support the two-systems hypothesis.
