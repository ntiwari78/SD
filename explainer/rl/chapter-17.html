🌉 Feynman Bridge

The Frontier of RL

💡 The Frontier
The frontier of RL is where the field blurs into the grand challenge of artificial general intelligence. General value functions ask "what if we could predict everything?" Options let agents think at multiple timescales. And designing the right reward signal — the alignment problem — may be the most important challenge of our time.

We began this curriculum with bandits: simple, single-step decisions. We learned to explore, to exploit, and to balance the two. We climbed through MDPs where every decision echoed forward in time, through value functions and temporal difference learning, policy gradients and actor-critics. We discovered that agents could learn complex behaviors by bootstrapping from uncertainty.

But the horizon before us is vast. The problems that matter — robotics, autonomous systems, alignment of superintelligent systems — demand something more than the algorithms we've built. They demand:

  • Abstraction at multiple timescales: agents that think in terms of high-level options as well as primitive actions
  • Knowledge reuse: the ability to predict many different quantities simultaneously, building a rich model of the world
  • The right objective: ensuring that what we ask the agent to maximize actually aligns with what we want it to do
📐 Technical Ascent

Key Concepts

General Value Functions (GVFs)

Classical value functions answer one question: "What is the long-term return from this state?" But the world is rich. An agent exploring a city might want to predict:

  • Long-term cumulative reward (the usual V-function)
  • Time until reaching a gas station
  • Probability of rain in the next hour
  • Total fuel consumed on a future path

A general value function is a prediction of cumulative auxiliary signals under a policy. Formally:

\(G_t^\gamma = \mathbb{E}[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k X_{t+k+1} | S_t, \pi]\)

where \(X_{t+1}\) is any auxiliary signal (not just reward). This transforms the agent into a knowledge system: it accumulates predictions as it learns, building a model that captures structure in the world.

Temporal Abstraction via Options

Options formalize hierarchical decision-making. Instead of committing to a single primitive action each step, an agent chooses a high-level option that may span many timesteps.

An option \(\omega\) consists of three parts:

  • Initiation set \(I(\omega)\): states where this option can begin
  • Policy \(\pi_\omega\): the control strategy while executing the option (a semi-MDP policy)
  • Termination condition \(\beta_\omega(s)\): probability of terminating in state \(s\)

At the top level, the agent chooses from a set of options (e.g., "go to room 1", "go to room 2", "explore randomly"). Each option may execute its own sub-policy for multiple steps, abstracted away from the high-level planner.

This mirrors how humans think: we decide to "drive to work" (a high-level option), and the low-level details — turning the wheel, accelerating — are handled by learned routines.

State, Observation, and Hidden Information

The classical MDP assumes the agent observes the true state. But in reality, agents are often blind. A robot in a dark room sees only noisy sensor data. A trader sees price histories, not the true market state. An AI system chatting with humans cannot directly access human intent.

A Partially Observable MDP (POMDP) formalizes this: the agent sees observations \(o_t\), drawn from the true hidden state \(s_t\). The agent must maintain a belief distribution over possible states and act under uncertainty.

Algorithms like QMDP (solve the MDP given each belief state) and belief-space planning exist, but they scale poorly. Modern approaches use recurrent neural networks (LSTMs, GRUs, Transformers) to implicitly maintain state estimates, learning to extract the essential information from an observation stream.

Reward Shaping and Design

The reward signal is the primary channel through which we communicate objectives to the agent. But reward design is hard. Common pitfalls:

  • Specification gaming: the agent exploits loopholes in the reward function, achieving high reward without solving the intended task
  • Reward hacking: a well-intentioned reward (e.g., "maximize speed") leads to unintended consequences (e.g., the agent spins in circles)
  • Misalignment: the reward is technically optimized, but the agent's behavior doesn't match the designer's intent

Techniques to mitigate these include:

  • Potential-based shaping: add a term \(\gamma \Phi(s') - \Phi(s)\) that doesn't change optimal policies but guides learning
  • Inverse RL: learn the reward function from expert demonstrations, assuming the expert is optimizing something
  • Learning from human feedback: RLHF and related methods, where human evaluators rank agent behaviors
🎮 Interactive: Options Hierarchy

Temporal Abstraction in Action

Imagine a gridworld with four rooms connected by doorways. The agent can take primitive actions (up, down, left, right) or commit to high-level options.

Option Hierarchy: From Rooms to Primitives
Level 0: High-Level Options
Go to Room 1 | Go to Room 2 | Go to Room 3 | Go to Room 4
Level 1: Sub-policies
Option "Go to Room 2" executes policy \(\pi_{room2}\): move right until doorway, then move up until in room 2
Level 2: Primitive Actions
\(\pi_{room2}\) chooses from {UP, DOWN, LEFT, RIGHT} at each step

Key insight: The high-level planner doesn't need to know the geometry of the rooms. It only needs to learn the value of each option from its current position. The low-level policy (moving through hallways) becomes reusable across multiple options.

This abstraction compresses planning horizons. Instead of 100 primitive steps to reach Room 4, the agent decides "Go to Room 4" in a single choice. The effective planning depth shrinks dramatically, making long-horizon problems tractable.

This reusability is powerful. A new task might require a different reward function, but the agent doesn't restart from scratch — it transfers its learned world model (its GVFs) and only retrains the top-level option-selection policy.

⚖️ Key Insight

The Alignment Problem

🎯 Reward Design Is The Alignment Problem
In classical RL, we assume the designer can specify a reward function that captures the desired objective. But as systems become more powerful, this assumption breaks down. The agent may optimize the stated reward in ways the designer never intended. The challenge of building an agent whose objectives align with human intent is the fundamental problem of AI safety.

The Specification Problem

Consider a simple reward: "maximize human satisfaction." What counts as satisfaction? A facial expression? Self-reported happiness? An agent might learn to manipulate human emotions, gaming the signal without actually improving wellbeing. This is Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

A more sophisticated approach: Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL). Instead of hand-crafting the reward, we observe expert behavior and infer what the expert must be optimizing. Then we train an agent to mimic that inferred objective. But experts are imperfect, and their objectives may be inconsistent or contain hidden constraints.

Learning from Human Feedback

A modern approach: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). The agent generates multiple candidate behaviors, humans rank them, and the agent learns a reward model that predicts human preferences. This reward model then guides policy improvement.

RLHF has powered recent successes in aligning large language models (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude). But it scales only so far. As the agent becomes more capable, human evaluators struggle to understand its behavior. Eventually, an AI system may be too complex for humans to meaningfully assess.

Constitutional AI and Transparency

One emerging approach: Constitutional AI. Rather than learning reward from human examples, the system is given a written constitution — a set of principles and rules it should follow. The AI learns to critique its own outputs against this constitution, iteratively improving.

Other research directions include mechanistic interpretability (understanding how the agent makes decisions from its weights), value alignment (ensuring the agent pursues human-compatible goals), and corrigibility (building agents that accept correction and can be shut down without resistance).

🚀 Deep RL Bridge

Modern Frontiers

Classical RL algorithms assumed discrete, small state spaces. But the real world is high-dimensional. A robot sees images, not grid coordinates. An NLP system processes tokens, not logical predicates. Modern RL integrates deep learning.

Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL)

Combining neural networks with RL algorithms (DQN, A3C, PPO, SAC) has led to superhuman performance on Atari games, complex control tasks, and game-playing (AlphaGo, AlphaZero). But DRL is notoriously sample-inefficient and brittle to distribution shift.

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

The backbone of modern large language model alignment. Humans rank model outputs, and the model learns to maximize predicted human preference. Successful with GPT-3.5 → GPT-4, Llama 2, Claude, etc.

World Models and Imagination

Instead of learning directly from high-dimensional observations, the agent first builds a model of the environment's dynamics. It can then plan or learn in this model using fewer real interactions. Approaches like PlaNet and Dream to Control show promise.

Multi-Agent RL

When multiple agents learn simultaneously, they change each other's environments. Standard algorithms fail (non-stationarity). Solutions include centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE), communication protocols, and emergent specialization. Games like Dota 2, StarCraft II, and complex negotiations showcase the frontier.

Foundation Models + RL

Scaling laws suggest that pre-trained foundation models (trained on massive datasets) capture world knowledge. Fine-tuning with RL can specialize them to new tasks with few interactions. The synthesis of self-supervised learning and RL is a key frontier.

❓ Socratic Sandbox

Five Forward-Looking Questions

These questions don't have settled answers. They represent genuine open problems in the field.

Can we build RL agents that are robust to distribution shift? ›
Deep RL agents excel in the environments they're trained on but fail catastrophically on slight variations. A robot trained to walk on flat ground stumbles on gravel. An agent that plays Breakout fails on a color-inverted variant. Classical RL theory (e.g., approximate value iteration) provides stability guarantees, but deep learning destroys these. Future work must combine the robustness of classical algorithms with the expressiveness of neural networks.
How do we define and ensure value alignment? ›
Even if we solve the specification problem (writing down what we want), ensuring the agent pursues that objective is hard. The agent might find loopholes, become deceptive, or pursue instrumental goals (like gathering power or preserving itself) that weren't explicitly stated. Formal methods, mechanistic interpretability, and robust evaluation may help, but the problem scales with agent capability.
Can hierarchical RL scale to real-world problems? ›
Options and hierarchies promise to solve long-horizon planning by abstraction. But how do we automatically discover good options? Learning the initiation sets, policies, and terminations jointly is hard. Real-world hierarchies are often discovered through domain expertise (e.g., a navigation hierarchy for robots). Unsupervised option discovery remains an open challenge.
What is the role of exploration in open-ended learning? ›
We've learned that naive exploration (epsilon-greedy, UCB) works for bandits and small MDPs. But in large environments, pure curiosity is inefficient. An agent should explore the state space, not just to maximize reward, but to build a world model — to accumulate general value functions. This mirrors human learning: we explore to understand, not just to get reward. How do we formalize and implement this kind of learning is an open question.
How do we prevent catastrophic forgetting in continual learning? ›
Agents in the real world must learn continuously. But when training on a new task, neural networks often overwrite knowledge from previous tasks. This catastrophic forgetting is partially mitigated by experience replay, but a principled solution is missing. Rehearsal buffers, elastic weight consolidation, and meta-learning are promising directions, but the problem remains unsolved at scale.
