🎯 The Feynman Bridge

Choosing Restaurants: Explore or Exploit?

Imagine you're a tourist in a new city with 10 restaurants nearby. You don't know which ones are good. Each night you must pick one to eat at. Every restaurant has a hidden quality (its "true value") that you'll only discover by trying it.

Here's your dilemma:

The Exploration-Exploitation Trade-off

Exploit: You found a decent restaurant on night 1. Keep going back — it's reliable and you know what to expect.

Explore: Try the fancy new place that opened last week. Maybe it's amazing. Maybe it's terrible. You won't know until you try.

Each night you choose one restaurant (pull one "arm"). You get a reward (how good the meal was — a number, say 1–10). Over many nights, you want to maximize your total satisfaction. The challenge: with limited nights, how do you balance trying new places against returning to known favorites?

This simple decision problem is called the k-armed bandit problem. The "arms" are your action choices (restaurants). The "bandit" is the RL agent (you). Each time you pull an arm, it stochastically "pays out" a reward. The goal is to maximize total reward.

This problem is simpler than full RL (no states, no sequences — just single decisions), but it captures the core tension that appears everywhere in reinforcement learning:

Why Bandits Matter

Bandits are the seed problem from which all of RL grows. Even though they look toy-like, the exploration strategies you learn here — ε-greedy, optimistic initialization, upper confidence bounds (UCB), Thompson sampling — directly power modern recommender systems, A/B testing, and even the exploration strategies in deep Q-learning. Mastering bandits is mastering the exploration-exploitation frontier.

📐 The Technical Ascent

Action Values, Sample Averages & Update Rules

In the k-armed bandit, you must estimate the value of each arm and then use those estimates to decide which to pull next. Let's build this from first principles.

The true action value of arm \(a\) is the expected reward you'd get if you pulled it forever:

$$q_*(a) = \mathbb{E}[R_t \mid A_t = a]$$

You don't know \(q_*(a)\), but you can estimate it by averaging the rewards you've received from arm \(a\):

🧮 Equation 2.1: Sample-Average Estimate ▼

The simplest estimator is the sample mean. If arm \(a\) has been selected \(N_t(a)\) times by time \(t\) and the rewards were \(R_1, R_2, \ldots, R_{N_t(a)}\), then:

$$Q_t(a) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N_t(a)} R_i}{N_t(a)}$$

This converges to \(q_*(a)\) as \(N_t(a) \to \infty\) (law of large numbers). It's unbiased and simple, but can be slow to update.

Action selection: The simplest strategy is ε-greedy. With probability \(\varepsilon\), explore (pick a random arm). With probability \(1-\varepsilon\), exploit (pick the arm with the highest estimated value):

🧮 Equation 2.2: ε-Greedy Policy ▼
$$A_t = \begin{cases} \text{random arm} & \text{with probability } \varepsilon \\ \arg\max_a Q_t(a) & \text{with probability } 1-\varepsilon \end{cases}$$

The ε-greedy method is the simplest exploration strategy. It explores uniformly (not intelligently), but because it explores forever (albeit rarely if ε is small), it will eventually estimate all action values accurately.

Incremental update rule: Recomputing the full average every time is wasteful. Instead, update incrementally:

🧮 Equation 2.3: Incremental Update (Sample Average) ▼
$$Q_{n+1} = Q_n + \frac{1}{n}\Big[R_n - Q_n\Big]$$

Where \(n\) is the number of times the arm has been selected and \(R_n\) is the most recent reward. The term \([R_n - Q_n]\) is the error, and we move \(Q_n\) toward it by a fraction \(1/n\).

This can be rewritten in the general form:

🧮 Equation 2.4: General Update Form ▼
$$\text{NewEstimate} \leftarrow \text{OldEstimate} + \text{StepSize} \times [\text{Target} - \text{OldEstimate}]$$

This pattern appears everywhere in RL: the step-size controls learning rate, the target is the desired value, and the bracketed term is the error. Different algorithms use different targets. In sample-average bandits, Target = \(R_n\).

Nonstationary environments: In many real problems, the true values change over time. Sample averages give equal weight to old and new rewards, but recent rewards matter more. Use a constant step-size instead:

🧮 Equation 2.5: Constant Step-Size Update ▼
$$Q_{n+1} = Q_n + \alpha \Big[R_n - Q_n\Big]$$

With constant \(\alpha \in (0,1]\). This gives more weight to recent rewards. For \(\alpha = 1/n\), it's the sample-average rule. For constant \(\alpha\), it tracks recent changes.

The constant step-size can be rewritten as a weighted average of all past rewards:

🧮 Equation 2.6: Exponential Recency-Weighted Average ▼
$$Q_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (1-\alpha)^{n-i} \alpha R_i + (1-\alpha)^n Q_1$$

Recent rewards get weight \(\alpha, \alpha(1-\alpha), \alpha(1-\alpha)^2, \ldots\) (exponentially decaying). The total weight sums to 1. This is useful for nonstationary problems where old data is less relevant.

Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategy: Instead of exploring uniformly at random, explore "smartly" — prioritize arms that are promising or where your estimate is uncertain:

🧮 Equation 2.10: UCB Action Selection ▼
$$A_t = \arg\max_a \left[ Q_t(a) + c \sqrt{\frac{\ln t}{N_t(a)}} \right]$$

The first term \(Q_t(a)\) is the estimated value. The second term is an uncertainty bonus: \(\sqrt{\ln t / N_t(a)}\) is large when the arm hasn't been tried much. The parameter \(c > 0\) controls exploration. UCB balances exploitation (high \(Q_t(a)\)) with exploration (low \(N_t(a)\)).

Gradient Bandit (Softmax Preference): Instead of learning action values \(Q(a)\), learn action preferences \(H(a)\). The policy is softmax over preferences:

🧮 Equation 2.11: Softmax Policy ▼
$$\pi_t(a) = \frac{e^{H_t(a)}}{\sum_b e^{H_t(b)}}$$

Higher preferences lead to higher probabilities. This naturally explores but biases toward better arms. The preference is updated using the policy gradient theorem:

🧮 Equation 2.12: Preference Update (Policy Gradient) ▼
$$H_{n+1}(a) = H_n(a) + \alpha [R_n - \bar{R}_n] \pi_n(a)$$

Where \(\bar{R}_n\) is the average reward (baseline). If the reward exceeded the baseline, increase the preference for the action taken. The update is proportional to the policy \(\pi_n(a)\), making this a stochastic gradient ascent on expected reward.

💻 Algorithm Implementation

Complete Bandit Algorithms in Python

Below is the full annotated implementation of all major bandit exploration strategies. Every line maps back to the equations above.

Python / NumPy 
import numpy as np
from typing import List, Tuple

class BanditEnvironment:
    """
    A k-armed bandit where each arm has a fixed (unknown)
    true value drawn from N(0, 1).
    """
    def __init__(self, k: int, seed: int = None):
        if seed: np.random.seed(seed)
        self.k = k
        # True values: q*(a) for each arm
        self.q_star = np.random.randn(k)

    def step(self, action: int) -> float:
        """Pull arm 'action', return reward from N(q*(action), 1)"""
        return self.q_star[action] + np.random.randn()


class EpsilonGreedyAgent:
    """
    Epsilon-greedy bandit agent (Eq 2.2).

    With probability ε: explore (random arm)
    With probability 1-ε: exploit (greedy: highest Q_t(a))
    """
    def __init__(self, k: int, epsilon: float = 0.1, alpha: float = None):
        self.k = k
        self.epsilon = epsilon
        self.alpha = alpha  # None = sample-average, else = constant step-size
        self.Q = np.zeros(k)      # Q_t(a) estimates (Eq 2.1 or 2.5)
        self.N = np.zeros(k, dtype=int)  # N_t(a): times arm a selected

    def select_action(self) -> int:
        """ε-greedy action selection (Eq 2.2)"""
        if np.random.random() < self.epsilon:
            # Explore: random arm
            return np.random.randint(self.k)
        else:
            # Exploit: greedy arm (break ties randomly)
            best_actions = np.where(self.Q == self.Q.max())[0]
            return np.random.choice(best_actions)

    def update(self, action: int, reward: float):
        """Update Q-value using sample-average or constant step-size"""
        self.N[action] += 1
        if self.alpha is None:
            # Sample-average: Q_{n+1} = Q_n + (1/n)[R_n - Q_n] (Eq 2.3)
            self.Q[action] += (1 / self.N[action]) * (reward - self.Q[action])
        else:
            # Constant step-size: Q_{n+1} = Q_n + α[R_n - Q_n] (Eq 2.5)
            self.Q[action] += self.alpha * (reward - self.Q[action])


class UCBAgent:
    """
    Upper Confidence Bound agent (Eq 2.10).

    Select arm that maximizes: Q_t(a) + c·sqrt(ln(t) / N_t(a))

    The bonus term prioritizes uncertain (rarely-tried) arms.
    """
    def __init__(self, k: int, c: float = 1.0, alpha: float = None):
        self.k = k
        self.c = c
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.Q = np.zeros(k)
        self.N = np.zeros(k, dtype=int)
        self.t = 0  # Total pulls

    def select_action(self) -> int:
        """UCB action selection (Eq 2.10)"""
        self.t += 1

        # Avoid log(0) on first pulls: pull each arm at least once
        if self.t <= self.k:
            return self.t - 1

        # Upper confidence bounds for each arm
        ucb_values = self.Q + self.c * np.sqrt(np.log(self.t) / self.N)
        best_actions = np.where(ucb_values == ucb_values.max())[0]
        return np.random.choice(best_actions)

    def update(self, action: int, reward: float):
        """Update Q-value"""
        self.N[action] += 1
        if self.alpha is None:
            self.Q[action] += (1 / self.N[action]) * (reward - self.Q[action])
        else:
            self.Q[action] += self.alpha * (reward - self.Q[action])


class GradientBanditAgent:
    """
    Gradient bandit / softmax preference learner (Eq 2.11, 2.12).

    Learn action preferences H(a), not values.
    Policy is softmax: π_t(a) = e^{H_t(a)} / Σ e^{H_t(b)}
    Update: H_{n+1}(a) = H_n(a) + α[R_n - R̄_n]π_n(a)
    """
    def __init__(self, k: int, alpha: float = 0.1):
        self.k = k
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.H = np.zeros(k)      # Preferences (initially equal)
        self.R_bar = 0.0          # Running average reward (baseline)
        self.t = 0                # Total pulls

    def select_action(self) -> int:
        """Sample action from softmax policy (Eq 2.11)"""
        # Compute softmax probabilities
        exp_H = np.exp(self.H - self.H.max())  # Subtract max for numerical stability
        self.pi = exp_H / exp_H.sum()
        # Sample from policy
        return np.random.choice(self.k, p=self.pi)

    def update(self, action: int, reward: float):
        """Update preferences using policy gradient (Eq 2.12)"""
        self.t += 1
        # Update baseline (running average of rewards)
        self.R_bar += (1 / self.t) * (reward - self.R_bar)
        # Baseline (to reduce variance): any constant subtracts from all actions
        baseline = self.R_bar
        # Policy gradient update (Eq 2.12)
        # Only the selected action gets updated (others implicitly: H_i -= α·baseline·π_i)
        for a in range(self.k):
            if a == action:
                self.H[a] += self.alpha * (reward - baseline) * self.pi[a]
            else:
                self.H[a] -= self.alpha * (reward - baseline) * self.pi[a]


class OptimisticInitAgent:
    """
    ε-greedy with optimistic initial values.

    Start Q_1(a) = q_init (usually high, e.g., 5).
    This forces exploration: all arms look promising at first,
    but as you learn they're bad, you naturally explore less.
    """
    def __init__(self, k: int, q_init: float = 2.0, epsilon: float = 0.01, alpha: float = None):
        self.k = k
        self.epsilon = epsilon
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.Q = np.full(k, q_init)  # Initialize optimistically
        self.N = np.zeros(k, dtype=int)

    def select_action(self) -> int:
        """ε-greedy selection"""
        if np.random.random() < self.epsilon:
            return np.random.randint(self.k)
        else:
            best_actions = np.where(self.Q == self.Q.max())[0]
            return np.random.choice(best_actions)

    def update(self, action: int, reward: float):
        """Update Q-value"""
        self.N[action] += 1
        if self.alpha is None:
            self.Q[action] += (1 / self.N[action]) * (reward - self.Q[action])
        else:
            self.Q[action] += self.alpha * (reward - self.Q[action])


def run_bandit_experiment(
    agent, env: BanditEnvironment, steps: int = 1000
) -> Tuple[List[float], List[int]]:
    """
    Run one episode of a bandit agent in an environment.

    Returns:
      - rewards: list of rewards at each step
      - actions: list of actions taken
    """
    rewards = []
    actions = []

    for _ in range(steps):
        # Agent observes state (no state in bandits) and selects action
        action = agent.select_action()
        actions.append(action)

        # Environment gives reward
        reward = env.step(action)
        rewards.append(reward)

        # Agent updates value estimates
        agent.update(action, reward)

    return rewards, actions


if __name__ == '__main__':
    # Quick comparison: run each agent in a 10-armed bandit
    np.random.seed(42)
    env = BanditEnvironment(k=10, seed=42)

    agents = {
        'ε-greedy (ε=0.1)': EpsilonGreedyAgent(k=10, epsilon=0.1),
        'UCB (c=2)': UCBAgent(k=10, c=2.0),
        'Optimistic (Q_init=5)': OptimisticInitAgent(k=10, q_init=5.0),
        'Gradient (α=0.1)': GradientBanditAgent(k=10, alpha=0.1),
    }

    print(f"True arm values (q*):\n{env.q_star}\n")
    print(f"Optimal arm: {np.argmax(env.q_star)} (value={env.q_star.max():.3f})\n")

    for name, agent in agents.items():
        rewards, actions = run_bandit_experiment(agent, env, steps=1000)
        avg_reward = np.mean(rewards[-100:])  # Last 100 steps
        optimal_pct = (np.array(actions)[-100:] == np.argmax(env.q_star)).mean() * 100
        print(f"{name:25s} | Avg reward (last 100): {avg_reward:6.3f} | Optimal arm %: {optimal_pct:5.1f}%")

🧪 Socratic Sandbox

Debugging a Stuck Bandit Agent

You've trained a 10-armed bandit agent with ε-greedy (ε=0.1) for 1000 pulls. Its average reward plateaued at 1.2, but the true optimal arm has value ~1.55. The agent seems to waste many pulls on clearly bad arms and hasn't discovered the best one yet.

Scenario: The Learning Plateau

You examine the agent's learned values Q_t(a) for each arm. Eight arms are correctly identified as bad (Q ~ 0 to 0.5). But the best arm has Q = 1.1 (close to optimal 1.55), and a mediocre arm also has Q ~ 1.1. The agent can't distinguish them — it alternates between them and misses the exploration needed to confirm which is truly best. With only 100 pulls left, you won't converge.

1 Why does ε-greedy waste pulls on arms it's already learned are bad? ▼

ε-greedy explores uniformly at random — every non-greedy action has equal probability. If the agent has ruled out 8 arms as bad but still explores, it explores all 10 equally, wasting pulls on the 8 it knows are poor. UCB and Thompson sampling solve this: they explore the high-uncertainty arms (arms 1 and 9, which are close in value) while ignoring arms they're confident about. This is "smart exploration" — it doesn't repeat lessons it's already learned.

2 You have 100 pulls left. Should you set ε=0 (pure exploitation) to squeeze maximum reward, or keep exploring? ▼

Keep exploring, but reduce ε (don't eliminate it). If you set ε=0, you'll forever exploit the greedy arm, never resolving the tie between arms 1 and 9. With remaining budget, the opportunity cost of exploration is high — each exploratory pull could be a greedy pull. But exploration is still profitable if the uncertainty is large. This is the essence of the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. The "optimal" strategy depends on time-horizon: with unlimited time, exploration is free. With finite time, it's expensive.

3 What if you restart with optimistic initialization (Q_init=5) instead of Q_init=0.5? How does that change learning? ▼

With Q_init=5 (very high), every arm initially looks amazing. The agent will explore all arms aggressively in early steps, quickly learning which are bad and ruling them out. Bad arms drop from 5 toward 0 rapidly, creating high TD errors and informative updates. The agent naturally decays exploration — it doesn't need ε-greedy's explicit randomness. Optimistic init is often better for bandits, but requires careful tuning of Q_init. Too low and you underexplore; too high and you overshoot estimates.

4 The environment is stationary (true arm values don't change). Should you use sample-average (1/n learning rate) or constant step-size α? ▼

In stationary environments, sample-average is theoretically superior — it converges to the true value with zero asymptotic error. Constant α creates a bias (older rewards matter less), but that's not a problem in stationary settings. However, sample-average updates slow down over time (step-size = 1/n shrinks), so early learning is fast but refinement is slow. In practice, many practitioners use constant α anyway for simplicity and better online performance. The tradeoff: sample-average = consistency; constant α = responsiveness.

5 How would you modify the bandit to make it nonstationary? And what would that mean for your exploration strategy? ▼

Nonstationary: the true arm values drift over time. For example, restaurants change quality, or ad click-through rates evolve as users learn. In nonstationary bandits, constant step-size is critical — sample-average forgets new data. You'd also want higher ε or more exploration, because the "best arm" identified at step 500 might be suboptimal at step 1000. Thompson sampling with Bayesian updates naturally handles nonstationarity: you update your belief about each arm's distribution as evidence accumulates. This is why contextual bandits and Thompson sampling are dominant in real-world recommendation systems.

🔗 Modern Applications

Connecting Bandits to Deep RL and Beyond

Contextual Bandits → Recommendation Systems

The jump: Multi-armed bandits assume no state. Contextual bandits extend this: at each time, you observe a context (user features, news article embedding), then select an action. The reward depends on the context and action. This is the foundation of modern recommender systems: Netflix, YouTube, and Spotify all use contextual bandits (or close variants) to balance showing you content you'll click with exploring new content you might like. The exploration-exploitation tradeoff you learned here directly powers billions of daily recommendations.

Thompson Sampling → Bayesian Deep Learning

The jump: Thompson sampling is a Bayesian exploration method: maintain a posterior distribution over arm values and sample from it. This naturally balances exploitation (sampling from modes of the posterior) with exploration (respecting uncertainty). Modern Bayesian deep learning applies similar ideas to neural networks: maintain uncertainty estimates over network weights and use them to drive exploration in RL agents. Uncertainty-driven exploration is a frontier in safe and sample-efficient RL.

UCB → Bonus-Based Exploration in DQN

The jump: In the next chapters, you'll learn Q-learning and Deep Q-Networks (DQN). The standard DQN uses ε-greedy exploration, which is simple but inefficient. Modern variants (Rainbow, etc.) incorporate UCB-style bonuses: add an uncertainty or visitation-count bonus to the Q-values, prioritizing uncertain or rarely-visited states. This is UCB applied to deep RL: instead of uncertainty over action values, it's uncertainty over state-action values in a high-dimensional environment. The core insight — "explore the uncertain" — transfers directly from Chapter 2 to Chapter 7.

Gradient Bandits → Policy Gradient Methods

The jump: In Chapters 13–15, you'll study policy gradient methods like REINFORCE and Actor-Critic algorithms. These directly generalize the gradient bandit from Chapter 2: instead of learning preferences H(a) over discrete actions, you learn a parameterized policy π_θ(a|s) over states and actions. The update rule is nearly identical: move parameters to increase the log-probability of high-reward actions. The gradient bandit is the simplest instance of policy gradient learning — all of modern policy gradient RL builds on this foundation.
