🎯 The Feynman Bridge

Policy Gradients: Learning to Throw Darts

You're learning to play darts. There are two fundamentally different ways to improve:

Value-Based (Chapters 11–12): You memorize the value of hitting each position on the board. Then, to improve, you compute which position has the highest value and aim there. You're thinking about the target.

Policy-Based (This Chapter): You don't memorize values. Instead, you directly improve your throwing technique. After each throw, you slightly adjust your aim in the direction that would have scored more points. Good throws reinforce themselves. Bad throws get discouraged. You're learning the motion itself.

Key Insight

Policy gradients are direct learning of behavior. Instead of asking "what's the best action?" (value), you ask "how do I improve my action probabilities?" The answer is: nudge your policy in the direction that recent rewards pointed.

This idea is the foundation of modern deep RL. ChatGPT's training uses policy gradients (via RLHF). AlphaGo used them. They're the workhorses of modern AI.

⚙️ Technical Ascent

Policy Approximation and Its Advantages

Why parameterize the policy? Instead of storing a value for every state (as in tabular methods), we learn a function that maps states to action probabilities. This function is parameterized by weights θ.

The policy is: — the probability of action a in state s, given parameters θ.

Definition: Policy Parameterization

A parameterized policy is a function that outputs action probabilities. Common choices: softmax over linear features, neural networks.

Advantages of Policy Gradients:

  • Natural for stochastic policies: In games like poker, you need randomness. Value methods force you to construct stochastic policies from deterministic values. Policy methods are inherently stochastic.
  • Better convergence: Directly optimizing the objective (expected return) can avoid local optima that plague value iteration.
  • Handles continuous action spaces: Value methods struggle with continuous spaces. Policy methods use Gaussian policies: .
  • More data-efficient: One policy update can affect the probability of multiple actions, not just the greedy action.
  • Basis for deep RL: Policy gradients scale to deep networks, enabling the trillion-parameter models of today.
👑 The Crown Jewel

The Policy Gradient Theorem

The Policy Gradient Theorem is the mathematical heart of everything that follows. It tells us how to adjust the policy parameters to maximize expected return.

The Policy Gradient Theorem (Sutton & Barto 13.3)

$$\nabla J(\theta) \propto \sum_s \mu(s) \sum_a q_\pi(s,a) \nabla \ln \pi(a|s,\theta)$$

where:

  • $J(\theta) = v_{\pi_\theta}(s_0)$ is the expected return from the start state
  • $\mu(s)$ is the stationary distribution of states under $\pi_\theta$
  • $q_\pi(s,a)$ is the action-value function
  • $\nabla \ln \pi(a|s,\theta)$ is the score (gradient of log-policy)

What does it mean?

The gradient of expected return is proportional to a weighted sum of score functions, where the weights are action values. In words:

Intuition

Increase the probability of good actions, decrease the probability of bad actions. How much? By an amount proportional to how much better (or worse) that action is than average.

⊕ Proof Sketch ▼

Starting from $J(\theta) = v_{\pi_\theta}(s_0)$:

$$v_\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a|s,\theta) q_\pi(s,a)$$

Taking the gradient w.r.t. $\theta$ and applying the product rule:

$$\nabla_\theta v_\pi(s) = \sum_a \left[ \nabla_\theta \pi(a|s,\theta) q_\pi(s,a) + \pi(a|s,\theta) \nabla_\theta q_\pi(s,a) \right]$$

The second term (gradient of action-value) requires policy iteration ideas. The elegant part of the theorem is showing that, upon telescoping across the state distribution, only the first term (gradient of policy) survives. Thus:

$$\nabla J(\theta) \propto \sum_s \mu(s) \sum_a \pi(a|s,\theta) \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(a|s,\theta) q_\pi(s,a)$$

🔄 REINFORCE: Monte Carlo Policy Gradient

The Policy Gradient Theorem tells us the direction to move. REINFORCE is the simplest algorithm that acts on it.

The key insight: replace the unknown $q_\pi(s,a)$ with a Monte Carlo estimate. Run the policy, collect a trajectory, and use the discounted return $G_t$ as an estimate of $q_\pi(S_t, A_t)$.

REINFORCE Update Rule

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \gamma^t G_t \nabla \ln \pi(A_t | S_t, \theta_t)$$

where $G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k R_{t+k+1}$ is the return from time $t$.

What happens:

  • If $G_t > 0$ (good trajectory), we move $\theta$ in the direction of $\nabla \ln \pi(A_t | S_t, \theta_t)$, increasing the probability of actions we just took.
  • If $G_t < 0$ (bad trajectory), we move away, decreasing the probability of those actions.
  • The magnitude of the move is proportional to the return $G_t$.
High Variance Problem

REINFORCE uses full returns $G_t$, which are very noisy. A single lucky episode can push the policy in the wrong direction. This is why we usually need many episodes to converge.

📊 Variance Reduction Without Bias

Brilliant idea: subtract a baseline function from the return. This doesn't bias the gradient, but it cuts variance dramatically.

REINFORCE with Baseline

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \gamma^t (G_t - b(S_t)) \nabla \ln \pi(A_t | S_t, \theta_t)$$

where $b(S_t)$ is any baseline (typically the learned value function $\hat{v}(S_t, w)$).

The quantity $(G_t - b(S_t))$ is called the advantage: how much better is this trajectory than the baseline?

Why Does This Work?

The baseline doesn't introduce bias because $\mathbb{E}[b(S_t) | S_t] = b(S_t)$, and it cancels in the expectation. But it dramatically reduces variance because now we're comparing to a reference point, not to zero.

In practice, the best baseline is a learned value function. So we train two networks:

  • Policy network (actor): $\pi(a|s,\theta)$
  • Value network (critic): $\hat{v}(s,w)$

This is the birth of Actor-Critic methods.

🎭 Actor-Critic Methods

REINFORCE with baseline hints at a better idea: learn the value function. This gives us the Actor-Critic architecture, one of the most important patterns in modern RL.

The insight: Train two networks simultaneously:

ACTOR
Learns the policy
π(a|s,θ)
← TD Error → ⇄
CRITIC
Learns the value
v̂(s,w)

The critic computes the TD error (advantage):

TD Error / Advantage

$$\delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma \hat{v}(S_{t+1}, w) - \hat{v}(S_t, w)$$

This estimates how much better the immediate reward plus next state value is compared to the current value.

The actor uses this TD error to update the policy:

Actor-Critic Update

Actor (Policy):
$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \delta_t \nabla \ln \pi(A_t | S_t, \theta_t)$$

Critic (Value):
$$w_{t+1} = w_t + \beta \delta_t \nabla \hat{v}(S_t, w)$$

Why Actor-Critic?

  • Lower variance: The critic (value function) provides a much better baseline than zero, reducing gradient noise.
  • Online learning: We can update after every step, not just at episode end (REINFORCE is batch).
  • Bootstrapping: The critic bootstraps from future estimates, allowing learning from single transitions.
  • Modern backbone: A2C, A3C, PPO, and TRPO all build on Actor-Critic.
Actor-Critic Interpretation

Actor: "I'll try this action."
Critic: "That was better (or worse) than expected. Here's the TD error."
Actor: "Great feedback! I'll adjust my policy."

📐 Policy Gradient for Continuous Actions

Discrete actions are easy: softmax the output. But what about continuous actions (robot arm angles, vehicle acceleration)?

Solution: Use a Gaussian (normal distribution) as the policy. The network outputs the mean and variance.

Gaussian Policy for Continuous Actions

$$\pi(a|s,\theta) = \mathcal{N}(\mu(s,\theta), \sigma(s,\theta)) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(a-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

where the neural network outputs $\mu(s,\theta)$ (mean) and $\log \sigma^2(s,\theta)$ (log variance).

The log-probability (needed for REINFORCE) is:

$$\ln \pi(a|s,\theta) = -\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{a-\mu}2}{\sigma^2}\right) - \ln(\sigma\sqrt{2\pi})$$

And the gradient (score) is elegant:

$$\nabla_\theta \ln \pi(a|s,\theta) = \frac{a - \mu(s,\theta)}{\sigma^2} \nabla_\theta \mu(s,\theta)$$

Intuitively: if the action is above the mean ($a > \mu$), push the mean upward. If below, push downward. Proportional to how far off we were.

Advantage: Exploration

The variance $\sigma$ controls exploration. Early in training, the network keeps $\sigma$ high (explore widely). As the policy improves, $\sigma$ typically decreases (exploit). This natural curriculum emerges automatically!

🐍 Algorithm: REINFORCE & Actor-Critic

Here's annotated Python code for both algorithms. Study how each line maps to the mathematics above.

Python 
import numpy as np
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.optim as optim

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# POLICY NETWORK (Discrete Actions, Softmax)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
class PolicyNetwork(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, state_dim, action_dim, hidden=64):
        super().__init__()
        # Maps state → action logits
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(state_dim, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, action_dim)
        )

    def forward(self, s):
        """Returns action logits (before softmax)."""
        return self.net(s)

    def get_policy(self, s):
        """π(a|s,θ) = softmax over logits."""
        logits = self.forward(s)
        return torch.softmax(logits, dim=-1)

    def log_prob(self, s, a):
        """ln π(a|s,θ) = ln(softmax[a])."""
        logits = self.forward(s)
        log_probs = torch.log_softmax(logits, dim=-1)
        return log_probs[a]

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# VALUE NETWORK (for Critic / Baseline)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
class ValueNetwork(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, state_dim, hidden=64):
        super().__init__()
        # Maps state → scalar value estimate
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(state_dim, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, 1)
        )

    def forward(self, s):
        """v̂(s,w) scalar value."""
        return self.net(s).squeeze(-1)

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# REINFORCE: Monte Carlo Policy Gradient
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
def reinforce_episode(env, policy, gamma=0.99, alpha=0.01):
    """
    Run one episode of REINFORCE.
    Update rule: θ ← θ + α γ^t G_t ∇ln π(a|s,θ)
    """
    s, _ = env.reset()
    log_probs = []
    returns = []
    reward_sum = 0

    # Collect trajectory
    for t in range(500):  # max steps per episode
        s_tensor = torch.tensor(s, dtype=torch.float32)

        # Sample action from policy
        pi = policy.get_policy(s_tensor.unsqueeze(0))
        a = torch.multinomial(pi, 1).item()

        # Store log probability
        log_prob = policy.log_prob(s_tensor, a)
        log_probs.append(log_prob)

        # Take step in environment
        s_next, r, done, _, _ = env.step(a)
        reward_sum += r
        s = s_next

        if done:
            break

    # Compute returns (backwards from end of episode)
    G = 0
    for t in range(len(log_probs) - 1, -1, -1):
        G = reward_sum if t == len(log_probs) - 1 else (reward_sum * (gamma ** (len(log_probs) - 1 - t)))
        returns.insert(0, G)

    # REINFORCE update: ∇J(θ) ∝ Σ γ^t G_t ∇ln π
    policy_loss = 0
    for t, log_prob in enumerate(log_probs):
        # Update: θ ← θ + α * γ^t * G_t * ∇ln π
        policy_loss -= (gamma ** t) * returns[t] * log_prob

    policy_loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
    optimizer.zero_grad()

    return reward_sum

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# ACTOR-CRITIC: Online Policy Gradient with Value Baseline
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
def actor_critic_step(s, a, r, s_next, done, policy, value_net,
                       policy_opt, value_opt, gamma=0.99, alpha=0.01, beta=0.01):
    """
    One step of actor-critic learning.
    Actor update:  θ ← θ + α δ_t ∇ln π(a|s,θ)
    Critic update: w ← w + β δ_t ∇v̂(s,w)
    """
    s_tensor = torch.tensor(s, dtype=torch.float32).unsqueeze(0)
    s_next_tensor = torch.tensor(s_next, dtype=torch.float32).unsqueeze(0)

    # ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    # CRITIC: Compute TD error δ_t
    # ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    v_s = value_net(s_tensor)
    v_s_next = value_net(s_next_tensor)

    # TD error: δ_t = R_{t+1} + γ v̂(s',w) - v̂(s,w)
    if done:
        delta = torch.tensor(r, dtype=torch.float32) - v_s
    else:
        delta = (torch.tensor(r, dtype=torch.float32) +
                gamma * v_s_next - v_s)

    # ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    # CRITIC UPDATE: w ← w + β δ_t ∇v̂(s,w)
    # ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    value_loss = -(delta.detach() * v_s).mean()
    value_opt.zero_grad()
    value_loss.backward()
    value_opt.step()

    # ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    # ACTOR UPDATE: θ ← θ + α δ_t ∇ln π(a|s,θ)
    # ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    log_prob = policy.log_prob(s_tensor.squeeze(0), a)

    # Actor loss: maximize δ_t * ln π
    actor_loss = -(delta.detach() * log_prob).mean()
    policy_opt.zero_grad()
    actor_loss.backward()
    policy_opt.step()

    return float(delta.item())

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# MAIN TRAINING LOOP
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
if __name__ == "__main__":
    import gymnasium as gym

    env = gym.make("CartPole-v1")
    state_dim = env.observation_space.shape[0]
    action_dim = env.action_space.n

    policy = PolicyNetwork(state_dim, action_dim)
    value_net = ValueNetwork(state_dim)

    policy_opt = optim.Adam(policy.parameters(), lr=0.01)
    value_opt = optim.Adam(value_net.parameters(), lr=0.01)

    # Actor-Critic training
    for episode in range(500):
        s, _ = env.reset()
        ep_return = 0

        for step in range(500):
            s_tensor = torch.tensor(s, dtype=torch.float32)

            # Sample action
            pi = policy.get_policy(s_tensor.unsqueeze(0))
            a = torch.multinomial(pi, 1).item()

            # Take step
            s_next, r, done, _, _ = env.step(a)
            ep_return += r

            # Actor-Critic update
            actor_critic_step(s, a, r, s_next, done, policy, value_net,
                            policy_opt, value_opt)

            if done:
                break

            s = s_next

        if episode % 50 == 0:
            print(f"Episode {episode}: Return = {ep_return}")

    env.close()

Key lines to understand:

  • log_prob computation: Maps to $\nabla \ln \pi(a|s,\theta)$ in the theorem.
  • delta calculation: The TD error $\delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma v̂(s',w) - v̂(s,w)$.
  • Actor loss: We negate because PyTorch minimizes, but we want to maximize $\delta_t \ln \pi$.
  • Critic loss: Standard MSE-like loss to fit the value function.
🌉 Deep RL Bridge: From REINFORCE to RLHF

You might be thinking: "Why do I need to learn policy gradients when I could just use value methods?" Great question. Here's why policy gradients rule the modern RL world:

Evolution of Policy Gradient Methods
  • 1992: REINFORCE (Williams). First policy gradient. High variance, slow.
  • 2016: A2C/A3C (Mnih et al.). Asynchronous actor-critic with multiple parallel workers. Scales to many cores.
  • 2017: PPO (Schulman et al.). Trust Region Policy Optimization made practical. Used in GPT-3 and ChatGPT training.
  • 2018–2023: RLHF (Christiano et al., Ouyang et al.). Reward learning + policy gradients. Aligns LLMs to human values. This is how you train ChatGPT.

Why Policy Gradients for LLMs?

Generating text is a continuous policy problem. The "state" is the conversation so far. The "action" is the next token (from a 100K+ action space). Value-based methods struggle with this scale. Policy gradients handle it naturally.

RLHF Pipeline:

  1. Train a policy network (language model) with supervised learning.
  2. Collect human preferences (A vs B response — which is better?).
  3. Train a reward model to predict human preference.
  4. Use policy gradients (PPO) to fine-tune the LLM using the reward model. This maximizes the learned reward while staying close to the original policy.
The Moment It Clicked

ChatGPT's intelligence comes from large-scale supervised pretraining. But its ability to be helpful, harmless, and honest comes from RLHF, which is powered by policy gradients. You're learning the algorithm that aligns AI with human values.

Master Policy Gradient Theory

Test your understanding with these deep questions. Click to reveal answers.

1
Why do we parameterize the policy directly instead of deriving it from a value function (argmax over Q)?
▼

Short Answer: We could, but policy parameterization has advantages: naturally stochastic (good for exploration and multi-modal policies), works with continuous actions, and optimizes the actual objective (expected return) directly.

Long Answer: In tabular settings, deriving a deterministic policy from Q (greedy action) works fine. But in function approximation, extracting a stochastic policy from deterministic action-values is awkward. You'd have to add exploration noise on top. Policy methods are cleaner: the function itself outputs probabilities. Moreover, some problems (poker, games with mixed-strategy equilibria, continuous control) require inherently stochastic policies. Policy gradients give you this for free.

2
Why does subtracting a baseline reduce variance without biasing the gradient?
▼

Key Insight: The baseline $b(S_t)$ depends only on the state, not the action. So when we take the expectation:

$\mathbb{E}_a[-b(S_t) \nabla \ln \pi(a|s,\theta) | S_t] = -b(S_t) \mathbb{E}_a[\nabla \ln \pi(a|s,\theta) | S_t]$

$= -b(S_t) \nabla \mathbb{E}_a[\ln \pi(a|s,\theta) | S_t] = 0$ (score has zero mean)

The baseline cancels! But it reduces variance because $(G_t - b)$ has smaller magnitude than $G_t$ alone if $b$ is a good estimate of $\mathbb{E}[G_t | S_t]$.

3
When would you use REINFORCE vs. Actor-Critic?
▼

REINFORCE: Simple, sample-efficient in the sense that you only need trajectories. Good for problems with small horizon or episodic tasks. No value network to train.

Actor-Critic: Better variance, online updates (can learn from individual steps), works for continuing tasks. Requires training two networks, but the critic bootstraps from future estimates, reducing variance significantly.

In Practice: Actor-Critic dominates. REINFORCE is mostly of pedagogical interest (and it's great for that!). Modern algorithms (A2C, A3C, PPO) are all actor-critic variants.

4
How does a Gaussian policy for continuous actions compare to discretizing actions?
▼

Discretization: If you discretize a continuous space (e.g., robot arm angles into 100 bins), the action space explodes exponentially in dimensionality. A 5D continuous space might discretize to $100^5 = 10^{10}$ actions. Intractable.

Gaussian Policy: A neural network outputs two scalars: mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma$ for each action dimension. For a 5D arm, that's 10 outputs. Efficient and natural.

Added Benefit: The variance naturally captures exploration. Early in training, $\sigma$ is large (explore). As the policy improves, $\sigma$ decreases (exploit). This emerges automatically without explicit exploration strategies.

5
How is policy gradient training different from supervised learning, and why does this matter for RLHF?
▼

Supervised Learning: You have fixed targets (labels). You minimize the gap between predictions and targets. It's a one-way street: data → model → loss → update.

Policy Gradients: There are no fixed targets. The "target" is the return (which depends on the policy's behavior). The reward signal comes from the environment (or a learned reward model). It's a feedback loop: policy → behavior → reward → gradient → better policy.

Why It Matters for RLHF: In RLHF, you start with a supervised LLM. You can't just continue supervised learning (there are no more human labels). Instead, you use a learned reward model (human preference predictor) as the signal, then use policy gradients to optimize the LLM to maximize that reward while staying close to the original policy (via KL penalty or trust region). It's a fundamentally different learning paradigm.
