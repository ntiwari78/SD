🎯 The Feynman Bridge

RL Grew from Studying How Animals Learn

RL didn't emerge from computer science alone — it grew from studying how animals learn. Pavlov's dog salivating at a bell is classical conditioning. A rat pressing a lever for food is instrumental conditioning. The TD error in RL is the computational equivalent of the "surprise" that drives learning in the brain.

When you eat something poisoned, your brain instantly updates its prediction: "avoid this food." When you solve a tricky puzzle and feel a rush of dopamine, that's a reward signal updating your behavior. These are not metaphors — they are the same learning mechanisms.

The Bridge: From Behavior to Algorithms

Classical Conditioning: A stimulus (bell) predicts a reward (food). The brain learns the association and updates predictions over time — exactly what the Rescorla-Wagner model formalizes.

Instrumental Conditioning: An action (lever press) leads to a reward (pellet). The brain strengthens the action-reward association — this is what RL policy updates do.

Temporal-Difference Learning: The dopamine signal in the brain encodes a TD error: the difference between predicted and actual reward. Neurons in the ventral tegmental area literally compute this, firing more when outcomes are better than expected.

The remarkable truth: the algorithms we derive from first principles in RL match what evolution has built into animal brains.

📖 The Technical Ascent

Classical and Instrumental Conditioning

Classical Conditioning: Prediction Learning

Pavlov (1927): Ring a bell (CS), then present food (US). After many trials, the dog salivates at the bell alone. The brain has learned that the bell predicts food. This is fundamentally about learning to make predictions — the core of value function learning in RL.

Key phenomena emerge that both Pavlov and modern RL must explain:

  • Blocking: If a dog already predicts food from bell A, introducing bell A + light simultaneously won't make the light a strong predictor. Why? Because the outcome (food) is already fully predicted by A. The light is redundant. This was explained by Rescorla and Wagner (1972).
  • Higher-Order Conditioning: Bell 1 predicts bell 2, which predicts food. The dog learns bell 1 → bell 2 → food. The brain chains predictions together, building hierarchies of expectations.
  • Extinction: Ring the bell without food many times. The response fades. The brain updates its model: "bell no longer predicts food."
  • Spontaneous Recovery: After extinction, wait a while. Ring the bell again — the response partially returns! The old association isn't erased; it's inhibited by a newer, contradictory expectation.

Instrumental Conditioning: Action Learning

Skinner (1938): A rat in a box. Press a lever, get a food pellet. The rat learns to press the lever more often. This is action-value learning: Q(s, a) — the expected return from pressing the lever in this context.

Instruments are actions that control rewards. The brain learns to repeat actions with high expected value. This is exactly what Q-learning and policy gradient methods do: they maximize expected cumulative reward by learning which actions are valuable.

Delayed Reinforcement: Why TD Makes Sense

Neither classical nor instrumental conditioning assumes immediate rewards. A rat might press a lever, and food arrives seconds later. The brain must somehow credit the lever press for the delayed reward — this is the credit assignment problem. The solution: learn predictions at each step, and use temporal differences to update.

Why Delayed Rewards Matter for RL

In most real problems, actions have consequences far in the future. An investment decision today pays off in years. A chess move's value depends on moves 5 steps ahead. The brain solves this through prediction chains: each state predicts the next state's value, and errors at each step propagate backward. This is exactly bootstrapping in TD learning.

⚙️ Mathematical Foundation

The Rescorla-Wagner Model and Cognitive Maps

Rescorla-Wagner (1972): A Formal Model of Blocking

Rescorla and Wagner proposed a simple learning rule for the associative strength V of a stimulus:

📐 Rescorla-Wagner Update Rule
▼
$$\Delta V = \alpha \beta (\lambda - V_{total})$$

Where:
• $\Delta V$ = change in associative strength
• $\alpha$ = learning rate (salience of the CS)
• $\beta$ = learning rate (salience of the US)
• $\lambda$ = maximum associative strength (unconditioned response magnitude)
• $V_{total}$ = total predicted value (sum of all active stimuli)

Interpretation: Learning is proportional to the prediction error: $\lambda - V_{total}$. If you already predict the full reward, the error is zero, and you learn nothing. This explains blocking perfectly.

Why Rescorla-Wagner Explains Blocking:

  • Before bell A + light: bell A predicts food → $V_A = \lambda$ (fully learned). When light is added: $\Delta V_{light} = \alpha \beta (\lambda - (\lambda + V_{light})) = -\alpha \beta V_{light}$ (negative! decreases learning). The light doesn't predict anything new.
  • Blocking elegantly falls out of the error term. Only prediction errors drive learning.

Temporal-Difference Model: The Brain's Dopamine Signal

The Rescorla-Wagner model works for simultaneous stimuli, but it struggles with temporal chains. If bell → light → food (with delays), how should learning happen?

The answer: use predictions at each time step. This is the TD model:

📐 Temporal-Difference Learning
▼
$$V(s_t) \leftarrow V(s_t) + \alpha [r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)]$$

Where:
• $r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1})$ = bootstrapped estimate of return
• $r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$ = TD error (prediction error)
• $\alpha$ = learning rate

Key difference from Rescorla-Wagner: The error is not between final outcome and prediction, but between predicted return from this step and current value estimate. Errors propagate through time via bootstrapping.

The Brain's TD Signal: Dopamine neurons in the ventral tegmental area (VTA) fire in response to rewards, but they also fire to predictors of rewards. Crucially, they fire more when outcomes exceed predictions — the TD error! This neural signal literally computes $r + \gamma V(s') - V(s)$. The discovery (Schultz et al., 1997) that dopamine encodes a TD error was revolutionary, proving RL had deep biological roots.

Cognitive Maps and Habitual vs Goal-Directed

Tolman (1948): Rats build internal maps of their environment. They don't just learn stimulus-response associations; they learn the spatial structure and can take novel shortcuts. This is model-based RL: learning a world model and planning with it.

Modern neuroscience has identified place cells and grid cells in the hippocampus that literally encode position and geometry. These support mental simulation: imagining future states and planning paths through them.

Habitual vs Goal-Directed:

  • Habitual (Model-Free): Automatically press lever → food. No thought. Striatum learns action-values directly. Fast, but inflexible. If the reward changes or the environment shifts, habit is slow to update.
  • Goal-Directed (Model-Based): "Pressing the lever leads to food; I want food; therefore, press lever." Uses a model of the world. Slower (requires planning), but flexible. When contingencies change, goal-directed behavior adapts quickly.

The brain uses both, and they compete. Extensive training makes behavior habitual (striatum dominates). Devaluation of the goal (poison the food) makes behavior goal-directed again (prefrontal cortex + hippocampus take over, consulting the model).

📊 Comparative Analysis

Rescorla-Wagner vs Temporal-Difference Models

Both models explain classical conditioning through prediction errors, but they differ in scope and assumptions. Here's how they compare:

Dimension Rescorla-Wagner (1972) Temporal-Difference (1988+)
Scope Simultaneous/overlapping stimuli predicting a single outcome Temporal sequences; handles chains of predictions (Bell → Light → Food)
Update Signal $\lambda - V_{total}$: Error between final outcome and sum of predictions $r + \gamma V(s') - V(s)$: Error between bootstrapped estimate and current value
Blocking Phenomenon Perfectly explains: no learning when error is zero Also explains; more general treatment via value functions
Computational Form Associative learning rule; each stimulus has a weight Value function approximation; bootstrapping uses V(s') estimate
Real-World Advantage Elegant for static, simultaneous prediction problems Handles delayed rewards, long chains, and planning problems
Neural Evidence Explains classical conditioning behavior patterns well Matches dopamine neuron firing (Schultz et al., 1997)
Extension Capability Limited to linear combinations of stimuli Extends to function approximation, policy learning, model-based planning
Why TD is Stronger

Rescorla-Wagner is a special case. When all predictions are simultaneous and lead to a single outcome, RW is elegant. But in any temporal sequence, RW breaks down. TD's bootstrap mechanism — using V(s') to estimate the return — allows it to handle arbitrary temporal depth and complexity. This is why TD became the foundation of modern RL.

🧠 Core Concepts in Depth

Blocking, Higher-Order, and the TD Model's Reach

The Blocking Phenomenon

Experiment:

  • Stage 1: Bell A repeatedly paired with food. Dog learns: A → food.
  • Stage 2: Bell A + Light repeatedly paired with food. Dog learns A → food (no change). But light does not become a predictor of food.
  • Test: Light alone does not trigger salivation. Why?

Rescorla-Wagner Explanation: In stage 2, the outcome (food) is already fully predicted by A, so $\lambda - V_{total} = \lambda - \lambda = 0$. The light doesn't improve predictions, so it learns nothing. Prediction error is zero → no learning. Simple and beautiful.

Computational Insight for RL: This is exactly why sample efficiency matters. If you already have a good value estimate, new information that doesn't reduce prediction error is ignored. This is core to why TD learning is sample-efficient compared to Monte Carlo methods.

Higher-Order Conditioning

Experiment:

  • Bell A paired with food (dogs learn A → food).
  • Bell B paired with Bell A (no food). After enough trials, Bell B triggers salivation, even though B was never paired with food directly.

The Model: Bell B predicts Bell A, which predicts food. In TD terms, B's value is learned through the chain: V(B) learns to predict V(A), and V(A) predicts rewards. This is bootstrapping across a chain of predictions.

In modern RL, this is handled by multi-step lookahead: $r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \gamma^2 V(s_{t+2})$. The brain does the same thing, and it demonstrates that learning is fundamentally about building prediction chains.

Delayed Reinforcement and Credit Assignment

A rat presses a lever at time $t$, and food arrives at time $t+3$. Which action gets credit? The brain solves this by learning value functions at each step. The value at $t$ incorporates the expected value at $t+1$, which includes $t+2$, which includes $t+3$ when the reward arrives. This credit assignment is automatic when you use bootstrapping.

Habitual vs Goal-Directed Behavior

The Competition: After extensive training, behavior becomes habitual — run the learned policy automatically without thinking. But if the goal is devalued (food is now poisoned), goal-directed behavior quickly adapts. The brain has two systems:

  • Model-Free (Striatum): Directly learns Q(s,a) or V(s). Fast, but requires retraining on environment changes.
  • Model-Based (Prefrontal + Hippocampus): Learns world model and plans. Slower, but adapts instantly when goals or transitions change.

This dual-system competition is reflected in RL: algorithms like AlphaGo use both tree search (model-based planning with a learned model) and value networks (model-free estimates). The brain does the same.

Cognitive Maps in the Brain

Place cells in the hippocampus fire when an animal is in a specific location. Grid cells fire in repeating spatial patterns. Together, they encode an internal map of the environment. This allows mental simulation: imagining moving through the map without actually moving. In RL, this is planning with a learned model. The convergence is striking.

❓ Socratic Sandbox

Test Your Understanding: 5 Psychology-RL Connections

Click each question to reveal the answer. Think about how psychology and RL are really the same science, just studied from different angles.

1
Why does the blocking phenomenon prove that learning is driven by prediction error, not raw association?
▼

The Blocking Puzzle: If learning were just strengthening association between stimulus and outcome, then light should learn to predict food in stage 2 (even though A already does). But it doesn't.

The Error-Based Solution: Learning requires surprise — a gap between prediction and outcome. When A already predicts the full outcome, adding light doesn't increase surprise. The error $\lambda - V_{total} = 0$. Zero error, zero learning. This is the heart of Rescorla-Wagner and all prediction-error models, including TD learning in RL.

This proves that the brain is not a simple associator; it's a prediction-error minimizer. And that's exactly what RL algorithms do.

2
How does the TD model extend Rescorla-Wagner to handle temporal sequences like Bell → Light → Food?
▼

RW's Limitation: Rescorla-Wagner computes error as $\lambda - V_{total}$, where $\lambda$ is the final outcome. This assumes all stimuli converge on a single outcome value. In temporal chains, there is no single final value — each state has its own expected future return.

TD's Solution: Instead of comparing to a final outcome, compare each step's prediction to the next step's prediction plus immediate reward: $r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$. This "bootstraps" — uses existing value estimates to generate training targets. The error at each step updates that step's value, and errors naturally flow backward through time.

Why It Matters: TD handles arbitrary depth and complexity. A 100-step sequence of predictions? TD handles it naturally. The Rescorla-Wagner framework would collapse under temporal complexity.

3
Why do dopamine neurons fire to predictors of reward, not just to rewards themselves?
▼

The Experimental Observation: Schultz et al. (1997) found that dopamine neurons initially fire to food (the unconditioned stimulus). But after conditioning, they fire to the bell (conditioned stimulus) and stop firing to the food itself.

The TD Explanation: Dopamine encodes the TD error: $r + \gamma V(s') - V(s)$. Before learning, the food is surprising, so the error is large (dopamine fires). After learning, the bell perfectly predicts food, so when the bell arrives, the error is zero (dopamine doesn't fire). But when the bell arrives and the predicted food doesn't come, the error becomes negative (dopamine dips).

Dopamine tracks surprise, not reward. This is the neural signature of TD learning in the brain.

4
How do cognitive maps enable mental simulation, and what is this in RL terms?
▼

Cognitive Maps in the Brain: Rats build spatial maps (place cells, grid cells). These allow them to imagine future paths without moving. A rat can "think" about going left, then imagine what it would see, then decide if that's a good move.

In RL Terms: This is model-based planning. The rat has learned a model: state transitions $p(s'|s,a)$ and expected rewards $r(s,a)$. It can then use this model to plan: simulate trajectories and pick the best one. This is different from model-free learning, where the agent just memorizes action-values without understanding the world.

The Convergence: Modern RL combines both: AlphaGo uses a learned world model (neural network) to plan via tree search, combined with a value network (model-free estimate). The brain does the same with its two systems — planning with maps (model-based) and learned habits (model-free).

5
Why does devaluing a goal (e.g., poisoning the food) quickly change goal-directed behavior but not habitual behavior?
▼

The Experiment: After extensive training, a rat presses a lever for food automatically (habit). You then poison the food. A goal-directed rat instantly stops pressing. A habitual rat keeps pressing and gets poisoned.

The Systems Explanation:

Model-Based (Goal-Directed): "Pressing lever → food; food is poisoned; don't press." The rat consults its world model and instantly recomputes the optimal policy. Change to one value → instant behavior change.

Model-Free (Habitual): "Pressing lever has high Q-value." The striatum has learned this through repeated experience. A single outcome change doesn't update all the old training. The habit persists until extinction training (repeated non-rewarded presses) updates Q(s,a).

In RL: This is the sample-efficiency vs. flexibility tradeoff. Model-free (Q-learning) is sample-efficient after training but slow to adapt. Model-based (planning with learned model) is slower per sample but adapts instantly to new information. The brain uses both, and modern RL should too.
