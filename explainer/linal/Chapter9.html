9.1

LU-Decompositions

Gaussian elimination is powerful but wasteful if you need to solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ for many different right-hand sides $\mathbf{b}$. The LU-decomposition factorizes $A=LU$ once, recording the elimination steps, so each new $\mathbf{b}$ costs only two triangular solves — not a full elimination.

Think of LU like precomputing the "recipe" to cook a meal. The LU factorization is doing all the prep work once (chopping vegetables, making sauces). Then serving each guest ($\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \ldots$) is fast — just assemble and plate. Without LU, you'd re-chop everything for each guest. With LU, the expensive prep is amortized.

LU-DECOMPOSITION

A square matrix $A$ has an LU-decomposition $A=LU$ if:

  • $L$ is a unit lower triangular matrix (lower triangular with 1's on diagonal)
  • $U$ is an upper triangular matrix

To solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$, split into two triangular systems:

$$LU\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \;\Longrightarrow\; L\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{b}\;\text{(forward substitution)},\quad U\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{y}\;\text{(back substitution)}$$

Computing the LU Factorization

Perform Gaussian elimination on $A$ to produce $U$ (no row swaps needed for LU without pivoting). Record the multipliers $m_{ij}=a_{ij}/a_{jj}$ used to eliminate entry $(i,j)$.
$U$ = the upper triangular result of elimination (including the diagonal pivots).
$L$ = the unit lower triangular matrix whose below-diagonal entry $(i,j)$ is the multiplier $m_{ij}$. Diagonal entries of $L$ are all 1.
Verify: $LU = A$.
For each new right-hand side $\mathbf{b}$: solve $L\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{b}$ by forward substitution, then $U\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{y}$ by back substitution.
Example — 3×3 LU Decomposition. $$A=\begin{pmatrix}2&4&-2\\4&9&-3\\-2&-3&7\end{pmatrix}$$

Step 1: Eliminate column 1. $m_{21}=4/2=2$, $m_{31}=-2/2=-1$.

After row ops: $\begin{pmatrix}2&4&-2\\0&1&1\\0&1&5\end{pmatrix}$

Step 2: Eliminate column 2. $m_{32}=1/1=1$.

$U=\begin{pmatrix}2&4&-2\\0&1&1\\0&0&4\end{pmatrix}$, $\quad L=\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\2&1&0\\-1&1&1\end{pmatrix}$

Verify: $LU=A$ ✓. Now $\det(A)=\det(L)\cdot\det(U)=1\cdot(2\cdot 1\cdot 4)=8$.

THEOREM — Existence of LU

A square matrix $A$ has an LU decomposition (without row interchanges) if and only if all leading principal submatrices of $A$ are non-singular. If row interchanges are required, use the PLU decomposition: $PA=LU$ where $P$ is a permutation matrix.

Complexity: LU costs $O(n^3/3)$ multiplications to compute, then each solve costs $O(n^2)$. For $k$ right-hand sides, total cost is $O(n^3/3 + kn^2)$ vs. $O(kn^3/3)$ without factorization.

LU and Determinants. Once $A=LU$, computing $\det(A)$ is trivial: $$\det(A) = \det(L)\cdot\det(U) = 1 \cdot (u_{11}\cdot u_{22}\cdots u_{nn}) = \prod_{i=1}^n u_{ii}$$ This is $O(n^2)$ extra work after paying the $O(n^3)$ LU cost — far better than cofactor expansion's $O(n!)$.

The LU factorization of $A=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&1\end{pmatrix}$ fails without row interchanges. Why? What permutation matrix $P$ fixes it?
Show Answer

The $(1,1)$ entry is $0$, so the first pivot is zero — elimination fails immediately. Use $P=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ (swap rows 1 and 2): $PA=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$, which already is $U$ with $L=I$. So $PA=LU$ with $L=I$, $U=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$.

9.2

The Power Method

The Power Method is the simplest iterative algorithm for finding the dominant eigenvalue (the one largest in absolute value) and its eigenvector. It works by repeatedly multiplying a vector by $A$: dominant components grow, others shrink, until only the dominant eigenvector survives.

Imagine you start with a random mixture of musical notes playing simultaneously. You pass the mixture through an amplifier that boosts certain frequencies more than others. After many passes, the loudest frequency — the dominant one — drowns out all others. The Power Method does the same with eigenvectors: repeated multiplication by $A$ amplifies the component in the direction of the largest eigenvalue, until it dominates.

THE POWER METHOD ALGORITHM

Given $A$ with dominant eigenvalue $|\lambda_1|>|\lambda_2|\geq\cdots$. Start with any vector $\mathbf{x}_0$ (not orthogonal to the dominant eigenvector).

For $k=1,2,3,\ldots$:

  1. Compute $\mathbf{y}_k=A\mathbf{x}_{k-1}$
  2. Let $m_k=\max_i |y_{k,i}|$ (the largest-magnitude component — the Rayleigh quotient approximation)
  3. Normalize: $\mathbf{x}_k = \mathbf{y}_k / m_k$
  4. As $k\to\infty$: $m_k\to\lambda_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_k\to$ dominant eigenvector $\mathbf{p}_1$

Convergence rate: $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|^k \to 0$ — faster when the eigenvalue gap is large.

Why it works. Write $\mathbf{x}_0=c_1\mathbf{p}_1+c_2\mathbf{p}_2+\cdots$ in the eigenbasis. Then $A^k\mathbf{x}_0=c_1\lambda_1^k\mathbf{p}_1+c_2\lambda_2^k\mathbf{p}_2+\cdots = \lambda_1^k\bigl(c_1\mathbf{p}_1 + c_2(\lambda_2/\lambda_1)^k\mathbf{p}_2+\cdots\bigr)$. Since $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|<1$, the remaining terms vanish, leaving $\lambda_1^k c_1\mathbf{p}_1$. Normalization removes the $\lambda_1^k$ scalar, revealing $\mathbf{p}_1$.

INVERSE POWER METHOD

To find the smallest eigenvalue (in magnitude), apply the Power Method to $A^{-1}$:

  • Eigenvalues of $A^{-1}$ are $1/\lambda_i$, so the smallest $|\lambda_i|$ becomes the largest $|1/\lambda_i|$
  • Each iteration requires solving $A\mathbf{y}_k=\mathbf{x}_{k-1}$ (use LU!) — no explicit inversion needed

Shifted inverse power method: To find the eigenvalue nearest to $\sigma$, apply inverse power to $(A-\sigma I)$.

What goes wrong with the Power Method if $A$ has two eigenvalues equal in magnitude, e.g., $\lambda_1=3$ and $\lambda_2=-3$?
Show Answer

The method fails to converge: since $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|=1$, the ratio doesn't decay. The iterates oscillate between two vectors, never stabilizing. Fix: use $A^2$ (which has dominant eigenvalue $9 > 9 = (-3)^2$... wait, both equal 9). Actually $A^2$ has eigenvalue $9$ with multiplicity 2 — still degenerate. Need a more sophisticated approach (e.g., QR algorithm) for equal-magnitude eigenvalues.

9.3

Comparison of Methods for Linear Systems

We now have several strategies for solving $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$. Understanding their trade-offs — in accuracy, speed, and memory — is essential for building reliable numerical software.

Choosing a solver is like choosing a route to work. Gaussian elimination is the straightforward highway — reliable, general-purpose. LU decomposition is a toll road — upfront cost but fast for repeat trips. Iterative methods are back roads — slower per trip but much less memory, ideal for huge sparse problems. The right choice depends on your traffic conditions (matrix structure) and how many trips you're making.

CONDITION NUMBER — Numerical Sensitivity

The condition number of $A$ (for 2-norm) is:

$$\kappa(A)=\|A\|\cdot\|A^{-1}\|=\frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_n}$$

where $\sigma_1\geq\sigma_n>0$ are the largest and smallest singular values. Key meaning:

  • $\kappa=1$: perfectly conditioned (orthogonal matrices)
  • $\kappa\sim 10^k$: you lose roughly $k$ digits of precision in the solution
  • $\kappa\gg 1$: ill-conditioned — small errors in $\mathbf{b}$ cause huge errors in $\mathbf{x}$
The Perturbation Bound. If $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ and $A(\mathbf{x}+\delta\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{b}+\delta\mathbf{b}$, then: $$\frac{\|\delta\mathbf{x}\|}{\|\mathbf{x}\|}\leq\kappa(A)\frac{\|\delta\mathbf{b}\|}{\|\mathbf{b}\|}$$ The condition number amplifies relative errors. With $\kappa=10^8$ and IEEE double precision ($\varepsilon\approx 10^{-16}$), you only have $16-8=8$ reliable digits in the solution.

CHOLESKY DECOMPOSITION

If $A$ is symmetric positive definite (SPD), then $A=LL^T$ where $L$ is a unique lower triangular matrix with positive diagonal. Advantages:

  • Stores only $n(n+1)/2$ entries (lower triangle) — half the storage
  • $\approx n^3/6$ operations — twice as fast as LU
  • Always numerically stable (no pivoting needed for SPD)
  • Applications: covariance matrix inversions, normal equations in regression
Condition Numbers in Machine Learning. The condition number of the Gram matrix $X^TX$ determines the stability of least-squares regression: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}=(X^TX)^{-1}X^T\mathbf{y}$. Multicollinearity (near-linear dependence among features) drives $\kappa(X^TX)$ sky-high, making coefficients wildly unstable. Ridge regression ($\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}=(X^TX+\lambda I)^{-1}X^T\mathbf{y}$) adds $\lambda$ to all singular values, reducing $\kappa$ from $\sigma_1/\sigma_n$ to $(\sigma_1+\lambda)/(\sigma_n+\lambda)$.

9.4

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

The Singular Value Decomposition is arguably the most important matrix factorization in all of applied mathematics. Unlike eigendecomposition (restricted to square matrices), SVD works for any matrix — revealing the fundamental geometric action as a rotation, followed by a scaling, followed by another rotation.

Every matrix $A$ — regardless of shape — performs a sequence of three geometric operations: (1) rotate/reflect in the input space, (2) stretch/compress along orthogonal axes (possibly change dimension), (3) rotate/reflect in the output space. SVD finds these three operations explicitly: $A=U\Sigma V^T$. The "stretching factors" $\sigma_i$ are the singular values — they tell you exactly how much the transformation magnifies each direction.

SINGULAR VALUE DECOMPOSITION

For any $m\times n$ matrix $A$ (real), there exist orthogonal matrices $U_{m\times m}$ and $V_{n\times n}$, and a "diagonal" matrix $\Sigma_{m\times n}$ with non-negative entries, such that:

$$A = U\Sigma V^T$$

  • $U$: columns are the left singular vectors (eigenvectors of $AA^T$)
  • $\Sigma$: diagonal $\sigma_1\geq\sigma_2\geq\cdots\geq 0$ are the singular values
  • $V$: columns are the right singular vectors (eigenvectors of $A^TA$)

Key relation: $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^TA)} = \sqrt{\lambda_i(AA^T)}$ (non-negative square roots of eigenvalues of $A^TA$).

Computing SVD: Step-by-Step

Form $A^TA$ (symmetric, positive semidefinite). Find its eigenvalues $\lambda_1\geq\lambda_2\geq\cdots\geq 0$ and orthonormal eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_n$.
Singular values: $\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i}$. The rank of $A$ equals the number of non-zero $\sigma_i$.
For $i=1,\ldots,r$ (rank): compute $\mathbf{u}_i=\frac{1}{\sigma_i}A\mathbf{v}_i$ (these are orthonormal by construction).
Extend $\{\mathbf{u}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{u}_r\}$ to a full orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^m$ (e.g., by Gram-Schmidt). Assemble $U,\Sigma,V$.
Verify $A=U\Sigma V^T$ and check that $\|A\|_2=\sigma_1$ (the largest singular value is the operator norm).
Example — SVD of a 2×2 Matrix. $$A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$$

$A^TA=\begin{pmatrix}10&6\\6&10\end{pmatrix}$. Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1=16,\lambda_2=4$. Singular values: $\sigma_1=4, \sigma_2=2$.

Eigenvectors of $A^TA$: $\mathbf{v}_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1)^T$, $\mathbf{v}_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1)^T$.

$\mathbf{u}_1=\frac{1}{4}A\mathbf{v}_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1)^T$, $\mathbf{u}_2=\frac{1}{2}A\mathbf{v}_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1)^T$.

Since $A$ is symmetric, $U=V$: $A=U\begin{pmatrix}4&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}U^T$ — the spectral decomposition from Chapter 7.

FUNDAMENTAL THEOREM OF SVD

Every $m\times n$ matrix $A$ of rank $r$ has SVD. Furthermore:

  • Rank: $\text{rank}(A)=$ number of non-zero singular values
  • Norm: $\|A\|_2=\sigma_1$ (largest singular value)
  • Frobenius Norm: $\|A\|_F=\sqrt{\sigma_1^2+\cdots+\sigma_r^2}$
  • Condition Number: $\kappa_2(A)=\sigma_1/\sigma_r$ (for square invertible $A$)
  • Pseudoinverse: $A^+=V\Sigma^+U^T$ where $\Sigma^+$ replaces each $\sigma_i$ with $1/\sigma_i$

SVD is the mathematical heart of: PCA (left singular vectors of centered data = principal components), Latent Semantic Analysis (SVD of term-document matrix), Collaborative Filtering (Netflix/Spotify recommendation: SVD of user-item rating matrix), image compression, and solving ill-conditioned least-squares problems. If $A$ is an $m\times n$ matrix with $m<n$ (wide matrix), what is the maximum possible rank of $A$? How many non-zero singular values can it have?
Show Answer

$\text{rank}(A)\leq\min(m,n)=m$. So at most $m$ non-zero singular values, even though $A$ has $n$ columns. Geometrically: a wide matrix sends $\mathbb{R}^n$ into an at-most-$m$-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$. The kernel is at least $(n-m)$-dimensional.

9.5

SVD Applications: Low-Rank Approximation

The SVD enables the most powerful compression and approximation theorem in linear algebra: the Eckart-Young Theorem. Truncating the SVD to the top $k$ terms gives the best rank-$k$ approximation of a matrix in any unitarily invariant norm.

Every matrix is a sum of rank-1 "outer products" — each one a scaled snapshot of a single direction. The SVD lists these snapshots in order of importance: $A=\sigma_1\mathbf{u}_1\mathbf{v}_1^T + \sigma_2\mathbf{u}_2\mathbf{v}_2^T + \cdots$. The first snapshot captures the most important pattern; the second captures the next most important. Keeping only the top $k$ snapshots gives the best $k$-dimensional summary — like keeping only the top $k$ themes in a document corpus.

ECKART-YOUNG THEOREM

Let $A=\sum_{i=1}^r\sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^T$ be the full SVD. Define the rank-$k$ truncated SVD:

$$A_k=\sum_{i=1}^k\sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^T$$

Then $A_k$ is the best rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ in both the 2-norm and Frobenius norm:

$$\min_{\text{rank}(B)\leq k}\|A-B\|_2=\|A-A_k\|_2=\sigma_{k+1}$$

$$\min_{\text{rank}(B)\leq k}\|A-B\|_F=\|A-A_k\|_F=\sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2+\cdots+\sigma_r^2}$$

Energy Captured by Top $k$ Components. $$\text{Fraction of "energy"} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k\sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^r\sigma_i^2}=\frac{\|A_k\|_F^2}{\|A\|_F^2}$$ In PCA, this is the "explained variance ratio." Choosing $k$ such that this ratio exceeds 0.95 (95% of variance) is a standard rule of thumb for dimensionality reduction.
Image Compression via SVD. A grayscale image of size $m\times n$ is a matrix $A$ with entries in $[0,255]$. Its rank-$k$ approximation $A_k$ requires storing $k(m+n+1)$ numbers instead of $mn$. The compression ratio is $mn / [k(m+n+1)]$.
  • For a $512\times 512$ image: $mn=262{,}144$ numbers
  • With $k=10$: store $10(512+512+1)=10{,}250$ — compression ratio $\approx 25\times$
  • With $k=50$: store $51{,}250$ — still $5\times$ compression with visually good quality

PSEUDOINVERSE (MOORE-PENROSE)

For any $m\times n$ matrix $A=U\Sigma V^T$, the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse is:

$$A^+=V\Sigma^+U^T$$

where $\Sigma^+$ is obtained from $\Sigma$ by replacing each non-zero $\sigma_i$ with $1/\sigma_i$ (and keeping zeros as zeros). Properties:

  • $AA^+A=A$, $A^+AA^+=A^+$, $(AA^+)^T=AA^+$, $(A^+A)^T=A^+A$
  • Least-squares solution: $\hat{\mathbf{x}}=A^+\mathbf{b}$ minimizes $\|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|$ with minimum norm
  • When $A$ is invertible: $A^+=A^{-1}$

Recommendation Systems. Netflix's collaborative filtering works by computing the SVD of the user×movie rating matrix $R$ (mostly sparse/missing entries). The rank-$k$ approximation $R_k=U_k\Sigma_k V_k^T$ fills in missing ratings. SVD ≠ Eigendecomposition (for rectangular matrices). Eigendecomposition requires a square matrix and may not exist (non-diagonalizable). SVD exists for every matrix of any shape. For a symmetric positive definite matrix, $A=U\Sigma V^T$ with $U=V$ reduces to $A=Q\Lambda Q^T$ (spectral theorem) — but the two factorizations coincide only in this special case.

Chapter 9 Quiz

Test your mastery of LU-decomposition, the Power Method, condition numbers, SVD, and low-rank approximations.

1. In the LU decomposition $A=LU$, what are the diagonal entries of $L$?
In the standard LU decomposition, $L$ is a unit lower triangular matrix — its diagonal entries are all 1. The pivot elements appear on the diagonal of $U$ (the upper triangular factor).
2. You need to solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ for 1000 different right-hand sides $\mathbf{b}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{b}_{1000}$ with the same $n\times n$ matrix $A$. Which strategy is most efficient?
LU is optimal: compute $A=LU$ once ($O(n^3/3)$), then each of the 1000 solves costs $O(n^2)$. Computing $A^{-1}$ explicitly is as expensive as 1000× LU solves AND introduces additional numerical error. Cramer's rule is $O(n!)$ — catastrophically slow.
3. The Power Method converges to the eigenvector corresponding to the eigenvalue with the:
The Power Method converges to the dominant eigenvalue — the one with the largest absolute value. Each matrix multiplication amplifies the component in the direction of the dominant eigenvector and suppresses all others, at a rate of $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|^k$.
4. A matrix has condition number $\kappa(A)=10^8$. If computations use IEEE 64-bit float (16 digits of precision), approximately how many reliable digits remain in the solution?
$16-\log_{10}(\kappa)=16-8=8$ reliable digits. The condition number "consumes" $\log_{10}(\kappa)$ digits of floating-point precision, leaving $16-8=8$ reliable digits in the computed solution.
5. For a matrix $A=U\Sigma V^T$, the columns of $V$ are:
The columns of $V$ are the right singular vectors — orthonormal eigenvectors of $A^TA$. The columns of $U$ are the left singular vectors — eigenvectors of $AA^T$. The diagonal of $\Sigma$ contains the singular values $\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i(A^TA)}$.
6. The Eckart-Young theorem says the best rank-$k$ approximation $A_k$ to $A$ satisfies $\|A-A_k\|_2 = $?
$\|A-A_k\|_2=\sigma_{k+1}$. The truncated SVD $A_k=\sum_{i=1}^k\sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^T$ discards all terms from $i=k+1$ onward. The 2-norm of the error equals the first discarded singular value $\sigma_{k+1}$.
7. Which matrix decomposition is twice as fast as LU for symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrices?
Cholesky decomposition exploits symmetry to cost $O(n^3/6)$ — half the operations of LU ($O(n^3/3)$). It also requires only half the storage (lower triangle). It's the method of choice for SPD matrices in statistics (covariance matrices) and optimization (Hessians in Newton's method).
8. The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse $A^+$ of a matrix $A=U\Sigma V^T$ is:
$A^+=V\Sigma^+U^T$ where $\Sigma^+$ replaces each non-zero $\sigma_i$ with $1/\sigma_i$. Note: $(A^TA)^{-1}A^T$ is the left pseudoinverse and equals $A^+$ only when $A$ has full column rank. The SVD formula handles all cases including rank-deficient matrices.
9. The convergence rate of the Power Method depends on the ratio:
Convergence rate is $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|^k$. After $k$ iterations, the error in the eigenvector estimate decays like this ratio raised to the $k$-th power. Large spectral gap (small $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|$) means fast convergence; ratio near 1 means very slow convergence.
10. In SVD-based image compression keeping only the top $k$ singular values, the compression ratio for an $m\times n$ image is approximately:
Storing rank-$k$ SVD requires $k$ left singular vectors ($km$ entries), $k$ singular values ($k$), and $k$ right singular vectors ($kn$ entries): total $k(m+n+1)$. Original image: $mn$ entries. Ratio: $mn/[k(m+n+1)]$.
