6.1

Inner Products

An inner product is an abstract operation that assigns a scalar to two vectors, satisfying four axioms. It generalizes the dot product to any vector space, giving us geometry everywhere.

The dot product in $\mathbb{R}^n$ gives you length and angle. But what does "perpendicular" mean for polynomials or functions? An inner product is the answer: a mathematical recipe that injects geometry into any vector space. Different inner products create different geometries — like choosing a different ruler and protractor for the same space.

DEFINITION — Inner Product

An inner product on a real vector space $V$ is a function $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle: V\times V\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfying for all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in V$ and $k\in\mathbb{R}$:

  1. Symmetry: $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle$
  2. Linearity: $\langle k\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle=k\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle$ and $\langle\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w}\rangle+\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle$
  3. Positive definiteness: $\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle\ge 0$, and $\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle=0\Leftrightarrow\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$

A vector space with an inner product is called an inner product space.

INDUCED NORM AND DISTANCE

Every inner product induces a norm $\|\mathbf{v}\|=\sqrt{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle}$ and a distance $d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})=\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|$.

THEOREM — Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

$|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle|\le\|\mathbf{u}\|\cdot\|\mathbf{v}\|$

Equality holds iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are proportional. Consequence: $-1\le\dfrac{\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|}\le 1$, so $\cos\theta=\dfrac{\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|}$ is well-defined.

Weighted inner product on $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $w_1=2, w_2=3$: $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle=2u_1v_1+3u_2v_2$.
$\|(1,1)\|=\sqrt{2(1)^2+3(1)^2}=\sqrt{5}\approx 2.24$ (vs. standard norm $\sqrt{2}$). ✓ This inner product "stretches" the $y$-direction more.
Kernel Methods. In ML, a kernel function $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$ implicitly defines an inner product in a high-dimensional feature space — without ever computing that space explicitly. The RBF kernel $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})=e^{-\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\|^2/2\sigma^2}$ is an inner product in an infinite-dimensional space. This "kernel trick" powers SVMs and Gaussian Processes.
6.2

Angle & Orthogonality in Inner Product Spaces

With an inner product we can define angles between vectors in any space — including between polynomials. Orthogonality (zero inner product) is the key to decomposing spaces cleanly.

DEFINITIONS — Orthogonality

  • $\mathbf{u}\perp\mathbf{v}$ iff $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle=0$
  • A set $S$ is orthogonal if any two distinct elements have zero inner product.
  • A set $S$ is orthonormal if it is orthogonal and every element has norm 1: $\langle\mathbf{v}_i,\mathbf{v}_j\rangle=\delta_{ij}$.
  • $W^\perp = \{\mathbf{v}\in V : \langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle=0\;\forall\mathbf{w}\in W\}$ is the orthogonal complement of $W$.

THEOREM — Orthogonal Decomposition

If $W$ is a finite-dimensional subspace of an inner product space $V$, then every $\mathbf{v}\in V$ has a unique decomposition:

$\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{w} + \mathbf{w}^\perp, \quad \mathbf{w}\in W,\;\mathbf{w}^\perp\in W^\perp$

If $\{\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_k\}$ is an orthonormal basis for $W$, then $\mathbf{w}=\sum_{i=1}^k\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{q}_i\rangle\mathbf{q}_i$ (the orthogonal projection).

THEOREM — Generalized Pythagorean Theorem

If $\mathbf{u}\perp\mathbf{v}$, then $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2=\|\mathbf{u}\|^2+\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$.

For orthonormal set $\{\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_k\}$ and $\mathbf{v}=\sum c_i\mathbf{q}_i$: $\|\mathbf{v}\|^2=\sum c_i^2$ (Parseval-type identity).

Are $p=1$ and $q=x-\tfrac{1}{2}$ orthogonal in $P_1$ with $\langle f,g\rangle=\int_0^1 f(x)g(x)\,dx$?
$\langle 1, x-\tfrac{1}{2}\rangle=\int_0^1(x-\tfrac{1}{2})\,dx=\left[\tfrac{x^2}{2}-\tfrac{x}{2}\right]_0^1=\tfrac{1}{2}-\tfrac{1}{2}=0$. ✓
Yes, orthogonal! This is the Legendre polynomial structure — polynomials can be perpendicular.
Orthogonality in Neural Networks. Weight vectors in different layers ideally should be orthogonal — they capture independent features. Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of weight matrices appears in techniques like orthogonal initialization and spectral normalization, which stabilize training by ensuring the linear maps preserve norms.
If $\dim(W)=k$ and $V$ is $n$-dimensional, what is $\dim(W^\perp)$? (Hint: think about rank-nullity!)
6.3

Gram-Schmidt Process & QR-Decomposition

The Gram-Schmidt process converts any basis into an orthonormal one by iteratively subtracting projections. The output is the QR-decomposition, the workhorse of numerical linear algebra.

Gram-Schmidt is like building a perfectly square room from crooked wooden planks. Take the first plank as your first wall direction. The second plank: subtract the part pointing the same way as the first, leaving only the perpendicular part. Repeat for each plank. You get walls that are all perfectly perpendicular — an orthonormal frame built from whatever you started with.

GRAM-SCHMIDT ALGORITHM

Given linearly independent $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_n\}$, produce orthonormal $\{\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_n\}$:

$\mathbf{u}_1=\mathbf{v}_1,\quad\mathbf{q}_1=\dfrac{\mathbf{u}_1}{\|\mathbf{u}_1\|}$

$\mathbf{u}_2=\mathbf{v}_2-\langle\mathbf{v}_2,\mathbf{q}_1\rangle\mathbf{q}_1,\quad\mathbf{q}_2=\dfrac{\mathbf{u}_2}{\|\mathbf{u}_2\|}$

$\mathbf{u}_k=\mathbf{v}_k-\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\langle\mathbf{v}_k,\mathbf{q}_j\rangle\mathbf{q}_j,\quad\mathbf{q}_k=\dfrac{\mathbf{u}_k}{\|\mathbf{u}_k\|}$

Each $\mathbf{u}_k$ is $\mathbf{v}_k$ minus its projection onto the space already spanned.

THEOREM — QR-Decomposition

Every matrix $A$ with linearly independent columns can be written $A=QR$ where:

  • $Q$ has orthonormal columns (the Gram-Schmidt output)
  • $R$ is upper triangular with positive diagonal entries

The entries $R_{jk}=\langle\mathbf{v}_k,\mathbf{q}_j\rangle$ for $j\le k$.

QR for Solving Least Squares. QR-decomposition is the numerically stable way to solve least-squares problems $A\mathbf{x}\approx\mathbf{b}$. Write $A=QR$; then the normal equations become $R\mathbf{x}=Q^T\mathbf{b}$, which is triangular — solved in $O(n^2)$ by back-substitution. This avoids the $O(n^3)$ numerical instability of forming $A^TA$ (squaring the condition number).
6.4

Best Approximation & Least Squares

The best approximation of a vector $\mathbf{b}$ in a subspace $W$ is its orthogonal projection onto $W$. This is the foundation of least-squares regression.

Drop a perpendicular from a point to a subspace — the foot of that perpendicular is the closest point in the subspace. This is exactly what least-squares does: when $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ has no solution, find the $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ making $A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ the closest point in the column space to $\mathbf{b}$. The residual $\mathbf{b}-A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ is perpendicular to every column of $A$.

THEOREM — Best Approximation Theorem

If $W$ is a finite-dimensional subspace of an inner product space $V$ and $\mathbf{b}\in V$, then $\hat{\mathbf{w}}=\text{proj}_W\mathbf{b}$ is the unique vector in $W$ closest to $\mathbf{b}$:

$\|\mathbf{b}-\hat{\mathbf{w}}\|\le\|\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{w}\|\quad\text{for all }\mathbf{w}\in W$

DEFINITION — Least-Squares Solution

For an inconsistent system $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ ($m\times n$, $m>n$), the least-squares solution $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ minimizes $\|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|^2$. It satisfies the normal equations:

$A^TA\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T\mathbf{b}$

If $A$ has full column rank, the unique solution is $\hat{\mathbf{x}}=(A^TA)^{-1}A^T\mathbf{b}$.

Predicted values $\hat{\mathbf{y}}=H\mathbf{y}$; residuals $\mathbf{e}=(I-H)\mathbf{y}\perp\text{col}(X)$.
6.5

Mathematical Modeling Using Least Squares

Least squares extends beyond lines — we can fit polynomials, exponentials, and other model families by transforming them into a linear problem in the parameters.

POLYNOMIAL LEAST SQUARES

To fit degree-$k$ polynomial $y=c_0+c_1x+\cdots+c_kx^k$ to data $(x_1,y_1),\ldots,(x_m,y_m)$ ($m>k+1$), form the Vandermonde matrix:

$V=\begin{bmatrix}1&x_1&x_1^2&\cdots&x_1^k\\ \vdots&\vdots&\vdots&&\vdots\\1&x_m&x_m^2&\cdots&x_m^k\end{bmatrix}$

Solve $V^TV\mathbf{c}=V^T\mathbf{y}$ for the coefficient vector $\mathbf{c}=(c_0,\ldots,c_k)^T$.

Overfitting vs. Underfitting. Fitting degree-$(m-1)$ polynomial to $m$ points always gives zero residual — but the model memorizes noise. The optimal model minimizes a bias-variance tradeoff: too simple (underfitting, high bias) or too complex (overfitting, high variance). Cross-validation or regularization (ridge regression: minimize $\|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|^2+\lambda\|\mathbf{x}\|^2$) selects the right balance.
Ridge Regression = Regularized Least Squares. Add $\lambda\|\mathbf{x}\|^2$ to the objective. Normal equations become $(A^TA+\lambda I)\hat{\mathbf{x}}=A^T\mathbf{b}$. The extra $\lambda I$ makes the matrix invertible (well-conditioned) even when $A^TA$ is singular — shrinking coefficients toward zero. This is the geometric meaning of $L_2$ regularization in ML.
6.6

Function Approximation & Fourier Series

In the function space $C[0,2\pi]$ with the integral inner product, sine and cosine functions form an orthonormal basis. A function's "coordinates" in this basis are its Fourier coefficients.

Every sound is a superposition of pure tones (sine waves). The Fourier series decomposes any periodic function into its harmonic ingredients: a DC component, a fundamental frequency, and overtones. It's the ultimate inner product calculation — projecting a function onto each basis sinusoid. This is why MP3, JPEG, and WiFi all use Fourier analysis.

DEFINITION — Fourier Series

For $f\in C[0,2\pi]$ with inner product $\langle f,g\rangle=\dfrac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}f(x)g(x)\,dx$, the orthonormal basis $\left\{\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\cos x,\sin x,\cos 2x,\sin 2x,\ldots\right\}$ gives the Fourier series:

$f(x)\approx\dfrac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^N(a_n\cos nx+b_n\sin nx)$

where $a_n=\dfrac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}f(x)\cos(nx)\,dx$, $\;b_n=\dfrac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}f(x)\sin(nx)\,dx$.

THEOREM — Parseval's Identity

For the Fourier series of $f\in C[0,2\pi]$:

$\dfrac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}[f(x)]^2\,dx=\dfrac{a_0^2}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty(a_n^2+b_n^2)$

The total energy of a signal equals the sum of energies at each frequency. This is the Pythagorean theorem in infinite dimensions!

Fourier series of the square wave $f(x)=\begin{cases}1&0\le x\le\pi\\-1&\pi
$a_0=0$, $a_n=0$ (odd function in disguise), $b_n=\dfrac{2}{n\pi}(1-\cos n\pi)=\begin{cases}4/(n\pi)&n\text{ odd}\\0&n\text{ even}\end{cases}$
Series: $f(x)\approx\dfrac{4}{\pi}\left(\sin x+\dfrac{\sin 3x}{3}+\dfrac{\sin 5x}{5}+\cdots\right)$
Parseval check: $\sum_{k=0}^\infty\dfrac{1}{(2k+1)^2}=\dfrac{\pi^2}{8}$.
Spectral Analysis in Signal Processing and ML. Fourier series is the bridge to the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). In deep learning, attention mechanisms in Transformers can be interpreted through the lens of spectral methods. Graph Neural Networks use the graph Laplacian's eigenvectors as a "Fourier basis" for graph-structured data. Audio ML models (Whisper, WaveNet) operate on Fourier spectrograms.

Chapter 6 Knowledge Check

10 questions on inner products, orthogonality, Gram-Schmidt, least squares, and Fourier series.

Q1. Which of the following is NOT an axiom for an inner product?

C. The triangle inequality is a consequence of inner product axioms (via Cauchy-Schwarz), not an axiom itself. The four axioms are: symmetry, linearity, additivity, and positive definiteness.

Q2. The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality states that for an inner product space:

B. Cauchy-Schwarz: |⟨u,v⟩| ≤ ‖u‖‖v‖. This guarantees cos θ = ⟨u,v⟩/(‖u‖‖v‖) lies in [−1,1], so the angle between vectors is well-defined in any inner product space.

Q3. In Gram-Schmidt, $\mathbf{u}_2=\mathbf{v}_2-\langle\mathbf{v}_2,\mathbf{q}_1\rangle\mathbf{q}_1$ ensures:

B. We subtract the projection of v₂ onto q₁, leaving only the component perpendicular to q₁. Verify: ⟨u₂,q₁⟩ = ⟨v₂,q₁⟩ − ⟨v₂,q₁⟩⟨q₁,q₁⟩ = ⟨v₂,q₁⟩ − ⟨v₂,q₁⟩·1 = 0. ✓

Q4. The normal equations $A^TA\hat{\mathbf{x}}=A^T\mathbf{b}$ arise because the residual $\mathbf{b}-A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ must be:

C. The orthogonal projection of b onto col(A) is Ax̂. The residual b−Ax̂ must lie in (col(A))⊥ = null(Aᵀ). This means Aᵀ(b−Ax̂)=0 → AᵀAx̂=Aᵀb. The geometric insight: the shortest distance from a point to a subspace is along the perpendicular.

Q5. The QR-decomposition $A=QR$ is useful for least squares because:

A. Forming AᵀA in normal equations squares the condition number, causing numerical instability. QR gives Rx̂=Qᵀb — a triangular system solved stably by back-substitution. This is why LAPACK/NumPy use QR for least squares.

Q6. Two polynomials $p,q$ in $C[0,1]$ are orthogonal when their integral inner product $\int_0^1 p(x)q(x)\,dx$ equals:

C. Orthogonality means ⟨p,q⟩ = 0 in any inner product space. For the integral inner product, ∫₀¹ p(x)q(x) dx = 0. This is how Legendre polynomials {P₀,P₁,P₂,...} are constructed — each one is orthogonal to all previous ones under this integral.

Q7. The Fourier coefficient $b_n=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}f(x)\sin(nx)\,dx$ is:

B. The Fourier coefficient bₙ = ⟨f, sin(nx)⟩/‖sin(nx)‖² gives the "coordinate" of f in the direction of sin(nx) — exactly like finding coordinates in any orthonormal basis. The 1/π factor normalizes ‖sin(nx)‖² = π.

Q8. Parseval's identity says the energy of a signal equals:

B. Parseval: ‖f‖² = a₀²/2 + Σ(aₙ²+bₙ²). This is the Pythagorean theorem in infinite dimensions — total energy = sum of energies at each frequency. It's used in signal processing to verify that no energy is lost when computing Fourier transforms.

Q9. Ridge regression (minimize $\|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|^2+\lambda\|\mathbf{x}\|^2$) leads to the solution:

B. The modified normal equations are (AᵀA+λI)x̂=Aᵀb. Adding λI to AᵀA shifts all eigenvalues up by λ, making the matrix positive definite (invertible) even if AᵀA is singular. This is L₂ regularization = Tikhonov regularization.

Q10. The orthogonal complement $W^\perp$ of a $k$-dimensional subspace $W$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ has dimension:

C. dim(W) + dim(W⊥) = n, so dim(W⊥) = n−k. The whole space splits orthogonally: ℝⁿ = W ⊕ W⊥. This is the Orthogonal Decomposition Theorem — every vector has a unique sum of a W-part and a W⊥-part.
