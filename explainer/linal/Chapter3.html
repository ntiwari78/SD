Euclidean Vector Spaces

Where algebra gains geometry — length, angle, and perpendicularity

Vectors are the atoms of linear algebra. This chapter gives them geometric life: length (norm), angle (dot product), perpendicularity (orthogonality), and the powerful cross product for 3D orientation. The dot product is the inner machinery behind cosine similarity, projections, and least-squares — foundational to modern machine learning.

§ 3.1

Vectors in 2-Space, 3-Space, and $n$-Space

A vector is an ordered list of numbers that simultaneously encodes a point in space and a direction of travel. Extending from 2D to $n$D is purely algebraic — the geometric intuition carries over perfectly.

🎓 Feynman Lens

A vector is a move. The vector $(3, -2, 5)$ means "go 3 east, 2 south, 5 up." Its tail can be anywhere — what matters is the displacement. When you add two moves, you chain them: walk the first path, then the second. The total displacement is the sum vector — this is why vector addition is the parallelogram law.

Definition — Vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$
A vector $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is an ordered $n$-tuple: $\mathbf{v} = (v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n)$. Key operations:
  • Addition: $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = (u_1+v_1,\, u_2+v_2,\, \ldots,\, u_n+v_n)$
  • Scalar multiplication: $c\mathbf{v} = (cv_1, cv_2, \ldots, cv_n)$
  • Zero vector: $\mathbf{0} = (0, 0, \ldots, 0)$ — the identity for addition
  • Negative: $-\mathbf{v} = (-v_1, \ldots, -v_n)$
  • Linear combination: $c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k$
Theorem — Properties of Vector Arithmetic in $\mathbb{R}^n$
For all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and scalars $c, d$:
  • $\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}+\mathbf{u}$
  • $(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})+\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{u}+(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})$
  • $\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{u}$
  • $\mathbf{u}+(-\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{0}$
  • $c(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}) = c\mathbf{u}+c\mathbf{v}$
  • $(c+d)\mathbf{u} = c\mathbf{u}+d\mathbf{u}$
  • $c(d\mathbf{u}) = (cd)\mathbf{u}$
  • $1\cdot\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{u}$

Worked ExampleLinear Combination in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Express $\mathbf{w} = (1, -1, 5)$ as a linear combination of $\mathbf{u}=(1,2,-1)$ and $\mathbf{v}=(0,1,3)$, if possible.

  1. Set up $c_1(1,2,-1) + c_2(0,1,3) = (1,-1,5)$.
  2. Component equations: $c_1 = 1$; $2c_1+c_2 = -1$; $-c_1+3c_2 = 5$.
  3. From eq 1: $c_1=1$. Substitute into eq 2: $c_2 = -3$. Check eq 3: $-1+3(-3)=-10\neq 5$.
  4. Inconsistent — $\mathbf{w}$ is not in the span of $\{\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\}$.
📊 Data Science Bridge

Every data point (a row in your dataset) is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ where $n$ = number of features. Word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) encode semantic relationships as vector arithmetic: $\text{king} - \text{man} + \text{woman} \approx \text{queen}$. Neural network activations are vectors. Batches are matrices (collections of vectors).

§ 3.2

Norm, Dot Product, and Distance in $\mathbb{R}^n$

The dot product is the algebraic engine behind angle and projection — the "ruler and protractor" of $\mathbb{R}^n$. It turns the abstract set of vectors into a metric space with a rich geometric structure.

🎓 Feynman Lens

The dot product $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta$ tells you "how much of $\mathbf{v}$ points in the direction of $\mathbf{u}$." If both vectors point exactly the same way ($\theta=0$), their dot product is maximum. If they're perpendicular ($\theta=90°$), the dot product is zero — they share no common direction. If they point opposite ways, it's negative.

Definitions — Norm, Dot Product, Distance
For $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:
  • Euclidean norm (length): $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2} = \sqrt{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}}$
  • Unit vector: $\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{v}/\|\mathbf{v}\|$ (normalised to length 1)
  • Dot product: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + \cdots + u_nv_n$
  • Angle between vectors: $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|}$   ($0\leq\theta\leq\pi$)
  • Euclidean distance: $d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|$
Theorem — Cauchy–Schwarz & Triangle Inequalities
  • Cauchy–Schwarz: $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$  (equality iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are parallel)
  • Triangle inequality: $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\| + \|\mathbf{v}\|$
  • Parallelogram law: $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 2(\|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2)$

Worked ExampleFinding Angle Between Vectors

Find the angle between $\mathbf{u}=(1,2,-2)$ and $\mathbf{v}=(3,0,4)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

  1. $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = (1)(3)+(2)(0)+(-2)(4) = 3+0-8 = -5$.
  2. $\|\mathbf{u}\| = \sqrt{1+4+4} = 3$; $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{9+0+16} = 5$.
  3. $\cos\theta = \dfrac{-5}{3\cdot 5} = -\dfrac{1}{3}$.
  4. $\theta = \arccos(-1/3) \approx 109.47°$. Since $\cos\theta < 0$, the vectors form an obtuse angle.
📊 Data Science Bridge

Cosine similarity $= \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}/(\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|)$ is the fundamental similarity metric in information retrieval, NLP, and recommendation systems — it measures the angle between two document or user vectors regardless of magnitude. The softmax function in neural networks uses dot products: $\text{softmax}(Wx)_i \propto e^{w_i\cdot x}$. $k$-NN classifiers use Euclidean distance.

§ 3.3

Orthogonality

Two vectors are orthogonal when their dot product is zero — they are geometrically perpendicular. Orthogonality is the single most powerful concept in applied linear algebra, underpinning least-squares, Fourier analysis, and every form of PCA.

🎓 Feynman Lens

Think of a shadow on the floor. The shadow is the projection of you onto the floor — it's the component of your body that "points horizontally." The remaining component points straight up, perpendicular to the floor. Projection + perpendicular component = full vector. Orthogonality is exactly this decomposition: $\mathbf{u} = \text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{u}_\perp$.

Definitions — Orthogonality & Projection
  • Orthogonal vectors: $\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$ iff $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$.
  • Orthogonal projection of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$: $$\text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|^2}\,\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}}\,\mathbf{v}$$
  • Vector component of $\mathbf{u}$ orthogonal to $\mathbf{v}$: $\mathbf{u}_\perp = \mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}$
  • Orthogonal set: $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}$ with $\mathbf{v}_i\cdot\mathbf{v}_j = 0$ for all $i\neq j$.
  • Orthonormal set: orthogonal and $\|\mathbf{v}_i\|=1$ for all $i$.
Theorem — Pythagorean Theorem in $\mathbb{R}^n$
If $\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$, then $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$.

Orthogonal sets are linearly independent — every non-zero orthogonal set $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}$ is linearly independent. This gives a free basis with very easy coordinate formulas: $c_i = \dfrac{\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{v}_i}{\|\mathbf{v}_i\|^2}$.

Worked ExampleDecomposing a Vector via Projection

Decompose $\mathbf{u} = (2, 3, -1)$ into components parallel and orthogonal to $\mathbf{v} = (1, 2, 0)$.

  1. $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 2+6+0 = 8$; $\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 1+4+0 = 5$.
  2. $\text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u} = \dfrac{8}{5}(1,2,0) = \left(\dfrac{8}{5}, \dfrac{16}{5}, 0\right)$.
  3. $\mathbf{u}_\perp = (2,3,-1) - (8/5, 16/5, 0) = (2/5, -1/5, -1)$.
  4. Verify orthogonality: $\mathbf{u}_\perp \cdot \mathbf{v} = 2/5 + (-2/5) + 0 = 0$ ✓
📊 Data Science Bridge

PCA finds orthogonal directions (principal components) of maximum variance — it is literally an orthogonal projection of data. Gram–Schmidt (Chapter 6) converts any basis to an orthonormal one, the foundation of QR decomposition. Least-squares regression projects the response vector $\mathbf{y}$ onto the column space of $X$ — the residual $\mathbf{y}-\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ is orthogonal to every column of $X$.

§ 3.4

The Geometry of Linear Systems

Every linear equation $a_1x_1 + a_2x_2 + \cdots + a_nx_n = b$ is a hyperplane in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Solving a linear system means finding the intersection of hyperplanes. The coefficient vector $(a_1,\ldots,a_n)$ is the normal vector to that hyperplane — it points perpendicular to the solution set.

🎓 Feynman Lens

In 2D, $ax + by = c$ is a line. The vector $(a, b)$ is an arrow pointing perpendicular to that line. In 3D, $ax+by+cz=d$ is a plane with normal $(a,b,c)$. In $n$D, it's a hyperplane with normal $(a_1,\ldots,a_n)$. A system of $m$ equations means $m$ hyperplanes — the solution set is their intersection: another flat geometric object (a point, line, plane, etc.).

Geometric Objects in $\mathbb{R}^n$
Equation Object Normal vector
$ax+by=c$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$Line$(a,b)$
$ax+by+cz=d$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$Plane$(a,b,c)$
$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{x}=b$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$Hyperplane$\mathbf{a}$

Theorem — Solution Sets as Geometric Objects
For a consistent system $Ax = b$:
  • If $Ax=b$ has a unique solution: a single point.
  • If it has free variables: the solution set is a flat (translate of a subspace), also called an affine subspace.
  • The general solution: $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_p + \mathbf{x}_h$, where $\mathbf{x}_p$ is any particular solution and $\mathbf{x}_h$ is the homogeneous solution ($Ax_h=0$).
  • $\mathbf{x}_h$ lies in the null space of $A$ — a subspace of dimension = number of free variables.
Worked ExampleGeometric Description of a Solution Set

Describe geometrically the solution set of $x_1 - 2x_2 + x_3 = 4$.

  1. This is a single equation in 3 unknowns — a plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
  2. Normal vector: $\mathbf{n} = (1, -2, 1)$. The plane is perpendicular to $\mathbf{n}$.
  3. Particular solution: set $x_2 = x_3 = 0 \Rightarrow x_1 = 4$. Point: $(4, 0, 0)$.
  4. Null space: $x_1 - 2x_2 + x_3 = 0$, free variables $x_2, x_3$. Homogeneous solutions span: $\{(2,1,0), (-1,0,1)\}$.
  5. Full solution: $\mathbf{x} = (4,0,0) + s(2,1,0) + t(-1,0,1)$ — a plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
💡 Geometric Insight — Why Rank Matters

The rank of $A$ = number of non-redundant hyperplanes = dimension of the column space. The Rank–Nullity Theorem (Chapter 4): $\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n$ (number of variables). So more independent equations → smaller solution space → closer to a unique point.

📊 Data Science Bridge

Support Vector Machines (SVMs) find the maximum-margin hyperplane separating two classes — a hyperplane $\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}=b$ in feature space. Its normal $\mathbf{w}$ is the weight vector. Logistic regression is a probabilistic version of the same idea. The bias term $b$ shifts the hyperplane away from the origin — the affine solution structure of §3.4 in action.

§ 3.5

Cross Product

The cross product is unique to $\mathbb{R}^3$: given two vectors, it produces a third vector perpendicular to both. It encodes 3D orientation, computes areas and volumes, and drives the physics of torque, angular momentum, and magnetic force.

🎓 Feynman Lens

Grab a screwdriver and point it along $\mathbf{u}$. Now align it to point along $\mathbf{v}$, rotating through the smaller angle. The direction your thumb points when your fingers curl from $\mathbf{u}$ to $\mathbf{v}$ is the direction of $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}$. The magnitude is the area of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$. This is the right-hand rule.

Definition — Cross Product
For $\mathbf{u}=(u_1,u_2,u_3)$ and $\mathbf{v}=(v_1,v_2,v_3)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$: $$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\u_1&u_2&u_3\\v_1&v_2&v_3\end{vmatrix} = (u_2v_3-u_3v_2)\,\mathbf{i} - (u_1v_3-u_3v_1)\,\mathbf{j} + (u_1v_2-u_2v_1)\,\mathbf{k}$$ Key fact: $\|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}\| = \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\sin\theta$ = area of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.
Properties of the Cross Product
  • $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = -(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{u})$  (anti-commutative)
  • $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}$ is orthogonal to both $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$
  • $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}$ (parallel vectors give zero cross product)
  • $c(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}) = (c\mathbf{u})\times\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}\times(c\mathbf{v})$
  • $\mathbf{u}\times(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{w}$
  • Scalar triple product: $\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w}) = \det\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{u}\\\mathbf{v}\\\mathbf{w}\end{bmatrix}$ = signed volume of the parallelepiped.

Worked ExampleArea of a Triangle using Cross Product

Find the area of the triangle with vertices $P=(1,0,0)$, $Q=(0,2,0)$, $R=(0,0,3)$.

  1. Edge vectors from $P$: $\mathbf{u}=\overrightarrow{PQ}=(-1,2,0)$ and $\mathbf{v}=\overrightarrow{PR}=(-1,0,3)$.
  2. $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = (2\cdot3-0\cdot0,\;0\cdot(-1)-(-1)\cdot3,\;(-1)\cdot0-2\cdot(-1)) = (6,3,2)$.
  3. $\|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{36+9+4} = 7$.
  4. Area of triangle $= \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}\| = \boxed{7/2 = 3.5}$
🤔 Socratic Check

If $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are parallel (one is a scalar multiple of the other), what is $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}$? Why does this make geometric sense?

Reveal answer

$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$. Geometrically, parallel vectors span a degenerate parallelogram with zero area — they collapse onto a line. Since the cross product magnitude equals area, it must be zero. Algebraically: $\sin\theta=0$ when $\theta=0$ or $\pi$.

📊 Data Science Bridge

In 3D computer graphics and robotics, the cross product computes surface normals (for lighting/shading), torque vectors, and rotation axes. The scalar triple product $|\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w})|$ gives the volume of a voxel in 3D rendering. In physics-based simulation and collision detection (central to game engines and AR/VR), cross products drive every angular momentum calculation.
