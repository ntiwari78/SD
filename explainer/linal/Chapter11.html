Inner Product Spaces

Geometry lives inside algebra. Inner products give us length, angle, and orthogonality in any vector space — from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to function spaces — unifying all of linear algebra into one elegant geometric framework.

11.1

Inner Products

An inner product is an abstract generalization of the dot product that endows any vector space with geometric structure — length, distance, and angle. The axioms are simple but their consequences are profound: they give us the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, the triangle inequality, and ultimately the foundations of all Hilbert space theory.

Think of an inner product as a "voting machine" for similarity. Feed it two vectors; it returns a number measuring how much they "agree." In $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the dot product, parallel vectors give maximum agreement (product of their lengths), perpendicular vectors give zero. The genius is that the same idea works for polynomials, functions, matrices — anything you can add and scale.

INNER PRODUCT SPACE

An inner product on a real vector space $V$ is a function $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle: V\times V\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfying for all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in V$ and scalars $k$:

  • Symmetry: $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle$
  • Additivity: $\langle\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w}\rangle+\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle$
  • Homogeneity: $\langle k\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle = k\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle$
  • Positive definiteness: $\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle\geq 0$, with equality iff $\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$

A vector space with an inner product is an inner product space. The induced norm is $\|\mathbf{v}\|=\sqrt{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle}$.

Four Fundamental Examples

SpaceInner Product $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle$Notes
$\mathbb{R}^n$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=\sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i$Standard dot product
$\mathbb{R}^n$ (weighted)$\sum_{i=1}^n w_i u_i v_i$, all $w_i>0$Weighted Euclidean

CAUCHY-SCHWARZ INEQUALITY

For any inner product space: $|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle|\leq\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$

Equality holds iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are scalar multiples of each other (linearly dependent). This is one of the most powerful inequalities in all of mathematics — it implies the triangle inequality $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|\leq\|\mathbf{u}\|+\|\mathbf{v}\|$ and validates the definition of angle.

Weighted Inner Product. Let $w_1=2,w_2=3$ and define $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle_w=2u_1v_1+3u_2v_2$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$. For $\mathbf{u}=(1,2)^T$ and $\mathbf{v}=(3,-1)^T$: $$\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle_w=2(1)(3)+3(2)(-1)=6-6=0$$

These vectors are orthogonal under the weighted inner product, even though their standard dot product is $1\cdot 3+2\cdot(-1)=1\neq 0$. Orthogonality is relative to the chosen inner product!

Machine Learning Kernels are Inner Products. The "kernel trick" in SVMs and kernel PCA replaces the standard dot product $\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y}$ with a kernel function $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$ that implicitly computes $\langle\phi(\mathbf{x}),\phi(\mathbf{y})\rangle$ in a possibly infinite-dimensional feature space $\mathcal{H}$. The RBF kernel $K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})=e^{-\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\|^2/(2\sigma^2)}$ corresponds to an infinite-dimensional inner product. All of kernel methods are exactly inner product space theory.
Is $\langle f,g\rangle=f(0)g(0)$ a valid inner product on $C[0,1]$? Why or why not?
Show Answer

No. It fails positive definiteness. Take $f(x)=x$ (a nonzero function). Then $\langle f,f\rangle=f(0)^2=0^2=0$, yet $f\neq\mathbf{0}$. A valid inner product requires $\langle f,f\rangle>0$ for all $f\neq 0$. The integral $\int_0^1 f^2\,dx$ is a valid inner product because $f^2\geq 0$ and $\int_0^1 f^2\,dx=0$ forces $f\equiv 0$ on $[0,1]$.

11.2

Angle and Orthogonality in Inner Product Spaces

Once you have an inner product, you have geometry. The cosine formula from $\mathbb{R}^n$ extends to every inner product space, giving a rigorous notion of angle between functions, matrices, or any vectors. Orthogonality — zero inner product — becomes the key structural property underpinning decompositions, projections, and least squares.

ANGLE AND ORTHOGONALITY

In an inner product space, the angle $\theta$ between nonzero vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ is defined by:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|}\qquad(0\leq\theta\leq\pi)$$

Vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are orthogonal (written $\mathbf{u}\perp\mathbf{v}$) if $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle=0$. A set $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}$ is an orthogonal set if $\langle\mathbf{v}_i,\mathbf{v}_j\rangle=0$ for all $i\neq j$. If additionally each $\|\mathbf{v}_i\|=1$, it is an orthonormal set.

ORTHOGONAL SETS ARE LINEARLY INDEPENDENT

If $S=\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}$ is an orthogonal set of nonzero vectors, then $S$ is linearly independent. Proof: if $c_1\mathbf{v}_1+\cdots+c_k\mathbf{v}_k=\mathbf{0}$, take inner product with $\mathbf{v}_i$ to get $c_i\|\mathbf{v}_i\|^2=0$, so $c_i=0$. Orthogonality "bakes in" independence automatically.

GENERALIZED PYTHAGOREAN THEOREM

If $\mathbf{u}\perp\mathbf{v}$, then $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2=\|\mathbf{u}\|^2+\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$.

Proof: $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2=\langle\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\rangle=\|\mathbf{u}\|^2+2\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle+\|\mathbf{v}\|^2=\|\mathbf{u}\|^2+\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$.

Extends to any finite orthogonal set: $\|\mathbf{v}_1+\cdots+\mathbf{v}_k\|^2=\|\mathbf{v}_1\|^2+\cdots+\|\mathbf{v}_k\|^2$.

Orthogonal Functions. On $C[0,2\pi]$ with inner product $\langle f,g\rangle=\int_0^{2\pi}f(x)g(x)\,dx$, show $\sin(x)\perp\cos(x)$: $$\langle\sin x,\cos x\rangle=\int_0^{2\pi}\sin(x)\cos(x)\,dx=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}\sin(2x)\,dx=\left[-\frac{\cos 2x}{4}\right]_0^{2\pi}=0$$

The entire family $\{1,\cos x,\sin x,\cos 2x,\sin 2x,\ldots\}$ is mutually orthogonal under this inner product — the foundation of Fourier series.

Cosine Similarity in NLP is Just Inner Product Geometry. In word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, BERT), each word/document is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^{300}$ or higher. Can two nonzero vectors in an inner product space satisfy $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle>\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$?
Show Answer

No. The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality guarantees $|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle|\leq\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$ for any inner product. This means $\cos\theta\in[-1,1]$ is always well-defined. Equality $|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle|=\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$ holds iff $\mathbf{u}=c\mathbf{v}$ for some scalar $c$ (parallel vectors, $\theta=0$ or $\theta=\pi$).

11.3

Gram-Schmidt Process & QR Decomposition

The Gram-Schmidt process is an algorithm that converts any basis into an orthonormal basis by systematically removing the component of each new vector along all previous ones. Its matrix form is the QR decomposition $A=QR$ — one of the most important factorizations in numerical linear algebra.

Picture building a set of perfectly perpendicular rulers. You have three crooked sticks (your basis). First ruler: just normalize stick 1. Second ruler: take stick 2, cancel out any component parallel to ruler 1, then normalize. Third ruler: take stick 3, cancel components parallel to rulers 1 and 2, then normalize. Each step subtracts the "shadow" of the new stick on existing rulers. The result: three perfectly orthogonal, unit-length rulers that span the same space.

GRAM-SCHMIDT PROCESS

Given a basis $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_n\}$ for an inner product space, construct an orthonormal basis $\{\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_n\}$ as follows. Set $\mathbf{u}_1=\mathbf{v}_1$, then for $k=2,\ldots,n$:

$$\mathbf{u}_k = \mathbf{v}_k - \sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\frac{\langle\mathbf{v}_k,\mathbf{u}_j\rangle}{\langle\mathbf{u}_j,\mathbf{u}_j\rangle}\mathbf{u}_j$$

The term $\dfrac{\langle\mathbf{v}_k,\mathbf{u}_j\rangle}{\langle\mathbf{u}_j,\mathbf{u}_j\rangle}\mathbf{u}_j$ is the orthogonal projection of $\mathbf{v}_k$ onto $\mathbf{u}_j$. Finally normalize: $\mathbf{q}_k=\mathbf{u}_k/\|\mathbf{u}_k\|$.

Result: $\{\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_n\}$ is orthonormal and $\text{span}\{\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_k\}=\text{span}\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}$ for each $k$.

QR DECOMPOSITION

Every matrix $A$ with linearly independent columns can be written as $A=QR$ where:

  • $Q$ has orthonormal columns (the Gram-Schmidt output $\mathbf{q}_i$)
  • $R$ is upper triangular with positive diagonal entries

The columns of $A$ express as: $\mathbf{a}_k=r_{1k}\mathbf{q}_1+\cdots+r_{kk}\mathbf{q}_k$ where $r_{jk}=\langle\mathbf{a}_k,\mathbf{q}_j\rangle$. In matrix form $A=QR$. Since $Q^TQ=I$ (orthonormal columns), we can solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ via $QR\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}\Rightarrow R\mathbf{x}=Q^T\mathbf{b}$ (back-substitution only).

Gram-Schmidt on $\mathbb{R}^3$. Given $\mathbf{v}_1=(1,1,0)^T$, $\mathbf{v}_2=(1,0,1)^T$, $\mathbf{v}_3=(0,1,1)^T$: $$\mathbf{u}_1=(1,1,0)^T,\quad\mathbf{q}_1=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1,0)^T$$ $$\mathbf{u}_2=\mathbf{v}_2-\frac{\langle\mathbf{v}_2,\mathbf{u}_1\rangle}{\|\mathbf{u}_1\|^2}\mathbf{u}_1=(1,0,1)^T-\tfrac{1}{2}(1,1,0)^T=(\tfrac{1}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},1)^T$$ $$\mathbf{q}_2=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3/2}}(\tfrac{1}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},1)^T=\tfrac{\sqrt{6}}{3}(\tfrac{1}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2},1)^T$$

Then subtract projections of $\mathbf{v}_3$ onto $\mathbf{q}_1$ and $\mathbf{q}_2$ to get $\mathbf{u}_3$, normalize to $\mathbf{q}_3$.

QR Decomposition is Everywhere in ML. QR underpins: (1) stable least-squares solvers (scipy, LAPACK use QR internally when $A^TA$ is ill-conditioned); (2) the QR algorithm for computing all eigenvalues of a matrix; (3) orthogonal weight initialization in neural networks (Saxe et al., 2013 — orthogonal init speeds up deep network training by preserving gradient norms); (4) principal components are eigenvectors found via the QR iteration. Why does the QR decomposition have $R$ upper triangular (not lower triangular)?
Show Answer

Gram-Schmidt builds $\mathbf{q}_k$ from $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}$. Reversing: $\mathbf{v}_k=r_{1k}\mathbf{q}_1+\cdots+r_{kk}\mathbf{q}_k$ (only $\mathbf{q}_1$ through $\mathbf{q}_k$ appear, not later ones). Writing columns of $A$ as $A=[\mathbf{v}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{v}_n]$ and $Q=[\mathbf{q}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{q}_n]$, the matrix of $r_{jk}$ coefficients is upper triangular: column $k$ of $A$ involves only $\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_k$ (no $\mathbf{q}_{k+1},\ldots$), so $r_{jk}=0$ for $j>k$.

11.4

Orthogonal Complements & Best Approximation

Every subspace $W$ of an inner product space splits the whole space into two orthogonal halves: $W$ and its orthogonal complement $W^\perp$. The orthogonal projection onto $W$ gives the best approximation to any vector — this is the geometric heart of least squares, signal decomposition, and principal component analysis.

ORTHOGONAL COMPLEMENT

The orthogonal complement of a subspace $W$ in an inner product space $V$ is:

$$W^\perp=\{\mathbf{v}\in V : \langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle=0 \text{ for all } \mathbf{w}\in W\}$$

Key properties: (1) $W^\perp$ is a subspace of $V$; (2) $W\cap W^\perp=\{\mathbf{0}\}$; (3) If $V$ is finite-dimensional, $V=W\oplus W^\perp$ (direct sum) and $\dim W+\dim W^\perp=\dim V$; (4) $(W^\perp)^\perp=W$.

ORTHOGONAL DECOMPOSITION THEOREM

If $W$ is a finite-dimensional subspace of an inner product space $V$, then every $\mathbf{v}\in V$ can be written uniquely as:

$$\mathbf{v}=\hat{\mathbf{v}}+\mathbf{v}^\perp,\quad \hat{\mathbf{v}}\in W,\;\mathbf{v}^\perp\in W^\perp$$

The component $\hat{\mathbf{v}}=\text{proj}_W\mathbf{v}$ is the orthogonal projection of $\mathbf{v}$ onto $W$. If $\{\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_k\}$ is an orthonormal basis for $W$:

$$\hat{\mathbf{v}}=\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{q}_1\rangle\mathbf{q}_1+\cdots+\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{q}_k\rangle\mathbf{q}_k=QQ^T\mathbf{v}$$

BEST APPROXIMATION THEOREM

The orthogonal projection $\hat{\mathbf{v}}=\text{proj}_W\mathbf{v}$ is the closest point in $W$ to $\mathbf{v}$:

$$\|\mathbf{v}-\hat{\mathbf{v}}\| \leq \|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w}\|\quad\text{for all }\mathbf{w}\in W$$

with equality iff $\mathbf{w}=\hat{\mathbf{v}}$. This is the theoretical foundation of all least-squares problems: the least-squares solution $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ of $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ gives $\hat{\mathbf{b}}=A\hat{\mathbf{x}}=\text{proj}_{\text{col}(A)}\mathbf{b}$ — the projection of $\mathbf{b}$ onto the column space.

Orthogonal Complement of a Plane. Let $W=\text{span}\{(1,0,1)^T,(0,1,1)^T\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$. To find $W^\perp$, solve $A^T\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ where rows of $A$ are the basis vectors: $$\begin{pmatrix}1&0&1\\0&1&1\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\implies v_1=-v_3,\;v_2=-v_3$$

So $W^\perp=\text{span}\{(-1,-1,1)^T\}$ — a line orthogonal to the plane. Check: $(1,0,1)\cdot(-1,-1,1)=-1+0+1=0$ ✓ and $(0,1,1)\cdot(-1,-1,1)=0-1+1=0$ ✓.

PCA is Orthogonal Projection. Principal Component Analysis finds the $k$-dimensional subspace $W_k$ that captures maximum variance — then projects all data points onto $W_k$. Each projected point is the orthogonal projection onto the span of the top-$k$ eigenvectors of the covariance matrix. The reconstruction error $\|\mathbf{x}-\text{proj}_{W_k}\mathbf{x}\|$ is minimized (not some other projection), exactly because of the Best Approximation Theorem. PCA is literally Gram-Schmidt applied to the eigenvectors, then optimal projection.
If $\hat{\mathbf{v}}=QQ^T\mathbf{v}$ is the projection onto the column space of $Q$ (orthonormal columns), why is $QQ^T$ called a "projection matrix" and why does $(QQ^T)^2=QQ^T$?
Show Answer

$(QQ^T)^2=QQ^TQQ^T=Q(Q^TQ)Q^T=QIQ^T=QQ^T$ since $Q^TQ=I$ (orthonormal columns). Projecting twice equals projecting once — the second projection doesn't move the already-projected vector. This idempotency property $(P^2=P)$ is the defining algebraic property of a projection matrix. Additionally $QQ^T$ is symmetric: $(QQ^T)^T=QQ^T$, making it an orthogonal projection (not just oblique).

11.5

Fourier Series & Least Squares in Function Spaces

Orthogonal projection onto trigonometric functions — the most important inner product computation in all of science

🌊
Every Periodic Signal is a Sum of Sines and Cosines. Fourier's insight (1822): any "reasonable" periodic function can be represented as an infinite sum of sines and cosines. The Fourier coefficients are exactly orthogonal projections — inner products of the function with the basis functions $\{1,\cos x,\sin x,\cos 2x,\sin 2x,\ldots\}$. This unifies signal processing, heat equations, quantum mechanics, and audio compression under one linear algebra framework.

FOURIER SERIES

On $C[-\pi,\pi]$ with inner product $\langle f,g\rangle=\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)g(x)\,dx$, the set

$$\left\{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}},\;\frac{\cos x}{\sqrt{\pi}},\;\frac{\sin x}{\sqrt{\pi}},\;\frac{\cos 2x}{\sqrt{\pi}},\;\frac{\sin 2x}{\sqrt{\pi}},\ldots\right\}$$

is an orthonormal set. The $n$th-order Fourier approximation of $f$ is the projection onto $\text{span}\{1,\cos x,\sin x,\ldots,\cos nx,\sin nx\}$:

$$f(x)\approx\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{k=1}^n\left(a_k\cos kx+b_k\sin kx\right)$$

where the Fourier coefficients are inner products (projections):

$$a_k=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\cos(kx)\,dx,\qquad b_k=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\sin(kx)\,dx$$

PARSEVAL'S THEOREM (ENERGY CONSERVATION)

For a function $f\in C[-\pi,\pi]$ with Fourier series, the total energy splits perfectly across frequencies:

$$\|f\|^2=\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)^2\,dx=\frac{\pi a_0^2}{2}+\pi\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}(a_k^2+b_k^2)$$

This is the infinite-dimensional Pythagorean theorem: the squared norm of $f$ equals the sum of squared Fourier coefficients (times $\pi$). It guarantees no energy is "lost" in the Fourier representation. The same principle underlies the DFT's unitarity and audio codecs' energy preservation.

Fourier Coefficients for the Square Wave

The square wave $f(x)=\text{sgn}(x)$ (value $+1$ for $x>0$, $-1$ for $x<0$) on $[-\pi,\pi]$:

$$a_k=0\text{ (odd function)},\quad b_k=\frac{2}{\pi k}(1-\cos k\pi)=\begin{cases}4/(\pi k)&k\text{ odd}\\0&k\text{ even}\end{cases}$$ $$f(x)\approx\frac{4}{\pi}\left(\sin x+\frac{\sin 3x}{3}+\frac{\sin 5x}{5}+\cdots\right)$$

Only odd harmonics. The more terms added, the closer the approximation — though the Gibbs phenomenon (overshoot near discontinuities) always persists at ~9% regardless of order.

🤖 Data Science Bridge
Fourier Transform → Deep Learning Attention. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) is an $O(n\log n)$ algorithm for computing all $n$ Fourier coefficients simultaneously — it decomposes the DFT matrix (a unitary matrix!) using the structure of roots of unity. Modern neural networks use Fourier features (random Fourier approximation to RBF kernels), FNO (Fourier Neural Operators) for PDE solving, and the attention mechanism itself can be viewed as a learned inner product. The connection: attention score $=\text{softmax}(QK^T/\sqrt{d})$ where $QK^T$ is an $n\times n$ matrix of inner products between queries and keys.
🤔 Socratic Check
Why does the Fourier approximation of order $n$ give the best approximation among all trigonometric polynomials of degree $\leq n$ in the $L^2$ norm?
Show Answer

Because it is the orthogonal projection onto the subspace $T_n=\text{span}\{1,\cos x,\sin x,\ldots,\cos nx,\sin nx\}$. By the Best Approximation Theorem, the orthogonal projection $\hat{f}=\text{proj}_{T_n}f$ minimizes $\|f-g\|_{L^2}$ over all $g\in T_n$. The Fourier coefficients $a_k=\langle f,\cos kx\rangle/\|\cos kx\|^2$ are exactly the inner products that define this projection. Any other choice of coefficients gives a larger $L^2$ error.
