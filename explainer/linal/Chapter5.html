5.1

Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

An eigenvector of a matrix $A$ is a non-zero vector that the matrix merely stretches or flips ‚Äî never rotating it off its line. The stretch factor is the eigenvalue.

Think of a spinning top. Most points on the top trace out complicated circles. But the spin axis itself just stays put (or points in the same direction): it only gets "scaled" by 1. That axis is an eigenvector of the rotation matrix. Eigenvalues measure how much each special direction gets stretched. Finding these axes reveals the deepest structure of any transformation.

DEFINITION ‚Äî Eigenvalue & Eigenvector

If $A$ is an $n\times n$ matrix, a non-zero vector $\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^n$ is an eigenvector of $A$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$ if:

$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$

Rearranging: $(\lambda I - A)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ has a non-trivial solution $\Leftrightarrow$ $\det(\lambda I - A)=0$.

Step 1 ‚Äî Characteristic Equation: Compute $p(\lambda)=\det(\lambda I - A)=0$. This gives the characteristic polynomial of $A$.
Step 2 ‚Äî Find Eigenvalues: Solve $p(\lambda)=0$ for $\lambda$. Each solution is an eigenvalue.
Step 3 ‚Äî Find Eigenvectors: For each $\lambda$, solve $(\lambda I - A)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ by row reduction. The non-trivial solutions are the eigenvectors.
Step 4 ‚Äî Eigenspace: The eigenspace $E_\lambda = \ker(\lambda I - A) = \text{null}(\lambda I - A)$ is a subspace containing all eigenvectors for $\lambda$ plus $\mathbf{0}$.
Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A=\begin{bmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{bmatrix}$.

Step 1: $\det(\lambda I - A)=\det\begin{bmatrix}\lambda-3&-1\\-1&\lambda-3\end{bmatrix}=(\lambda-3)^2-1=\lambda^2-6\lambda+8=(\lambda-4)(\lambda-2)=0$.

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1=4$, $\lambda_2=2$.

$\lambda_1=4$: $(4I-A)\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}1&-1\\-1&1\end{bmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ ‚Üí $v_1=v_2$ ‚Üí $\mathbf{v}_1=\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$
$\lambda_2=2$: $(2I-A)\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}-1&-1\\-1&-1\end{bmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ ‚Üí $v_1=-v_2$ ‚Üí $\mathbf{v}_2=\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\end{bmatrix}$

KEY THEOREMS ‚Äî Eigenvalue Properties

  • $\lambda=0$ is an eigenvalue $\Leftrightarrow$ $A$ is singular ($\det A=0$).
  • $\det(A) = \lambda_1\lambda_2\cdots\lambda_n$ (product of all eigenvalues, with multiplicity).
  • $\text{tr}(A) = \lambda_1+\lambda_2+\cdots+\lambda_n$ (trace = sum of eigenvalues).
  • If $A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}$ then $A^k\mathbf{v}=\lambda^k\mathbf{v}$ for any integer $k$.
  • Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent.
  • A symmetric matrix has only real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.

DEFINITION ‚Äî Algebraic vs. Geometric Multiplicity

For eigenvalue $\lambda$:

  • Algebraic multiplicity $m_a(\lambda)$: multiplicity as a root of $p(\lambda)$.
  • Geometric multiplicity $m_g(\lambda)$: $\dim(E_\lambda)=$ nullity$(\lambda I-A)$.
  • Always: $1\le m_g(\lambda)\le m_a(\lambda)$.
Eigenvalues Everywhere in ML. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ of a dataset has eigenvectors = principal components and eigenvalues = variances along those directions. The largest eigenvalue points toward maximum variance. Google's PageRank is the dominant eigenvector of a web-link matrix. Neural network training speed depends on the eigenvalue spectrum of the Hessian. Eigenvalues are the heartbeat of data science.
A $3\times 3$ matrix has eigenvalues $\lambda=2,2,5$. What is $\det(A)$? What is $\text{tr}(A)$?
5.2

Diagonalization

A matrix is diagonalizable if it can be written $A=PDP^{-1}$ where $D$ is diagonal. In this eigenbasis, the transformation reduces to pure scaling ‚Äî the simplest possible form.

Diagonalization is like finding the "natural coordinate system" of a transformation. In standard coordinates a matrix looks complicated. But in the eigenbasis, it's just a list of stretch factors on the diagonal. It's like describing music with Fourier frequencies instead of raw waveforms ‚Äî the same signal, incomparably simpler to analyze.

DEFINITION ‚Äî Diagonalizable Matrix

An $n\times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if there exists an invertible matrix $P$ and a diagonal matrix $D$ such that $A = PDP^{-1}$, or equivalently $P^{-1}AP = D$.

The columns of $P$ are eigenvectors of $A$, and the diagonal entries of $D$ are the corresponding eigenvalues.

THEOREM ‚Äî Diagonalizability Criterion

An $n\times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if and only if $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. This is guaranteed when:

  • $A$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues (sufficient but not necessary), or
  • For every eigenvalue $\lambda$: geometric multiplicity $=$ algebraic multiplicity.
Find all eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_k$ from $\det(\lambda I-A)=0$.
Find eigenvectors ‚Äî a basis for each eigenspace $E_{\lambda_i}=\ker(\lambda_i I-A)$.
Check count ‚Äî if total number of independent eigenvectors $= n$, $A$ is diagonalizable.
Form $P$ ‚Äî columns are the eigenvectors. Form $D$ ‚Äî diagonal entries are corresponding eigenvalues.
Verify ‚Äî compute $AP = PD$ (or equivalently $P^{-1}AP = D$).
Diagonalize $A=\begin{bmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{bmatrix}$ from ¬ß5.1.

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1=4$, $\lambda_2=2$. Eigenvectors: $\mathbf{v}_1=(1,1)^T$, $\mathbf{v}_2=(1,-1)^T$.

$P=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}$, $D=\begin{bmatrix}4&0\\0&2\end{bmatrix}$

$P^{-1}=\tfrac{1}{-2}\begin{bmatrix}-1&-1\\-1&1\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1/2&1/2\\1/2&-1/2\end{bmatrix}$

Verify: $P^{-1}AP=\begin{bmatrix}1/2&1/2\\1/2&-1/2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}4&0\\0&2\end{bmatrix}=D$ ‚úì

THEOREM ‚Äî Powers via Diagonalization

If $A=PDP^{-1}$ then $A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$, where $D^k=\text{diag}(\lambda_1^k,\ldots,\lambda_n^k)$. This reduces matrix exponentiation from $O(n^3)$ per step to a single matrix product.

Not every matrix is diagonalizable! $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$ has $\lambda=1$ (double), but only one independent eigenvector: $(1,0)^T$. It cannot be diagonalized over $\mathbb{R}$ ‚Äî only brought to Jordan form. Such matrices are called defective.
PCA = Diagonalization of the Covariance Matrix. PCA computes eigenvalues/eigenvectors of $\Sigma = X^TX/n$. Since $\Sigma$ is symmetric, it's always diagonalizable: $\Sigma = Q\Lambda Q^T$ (orthogonal $Q$). The eigenvalues in $\Lambda$ are variances; the eigenvectors (columns of $Q$) are principal components. Projecting data onto top eigenvectors performs dimensionality reduction.
5.3

Complex Vector Spaces

Real matrices can have complex eigenvalues. To handle them, we extend to complex vector spaces. This unlocks rotation matrices, quantum states, signal processing, and Fourier analysis.

Imagine you're trying to find the "spin axis" of a rotation matrix. A pure rotation in 2D has no real eigenvectors ‚Äî nothing stays on its own line! But in the complex plane, the eigenvalues $e^{i\theta}$ and $e^{-i\theta}$ capture the rotation angle. Complex numbers aren't mysterious ‚Äî they're the natural language for oscillation and rotation.

DEFINITION ‚Äî Complex Vector Space $\mathbb{C}^n$

$\mathbb{C}^n$ is the set of $n$-tuples of complex numbers with component-wise addition and scalar multiplication over $\mathbb{C}$. Key vocabulary:

  • Complex conjugate: $\bar{z}=a-bi$ for $z=a+bi$
  • Conjugate transpose: $A^*=\bar{A}^T$ (also written $A^\dagger$)
  • Hermitian matrix: $A=A^*$ (complex analogue of symmetric)
  • Unitary matrix: $U^*U=I$ (complex analogue of orthogonal)
  • Normal matrix: $A^*A=AA^*$

THEOREM ‚Äî Eigenvalues of Real Matrices

Complex eigenvalues of a real matrix always come in conjugate pairs: if $\lambda=a+bi$ is an eigenvalue with eigenvector $\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{p}+i\mathbf{q}$, then $\bar{\lambda}=a-bi$ is also an eigenvalue with eigenvector $\bar{\mathbf{v}}=\mathbf{p}-i\mathbf{q}$.

Find eigenvalues of the rotation matrix $A=\begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{bmatrix}$.

$p(\lambda)=(\lambda-\cos\theta)^2+\sin^2\theta=\lambda^2-2\cos\theta\cdot\lambda+1=0$
$\lambda=\cos\theta\pm i\sin\theta = e^{\pm i\theta}$

For $\theta=90¬∞$: $\lambda=\pm i$ ‚Äî pure imaginary! The rotation has no real eigenvectors. In $\mathbb{C}^2$: eigenvector for $\lambda=i$ is $(1,-i)^T$, for $\lambda=-i$ is $(1,i)^T$.

THEOREM ‚Äî Spectral Theorem (Hermitian Case)

Every Hermitian matrix $A=A^*$ satisfies:

  • All eigenvalues are real.
  • Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal (in the complex inner product).
  • $A$ is unitarily diagonalizable: $A=U\Lambda U^*$ for unitary $U$.

Real symmetric matrices are a special case of Hermitian matrices.

DEFINITION ‚Äî Complex Inner Product

For $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{C}^n$, the complex inner product is:

$\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle = \bar{\mathbf{u}}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \bar{u}_1v_1+\cdots+\bar{u}_nv_n$

Note: $\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle=\|\mathbf{v}\|^2\ge 0$. The conjugation in the first argument ensures this is real and non-negative.

Fourier Transform = Unitary Diagonalization. The Discrete Fourier Transform matrix $F$ is unitary: $F^*F=nI$. DFT diagonalizes circulant matrices ‚Äî matrices where each row is a cyclic shift of the previous. Signal processing, image compression (JPEG), and audio analysis all exploit this: convolution in time = multiplication in frequency (eigenvalue) space.
