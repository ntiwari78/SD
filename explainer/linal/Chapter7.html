7.1

Orthogonal Matrices

An orthogonal matrix $Q$ is one whose columns form an orthonormal basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$. Geometrically, $Q$ represents a rigid motion: it preserves lengths and angles.

An orthogonal matrix is the linear algebra equivalent of a rigid body rotation (or reflection). When you multiply by $Q$, you're "rotating the coordinate axes" without stretching, shearing, or flipping distances. Think of it as rotating a perfectly rigid cube: every point moves, but distances between points and angles between edges are unchanged.

DEFINITION — Orthogonal Matrix

A square matrix $Q$ is orthogonal if $Q^TQ = QQ^T = I$, i.e., $Q^{-1}=Q^T$. Equivalently:

  • The columns of $Q$ form an orthonormal set.
  • The rows of $Q$ form an orthonormal set.
  • $\|Q\mathbf{x}\|=\|\mathbf{x}\|$ for all $\mathbf{x}$ (isometry).
  • $\det(Q)=\pm 1$: $+1$ for rotations, $-1$ for reflections.

THEOREM — Properties of Orthogonal Matrices

  • $Q^{-1}=Q^T$ (inverse is free — just transpose!)
  • $\det(Q)=\pm 1$
  • Product of two orthogonal matrices is orthogonal.
  • $\langle Q\mathbf{u}, Q\mathbf{v}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle$ (inner product preserved).
  • Eigenvalues of a real orthogonal matrix have $|\lambda|=1$.
Orthogonal Transformations in Deep Learning. Orthogonal weight matrices preserve the norm of activations through layers, preventing vanishing/exploding gradients. Techniques like OrthogonalRNN, WaveRNN, and unitary evolution RNNs explicitly maintain orthogonal weights. The orthogonal group $O(n)$ is a Lie group — its structure is exploited in geometric deep learning and equivariant networks.
7.2

Orthogonal Diagonalization & Spectral Decomposition

The Spectral Theorem guarantees that every real symmetric matrix can be orthogonally diagonalized: $A=Q\Lambda Q^T$. This is the deepest theorem in this chapter.

Regular diagonalization says $A=PDP^{-1}$ with some invertible $P$. The Spectral Theorem says: for symmetric $A$, we can choose $P=Q$ to be orthogonal ($Q^{-1}=Q^T$), so $A=Q\Lambda Q^T$. This means the "natural axes" of a symmetric transformation are actually perpendicular — like the principal axes of an ellipse. Symmetry and perpendicularity are inseparably linked.

THEOREM — Spectral Theorem (Real Symmetric Matrices)

If $A$ is a real symmetric matrix ($A=A^T$), then:

  1. All eigenvalues of $A$ are real.
  2. Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
  3. $A$ is orthogonally diagonalizable: there exists orthogonal $Q$ and diagonal $\Lambda$ such that $A=Q\Lambda Q^T$.

DEFINITION — Spectral Decomposition

If $A=Q\Lambda Q^T$ where $Q=[\mathbf{q}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{q}_n]$ and $\Lambda=\text{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n)$, then expanding:

$A = \lambda_1\mathbf{q}_1\mathbf{q}_1^T + \lambda_2\mathbf{q}_2\mathbf{q}_2^T + \cdots + \lambda_n\mathbf{q}_n\mathbf{q}_n^T$

Each rank-1 matrix $\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$ is the orthogonal projector onto the $i$-th eigenvector direction. $A$ is a weighted sum of perpendicular projections.

Find eigenvalues of $A$ (all real since $A=A^T$).
Find eigenvectors for each eigenvalue. For repeated $\lambda$: apply Gram-Schmidt within the eigenspace to get orthonormal eigenvectors.
Form $Q$ with these orthonormal eigenvectors as columns. Form $\Lambda=\text{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n)$.
Verify: $Q^TQ=I$ (check columns orthonormal) and $Q^TAQ=\Lambda$.

The spectral decomposition shows $\Sigma\approx\sum_{i=1}^k\lambda_i\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$ — a rank-$k$ approximation capturing the most variance.
7.3

Quadratic Forms

A quadratic form is a polynomial with all degree-2 terms: $Q(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}$. Its matrix $A$ is always taken symmetric. The eigenvalues of $A$ classify the shape of the quadratic surface.

A quadratic form is a "bowl-shaped" function — or maybe a saddle, or an upside-down bowl. Eigenvalues tell you the curvatures of the bowl along each principal axis. All positive eigenvalues → opening upward (positive definite, a minimum). Mix of signs → saddle point. All negative → opening downward. This is the second-derivative test for multivariable calculus, stated in linear algebra terms.

DEFINITION — Quadratic Form

A quadratic form in $n$ variables is $Q(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}$ where $A$ is a symmetric $n\times n$ matrix. In components:

$Q(\mathbf{x})=\sum_{i=1}^n a_{ii}x_i^2 + 2\sum_{i < j}a_{ij}x_ix_j$

The matrix $A$ is uniquely determined by taking $a_{ij}=\tfrac{1}{2}(\text{coefficient of }x_ix_j)$ for $i\ne j$.

THEOREM — Principal Axes Theorem

Every quadratic form $Q(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}$ can be transformed to a sum of squares with no cross terms by the substitution $\mathbf{x}=Q\mathbf{y}$ (where $Q$ orthogonally diagonalizes $A$):

$Q(\mathbf{x})=\lambda_1 y_1^2+\lambda_2 y_2^2+\cdots+\lambda_n y_n^2$

The axes of the quadratic surface are the eigenvectors of $A$, and the curvatures along them are the eigenvalues.

Loss Landscapes in Deep Learning. Near a local minimum, the loss function looks like a quadratic form $L(\theta)\approx\tfrac{1}{2}(\theta-\theta^*)^TH(\theta-\theta^*)$ where $H$ is the Hessian. The eigenvalues of $H$ determine convergence speed: small eigenvalues = flat directions (slow learning); large eigenvalues = steep directions (fast learning, risk of oscillation). The ratio $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ is the condition number — it governs the difficulty of optimization.
7.4

Optimization Using Quadratic Forms

Eigenvalues solve the constrained optimization problem: find the extreme values of $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}$ on the unit sphere. The answer is simply the largest and smallest eigenvalues.

THEOREM — Rayleigh-Ritz (Constrained Extrema)

Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ symmetric matrix with eigenvalues $\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\cdots\ge\lambda_n$. Then for $\mathbf{x}\ne\mathbf{0}$:

$\lambda_n\le\dfrac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\|\mathbf{x}\|^2}\le\lambda_1$

The quantity $R(\mathbf{x})=\dfrac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\|\mathbf{x}\|^2}$ is the Rayleigh quotient. It equals $\lambda_1$ when $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{q}_1$ and $\lambda_n$ when $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{q}_n$ (eigenvectors).

Imagine stretching a rubber sphere with the matrix $A$. The sphere becomes an ellipsoid. The longest axis of the ellipsoid is the eigenvector of $\lambda_{\max}$ — the direction $A$ "stretches most." The shortest axis corresponds to $\lambda_{\min}$. The Rayleigh quotient tells you how much any direction gets stretched, and is bounded by these extreme eigenvalues.

THEOREM — Min/Max with Orthogonality Constraint

With $A$ symmetric and $\lambda_1\ge\cdots\ge\lambda_n$, and $\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_n$ the corresponding orthonormal eigenvectors:

  • $\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|=1}\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}=\lambda_1$, attained at $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{q}_1$.
  • $\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|=1,\,\mathbf{x}\perp\mathbf{q}_1}\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}=\lambda_2$, attained at $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{q}_2$.
  • In general: $\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|=1,\,\mathbf{x}\perp\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_{k-1}}\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}=\lambda_k$.
SVM, PCA, and the Rayleigh Quotient. PCA maximizes variance (= Rayleigh quotient of $\Sigma$) subject to orthogonality constraints — the solution is exactly the sequence of eigenvectors of $\Sigma$. Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) maximizes a generalized Rayleigh quotient $\mathbf{w}^TS_B\mathbf{w}/\mathbf{w}^TS_W\mathbf{w}$. The SVM margin equals the smallest eigenvalue problem. The Rayleigh quotient is the unifying optimization principle behind dozens of ML methods.
7.5

Hermitian, Unitary & Normal Matrices

Over $\mathbb{C}$, symmetric and orthogonal generalize to Hermitian and unitary. The most general class admitting unitary diagonalization is the normal matrices: $A^*A=AA^*$.

DEFINITIONS — Complex Matrix Classes

ClassConditionReal AnalogueEigenvalues
Hermitian$A=A^*$Symmetric ($A=A^T$)Real
Skew-Hermitian$A=-A^*$Skew-symmetricPure imaginary
Unitary$U^*U=I$Orthogonal ($Q^TQ=I$)On unit circle $|\lambda|=1$
Normal$A^*A=AA^*$Normal ($A^TA=AA^T$)Any complex

THEOREM — Complex Spectral Theorem

A matrix $A\in M_n(\mathbb{C})$ is unitarily diagonalizable ($A=U\Lambda U^*$ for some unitary $U$) if and only if $A$ is normal ($A^*A=AA^*$).

Special cases: Hermitian $\Rightarrow$ Normal $\Rightarrow$ Unitarily diagonalizable. Unitary $\Rightarrow$ Normal.

Is $A=\begin{bmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}$ normal?
$A^T=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{bmatrix}=-A$ (skew-symmetric = skew-Hermitian for real matrices).
$AA^T=\begin{bmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$
$A^TA=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$
$AA^T=A^TA=I$ ✓ $A$ is normal (it's also orthogonal — a 90° rotation!). Eigenvalues: $\pm i$ (on unit circle).
Schur Decomposition. Every square matrix $A$ (even non-normal) can be written $A=UTU^*$ where $U$ is unitary and $T$ is upper triangular (Schur triangular form). If $A$ is normal, $T$ is diagonal. This is the computational backbone of the QR algorithm — the standard method for computing eigenvalues numerically.
Quantum Computing Connection. In quantum mechanics, observables are Hermitian operators (guaranteeing real measurement outcomes). State evolution is governed by unitary operators (preserving probability norms). The eigenvalues of a Hermitian Hamiltonian are the energy levels of a system. Quantum circuits are products of unitary matrices — the entire field of quantum computing rests on Chapter 7 of linear algebra.

Chapter 7 Knowledge Check

10 questions covering orthogonal matrices, spectral decomposition, quadratic forms, and optimization.

Q1. A matrix $Q$ is orthogonal if and only if:

B. The defining condition is Q⁻¹=Qᵀ (equivalently QᵀQ=I). Note: det(Q)=±1 (not necessarily +1), so A is wrong. C is wrong: orthogonal ≠ symmetric in general.

Q2. An orthogonal matrix $Q$ satisfies $\|Q\mathbf{x}\|=\|\mathbf{x}\|$ because:

B. The proof is direct: ‖Qx‖² = (Qx)ᵀ(Qx) = xᵀQᵀQx = xᵀIx = ‖x‖². The key step uses QᵀQ=I. This shows Q is an isometry — it preserves distances and angles.

Q3. The Spectral Theorem says every real symmetric matrix $A$ can be written as $A=Q\Lambda Q^T$ where:

B. The Spectral Theorem guarantees Q is orthogonal (QᵀQ=I) and Λ=diag(λ₁,...,λₙ) with all real λᵢ. This holds even for repeated eigenvalues — you apply Gram-Schmidt within each repeated eigenspace to get orthonormal eigenvectors.

Q4. The quadratic form $Q(\mathbf{x})=x_1^2+4x_1x_2+x_2^2$ has symmetric matrix:

B. For the cross term 4x₁x₂, we split equally: a₁₂=a₂₁=4/2=2. So A=[[1,2],[2,1]]. Verify: xᵀAx = x₁²+2x₁x₂+2x₂x₁+x₂² = x₁²+4x₁x₂+x₂². ✓

Q5. A quadratic form $Q(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}$ is positive definite when:

C. Positive definite ↔ all eigenvalues > 0. In principal axes: Q = λ₁y₁²+...+λₙyₙ² > 0 for y≠0 iff all λᵢ > 0. Note: det(A)>0 is necessary but not sufficient (e.g., A=diag(−2,−2) has det=4>0 but is negative definite).

Q6. For the quadratic form $Q=3x^2+2xy+3y^2$, the level curve $Q=1$ is:

C. Matrix A=[[3,1],[1,3]] has eigenvalues λ₁=4, λ₂=2 (both positive → positive definite). In principal axes Q=4y₁²+2y₂²=1 is an ellipse. Positive definite ↔ ellipses (or circles when all λ are equal).

Q7. The Rayleigh quotient $R(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}/\|\mathbf{x}\|^2$ satisfies:

B. The Rayleigh-Ritz theorem: for symmetric A with eigenvalues λ_min≤...≤λ_max, the Rayleigh quotient is bounded: λ_min ≤ xᵀAx/‖x‖² ≤ λ_max. Equality holds at the corresponding eigenvectors.

Q8. A Hermitian matrix $A=A^*$ always has:

B. The Complex Spectral Theorem: Hermitian matrices have real eigenvalues and are unitarily diagonalizable (A=UΛU* with unitary U and real Λ). Hermitian is the complex generalization of real symmetric — all the nice spectral properties carry over.

Q9. Which matrix class is the most general that guarantees unitary diagonalizability?

C. The Complex Spectral Theorem: A is unitarily diagonalizable ↔ A is normal (A*A=AA*). Normal includes Hermitian, skew-Hermitian, and unitary as special cases. Non-normal matrices (e.g., [[0,1],[0,0]]) cannot be unitarily diagonalized.

Q10. For a symmetric $A$ with $\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2$, the maximum of $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}$ subject to $\|\mathbf{x}\|=1$ equals:

C. The maximum of the Rayleigh quotient on the unit sphere is λ₁ (the largest eigenvalue), attained at the corresponding unit eigenvector q₁. This is the Courant-Fischer theorem — the foundation of PCA: the first PC is the direction of maximum variance.
