§ 1.1

Introduction to Systems of Linear Equations

A linear equation in $n$ variables is the simplest possible constraint: a weighted sum equals a constant. But even the simplest constraint, repeated, can describe the entire physical world.

🔦 Feynman Analogy

Think of each equation as a laser beam sweeping through space. In 2D, each equation $ax + by = c$ is a straight line — like a flat laser cutting through a plane. Where two lasers cross is the solution. No crossing? Parallel beams — no solution. Same beam? Infinitely many crossings. Three equations in 3D? Three planes, and the solution is the single point (if it exists) where all three intersect at once.

Definition — Linear Equation
A linear equation in variables $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ has the form: $$a_1 x_1 + a_2 x_2 + \cdots + a_n x_n = b$$ where $a_1, \ldots, a_n, b$ are real constants and at least one $a_i \neq 0$. The $a_i$ are coefficients and $b$ is the constant term. Key: variables appear only to the first power — no $x^2$, $xy$, $\sin x$, etc.
Definition — System of Linear Equations
A system (or simultaneous equations) is a finite collection of linear equations sharing the same variables. A solution is an assignment of values $(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ satisfying every equation simultaneously. The solution set is the collection of all solutions. Every linear system falls into exactly one of three cases:

Consistent — Unique solution: exactly one point satisfies all equations (planes meet at a single point).
Consistent — Infinite solutions: equations are redundant (planes contain a common line or plane).
Inconsistent — No solution: contradictory constraints (parallel planes that never meet).
Worked ExampleSetting up and classifying a 2×2 system

Consider the system: $\quad x + y = 5, \quad 2x - y = 1$

  1. Write the augmented matrix $[A|\mathbf{b}]$ by reading off coefficients row by row:
    $$\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 1 & 5 \\ 2 & -1 & 1 \end{array}\right]$$
  2. Geometrically: Line 1 has slope $-1$, y-intercept $5$. Line 2 has slope $2$, y-intercept $-1$. They are not parallel, so they intersect at exactly one point → unique solution.
  3. Solve: From eq. 1, $y = 5 - x$. Substitute: $2x - (5-x) = 1 \Rightarrow 3x = 6 \Rightarrow x = 2, y = 3$.
  4. Check: $2+3=5$ ✓, $2(2)-3=1$ ✓. Solution: $(x,y) = (2,3)$.
Augmented Matrices

The augmented matrix $[A|\mathbf{b}]$ packages an entire linear system into a compact array. The vertical bar separates the coefficient matrix $A$ from the right-hand side $\mathbf{b}$. Every row represents one equation; every column (before the bar) represents one variable.

Worked Example3×3 system → augmented matrix

The system $\;2x_1 - x_2 + 3x_3 = 7,\; x_1 + 4x_2 - x_3 = 2,\; -3x_1 + 2x_2 + x_3 = -1$ becomes:

$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & -1 & 3 & 7 \\ 1 & 4 & -1 & 2 \\ -3 & 2 & 1 & -1 \end{array}\right]$$

Reading it back: row 1 encodes $2x_1 - x_2 + 3x_3 = 7$, etc. All information about the system lives in this matrix.

§ 1.2

Gaussian Elimination

Gaussian elimination is the cornerstone algorithm of linear algebra — a systematic, mechanical procedure that reduces any augmented matrix to a solved form. It is, at heart, the process of eliminating variables one at a time from the bottom up.

💡 Core Insight

The key idea: any elementary row operation on the augmented matrix $[A|\mathbf{b}]$ produces an equivalent system — same solution set, different appearance. We use this freedom to zero-out entries below each pivot until the matrix is upper triangular, then read solutions by back-substitution. It's like peeling an onion: each step reveals the next variable.

The Three Legal Row Operations
Type I — Interchange: Swap rows $i$ and $j$: $R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$
Type II — Scaling: Multiply row $i$ by nonzero scalar $c$: $cR_i \to R_i$
Type III — Replacement: Replace $R_i$ by $R_i + kR_j$ (for any scalar $k$): $R_i + kR_j \to R_i$

Each operation is reversible (corresponds to an invertible elementary matrix). The solution set is preserved under all three.
Row Echelon Form (REF) — Criteria
A matrix is in row echelon form iff:
1. All zero rows are at the bottom.
2. The first nonzero entry (the pivot / leading entry) of each nonzero row is to the right of the pivot in the row above.
3. All entries below each pivot are zero.

Reduced row echelon form (RREF) additionally requires: all pivots equal 1, and all entries above pivots are also zero.

Worked ExampleFull Gaussian Elimination — 3×3 system

Solve: $x + 2y + z = 8,\; 3x + 8y + z = 18,\; 2x + 7y + 2z = 14$ (note: this is the textbook Example 2 from §1.2).

  1. Augmented matrix:
    $$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&2&1&8\\3&8&1&18\\2&7&2&14\end{array}\right]$$
  2. Eliminate column 1: $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 3R_1$; $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 2R_1$
    $$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&2&1&8\\0&2&-2&-6\\0&3&0&-2\end{array}\right]$$
  3. Eliminate column 2: $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - \tfrac{3}{2}R_2$
    $$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&2&1&8\\0&2&-2&-6\\0&0&3&7\end{array}\right]$$
  4. Back-substitution: Row 3: $3z=7 \Rightarrow z=7/3$. Row 2: $2y-2(7/3)=-6 \Rightarrow y = -4/3$. Row 1: $x = 8-2(-4/3)-7/3 = 4$.
⚠ Common Mistakes
  • Multiplying a row by zero — this destroys information and is not a valid operation.
  • Forgetting to apply row operations to the entire row including the augmented column.
  • Confusing REF (sufficient for back-sub) with RREF (direct readout). Both are valid stopping points.
  • Missing a free variable — if a column has no pivot, that variable is free and solutions are infinite.

§ 1.3

Matrices and Matrix Operations

A matrix is not just a table of numbers — it is a mathematical object with its own arithmetic. Matrix multiplication, in particular, is not the element-wise product you might expect, but a structured operation that encodes linear transformations.

Matrix Equality & Arithmetic

An $m\times n$ matrix $A$ has $m$ rows and $n$ columns; entry in row $i$, column $j$ is $a_{ij}$. Two matrices are equal iff same size and all corresponding entries match.

Addition/Subtraction: $(A \pm B)_{ij} = a_{ij} \pm b_{ij}$ — only defined for same-size matrices. Scalar mult: $(cA)_{ij} = c\cdot a_{ij}$ — scale every entry.

Transpose: $(A^T)_{ij} = a_{ji}$ — rows become columns. $(A^T)^T = A$; $(A+B)^T = A^T+B^T$; $(AB)^T = B^T A^T$.

Matrix Multiplication — The Rule

For $A$ ($m\times k$) and $B$ ($k\times n$), the product $AB$ is $m\times n$ with:

$$(AB)_{ij} = \sum_{r=1}^k a_{ir}\, b_{rj} = \text{(row } i \text{ of } A)\cdot\text{(col } j \text{ of } B)$$

Critical: $AB \neq BA$ in general (matrix mult is non-commutative). Inner dimensions must match: $A_{m\times k} \cdot B_{k\times n}$.

  • $(AB)C = A(BC)$ — associative ✓
  • $A(B+C) = AB+AC$ — distributive ✓
  • $AB = 0$ does NOT imply $A=0$ or $B=0$ ✗
  • $AB = AC$ does NOT imply $B=C$ ✗
Matrix-Vector Product $A\mathbf{x}$

A system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ can be viewed three ways:

  • Row picture: each row of $A$ gives one linear equation (a hyperplane).
  • Column picture: $A\mathbf{x} = x_1\mathbf{a}_1 + x_2\mathbf{a}_2 + \cdots + x_n\mathbf{a}_n$ — a linear combination of $A$'s columns. Consistent iff $\mathbf{b}$ is in the span of the columns of $A$.
  • Transformation picture: $\mathbf{x} \mapsto A\mathbf{x}$ is a linear function from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}^m$.
Partitioned Matrices

Large matrices can be divided into blocks (submatrices). Block multiplication works like regular multiplication if block sizes are compatible. Very useful for theoretical proofs and parallel computation.

$$\begin{bmatrix}A_{11}&A_{12}\\A_{21}&A_{22}\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}B_{11}&B_{12}\\B_{21}&B_{22}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}A_{11}B_{11}+A_{12}B_{21}&\cdots\\\cdots&\cdots\end{bmatrix}$$

§ 1.4

Inverses; Algebraic Properties of Matrices

Not every matrix can be "undone" — but those that can possess an inverse, and the inverse is the gateway to solving systems, understanding transformations, and connecting linear algebra to the rest of mathematics.

Definition — Inverse Matrix
A square $n\times n$ matrix $A$ is invertible (or nonsingular) if there exists an $n\times n$ matrix $B$ such that $AB = BA = I_n$. We write $B = A^{-1}$ and call it the inverse of $A$. If no such $B$ exists, $A$ is called singular. The inverse, when it exists, is unique.
💡 Geometric Insight

Think of $A$ as a transformation: it "does something" to every vector. $A^{-1}$ undoes exactly that something. Rotate by 45°? The inverse rotates back by −45°. Stretch by factor 3? The inverse shrinks by factor 1/3. A singular matrix crushes space — it maps an entire dimension to zero. There's no way to undo that collapse: once information is lost, it cannot be recovered.

Formula — 2×2 Inverse
For $A = \begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}$ with $ad - bc \neq 0$: $$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{bmatrix}$$ The quantity $ad-bc$ is the determinant of $A$ (Chapter 2). If $\det(A) = 0$, the inverse does not exist.
Worked ExampleComputing 2×2 Inverse

Find $A^{-1}$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix}3&1\\5&2\end{bmatrix}$.

  1. Compute $\det(A) = 3\cdot2 - 1\cdot5 = 6-5 = 1 \neq 0$ ✓ (invertible)
  2. Apply formula: $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{1}\begin{bmatrix}2&-1\\-5&3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2&-1\\-5&3\end{bmatrix}$
  3. Verify: $AA^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}3&1\\5&2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}2&-1\\-5&3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6-5&-3+3\\10-10&-5+6\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$ ✓

Theorem — Properties of Inverses
If $A$ and $B$ are invertible $n\times n$ matrices, then:
 • $(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$  |  $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$ (reverse order!)  |  $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$  |  $(cA)^{-1} = \frac{1}{c}A^{-1}$ for $c\neq0$

⚡ Note the reversal: $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$ — "last on, first off" (like putting on and removing shoes and socks).
§ 1.5

Elementary Matrices & Finding A⁻¹

Elementary matrices translate row operations into matrix multiplication — making the algebra of Gaussian elimination precise and opening the door to the LU-decomposition.

Definition — Elementary Matrix
An elementary matrix $E$ is any matrix obtained from the identity $I_n$ by performing a single elementary row operation. Key fact: performing that same row operation on any matrix $A$ is equivalent to computing $EA$. All elementary matrices are invertible; their inverses are also elementary matrices (the reverse operation).
Worked ExampleFinding A⁻¹ via Gauss–Jordan (row-reducing [A|I])

Find the inverse of $A = \begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&7\end{bmatrix}$ using Gauss–Jordan elimination.

  1. Form the augmented matrix $[A|I]$:
    $$\left[\begin{array}{cc|cc}1&2&1&0\\3&7&0&1\end{array}\right]$$
  2. $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 3R_1$:
    $$\left[\begin{array}{cc|cc}1&2&1&0\\0&1&-3&1\end{array}\right]$$
  3. $R_1 \leftarrow R_1 - 2R_2$ (back-elimination to reach RREF):
    $$\left[\begin{array}{cc|cc}1&0&7&-2\\0&1&-3&1\end{array}\right]$$
  4. Left side = $I$, so right side = $A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}7&-2\\-3&1\end{bmatrix}$. Verify: $\det(A) = 7-6=1$ and formula gives same answer ✓
Theorem — Gauss–Jordan Method
Start with $[A | I_n]$ and apply row operations until the left side reaches $I_n$. The right side then becomes $A^{-1}$. If at any point the left side develops a row of zeros, then $A$ is singular (not invertible).
💡 Why Does This Work?

Each row operation multiplies by an elementary matrix $E_i$ on the left. After all operations: $E_k\cdots E_2 E_1 A = I$. Therefore $A^{-1} = E_k\cdots E_1$. But the right half of $[A|I]$ starts as $I$ and accumulates the same operations: it becomes $E_k\cdots E_1 \cdot I = A^{-1}$. Elegant!

§ 1.6

More on Linear Systems & Invertible Matrices

The invertibility of a matrix is intimately linked to the number of solutions of the linear system $Ax=b$. This section crystallises that relationship into one powerful theorem.

🎓 Feynman Lens

Think of $A$ as a machine. If $A$ is invertible, the machine has a perfect undo button: given any output $b$, you can recover the unique input $x = A^{-1}b$. A singular matrix is a machine that "crushes" information — you can't tell which input produced the output.

Theorem — Equivalent Conditions for Invertibility (Big List)
For an $n\times n$ matrix $A$, the following are ALL equivalent:
  1. $A$ is invertible.
  2. $Ax = 0$ has only the trivial solution $x = 0$.
  3. The RREF of $A$ is $I_n$.
  4. $A$ can be expressed as a product of elementary matrices.
  5. $Ax = b$ is consistent for every $b \in \mathbb{R}^n$.
  6. $Ax = b$ has exactly one solution for every $b$.
  7. $\det(A) \neq 0$.
🤔 Socratic Check

If a $3\times 3$ system $Ax=b$ has infinitely many solutions for some specific $b$, what does that tell you about the matrix $A$? Can $Ax=b$ be inconsistent for some other $b$?

Reveal answer

$A$ must be singular (not invertible). Yes — when $A$ is singular, some $b$ gives infinitely many solutions while other $b$ give no solutions at all. The two failure modes coexist.

Worked ExampleUsing Invertibility to Solve a System

Solve $Ax = b$ where $A = \begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&7\end{bmatrix}$ and $b = \begin{bmatrix}5\\18\end{bmatrix}$.

  1. We already found $A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}7&-2\\-3&1\end{bmatrix}$ in §1.5.
  2. $x = A^{-1}b = \begin{bmatrix}7&-2\\-3&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}5\\18\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}35-36\\-15+18\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1\\3\end{bmatrix}$.
  3. Verify: $A\begin{bmatrix}-1\\3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1+6\\-3+21\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}5\\18\end{bmatrix}$ ✓
📊 Data Science Bridge

The normal equations of least-squares regression are $(A^TA)x = A^Tb$. When $A^TA$ is invertible, the unique solution $\hat{x} = (A^TA)^{-1}A^Tb$ is the least-squares solution. Invertibility of the Gram matrix $A^TA$ is therefore the key condition for unique regression coefficients.

§ 1.7

Diagonal, Triangular & Symmetric Matrices

Special matrix structures allow us to solve systems in $O(n)$ time, diagonalise transformations, and exploit symmetry everywhere from physics to machine learning.

Definitions
TypeDefinitionExample
Diagonal$a_{ij}=0$ for $i\neq j$$\begin{bmatrix}3&0\\0&-2\end{bmatrix}$
Upper triangular$a_{ij}=0$ for $i>j$$\begin{bmatrix}1&4\\0&3\end{bmatrix}$
Lower triangular$a_{ij}=0$ for $i$\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\5&1\end{bmatrix}$
Symmetric$A = A^T$ (i.e. $a_{ij}=a_{ji}$)$\begin{bmatrix}1&3\\3&2\end{bmatrix}$
Skew-symmetric$A = -A^T$$\begin{bmatrix}0&-5\\5&0\end{bmatrix}$
Key Properties
  • A diagonal matrix $D = \text{diag}(d_1,\ldots,d_n)$ is invertible iff all $d_i \neq 0$; then $D^{-1} = \text{diag}(1/d_1,\ldots,1/d_n)$.
  • Products of upper (lower) triangular matrices are upper (lower) triangular.
  • A triangular matrix is invertible iff all diagonal entries are non-zero.
  • For any matrix $A$: both $AA^T$ and $A^TA$ are symmetric.
  • Every square matrix decomposes as $A = S + K$ where $S = \tfrac{1}{2}(A+A^T)$ (symmetric) and $K = \tfrac{1}{2}(A-A^T)$ (skew-symmetric).
Worked ExampleBack-substitution on a Triangular System

Solve the upper triangular system: $$\begin{bmatrix}2&3&-1\\0&1&4\\0&0&5\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}7\\6\\10\end{bmatrix}$$

  1. Row 3: $5x_3 = 10 \Rightarrow x_3 = 2$.
  2. Row 2: $x_2 + 4(2) = 6 \Rightarrow x_2 = -2$.
  3. Row 1: $2x_1 + 3(-2) + (-1)(2) = 7 \Rightarrow 2x_1 = 15 \Rightarrow x_1 = 7.5$.
  4. Solution: $(7.5,\,-2,\,2)$. Back-substitution is $O(n^2)$ — much faster than full elimination for large systems.
📊 Data Science Bridge

The LU-decomposition factors any invertible $A = LU$ (lower × upper triangular). Gaussian elimination is exactly this process. Triangular systems are then solved in two fast passes. Modern neural net weight matrices have structure (sparsity, symmetry) exploited for efficiency — and covariance matrices are always symmetric positive semi-definite.

💡 Geometric Insight

Symmetric matrices are the matrices that "look the same from every angle" — reflecting across the main diagonal leaves them unchanged. Geometrically, they correspond to transformations with perpendicular eigenvectors (real spectral theorem), which is why covariance matrices and Gram matrices are so well-behaved.

§ 1.8

Matrix Transformations

Every matrix $A$ defines a function $T(x) = Ax$ that maps vectors to vectors. Understanding how this function warps, rotates, and scales space is the geometric heart of linear algebra.

🎓 Feynman Lens

Think of a matrix as a pair of "landing spots" for the basis vectors $\hat{e}_1 = (1,0)$ and $\hat{e}_2 = (0,1)$. The columns of $A$ tell you exactly where $\hat{e}_1$ and $\hat{e}_2$ land. Everything else follows by linearity — like a rubber sheet stretched to those anchor points.

Definition — Matrix Transformation
A function $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ defined by $T(x) = Ax$ (where $A$ is $m\times n$) is called a matrix transformation (or linear transformation). It satisfies:
  • $T(u+v) = T(u) + T(v)$ (additivity)
  • $T(cu) = cT(u)$ (homogeneity)
  • $T(0) = 0$ always (linear transformations fix the origin)
Standard 2D Transformations
TransformationMatrix
Rotation by $\theta$ (CCW)$\begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{bmatrix}$
Reflection across $x$-axis$\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{bmatrix}$
Reflection across $y$-axis$\begin{bmatrix}-1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$
Horizontal shear$\begin{bmatrix}1&k\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$
Scaling by $(s_x,s_y)$$\begin{bmatrix}s_x&0\\0&s_y\end{bmatrix}$
Projection onto $x$-axis$\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{bmatrix}$

📊 Data Science Bridge

Image transformations in computer vision (rotate, shear, scale, flip) are exactly $2\times 2$ (or $3\times 3$ for 3D) matrix multiplications. Neural network layers compute $Wx + b$ — a matrix transformation followed by a translation. PCA rotates data into a new coordinate system using orthogonal transformations (rotation matrices).

💡 One-to-One & Onto

$T(x) = Ax$ is one-to-one (injective) iff $Ax = 0$ has only the trivial solution iff columns of $A$ are linearly independent. It is onto (surjective) iff $Ax = b$ is consistent for all $b$ iff columns of $A$ span $\mathbb{R}^m$. For square $A$: one-to-one $\Leftrightarrow$ onto $\Leftrightarrow$ invertible.

§ 1.9

Applications of Linear Systems

Linear systems model countless real-world phenomena. We'll explore four canonical applications: traffic flow, electrical circuits, chemical balancing, and polynomial interpolation.

🎓 Feynman Lens

At every intersection, what flows in must flow out (conservation). Write that rule as an equation for every node. Assemble the equations into a matrix. Solve. You've just automated the analysis of an entire road network — this is exactly how GPS routing computes optimal paths through millions of junctions.

Application 1Traffic Flow Network

Consider a one-way network with intersections A, B, C, D and measured inflow/outflow. At each intersection: flow in = flow out (conservation).

  1. Assign variables $x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4$ to the unknown road segments.
  2. Write a conservation equation at each node: e.g. Node A: incoming traffic = outgoing traffic.
  3. Assemble into $Ax = b$ and solve (often underdetermined → infinitely many solutions parameterised by free variables representing flexibility in routing).
  4. Any solution with all $x_i \geq 0$ is physically valid. Negative values indicate the assumed direction was wrong.
Application 2Balancing Chemical Equations

Balance: $x_1\,\text{C}_3\text{H}_8 + x_2\,\text{O}_2 \to x_3\,\text{CO}_2 + x_4\,\text{H}_2\text{O}$

  1. Carbon: $3x_1 = x_3$
  2. Hydrogen: $8x_1 = 2x_4$
  3. Oxygen: $2x_2 = 2x_3 + x_4$
  4. Rewrite as $Ax = 0$; find the null space. Setting $x_1 = 1$: $x_3 = 3$, $x_4 = 4$, $x_2 = 5$.
  5. Balanced equation: $\text{C}_3\text{H}_8 + 5\text{O}_2 \to 3\text{CO}_2 + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}$ ✓
Application 3Polynomial Interpolation

Find the unique polynomial $p(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2$ passing through $(0, 1)$, $(1, 3)$, $(2, 7)$.

  1. Substitute each point: $p(0)=a_0=1$; $p(1)=a_0+a_1+a_2=3$; $p(2)=a_0+2a_1+4a_2=7$.
  2. The Vandermonde system: $\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\1&1&1\\1&2&4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}a_0\\a_1\\a_2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\3\\7\end{bmatrix}$.
  3. Solve: $a_0 = 1$, $a_1 = 1$, $a_2 = 1$. Thus $p(x) = 1 + x + x^2$.
  4. Verify: $p(0)=1$, $p(1)=3$, $p(2)=7$ ✓. The Vandermonde matrix is invertible whenever all $x$-values are distinct.
📊 Data Science Bridge

Polynomial interpolation generalises to spline regression and kernel methods. The Vandermonde matrix $V$ appears in spectral analysis of time series. Network flow equations underlie PageRank (Google's original ranking algorithm) and supply-chain optimisation. Every social network recommendation engine solves a sparse linear system.

§ 1.10

Leontief Input–Output Model

Nobel laureate Wassily Leontief (1973) modelled the US economy as a linear system: each industry consumes outputs from all other industries. The question "how much must each sector produce to meet final demand?" reduces to solving $(I-C)x = d$.

🎓 Feynman Lens

Imagine a factory that needs electricity to run, but the power plant needs steel to build generators, and the steel mill needs electricity to smelt ore. Everyone depends on everyone else. The Leontief model untangles this circular dependency by writing the equilibrium conditions as a linear system and solving for the production levels that satisfy everyone simultaneously.

The Leontief Model
  • Consumption matrix $C$: entry $c_{ij}$ = amount of industry $i$'s output consumed by industry $j$ per unit of $j$'s output.
  • Production vector $x$: $x_i$ = total output of industry $i$.
  • Final demand $d$: external (non-industrial) demand for each sector.
  • Equilibrium condition: $x = Cx + d$ (production = internal consumption + external demand)
  • Rearranging: $(I - C)x = d \Rightarrow x = (I-C)^{-1}d$
Worked ExampleTwo-Industry Leontief Model

Economy with Agriculture (A) and Manufacturing (M). Consumption matrix and demand:

$$C = \begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.2 \\ 0.4 & 0.1\end{bmatrix}, \quad d = \begin{bmatrix}12\\8\end{bmatrix}$$
  1. Form $I - C = \begin{bmatrix}0.7 & -0.2 \\ -0.4 & 0.9\end{bmatrix}$.
  2. $\det(I-C) = (0.7)(0.9) - (-0.2)(-0.4) = 0.63 - 0.08 = 0.55$.
  3. $(I-C)^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{0.55}\begin{bmatrix}0.9 & 0.2 \\ 0.4 & 0.7\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1.636&0.364\\0.727&1.273\end{bmatrix}$
  4. $x = (I-C)^{-1}d = \begin{bmatrix}1.636&0.364\\0.727&1.273\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}12\\8\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}22.5\\17.6\end{bmatrix}$
  5. Agriculture must produce ≈22.5 units, Manufacturing ≈17.6 units to meet demand $d$.

📊 Data Science Bridge

The Leontief model is the ancestor of modern graph-based neural networks and supply chain simulation. The matrix $(I-C)^{-1}$ is the "Leontief inverse" — its $(i,j)$ entry captures all direct and indirect requirements of sector $i$ per unit of final demand for sector $j$. The same structure appears in PageRank ($x = \alpha M x + (1-\alpha)\mathbf{1}/n$) and in message-passing algorithms on graphs.
