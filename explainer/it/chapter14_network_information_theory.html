Network Information Theory

Understanding how multiple senders and receivers can communicate optimally through shared channels

Scroll down to explore the fundamental limits of networked communication

The Cocktail Party Problem

Imagine you're at a crowded cocktail party. Multiple people are trying to have separate conversations simultaneously in the same room. The room is the shared channel, each speaker is a transmitter, each listener is a receiver. Despite the noise and interference, people manage to communicate.
This is the fundamental challenge of network information theory: How do you make sure everyone can communicate simultaneously through a shared medium? How much information can flow? What are the limits? And most importantly, how do we achieve those limits?

In network information theory, we ask: What's the maximum rate at which each transmitter can send information? What trade-offs exist between different senders? How does the channel capacity scale with more participants?

Mathematical Foundations

Multiple Access Channel (MAC) Capacity Region

Two senders transmit simultaneously to one receiver. The capacity region—the set of all simultaneously achievable rate pairs—is bounded by three constraints:

$$R_1 < I(X_1; Y | X_2)$$
Sender 1's rate is limited by the information it conveys to the receiver, given that the receiver knows Sender 2's codeword
$$R_2 < I(X_2; Y | X_1)$$
Similarly for Sender 2
$$R_1 + R_2 < I(X_1, X_2; Y)$$
The sum of both rates is limited by the total information both senders can convey together. These three inequalities define a pentagon in the (R₁, R₂) plane—the achievable region.

Gaussian Multiple Access Channel

In the Gaussian setting, the received signal is the sum of transmitted signals plus noise:

$$Y = X_1 + X_2 + Z, \quad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, N)$$

The individual rates are governed by the Shannon capacity formula with individual signal-to-noise ratios:

$$C(P/N) = \frac{1}{2} \log_2\left(1 + \frac{P}{N}\right)$$

And the sum rate grows as if both powers contribute to a single signal:

$$R_1 + R_2 < \frac{1}{2} \log_2\left(1 + \frac{m \cdot P}{N}\right)$$
where m is the number of senders and P is the average power per sender. The critical insight: sum rate grows logarithmically with the number of senders. In a cocktail party, adding more speakers does increase total throughput—but only logarithmically. The advantage of coordination becomes paramount.

Broadcast Channel Capacity Region

Now one sender transmits to multiple receivers. One receiver has better channel quality (lower noise N₁ < N₂). The capacity region is parameterized by power allocation:

$$R_1 < C\left(\frac{\alpha P}{N_1}\right), \quad R_2 < C\left(\frac{(1-\alpha)P}{\alpha P + N_2}\right)$$
The parameter \alpha \in [0,1] represents the fraction of power allocated to the strong receiver. The second inequality reveals a subtle principle: the weaker receiver's rate depends not just on its own noise, but also on the interference from the strong receiver's signal. By using successive interference cancellation, the strong receiver first decodes the weaker receiver's message (treating it as signal), subtracts it, then decodes its own message.

Slepian-Wolf Distributed Source Coding

Two correlated sources generate data. They compress independently, but a joint decoder knows both coded streams. How much total rate is needed?

$$R_1 \geq H(X | Y), \quad R_2 \geq H(Y | X), \quad R_1 + R_2 \geq H(X, Y)$$
Remarkably, each encoder needs only encode enough information to be recoverable given the other source. Encoder 1 need not know Y, yet only must convey H(X|Y) bits—the conditional entropy of X given Y. The decoder, seeing both streams, can disambiguate. This is achieved through random binning and syndrome coding: partition the output space into bins, transmit the bin index, and rely on the correlation to identify the exact codeword.

Implementation: Computing the MAC Capacity Region

Python: Multiple Access Channel Capacity
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def mac_capacity_region(P1, P2, N, num_points=200):
    """
    Compute the multiple access channel capacity region.

    The MAC with two senders has capacity region:
      R1 < C(P1/N)           -- Sender 1 alone
      R2 < C(P2/N)           -- Sender 2 alone
      R1 + R2 < C((P1+P2)/N) -- Both senders combined

    where C(x) = 0.5 * log2(1 + x) is the Gaussian capacity.

    Parameters:
        P1: Power of sender 1
        P2: Power of sender 2
        N: Noise variance
    Returns:
        vertices: Corner points of the capacity pentagon
    """
    def C(snr):
        """Shannon capacity: C = ½ log₂(1 + SNR)"""
        return 0.5 * np.log2(1 + snr)

    # Individual and sum capacities
    C1 = C(P1 / N)           # Max rate for sender 1
    C2 = C(P2 / N)           # Max rate for sender 2
    C_sum = C((P1 + P2) / N) # Max sum rate

    # The capacity region pentagon vertices:
    A = (0, C2)
    B = (C_sum - C2, C2)
    C_pt = (C1, C_sum - C1)
    D = (C1, 0)

    vertices = np.array([
        [0, 0], list(D), list(C_pt), list(B), list(A), [0, 0]
    ])
    return vertices

# Example: Equal power senders
P1, P2, N = 5.0, 5.0, 1.0
region = mac_capacity_region(P1, P2, N)

print("MAC Capacity Region Vertices:")
for v in region[:-1]:
    print(f"  R1={v[0]:.3f}, R2={v[1]:.3f} bits/transmission")

The Cocktail Party Paradox

You're designing a satellite uplink system. Three ground stations share a single satellite transponder. Station A has power budget 10W, Station B has 5W, Station C has 8W. The noise power is 2W. Despite increasing Station A's power to 20W, the total system throughput barely increases. Meanwhile, adding a fourth station with just 3W somehow doesn't reduce the others' rates much at all.

Click on each question to reveal the answer:

1
Why does doubling Station A's power NOT double its achievable rate?
▼
Because capacity grows as 12log2(1+PN), not linearly with power. Doubling power (10W → 20W, with N=2W) increases capacity from C(5) ≈ 2.32 bits to C(10) ≈ 2.81 bits—only 0.49 additional bits per transmission. This is the fundamental speed limit of communication: the logarithm exhibits diminishing returns. Quadrupling power only adds about 1 bit. This is why communication is hard at high SNRs.
2
How can the sum rate grow without bound as more stations join?
▼
The sum rate 12log2(1+mPN) grows as log2m where m is the number of senders. This grows without bound, but slowly. Each new station adds its power to the received signal, increasing the total SNR. In a cocktail party, more speakers CAN coexist if they coordinate properly—all their voices add up constructively at the listener's ear.
3
What happens to the capacity region if stations can cooperate?
▼
If stations can share information and coordinate, they transform the problem from a Multiple Access Channel to a MIMO (Multiple-Input Multiple-Output) system. Instead of all transmitting independently, they can cooperatively beamform: transmit synchronized, coherent signals that constructively interfere at the satellite. The capacity can increase dramatically—potentially achieving rates far beyond what independent transmission allows. This is why coordinated multi-antenna systems are so powerful.
4
In a broadcast channel, why does the better receiver always decode both messages?
▼
The better receiver (lower noise) uses a technique called successive interference cancellation (SIC). It first decodes the weaker receiver's message (treating it as signal, not noise), then subtracts that reconstructed signal from the received signal, and finally decodes its own message in the clean residual. This is counterintuitive but optimal: don't fight interference—decode it! The weaker receiver, having noisier channels, cannot perform this trick and only decodes its intended message.
5
Why is Slepian-Wolf surprising—how can we compress below H(X) without the encoder seeing Y?
▼
The trick: the decoder sees Y, even if the encoder of X doesn't! The X encoder only needs to send H(X|Y) bits—enough information for the decoder to recover X using its knowledge of Y. This is achieved through random binning: map each sequence x to a bin index. Many sequences map to the same bin. The encoder sends just the bin index. The decoder, observing Y, looks in that bin for the unique sequence consistent with Y. This is an example of how exploiting correlation at the decoder side, even without encoder knowledge, can beat independent compression.
