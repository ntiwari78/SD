Chapter 6

Gambling and Data Compression

From Thomas, Cover, and Joy: Elements of Information Theory, pp. 125–143.

The Feynman Mental Model

Imagine a horse race where you know the true odds better than the bookmaker. The Kelly Criterion tells you: bet in proportion to your edge. If you have side information giving you 1 extra bit about the winner, your wealth doubles per race on average. Information isn't just abstract — it's literally money.

This is the beating heart of information theory: the deep, surprising connection between how much you know and how fast you can grow your wealth. In this chapter, we'll see that gambling and data compression are two faces of the same coin.

6.1 The Horse Race: Odds, Betting, and Doubling Rate

Imagine m horses competing in a race. The true probability that horse i wins is pi, and the bookmaker offers odds oi.

A gambler divides her initial wealth into fractions b1, b2, ..., bm (where Σbi = 1), betting bi on horse i. If horse i wins, that fraction multiplies by the odds oi, and all other bets are lost.

$$W(\mathbf{b}, \mathbf{p}) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} p_i \log_2(b_i o_i)$$

This is the doubling rate: the expected log-wealth per race. It tells us the exponential growth rate: after n races, expected wealth is roughly 2nW.

The question: what betting strategy maximizes the doubling rate?

The Kelly Criterion (Optimal Strategy)

The answer is surprisingly elegant: bet proportionally to the true probabilities:

$$b_i^* = p_i$$

If you bet a fraction pi on each horse (proportional to its true winning probability), your doubling rate is maximized. This is the Kelly strategy, and it's optimal in the sense that no other betting strategy can achieve a higher long-run wealth growth rate.

Under Kelly with uniform odds (where oi = m for all i):

$$W^* = \log_2 m - H(\mathbf{p}) = \log_2 m + \sum_{i=1}^{m} p_i \log_2 p_i$$

This says: the optimal doubling rate equals the log of the number of horses minus the entropy of the probability distribution. The more concentrated the probabilities (lower entropy), the higher the doubling rate—because you can safely bet more on the favorites.

6.2 Gambling and Side Information: The Value of Knowledge

Now the story gets even more profound. Suppose before each race, you receive a side signal Y that gives you information about which horse will win (the outcome X).

Your new doubling rate using this side information is W(X|Y). The fundamental question: how much does this side information increase your wealth growth?

$$W(X|Y) - W(X) = I(X; Y)$$

The answer is breathtakingly simple: the increase in doubling rate equals the mutual information between the outcome and the side signal.

If I(X; Y) = 1 bit, your doubling rate increases by 1 bit, meaning your wealth grows roughly twice as fast. If the mutual information is 2 bits, your wealth grows roughly four times as fast.

This is the deep quantification of "information is money." Side information isn't just statistically useful—it translates directly into exponential wealth growth. The mutual information is literally the measure of its value.

6.4 The Entropy of English

Shannon used gambling to estimate the entropy of English text. Here's the experiment: show a person an English passage one character at a time, and ask them to guess the next character before seeing it.

The number of guesses required, on average, relates to the entropy. If a character source has entropy H bits, then on average you need to make about 2H guesses (in the best case).

Shannon's empirical estimates: English has an entropy of approximately 1.0 to 1.5 bits per character. This is far lower than the naive estimate of log₂(26) ≈ 4.7 bits, because English is highly redundant. Not all character sequences are equally likely; common words, patterns, and grammar structure the space of possibilities.

If you have a perfect language model (knowing the true conditional probabilities), you could compress English text to about 1.0–1.5 bits per character. Any compression algorithm that achieves this is optimal.

6.5 The Duality: Gambling and Data Compression

Here is the crown jewel of this chapter: optimal gambling and optimal data compression are the same problem.

Consider a source that generates symbols from an alphabet according to some probability distribution. A gambler bets on each symbol, proportional to its probability (Kelly strategy). A compressor assigns short binary codes to probable symbols (Huffman coding, arithmetic coding).

Both achieve the same thing: they exploit knowledge of the probability distribution to gain exactly H bits of advantage per symbol, where H is the entropy. The gambler's wealth grows at rate H, and the compressor saves H bits per symbol.

The duality is profound: you cannot design a better compressor than one that assigns code lengths proportional to log(1/pi). Similarly, you cannot design a better gambler than one who bets proportional to the probabilities. Both problems have the same optimal solution because they both reduce to exploiting the probability distribution.

This explains why entropy is so central to information theory. Whether you're trying to grow wealth or compress data, entropy sets the fundamental limit. You cannot do better; the source's probability structure determines the achievable rate.

Code Lab: Kelly Criterion in Python

Let's implement the Kelly criterion and verify it empirically.

import numpy as np import matplotlib.pyplot as plt def kelly_strategy(probabilities, odds): """ Compute Kelly betting fractions. Args: probabilities: array of true winning probabilities [p1, p2, ...] odds: array of odds [o1, o2, ...] Returns: optimal betting fractions [b1*, b2*, ...] """ return probabilities def doubling_rate(probabilities, odds, betting): """ Compute the doubling rate W(b, p). """ rates = probabilities * np.log2(betting * odds) return np.sum(rates) def simulate_races(probabilities, odds, betting, num_races): """ Simulate horse races and track wealth. """ wealth = np.ones(num_races + 1) for race in range(num_races): winner = np.random.choice(len(probabilities), p=probabilities) multiplier = betting[winner] * odds[winner] wealth[race + 1] = wealth[race] * multiplier return wealth # Example: 4 horses probs = np.array([0.4, 0.3, 0.2, 0.1]) odds = np.array([4.0, 4.0, 4.0, 4.0]) kelly_bets = kelly_strategy(probs, odds) rate = doubling_rate(probs, odds, kelly_bets) print(f"True probabilities: {probs}") print(f"Kelly optimal bets: {kelly_bets}") print(f"Expected doubling rate: {rate:.4f} bits/race") print(f"Wealth after 100 races: ~2^{100*rate:.1f}") # Simulate wealth = simulate_races(probs, odds, kelly_bets, 100) print(f"Actual final wealth (1 run): {wealth[-1]:.2f}")

Run this code and you'll see that the Kelly strategy consistently outperforms naive strategies (uniform betting, all-in bets) in long-run wealth accumulation. The doubling rate accurately predicts the exponential growth.

Socratic Sandbox: The Gambler's Edge

Q1: If you know the outcome of every race with certainty, what's your doubling rate?
A: Your doubling rate is W* = log₂(m), where m is the number of horses. You bet everything on the certain winner, multiplying your wealth by m each race. After 100 races with 4 horses, you'd have ~4^100 = 2^200 times your initial wealth. With perfect information, entropy is zero, so H(p) = 0, and the doubling rate is purely log₂(m).
Q2: Your friend says "I have a 60% accurate model for a fair coin flip, so I should bet 60% of my wealth." Is this Kelly-optimal?
A: No! The Kelly criterion for a fair binary bet (heads vs. tails, with equal odds) says you should bet 2p - 1 of your wealth on the predicted side. If p = 0.6 (60% accuracy), Kelly says bet 2(0.6) - 1 = 0.2, or only 20% of your wealth. Betting 60% is massive overbetting; it risks much faster ruin if you hit a streak of losses. Kelly is conservative: it maximizes long-run growth while minimizing the risk of drawdown.
Q3: How does the duality between gambling and compression work intuitively?
A: Both a gambler and a compressor are extracting value from the same source: the probability distribution. The gambler bets a larger fraction on outcomes that are more probable, concentrating capital on likely events. The compressor assigns shorter binary codes to probable outcomes, saving bits on the most common events. Both strategies exploit the same insight: probable events should be handled more efficiently. The amount of advantage both gain is exactly H bits, the entropy. If you have a better gambling strategy, you implicitly have a better compression algorithm, and vice versa.
Q4: Can the Kelly strategy ever lose money in the long run?
A: Yes, in any finite run. Bad luck can strike: you could have a string of losses and watch your wealth shrink significantly in the short term. However, the Kelly guarantee is about the long run: with probability approaching 1, Kelly wealth grows at the optimal rate W*. No other strategy can achieve a higher long-run growth rate. If you overbett (bet more than Kelly), you increase the growth rate in good times but risk ruin (wealth going to zero) with probability approaching 1. If you underbett, you reduce risk but also reduce long-run growth. Kelly is the unique sweet spot.

Key Takeaways

  • The Kelly Criterion: To maximize long-run wealth growth, bet a fraction pi on outcome i, proportional to its true probability.
  • Doubling Rate: Wealth grows exponentially at rate W(b, p) = Σ pi log(bioi) bits per trial.
  • Side Information Value: If a signal Y gives I(X; Y) bits of information about outcome X, your doubling rate increases by exactly I(X; Y) bits.
  • English Entropy: Empirically, English text has entropy ~1.0–1.5 bits per character, far below the naive log₂(26) ≈ 4.7 bits.
  • The Duality: Optimal gambling and optimal data compression are equivalent problems. Both exploit the probability distribution to achieve H bits of advantage.
