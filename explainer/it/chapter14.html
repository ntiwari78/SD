Network Information Theory

Chapter 14 | The Library of Entropy | Cover & Thomas, Information Theory

Richard Feynman's Intuition: "Imagine a cocktail party — multiple people talking and listening simultaneously over the same noisy air. Network information theory maps exactly how much total information can flow when senders and receivers share the same medium. The answers involve beautiful geometric regions in rate-space."

Introduction to Network Information Theory

In previous chapters, we explored point-to-point communication: one sender, one receiver. But the real world is a network. Wireless signals penetrate multiple receivers. Fiber optic cables carry conversations from many sources. Data centers route information through relay stations.

Network information theory asks: How much information can flow when multiple senders and receivers compete for a shared medium? The answer is not simply the sum of individual capacities. The geometry of the problem changes fundamentally.

This chapter explores six foundational scenarios, each revealing a different principle:

  • Multiple Access Channel (MAC): Multiple senders, one receiver
  • Slepian-Wolf Coding: Distributed compression without communication
  • Broadcast Channel: One sender, multiple receivers
  • Relay Channel: Cooperation through intermediate nodes
  • Source Coding with Side Information: Wyner-Ziv theorem
  • General Network: Complex topologies and open problems

The Multiple Access Channel (MAC)

Two Senders, One Receiver

The MAC is the simplest network: two independent sources simultaneously transmit to a single receiver over a noisy channel. The receiver hears both signals mixed together. It must decode both messages despite the interference.

Let \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) be the codewords from senders 1 and 2, and \(Y\) be the received signal. The capacity region — the set of all achievable rate pairs \((R_1, R_2)\) — is defined by three inequalities:

\[ R_1 \leq I(X_1; Y | X_2) \] \[ R_2 \leq I(X_2; Y | X_1) \] \[ R_1 + R_2 \leq I(X_1, X_2; Y) \]

This pentagon-shaped region in rate-space encodes a beautiful fact: the joint decoding at the receiver allows both users to achieve rates greater than they would in isolation. The sum rate \(R_1 + R_2\) can approach the mutual information \(I(X_1, X_2; Y)\), which is larger than either individual rate alone.

Slepian-Wolf Coding: Distributed Compression

Correlated Sources Without Communication

Now consider a new problem: two correlated sources, \(X\) and \(Y\), must be compressed separately (each encoder works independently, without communication) but decoded jointly. How much data must each encoder transmit?

Classical information theory says: compress each source independently at rate \(R_X \geq H(X)\) and \(R_Y \geq H(Y)\). But this ignores their correlation.

Slepian-Wolf theorem (1973): Even without communication between encoders, we can achieve:

\[ R_X \geq H(X | Y), \quad R_Y \geq H(Y | X), \quad R_X + R_Y \geq H(X, Y) \]

This is remarkable. Each encoder can compress at a rate conditioned on the other source, even though the encoder never sees that source. How?

The Random Binning Trick

The answer uses random binning:

  1. Encoder 1 observes \(X\) and assigns it to one of \(2^{nR_X}\) random bins. It transmits only the bin index.
  2. Encoder 2 does the same independently for \(Y\).
  3. The decoder receives both bin indices and observes both \(X\) and \(Y\) jointly. It searches for a pair \((X', Y')\) in the indicated bins that is jointly typical with the observed sequence.

The key insight: correlation creates redundancy. The decoder uses the observed \((X, Y)\) pair to resolve which specific sequence is in each bin. This works as long as:

  • \(R_X\) is large enough so \(X\) fits in its bin (≈ \(H(X|Y)\) bits needed)
  • \(R_Y\) is large enough so \(Y\) fits in its bin (≈ \(H(Y|X)\) bits needed)
  • \(R_X + R_Y\) is large enough so the joint bin pair is unambiguous (≈ \(H(X,Y)\) bits needed)

Slepian-Wolf is foundational for distributed source coding, used in sensor networks and wireless systems.

The Broadcast Channel (BC)

One Sender, Two Receivers

In the broadcast channel, a single transmitter sends information to multiple receivers over the same medium. Each receiver hears the transmission but has different channel conditions (or sometimes the same).

Unlike the MAC, the transmitter has common knowledge of its own message. This enables a powerful technique: superposition coding.

Superposition Coding

The transmitter encodes a common message \(M_c\) and private messages \(M_1\) and \(M_2\) by superimposing their signals:

  • The common message appears in everyone's signal.
  • Private messages are encoded in a way that receiver 1 can decode \(M_1\) (after removing \(M_c\)) and receiver 2 can decode \(M_2\).

For the degraded broadcast channel (where one receiver's signal is a noisier version of the other), the capacity region is fully characterized. For general channels, it remains an open problem.

The Relay Channel

Cooperation Through Intermediate Nodes

A relay channel has a source, a relay node, and a destination. The relay observes the source's transmission and can forward information to help the destination.

This models cooperative wireless networks: a base station can relay signals from one user to another, effectively extending coverage.

The achievable rates depend on the relay's strategy:

  • Decode-and-forward: The relay fully decodes the source message and re-encodes it. Works well when the relay-to-source link is good.
  • Amplify-and-forward: The relay simply amplifies and retransmits the received signal. Simpler but less effective in very noisy channels.
  • Compress-and-forward: The relay compresses its observation (Slepian-Wolf style) and sends it to the destination, which jointly decodes.

The capacity of the general relay channel is still unknown, but these strategies provide useful lower bounds.

Source Coding with Side Information: Wyner-Ziv

A related but distinct problem: encode a source \(X\) losslessly using at least \(H(X|Y)\) bits, even though the encoder never sees the side information \(Y\). The decoder, however, observes both the compressed bits and \(Y\).

This seems impossible — how can the encoder compress based on information it doesn't observe? The answer mirrors Slepian-Wolf: random binning.

Wyner-Ziv Theorem: The encoder can achieve \(R = H(X|Y)\) using random binning, even without access to \(Y\). The decoder uses \(Y\) to disambiguate the bin index.

Applications: distributed video coding, sensor networks where local decoders have side information.

Code Lab: Computing MAC Capacity Region

Below is Python pseudocode for computing the achievable rate region of a Gaussian MAC:

import numpy as np import matplotlib.pyplot as plt def gaussian_mutual_info(power, snr): """ Mutual information for Gaussian channel. I(X;Y) = 0.5 * log2(1 + SNR) """ return 0.5 * np.log2(1 + snr) def mac_capacity_region(P1, P2, noise_power=1.0): """ Compute MAC capacity region vertices. Returns the corners of the achievable region. """ snr1 = P1 / noise_power snr2 = P2 / noise_power snr_sum = (P1 + P2) / noise_power # Individual rates R1_alone = gaussian_mutual_info(P1, snr1) R2_alone = gaussian_mutual_info(P2, snr2) # Sum rate R_sum = gaussian_mutual_info(P1 + P2, snr_sum) # Boundary points region = { 'R1_alone': R1_alone, 'R2_alone': R2_alone, 'R_sum': R_sum } return region # Example P1, P2 = 1.0, 1.0 region = mac_capacity_region(P1, P2) print(f"R1 ≤ {region['R1_alone']:.3f}") print(f"R2 ≤ {region['R2_alone']:.3f}") print(f"R1 + R2 ≤ {region['R_sum']:.3f}")

Socratic Sandbox: Questions & Insights

Q1: In the MAC, why can the sum rate exceed what either user achieves alone?
A: Because the receiver can decode jointly, exploiting the different codebook structures. Imagine two people in a room speaking simultaneously. A listener who focuses carefully can separate the conversations by attending to different voice characteristics. The MAC decoder does the same: it leverages the structure of both codebooks together, allowing each user to transmit faster than in isolation. The sum rate is bounded by \(I(X_1, X_2; Y)\), the total information the receiver can extract from the channel about both signals combined.
Q2: Slepian-Wolf says we can compress correlated sources WITHOUT communication. How?
A: Each encoder uses random binning — it assigns random bin indices to its observation. The joint decoder receives both bin indices and uses the correlation structure to resolve ambiguities. The key insight: correlation creates redundancy that the decoder exploits. Suppose two encoders observe correlated temperature readings (nearly identical but with small independent noise). Encoder 1 bins its reading into 1 of 10 bins, transmitting 4 bits. Encoder 2 does the same independently. The decoder, seeing both readings, can pinpoint the exact pair even though each bin contains many sequences. The correlation ensures that only one pair of sequences (one from each bin) is jointly typical.
Q3: Does the broadcast channel have a single capacity region for all cases?
A: No! The general broadcast channel capacity region is still an open problem. Only special cases are fully solved: degraded channels (where one receiver's signal is a noisier version of the other), less-noisy channels, and Gaussian channels with specific structures. The challenge: multiple receivers with different channel qualities require the transmitter to balance power and information carefully. For arbitrary channels, finding the exact region remains elusive — a testament to how subtle network information theory can be.
Q4: How does 5G cellular relate to network information theory?
A: 5G uses techniques rooted directly in this chapter's theory:
  • Massive MIMO: Multiple antennas create a broadcast channel; superposition coding and precoding optimize the transmission.
  • Non-Orthogonal Multiple Access (NOMA): Similar to MAC principles, allowing multiple users to share spectrum; the base station decodes their superimposed signals.
  • Cooperative relaying: Decode-and-forward and compress-and-forward strategies extend coverage and capacity.
Every time your phone connects to a 5G network, network information theory is at work.

Summary

Network information theory expands Shannon's framework from point-to-point to networks:

  • Multiple Access: Multiple senders, joint decoding at receiver, pentagon-shaped capacity region.
  • Slepian-Wolf: Distributed compression using random binning, rate determined by conditional entropies.
  • Broadcast: One sender, multiple receivers; superposition coding for known messages.
  • Relay: Cooperative nodes; decode-and-forward, amplify-and-forward, compress-and-forward strategies.
  • Side Information: Wyner-Ziv: encode without seeing the side information used in decoding.
  • General Networks: Many open problems, but the theory guides practical systems.

These principles are not academic abstractions — they directly shape modern communication systems. Understanding network information theory is essential for designing efficient wireless, sensor, and data center networks.
