Maximum Entropy and Spectral Estimation

The Principle of Maximum Indifference

The Library of Entropy | Cover & Thomas, Chapter 11, pp. 266–278

Part I: The Principle of Maximum Entropy

"Given only what you know—say the mean and variance—what's the least presumptuous probability distribution? The one with maximum entropy. For fixed mean and variance, that's the Gaussian. It makes the fewest hidden assumptions while respecting your constraints."

— Richard Feynman (paraphrased)

The principle of maximum entropy answers a fundamental question in probability and inference: When you have partial information about a distribution, which distribution should you choose?

The answer is elegantly simple: choose the distribution that has the highest entropy subject to your constraints. This distribution makes the fewest additional assumptions beyond what you know. It is the most "honest" or "maximally noncommittal" distribution possible.

Why Maximum Entropy?

Entropy quantifies uncertainty. A high-entropy distribution is "spread out" and doesn't impose hidden structure. By maximizing entropy subject to what you know, you avoid smuggling in unjustified assumptions. This principle formalizes the idea that probability should reflect only what you can justify.

  • Principle of indifference: When completely uncertain, assign equal probability (uniform distribution, maximum entropy).
  • Principle of maximum entropy: When you have constraints, maximize entropy subject to those constraints.
  • No hidden information: The resulting distribution reveals no structure beyond the given constraints.

Part II: Maximum Entropy Distributions (11.1–11.2)

The Fundamental Result

Suppose you have constraints on a distribution:

$$\mathbb{E}[f_i(X)] = \alpha_i \quad \text{for } i = 1, 2, \ldots, m$$

That is, the expected value of function $f_i$ must equal $\alpha_i$. The distribution that maximizes entropy $H(X) = -\int p(x) \log p(x) \, dx$ subject to these constraints has the exponential form:

$$f^*(x) = \exp\left(\lambda_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{m} \lambda_i f_i(x)\right)$$

where $\lambda_0, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m$ are Lagrange multipliers chosen so the constraints are satisfied. The constant $\lambda_0$ normalizes the distribution.

Examples of Maximum Entropy Distributions

1. No constraints (support only)

If $X$ has support $[a, b]$ and we have no other constraints:

$$f^*(x) = \frac{1}{b-a} \quad \text{for } x \in [a,b]$$

Result: Uniform distribution. Maximum entropy under no constraints.

2. Fixed mean constraint

If $X \geq 0$ and $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$:

$$f^*(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \quad \text{where } \lambda = 1/\mu$$

Result: Exponential distribution. Maximum entropy for non-negative random variables with fixed mean.

3. Fixed mean and variance

If $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$:

$$f^*(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

Result: Gaussian (normal) distribution. The most important maximum entropy distribution in practice. Why? Because in many real scenarios, we know mean and variance but little else.

Why the Gaussian is "Most Random"

Among all distributions with the same mean and variance, the Gaussian has the highest entropy. This means:

  • It imposes no additional structure.
  • It assumes minimal knowledge beyond mean and variance.
  • It is the "most spread out" consistent with those moments.

This explains why the Gaussian appears so universally in nature: when multiple independent processes combine, their sum approaches a Gaussian (Central Limit Theorem), which is the maximum entropy distribution for its mean and variance.

Part III: Spectrum Estimation (11.4)

From Entropy to Spectral Density

The principle of maximum entropy extends beyond simple probability distributions to spectral density estimation—the problem of estimating the frequency content of a signal from limited data.

Given autocorrelation values at lags $0, 1, \ldots, m$, what spectral density $S(f)$ is most consistent with this information?

$$R(k) = \mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t+k}]$$

The answer: maximize the entropy of the spectral distribution subject to the autocorrelation constraints.

Burg's Maximum Entropy Method

Burg's method (1967) applies maximum entropy to spectral estimation. It provides:

  • High resolution spectral estimates from short data records.
  • Automatic parameter selection (Lagrange multipliers).
  • An autoregressive (AR) model that encodes the spectral information.

The method is widely used in signal processing, geophysics, and speech analysis.

Part IV: Burg's Maximum Entropy Theorem (11.6)

The Main Result

Among all spectral densities $S(f)$ that match a given set of autocorrelation values $R(0), R(1), \ldots, R(m)$, the one with maximum entropy is:

$$S(f) = \frac{G}{|1 - \sum_{k=1}^{p} a_k e^{-2\pi i f k}|^2}$$

where $a_1, \ldots, a_p$ are the coefficients of an autoregressive (AR) process and $G$ is a gain constant. This is the spectral density of an autoregressive model.

Interpretation

The maximum entropy spectral density is generated by an AR model:

$$X_t = \sum_{k=1}^{p} a_k X_{t-k} + \epsilon_t$$

where $\epsilon_t$ is white noise. This model:

  • Encodes the autocorrelation information.
  • Makes no other assumptions about the spectrum.
  • Is parsimonious: requires only $p$ parameters.

Why This Matters

Spectral estimation from finite data is ill-posed: infinitely many spectra match the observed autocorrelations. Maximum entropy selects the unique spectrum that:

  • Respects the data (matches autocorrelations).
  • Makes no hidden assumptions.
  • Has the highest information-theoretic entropy.

This principle unites information theory with signal processing.

Part V: Code Lab — Maximum Entropy Distribution Computation

Here's how to compute maximum entropy distributions using Python's scientific libraries:

import numpy as np from scipy.optimize import minimize from scipy.stats import norm def max_entropy_gaussian(mean, variance): """ Maximum entropy distribution with fixed mean and variance. Returns the Gaussian parameters. """ sigma = np.sqrt(variance) return { 'mean': mean, 'std': sigma, 'entropy': 0.5 * np.log(2 * np.pi * np.e * variance) } def max_entropy_exponential(mean): """ Maximum entropy distribution for X >= 0 with fixed mean. Returns the exponential parameter. """ lambda_param = 1.0 / mean return { 'lambda': lambda_param, 'mean': mean, 'entropy': 1 + np.log(mean) } def max_entropy_uniform(a, b): """ Maximum entropy distribution on [a, b] (no constraints). """ return { 'support': (a, b), 'entropy': np.log(b - a) } # Example usage result_gaussian = max_entropy_gaussian(mean=5, variance=2) print(f"Gaussian: mean={result_gaussian['mean']}, " f"std={result_gaussian['std']:.3f}, " f"entropy={result_gaussian['entropy']:.3f} nats") result_exp = max_entropy_exponential(mean=3) print(f"Exponential: lambda={result_exp['lambda']:.3f}, " f"entropy={result_exp['entropy']:.3f} nats") result_unif = max_entropy_uniform(0, 10) print(f"Uniform: entropy={result_unif['entropy']:.3f} nats")

Computing AR Coefficients (Burg's Method)

def burg_method(data, order): """ Burg's maximum entropy method for AR spectral estimation. Returns AR coefficients that maximize entropy. """ N = len(data) x = data.astype(float) # Initialize forward and backward predictions a = np.zeros(order) efp = x[:-1] # Forward prediction error ebp = x[1:] # Backward prediction error for m in range(order): # Compute reflection coefficient numerator = 2.0 * np.sum(efp[m:] * ebp[m:]) denominator = np.sum(efp[m:] ** 2 + ebp[m:] ** 2) reflection = numerator / denominator # Update coefficients a[m] = reflection if m > 0: a[:m] = a[:m] - reflection * a[m-1::-1] # Update error estimates efp_new = efp - reflection * ebp ebp_new = ebp - reflection * efp[:-1] efp = efp_new[:-1] ebp = ebp_new[1:] return a # Example data = np.random.randn(1000) ar_coeff = burg_method(data, order=4) print(f"AR coefficients: {ar_coeff}")

Part VI: Socratic Sandbox

Explore your understanding of maximum entropy through reflection:

❓ Why is the Gaussian the maximum entropy distribution for fixed mean and variance?

The Gaussian emerges as the unique maximum entropy solution to the Lagrangian optimization problem. When you maximize $H(X) = -\int p(x) \log p(x) \, dx$ subject to $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$ and $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \sigma^2 + \mu^2$, the Lagrange multiplier method yields the Gaussian form. Intuitively: among all distributions with the same spread (variance), the Gaussian is "most spread out" in the sense of having the highest entropy. It assumes nothing about heavy tails, skewness, or multimodality—it only respects the mean and variance you told it about.

❓ If I observe a signal with three known autocorrelation values, can I uniquely determine its spectrum?

No. Infinitely many spectra match a finite set of autocorrelation values. But Burg's maximum entropy theorem tells you which one to choose: the spectrum of a 3rd-order AR process. This AR spectrum maximizes entropy while respecting your three autocorrelation constraints. It is the "most honest" guess—it doesn't smuggle in assumptions about higher-order lags or spectral shape. Of the infinite possibilities, it is the most noncommittal.

❓ When would maximum entropy give you the wrong answer?

When your constraints are wrong or incomplete. If you constrain only the mean but the true distribution has skewness or is multimodal, maximum entropy will give you the exponential (for non-negative X) or Gaussian, which will be wrong. Maximum entropy is only as good as your constraints. It assumes your stated constraints are exhaustive—that everything else is unknown. If you have domain knowledge (e.g., "this signal is sparse" or "it has a low-frequency trend"), you must encode that as a constraint to get a better answer. Maximum entropy is powerful but not magical.

❓ Why doesn't maximum entropy say all distributions are equally good?

Because you have constraints. If you truly had no information at all, then yes, all distributions are equally "honest" (they'd all have the same entropy). But constraints break that symmetry. Once you say "I know the mean is 5," you've partitioned all distributions into two groups: those respecting this constraint and those violating it. Among those respecting it, maximum entropy picks the one with the highest entropy. Constraints are your domain knowledge; maximum entropy respects them and adds nothing beyond them.

Summary: Chapter 11

  • Maximum Entropy Principle: Among all distributions satisfying given constraints, choose the one with the highest entropy. This distribution makes the fewest hidden assumptions.
  • Exponential Form: Maximum entropy distributions have the form $f^*(x) = \exp(\lambda_0 + \sum \lambda_i f_i(x))$, where $\lambda_i$ are Lagrange multipliers.
  • Examples:
    • No constraints → uniform distribution.
    • Fixed mean (X ≥ 0) → exponential distribution.
    • Fixed mean & variance → Gaussian distribution.
  • Why Gaussian? Among distributions with the same mean and variance, the Gaussian has maximum entropy. It assumes nothing beyond these moments.
  • Spectral Estimation: Apply maximum entropy to spectral density estimation. Given autocorrelation values, the max-entropy spectrum is an autoregressive (AR) process.
  • Burg's Method: A practical algorithm for computing maximum entropy spectra from finite data. Widely used in signal processing and geophysics.
  • Principle: Maximum entropy unifies information theory with inference and signal processing. It formalizes the idea of "don't assume what you don't know."
