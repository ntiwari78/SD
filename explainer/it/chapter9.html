Chapter 09

Differential Entropy

Extending information theory from discrete to continuous

From Cover & Thomas, Elements of Information Theory (2nd ed.)
pp. 224–238
I

Feynman Mental Model

Core Insight

Moving from discrete to continuous random variables is like switching from counting coins to measuring water.

In discrete information theory, entropy is always non-negative—you need at least zero bits to specify a particular outcome. But differential entropy can be negative, which seems absurd until you realize it's a relative measure, not an absolute one.

A point mass at zero has differential entropy of −∞, because specifying its exact location requires infinite precision. Conversely, a very spread-out distribution has high entropy. The key insight: differential entropy measures spread relative to a reference measure (Lebesgue measure), not information in an absolute sense.

This shift from counting to measuring is fundamental. In the discrete world, we ask "how many bits?" In the continuous world, we ask "how many more bits than a reference distribution?"

II

Definitions (9.1)

The differential entropy of a continuous random variable $X$ with probability density function $f(x)$ is:

Definition 9.1.1 $$h(X) = -\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \log f(x) \, dx$$

If we use natural logarithm, this is measured in nats. Using $\log_2$ gives bits. The integral is only defined if $f(x) \log f(x) \to 0$ as $f(x) \to 0$.

Key Examples

Example 1

Uniform distribution on [0, a]:

$$h(X) = \log a$$

If $a < 1$, then $\log a < 0$—differential entropy is negative! This happens because the distribution is more concentrated than a standard reference.

Example 2

Gaussian distribution $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$h(X) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2) \text{ bits}$$

This is always positive (for $\sigma^2 > 0$) and depends only on the variance, not the mean. The Gaussian is special: it maximizes differential entropy among all distributions with a given variance.

II.5

Interactive: Discrete ↔ Continuous Transition

One of the deepest connections in information theory is how discrete entropy relates to differential entropy. When we quantize a continuous distribution into $n$ levels with spacing $\Delta$, the discrete entropy grows, but the gap stays constant.

Exploration

Quantize a Gaussian $N(0, 1)$ distribution into $n$ levels. Watch how $H(X^\Delta) + \log \Delta$ converges to $h(X)$ as resolution improves.

Quantization Levels: $n$ 16
Discrete Entropy: 1.234 bits
Differential Entropy: 1.419 bits
Gap ($\log \Delta$): −0.185 bits

As you increase quantization levels, the discrete entropy grows, but the difference from differential entropy decreases. The relationship $H(X^\Delta) + \log \Delta \approx h(X)$ reveals that differential entropy is what remains after accounting for quantization resolution.

III

Asymptotic Equipartition Property (9.2)

Just as the discrete AEP guided us to entropy as the fundamental quantity, the continuous AEP governs typical sequences of continuous random variables.

AEP for Continuous RVs

For $n$ i.i.d. samples $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ from density $f(x)$:

$$-\frac{1}{n}\log f(X_1, \ldots, X_n) \xrightarrow{P} h(X)$$

The volume of the typical set is approximately:

$$V_{\text{typical}} \approx 2^{nh(X)}$$

This parallels the discrete case: the typical set contains sequences whose joint probability is roughly $2^{-nh(X)}$.

This result underpins the theory of continuous channel capacity. The typical set shrinks exponentially if $h(X) < 0$ and expands if $h(X) > 0$—a geometric interpretation of what differential entropy measures.

IV

Relation to Discrete Entropy (9.3)

The bridge between discrete and continuous information theory is quantization. Let $X^\Delta$ be the quantized version of $X$, where $X^\Delta = \lfloor X / \Delta \rfloor \cdot \Delta$ with quantization spacing $\Delta$.

Quantization Relationship $$H(X^\Delta) + \log \Delta \to h(X) \quad \text{as} \quad \Delta \to 0$$

This fundamental equation shows that:

  • $H(X^\Delta)$ is the discrete entropy of the quantized random variable
  • $\log(1/\Delta)$ accounts for the precision of quantization
  • $h(X)$ is the intrinsic differential entropy, independent of discretization scheme

In other words: Total discrete entropy = Differential entropy + Quantization cost

This relationship justifies studying continuous entropy despite all real signals being digitized: $h(X)$ captures the essential information content, while $\log(1/\Delta)$ is purely a matter of engineering choice.

V

Joint and Conditional Differential Entropy (9.4)

The machinery of discrete information theory carries over directly to the continuous case.

Definitions

Joint differential entropy:

$$h(X, Y) = -\int \int f(x,y) \log f(x,y) \, dx \, dy$$

Conditional differential entropy:

$$h(X|Y) = -\int \int f(x,y) \log f(x|y) \, dx \, dy$$

Chain rule:

$$h(X, Y) = h(X) + h(Y|X) = h(Y) + h(X|Y)$$

All the intuitions from the discrete case apply: conditioning reduces entropy, and entropy decomposes additively. The only difference is that these are integrals instead of sums, and values can be negative.

VI

Relative Entropy and Mutual Information (9.5)

Relative Entropy (Kullback–Leibler Divergence) $$D(f \| g) = \int f(x) \log \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \, dx \geq 0$$

Equality holds if and only if $f = g$ almost everywhere. This fundamental inequality—Gibbs' inequality—holds in both discrete and continuous settings.

Mutual Information

The mutual information between continuous random variables $X$ and $Y$ is:

$$I(X; Y) = h(X) - h(X|Y) = h(X) + h(Y) - h(X,Y)$$

This can also be written as:

$$I(X; Y) = D(f(x,y) \| f(x)f(y)) = \int \int f(x,y) \log \frac{f(x,y)}{f(x)f(y)} \, dx \, dy$$

Key Property: Non-negativity

Mutual information is always non-negative, even for continuous variables. This follows directly from Gibbs' inequality: $I(X;Y) = D(f(x,y) \| f(x)f(y)) \geq 0$. The negative values that differential entropy can take are "relative," but mutual information remains absolute and always quantifies actual dependence.

VII

Properties of Differential Entropy (9.6)

Maximum Entropy for Fixed Variance

Among all distributions with a given variance $\sigma^2$, the Gaussian $N(0, \sigma^2)$ has the maximum differential entropy:

Maximum Entropy Theorem $$h(X) \leq \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2)$$

with equality if and only if $X \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$.

Proof sketch: For any distribution $f$ with variance $\sigma^2$ and Gaussian $\phi$ with the same variance, $D(f \| \phi) \geq 0$ gives:

$$\int f(x) \log f(x) \, dx \leq \int f(x) \log \phi(x) \, dx$$

Taking negatives: $h(X) \leq h(\phi)$, where $h(\phi) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2)$.

Behavior Under Linear Transformations

If $Y = AX + b$ where $A$ is invertible, then:

Linear Transform Property $$h(Y) = h(X) + \log|\det(A)|$$

The differential entropy changes by the log-determinant of the transformation matrix, which accounts for how the volume of the probability space is scaled.

VIII

Code Lab: Computing Differential Entropy

In practice, we often estimate differential entropy from samples or compute it analytically for known distributions. Here's a Python reference for common cases:

import numpy as np from scipy.stats import entropy, norm, uniform, expon from scipy.special import digamma # Differential entropy for common distributions (in nats) # 1. Uniform on [a, b] def h_uniform(a, b): return np.log(b - a) # 2. Gaussian N(mu, sigma^2) def h_gaussian(sigma): return 0.5 * np.log(2 * np.pi * np.e * sigma**2) # 3. Exponential with rate lambda def h_exponential(lam): return 1.0 - np.log(lam) # 4. Differential entropy via quantization (Monte Carlo) def h_differential_quantized(samples, bins=256): """Estimate h(X) using quantization.""" hist, _ = np.histogram(samples, bins=bins, density=True) delta = (samples.max() - samples.min()) / bins hist = hist[hist > 0] h_discrete = -np.sum(hist * np.log(hist)) * delta return h_discrete + np.log(delta) # Approximate h(X) # 5. Mutual information I(X; Y) def mi_gaussian(rho, sigma_x, sigma_y): """MI for bivariate Gaussian with correlation rho.""" return -0.5 * np.log(1 - rho**2) # Example: compute entropy of N(0, 1) sigma = 1.0 h = h_gaussian(sigma) print(f"h(N(0,1)) = {h:.4f} nats = {h/np.log(2):.4f} bits")

These functions compute differential entropy for standard distributions. For arbitrary distributions, quantization-based methods or kernel density estimation can provide estimates from sample data.

IX

Socratic Sandbox: Questions & Answers

Interactive Q&A
h(X) can be negative. Does this mean negative information?

No. Differential entropy isn't an absolute measure of information. Think of it like a height relative to a reference level: it can be negative, zero, or positive depending on what you're measuring against.

The reference measure for differential entropy is Lebesgue measure (uniform measure on the real line). A distribution more concentrated than uniform has negative entropy; one more spread out has positive entropy.

But mutual information $I(X;Y) = h(X) - h(X|Y)$ is always $\geq 0$, because it measures actual dependence, not a relative property. The subtraction of two relative measures yields an absolute quantity.

Why is the Gaussian the maximum entropy distribution for fixed variance?

By Gibbs' inequality: for any distribution $f$ with variance $\sigma^2$ and Gaussian $\phi$ with the same variance,

$D(f \| \phi) = \int f \log(f/\phi) \geq 0$

Rearranging:

$\int f \log f \leq \int f \log \phi$

Therefore $h(f) \leq h(\phi)$. Any deviation from the Gaussian shape (while keeping variance fixed) reduces entropy. The Gaussian is special because it's "maximally uncertain" subject to the variance constraint—no hidden correlations or structure.

What happens to h(X) as σ → 0?

For $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$h(X) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2) \to -\infty$ as $\sigma \to 0$

This makes intuitive sense: as the distribution concentrates on a single point, specifying $X$ exactly requires infinite precision, hence infinite bits. The entropy becomes more and more negative.

More generally, any distribution that concentrates (e.g., a point mass) has differential entropy $-\infty$. This is the continuous analog of a discrete random variable with zero entropy: entropy measures uncertainty, and a point mass leaves no uncertainty.

In practice, all signals are digitized. Why study continuous entropy at all?

Excellent question. The reason: differential entropy $h(X)$ appears directly in the capacity formula for continuous channels. For instance, the capacity of the Gaussian channel with noise power $N$ and input power constraint $P$ is:

$C = \frac{1}{2}\log(1 + P/N)$ bits/sample

This formula comes from the differential entropy of the Gaussian distribution. Without studying continuous entropy, we couldn't derive channel capacity or understand what limits communication rates.

Moreover, the quantization relation $H(X^\Delta) = h(X) + \log(1/\Delta)$ cleanly separates two questions:

  • Source structure: $h(X)$—the intrinsic entropy of the continuous signal
  • Resolution: $\log(1/\Delta)$—the engineering choice of how finely to quantize

This separation reveals why continuous entropy is indispensable: it captures properties of the signal that are independent of discretization.

Differential entropy extends the reach of information theory from finite alphabets to continuous signals, revealing that entropy is fundamentally a measure of relative uncertainty—and that mutual information, built from differential entropy, remains always non-negative.
