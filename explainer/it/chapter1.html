Chapter 01 · Introduction & Preview

What Is Information?

The first and last lecture — a panoramic sweep from entropy to channel capacity, from gambling to the stock market, from Kolmogorov complexity to the second law of thermodynamics.

Part I

The Feynman Mental Model

🧠 Zero-Jargon Analogy

Imagine playing the game of 20 Questions. Someone thinks of a number between 1 and 1,000,000. You get to ask yes-or-no questions. How many do you need?

If you split the remaining possibilities in half with each question — "Is it greater than 500,000?" — you need exactly 20 questions, because $2^{20} = 1{,}048{,}576 > 1{,}000{,}000$.

Each yes-or-no answer carries exactly one bit of information. That halving of uncertainty — that's the atomic unit of information theory. Shannon's entire edifice is built on this single, clean idea: information is the resolution of uncertainty.

This chapter serves as a panoramic preview. Information theory answers two fundamental questions in communication: what is the ultimate compression limit (answer: the entropy $H$), and what is the maximum transmission rate (answer: the channel capacity $C$). But its reach extends far beyond engineering — into physics, statistics, economics, computer science, and philosophy.

Part II

The Landscape: Where Information Theory Lives

Information theory sits at the crossroads of at least six major fields. Shannon's original motivation was engineering — he wanted to understand the limits of communication. But the mathematics he invented turned out to be universal.

Figure 1.1 — The relationship of information theory with other fields

The Two Extreme Points

All of communication sits between two limits. On one end: data compression — you want to describe a source using as few bits as possible. On the other: data transmission — you want to send bits reliably through a noisy pipe.

The Two Limits of Communication
$$\underbrace{\min I(X;\hat{X})}_{\text{compression limit}} \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad \underbrace{\max I(X;Y)}_{\text{transmission limit (capacity)}}$$

Everything in between — every modulation scheme, every codec, every error-correcting code — lives on the spectrum between these two extremes.

Part III

Mathematical Ascent: The Core Definitions

Let's formalize the key quantities. These definitions will be studied rigorously in Chapter 2, but meeting them here builds the scaffolding.

1. Entropy: The Measure of Uncertainty

The entropy of a discrete random variable $X$ with probability mass function $p(x)$ is:

Definition — Shannon Entropy (Eq. 1.1)
$$H(X) = -\sum_{x} p(x) \log_2 p(x)$$

Entropy measures the average number of bits needed to describe $X$. A uniform distribution over 32 outcomes has entropy $\log_2 32 = 5$ bits. A distribution concentrated on one outcome has entropy $0$ — no surprise, no information.

2. Mutual Information: Shared Knowledge

When we have two random variables $X$ and $Y$, the mutual information measures how much knowing one tells you about the other:

Definition — Mutual Information (Eq. 1.4)
$$I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y) = \sum_{x,y} p(x,y) \log \frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)\,p(y)}$$

Mutual information is always non-negative, symmetric in $X$ and $Y$, and equals zero if and only if $X$ and $Y$ are independent. It is the fundamental currency of both compression and communication.

3. Channel Capacity: The Speed Limit

A communication channel is defined by a conditional probability $p(y|x)$. The capacity is the maximum mutual information, optimized over all input distributions:

Definition — Channel Capacity (Eq. 1.5)
$$C = \max_{p(x)} I(X;Y)$$

4. Relative Entropy: The Distance Between Distributions

Mutual information is a special case of a deeper quantity — relative entropy (or Kullback-Leibler divergence):

Definition — Relative Entropy (Eq. 1.6)
$$D(p \| q) = \sum_{x} p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$$

Relative entropy is always $\geq 0$ and equals zero only when $p = q$. It is not symmetric, so it's not a true distance — but it behaves like one in many contexts. It appears as the error exponent in hypothesis testing, the penalty for using the wrong code, and the rate of convergence in large deviation theory.

5. The Doubling Rate: Information Meets Wealth

Perhaps the most surprising connection: the growth rate of wealth in a stock market parallels the entropy rate. For a market with distribution $F(\mathbf{x})$, the maximum doubling rate is:

Definition — Doubling Rate (Eq. 1.7)
$$W^* = \max_{\mathbf{b}: b_i \geq 0,\, \sum b_i = 1} \int \log \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{x}\, dF(\mathbf{x})$$

This deep duality between information theory and portfolio theory is explored in Chapters 6 and 15.

Part IV

Code Lab: Information Measures in Python

Let's implement the core definitions. Every line maps directly to the mathematics above.

entropy_basics.py Python 3 · NumPy 
import numpy as np

# ── Shannon Entropy ──────────────────────────────────────
# H(X) = -Σ p(x) log₂ p(x)
# Measures the average surprise (in bits) of a random variable.

def entropy(p):
    """Compute Shannon entropy H(X) in bits.

    Args:
        p: array-like, probability mass function (must sum to 1)
    Returns:
        H(X) in bits
    """
    p = np.asarray(p, dtype=float)
    p = p[p > 0]  # 0·log(0) = 0 by convention
    return -np.sum(p * np.log2(p))


# ── Relative Entropy (KL Divergence) ─────────────────────
# D(p||q) = Σ p(x) log₂(p(x)/q(x))
# Measures the "cost" of assuming distribution q
# when the true distribution is p.

def kl_divergence(p, q):
    """Compute KL divergence D(p||q) in bits."""
    p, q = np.asarray(p, dtype=float), np.asarray(q, dtype=float)
    mask = p > 0
    return np.sum(p[mask] * np.log2(p[mask] / q[mask]))


# ── Mutual Information ───────────────────────────────────
# I(X;Y) = Σ p(x,y) log₂(p(x,y) / (p(x)·p(y)))
# = H(X) - H(X|Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|X)
# The shared information between two variables.

def mutual_information(joint_pxy):
    """Compute I(X;Y) from joint distribution matrix."""
    joint = np.asarray(joint_pxy, dtype=float)
    px = joint.sum(axis=1)  # marginal of X
    py = joint.sum(axis=0)  # marginal of Y

    mi = 0.0
    for i in range(joint.shape[0]):
        for j in range(joint.shape[1]):
            if joint[i,j] > 0:
                mi += joint[i,j] * np.log2(
                    joint[i,j] / (px[i] * py[j])
                )
    return mi


# ── BSC Channel Capacity ─────────────────────────────────
# C = 1 - H(p) = 1 + p·log₂(p) + (1-p)·log₂(1-p)
# The maximum rate of reliable communication over
# a binary symmetric channel with crossover prob p.

def bsc_capacity(p):
    """Capacity of a Binary Symmetric Channel."""
    return 1.0 - entropy([p, 1 - p])


# ── Example: The Horse Race ──────────────────────────────
# 8 horses with probs (1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, 1/64×4)
horses = [1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, 1/64, 1/64, 1/64, 1/64]
print(f"Horse race entropy: {entropy(horses)} bits")
# Output: Horse race entropy: 2.0 bits

# The optimal code lengths match: [1,2,3,4,6,6,6,6]
# Average length = 1(1/2)+2(1/4)+3(1/8)+4(1/16)+6(4/64)
#                = 0.5+0.5+0.375+0.25+0.375 = 2.0 bits ✓
Part V

The Five Pillars: A Road Map

The chapter previews how the core quantities $H$, $I$, $C$, $D$, $K$, and $W$ arise naturally across different problem domains. Here is the road map for the rest of the book:

Data Compression → The entropy $H$ is a lower bound on the average description length. We can achieve within 1 bit of it. (Chapters 2, 3, 5)

Data Transmission → Using random codes, Shannon showed we can communicate at any rate below capacity $C$ with vanishing error. (Chapters 8, 10)

Inference & Statistics → Relative entropy $D$ controls the error exponent in hypothesis testing and the convergence rate in large deviations. (Chapter 12)

Complexity → Kolmogorov complexity $K$ — the length of the shortest program — is the individual-sequence analog of entropy. (Chapter 7)

Gambling & Investment → The doubling rate $W$ in a horse race equals the entropy of the source minus the code length — information literally is wealth growth. (Chapters 6, 15)

Part VI

Channel Examples: From Noiseless to Noisy

The Noiseless Binary Channel

The simplest channel: input is reproduced exactly at output. Every transmitted bit arrives perfectly. Capacity = $\max I(X;Y) = 1$ bit per use.

Figure 1.3 — Noiseless binary channel

The Noisy Four-Symbol Channel

Each input letter is received as itself with probability $\frac{1}{2}$ or as the next letter with probability $\frac{1}{2}$. If we use all four symbols, the output doesn't uniquely determine the input. But if we use only inputs 1 and 3, the output is always distinguishable — giving capacity $C = 1$ bit per use.

The Binary Symmetric Channel (BSC)

The workhorse of information theory. Each bit flips with probability $p$. The capacity is $C = 1 - H(p)$. When $p = 0$, no noise, full capacity. When $p = 0.5$, the channel is useless — output is independent of input.

Part VII

Socratic Sandbox

🏛 The Communication Dilemma

Scenario: You're an engineer at a satellite ground station. The uplink channel flips bits with probability $p = 0.11$. Your boss demands you increase the data rate from 0.5 bits/transmission to 0.6 bits/transmission — "just add more power." Work through these questions to discover the fundamental limit.

Q1 What is the capacity of a BSC with $p = 0.11$? Is 0.6 bits/transmission achievable?
$C = 1 - H(0.11) = 1 - (-0.11\log_2 0.11 - 0.89\log_2 0.89) \approx 1 - 0.500 = 0.500$ bits. The capacity is exactly 0.5 bits/transmission. So 0.6 bits/transmission exceeds capacity — it is impossible to achieve reliably, no matter how clever the code. Shannon's converse theorem guarantees this.
Q2 Your boss says "adding more power reduces the error rate — so we can go faster." What's wrong with this reasoning?
More power reduces $p$, which increases $C$. But capacity is a hard limit for a given channel. If the channel has $p = 0.11$, no amount of clever encoding pushes you past $C = 0.5$. The correct approach: reduce $p$ first (better hardware), then code up to the new capacity. You can't code your way past the physics.
Q3 At what crossover probability $p$ would the capacity reach 0.6? What does this require physically?
We need $1 - H(p) = 0.6$, i.e., $H(p) = 0.4$. Solving numerically: $p \approx 0.072$. To reduce the error rate from 0.11 to 0.072, you'd need roughly 1.8 dB more signal-to-noise ratio — a concrete engineering target.
Q4 Shannon's theorem says rates below $C$ are achievable. Does that mean 0.499 bits/transmission can be sent with zero errors?
Not zero errors — but vanishingly small error probability. Shannon proved that for any rate $R < C$, there exist codes of length $n$ whose error probability goes to zero as $n \to \infty$. The catch: these codes may be exponentially complex to decode. Practical codes (Turbo codes, LDPC codes) approach capacity with feasible decoding.
Q5 Why is the BSC capacity exactly 0 when $p = 0.5$? What does this mean physically?
When $p = 0.5$, each output bit is equally likely to be 0 or 1 regardless of the input — the channel output is a fair coin flip independent of what you sent. $I(X;Y) = 0$ for all input distributions. The channel has destroyed all information. You might as well not transmit at all.
Summary

Key Takeaways

Entropy $H$ measures uncertainty and sets the compression limit. Mutual information $I$ measures shared knowledge between variables. Channel capacity $C = \max I(X;Y)$ is the speed limit of reliable communication. Relative entropy $D$ measures the distance between distributions. Kolmogorov complexity $K$ is the ultimate measure of individual-sequence information. These five quantities — and the deep connections between them — are the substance of this entire book.
