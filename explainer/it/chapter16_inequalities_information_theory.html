The Unbreakable Laws of Uncertainty

How inequalities reveal the fundamental limits of information, computation, and physical reality

From Cover & Thomas: "Elements of Information Theory"

The Feynman Mental Model

Think of entropy as water flowing downhill. Just as physics has conservation laws—energy cannot be created or destroyed—information theory has its own inviolable rules. These inequalities are the "laws of thermodynamics" for information.

You can't compress data below its entropy (conservation), processing data can never create information (second law), and mixing things up always increases uncertainty (entropy increase). This chapter collects all these "laws" into one grand unified view, and reveals a stunning connection: the same math that bounds estimation error (Fisher information) also bounds how much entropy can increase when you add signals together (entropy power inequality), which in turn is connected to how volumes of shapes add (Brunn-Minkowski inequality from geometry!).

The Mathematical Ascent

Section A: Basic Inequalities

Jensen's Inequality
$$f(E[X]) \leq E[f(X)] \quad \text{for convex } f$$

The foundation of information theory. For a convex function, the function of the expectation is less than or equal to the expectation of the function. This simple fact generates almost all information inequalities.

Log-Sum Inequality
$$\sum_{i} a_i \log \frac{a_i}{b_i} \geq \left(\sum_i a_i\right) \log \frac{\sum_i a_i}{\sum_i b_i}$$

Follows directly from Jensen's applied to the concave log function. Proves that KL divergence is non-negative.

Information Inequality (KL Divergence Non-negativity)
$$D(p \| q) = \sum_x p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \geq 0$$

With equality if and only if $$p = q$$. This proves that no distribution is "closer" to the truth than itself.

Conditioning Reduces Entropy
$$H(X|Y) \leq H(X)$$

Observing additional information Y can never increase uncertainty about X. Information is power.

Chain Rule for Entropy
$$H(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} H(X_i | X_{i-1}, \ldots, X_1) \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} H(X_i)$$

The entropy of a joint distribution is at most the sum of marginal entropies. Independence maximizes total entropy.

Fano's Inequality
$$H(P_e) + P_e \log(|\mathcal{X}| - 1) \geq H(X | Y)$$

Sets a fundamental lower bound on classification error. If the conditional entropy is large, no classifier can achieve low error probability.

Data Processing Inequality
$$X \to Y \to Z \implies I(X;Z) \leq I(X;Y)$$

Processing information through a Markov chain can only lose information about the source. This is the second law of thermodynamics for information.

Section B: Differential Entropy Inequalities

Chain Rule for Differential Entropy
$$h(X_1, \ldots, X_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} h(X_i | X_{i-1}, \ldots, X_1) \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} h(X_i)$$

The differential entropy of continuous random variables also respects the chain rule structure.

Maximum Entropy of Continuous Distributions
$$h(X) \leq \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e)^n |K|$$

For an $$n$$-dimensional distribution with covariance matrix $$K$$, the entropy is maximized by the Gaussian distribution. This is why Gaussian channels have the smallest capacity!

Section C: Fisher Information & Entropy Power

Fisher Information
$$J(\theta) = \int f(x) \left[\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\right]^2 dx = E\left[\left(\frac{\partial \log f(X)}{\partial \theta}\right)^2\right]$$

Measures how much information a random variable contains about a parameter. Higher Fisher information means easier parameter estimation.

Cramér-Rao Lower Bound
$$\text{Var}(T) \geq \frac{1}{J(\theta)}$$

No unbiased estimator can have variance smaller than the inverse of Fisher information. Information limits estimation.

de Bruijn's Identity
$$\frac{d}{dt}h(X + \sqrt{t} Z) = \frac{1}{2} J(X + \sqrt{t}Z)$$

Shows that the rate of change of entropy when adding Gaussian noise is determined by Fisher information. A deep connection between estimation and entropy.

Entropy Power Inequality (EPI)
$$e^{2h(X+Y)} \geq e^{2h(X)} + e^{2h(Y)}$$

One of the deepest results in information theory. For independent random variables X and Y, the "entropy power" of their sum is at least as large as the sum of their entropy powers. This is the probabilistic analog of the Brunn-Minkowski inequality!

Section D: The Geometric Connection

Brunn-Minkowski Inequality
$$\text{Vol}(A + B)^{1/n} \geq \text{Vol}(A)^{1/n} + \text{Vol}(B)^{1/n}$$

A fundamental inequality from convex geometry. The volume of the Minkowski sum of two sets is related to the individual volumes. The EPI is the information-theoretic analog, where entropy power plays the role of volume.

The Unifying Vision: These inequalities form a grand unified theory connecting statistics (Cramér-Rao), information (entropy power), geometry (Brunn-Minkowski), and physics (thermodynamics). They all express the same deep principle: mixing increases disorder.

Python Implementation

Verifying fundamental information inequalities computationally — from entropy power to data processing:

import numpy as np def entropy(p): """H(X) = -Σ p(x) log₂ p(x), the bedrock quantity.""" p = np.asarray(p, dtype=float) p = p[p > 0] return -np.sum(p * np.log2(p)) def kl_divergence(p, q): """D(p||q) ≥ 0 (Theorem 16.1.7: Information inequality)""" p, q = np.asarray(p), np.asarray(q) mask = p > 0 return np.sum(p[mask] * np.log2(p[mask] / q[mask])) def mutual_information(p_joint): """I(X;Y) = D(p(x,y) || p(x)p(y)) ≥ 0""" px = p_joint.sum(axis=1) py = p_joint.sum(axis=0) mi = 0.0 for i in range(p_joint.shape[0]): for j in range(p_joint.shape[1]): if p_joint[i,j] > 0: mi += p_joint[i,j] * np.log2( p_joint[i,j] / (px[i] * py[j])) return mi def verify_entropy_power_ineq(h_X, h_Y): """ Entropy Power Inequality (Theorem 16.7): e^{2h(X+Y)} ≥ e^{2h(X)} + e^{2h(Y)} Connects to Brunn-Minkowski: Vol(A+B)^{1/n} ≥ Vol(A)^{1/n} + Vol(B)^{1/n} """ N_X = np.exp(2 * h_X) / (2 * np.pi * np.e) N_Y = np.exp(2 * h_Y) / (2 * np.pi * np.e) h_sum_min = 0.5 * np.log(2 * np.pi * np.e * (N_X + N_Y)) return {'N_X': N_X, 'N_Y': N_Y, 'h_min': h_sum_min} def data_processing_demo(eps1=0.1, eps2=0.2): """ Data Processing Inequality: X→Y→Z ⟹ I(X;Z) ≤ I(X;Y) Processing can only LOSE information, never create it. """ # X ~ Bernoulli(0.5), BSC channels p_xy = np.array([[0.5*(1-eps1), 0.5*eps1], [0.5*eps1, 0.5*(1-eps1)]]) p_y = p_xy.sum(axis=0) ch2 = np.array([[1-eps2, eps2], [eps2, 1-eps2]]) p_xz_joint = np.zeros((2, 2)) for i in range(2): for j in range(2): for k in range(2): p_xz_joint[i,k] += p_xy[i,j] * ch2[j,k] I_XY = mutual_information(p_xy) I_XZ = mutual_information(p_xz_joint) print(f"I(X;Y) = {I_XY:.4f} bits") print(f"I(X;Z) = {I_XZ:.4f} bits") print(f"DPI holds: I(X;Y) ≥ I(X;Z) → {I_XY >= I_XZ}") # Verify inequalities p = [0.5, 0.3, 0.2] q = [0.33, 0.33, 0.34] print(f"D(p||q) = {kl_divergence(p, q):.4f} ≥ 0 ✓") print(f"H(p) = {entropy(p):.4f} ≤ log₂(3) = {np.log2(3):.4f} ✓") data_processing_demo()

The Information Architect's Challenge

Scenario: You're designing a deep neural network for image classification. The network has 50 layers. You notice that the final layer's mutual information with the input is surprisingly low, yet the network classifies well. A colleague suggests adding skip connections to "preserve information." Another suggests that the entropy power of the hidden representations seems to follow a predictable pattern. What's really happening?

Explore These Questions

Q1: Why does the Data Processing Inequality guarantee information loss in deep networks?
Each layer forms a Markov chain: Input → Layer₁ → Layer₂ → ... → Output. By the Data Processing Inequality, I(Input; Layer_k) can only decrease with depth. Information about the input is progressively lost. However, the network must learn to preserve RELEVANT information while discarding noise. The low mutual information with final layers doesn't mean the network is failing—it's succeeding by removing irrelevant details while keeping what matters for classification. This is optimal information compression.
Q2: How does Fano's inequality set a fundamental limit on classification accuracy?
If the mutual information I(X;Ŷ) between input X and prediction Ŷ is limited, Fano's inequality gives:
$$P_e \geq \frac{H(X | \hat{Y}) - 1}{\log(|\text{classes}| - 1)}$$
Low mutual information → high error floor. No architecture trick can break this theoretical bound. It's not about how many layers you have, but about how much information your network can reliably extract about the true class from the input. This bound applies to any classifier, no matter how clever.
Q3: Why is Jensen's Inequality the "mother of all information inequalities"?
Almost every information inequality traces back to Jensen's. The non-negativity of KL divergence follows from Jensen's applied to -log(x). The entropy bound H(X) ≤ log|X| is Jensen's on the concave log function. The Data Processing Inequality is Jensen's in disguise. Even Fano's Inequality can be derived using Jensen's. It's the mathematical bedrock upon which information theory is built. When you understand Jensen's deeply, all other inequalities become natural consequences.
Q4: What does the Entropy Power Inequality tell us about adding noise to signals?
The EPI says: $$e^{2h(X+Y)} \geq e^{2h(X)} + e^{2h(Y)}$$

Adding noise (Y) to a signal (X) increases entropy by at least as much as adding a Gaussian with the same entropy. Gaussians are the WORST noise—they maximize entropy for a given power. This is why the Gaussian channel has the smallest capacity among all channels with the same power constraint. It's also why natural noise often approximates Gaussians: they're the "worst case" for information transmission.

Q5: How is the Brunn-Minkowski inequality from geometry related to entropy?
Brunn-Minkowski says: $$\text{Vol}(A+B)^{1/n} \geq \text{Vol}(A)^{1/n} + \text{Vol}(B)^{1/n}$$

The Entropy Power Inequality is its probabilistic counterpart: replacing volume with entropy power and Minkowski sum with convolution. Both say that "mixing" can only expand. This deep connection links information theory to geometry! It reveals that entropy isn't just an information concept—it has roots in the geometry of high-dimensional spaces. This is why high-dimensional Gaussian distributions concentrate near their surface.
