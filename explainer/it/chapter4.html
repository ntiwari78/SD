Part I: Foundation

Entropy Rates of a Stochastic Process

Understanding how information unfolds over time

The Feynman Mental Model

"In English, after the letter Q, you almost always see U. After 'TH', you expect 'E' or 'A'. This memory between letters means English has a much lower entropy rate than random characters. The entropy rate measures the genuine surprise per symbol once you've accounted for all the patterns and dependencies."

This intuition captures the heart of entropy rate: it's not about the raw complexity of individual symbols, but rather the conditional complexity given everything you've seen before. When you've read "Q", the next character is almost predetermined—there's very little surprise. But when you see a random character, the possibilities are endless.

Part II: Markov Chains (4.1)

A Markov chain is a sequence of random variables where the future depends only on the present, not on the entire history. Formally:

$$P(X_{n+1} = x_{n+1} \mid X_n = x_n, X_{n-1} = x_{n-1}, \ldots, X_1 = x_1) = P(X_{n+1} = x_{n+1} \mid X_n = x_n)$$

Key Properties

Stationary Distribution (π): A probability distribution over states that doesn't change as the chain evolves. If π is stationary, then πP = π.

Time-Invariant: Transition probabilities don't change over time. Pᵢⱼ is the same at every timestep.

Irreducibility: From any state, you can eventually reach any other state. The chain is connected.

Aperiodicity: No state has a fixed period. You can return to a state in a non-deterministic number of steps.

Ergodicity: A chain that is irreducible and aperiodic. For ergodic chains, the stationary distribution is unique and the chain converges to it regardless of initial state.

Part III: Entropy Rate (4.2)

The entropy rate measures the average information (in bits per symbol) produced by a stochastic process. It captures how much surprise we should expect per new symbol, given perfect knowledge of the past.

$$H(\mathcal{X}) = \lim_{n \to \infty} H(X_n \mid X_{n-1}, \ldots, X_1)$$

For a stationary stochastic process, this can also be expressed as:

$$H(\mathcal{X}) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} H(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n)$$

For a stationary, irreducible, aperiodic Markov chain with stationary distribution μ and transition matrix P:

$$H(\mathcal{X}) = -\sum_{i} \mu_i \sum_{j} P_{ij} \log P_{ij}$$

Interpretation

This formula computes the expected conditional entropy: spend time proportional to the stationary distribution π(i) in each state i, and in that state, measure the entropy of the outgoing transitions. The result is the average information per symbol.

Part IV: Random Walk on a Graph (4.3)

A random walk on a graph is a special case of a Markov chain where transitions are proportional to the degree of each node. The entropy rate of a random walk encodes both the structure of the graph and the stationary distribution.

$$H(\mathcal{X}) = \log(\text{average degree}) - H(\text{stationary distribution weighted by edges})$$

Intuition

Dense graphs (high degree) have high entropy rates: many choices at each step.

Sparse graphs (low degree) have low entropy rates: few choices at each step.

Uneven degree distribution (some high-degree hubs, some leaves) creates structure that reduces entropy rate below the theoretical maximum.

Part V: Hidden Markov Models (4.4)

In many real-world scenarios, we don't observe the Markov chain directly. Instead, we observe noisy outputs generated by a hidden chain.

Hidden Markov Model Structure

  • Hidden state: A Markov chain X₁, X₂, X₃, ... that follows the usual properties.
  • Observations: Y₁, Y₂, Y₃, ... where Yₙ depends only on Xₙ, not on previous observations.
  • Entropy rate: Still defined by the hidden chain, but filtered through the observation model.

Hidden Markov Models are fundamental in speech recognition, natural language processing, and biological sequence analysis. The entropy rate of the hidden chain determines the information content of the observations.

Part VI: Socratic Sandbox — English vs. Random

Let's apply entropy rate to understand why English is so compressible while random text is not.

Q1: English uses 26 letters. If each were equally likely, what's the entropy per character?

A: With uniform distribution over 26 symbols:

$$H = \log_2(26) \approx 4.7 \text{ bits/character}$$

At 8 bits per ASCII character, this uniform model suggests only 4.7 / 8 ≈ 59% compression efficiency could be achieved.

Q2: Shannon estimated English entropy at ~1.0–1.5 bits/character. Why so much lower than 4.7?

A: Because English has massive structure at multiple scales:

  • Letter frequencies: E, T, A are common; Q, X, Z are rare. This reduces entropy to ~4.0 bits.
  • Bigram dependencies: After Q comes U; after TH comes E or A. First-order Markov model reduces entropy further to ~3.0 bits.
  • Word patterns: THE is common; XQZ is impossible. Word-level structure cuts entropy to ~2.0 bits.
  • Grammar and semantics: Long-range dependencies (noun agreement, tense consistency, discourse coherence) reduce entropy toward 1.0–1.5 bits.
Q3: A first-order Markov model of English captures bigram frequencies. Is this sufficient to estimate the true entropy rate?

A: No. A first-order Markov model (bigrams) substantially overestimates entropy because:

  • Grammar: Many bigrams are grammatically valid but semantically nonsensical in context. "The walk jumped" follows bigram probabilities but violates subject-verb agreement in many contexts.
  • Long-range dependencies: Pronouns refer to nouns several words back. Articles and adjectives must agree with nouns across phrases. Tense consistency spans clauses.
  • Discourse: Narrative structure, topic coherence, and author style create dependencies spanning paragraphs or chapters.

Shannon's experiments (guessing game where humans predict the next letter in English text) suggest even trigram and 4-gram models underestimate, with the true entropy rate closer to 1.0 bit/character.

Q4: If the entropy rate of English is ~1 bit/character, what compression ratio should be theoretically achievable?

A: Shannon's source coding theorem tells us:

$$\text{Compression ratio} = \frac{\text{Bits per symbol (original)}}{\text{Entropy rate}} = \frac{8 \text{ bits/char}}{1 \text{ bit/char}} = 8:1$$

In other words, theoretically, English text could be compressed to 1/8 of its original size. However, real-world compressors achieve only 3:1 to 5:1 ratios because:

  • Algorithmic limitations: ZIP, gzip, and bzip2 use finite-order models and simple arithmetic; they don't capture all long-range dependencies.
  • Practical overhead: Dictionaries, headers, and framing add bytes.
  • Context depth: Capturing multi-paragraph dependencies would require enormous memory and computation.

The gap between 8:1 theoretical and 3:1–5:1 practical suggests that even state-of-the-art compressors leave significant room for improvement.

Part VII: Code Lab — Markov Chain Entropy Rate in Python

Let's compute the entropy rate of a simple Markov chain and verify our formula. 

import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import entropy

# Define transition matrix for a 2-state chain
# P[i][j] = probability of transitioning from state i to state j
P = np.array([
    [0.7, 0.3],  # From state 0
    [0.4, 0.6]   # From state 1
])

# Find stationary distribution by solving πP = π
# Equivalently: (P^T - I)π = 0
eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eig(P.T)
stationary = np.real(eigenvectors[:, 0])
stationary /= stationary.sum()  # Normalize

print(f"Stationary distribution: {stationary}")

# Compute entropy rate
# H = -Σᵢ μᵢ Σⱼ Pᵢⱼ log₂(Pᵢⱼ)
h_rate = 0
for i in range(len(P)):
    for j in range(len(P[i])):
        if P[i][j] > 0:
            h_rate -= stationary[i] * P[i][j] * np.log2(P[i][j])

print(f"Entropy rate: {h_rate:.4f} bits/symbol")

# Verify with empirical simulation
np.random.seed(42)
n_steps = 100000
state = 0
entropy_empirical = []

for _ in range(n_steps):
    # Sample next state
    next_state = np.random.choice([0, 1], p=P[state])
    entropy_empirical.append(next_state)
    state = next_state

# Compute empirical entropy of transitions
transitions = np.array(entropy_empirical)
empirical_h = entropy(np.bincount(transitions), base=2)
print(f"Empirical entropy (approximate): {empirical_h:.4f} bits/symbol")

Key Observations

  • Stationary distribution: Scales the contribution of each state.
  • Transition entropy: Varies by state; states with more uniform transitions have higher entropy.
  • Empirical vs. theoretical: Simulation converges to the theoretical value as n → ∞.

Part VIII: Summary

Key Takeaways

  • Entropy rate is the average information per symbol in a stochastic process, accounting for dependencies.
  • Markov chains are the simplest models of dependency: the future depends only on the present.
  • Ergodic Markov chains converge to a unique stationary distribution, making entropy rate well-defined.
  • English has low entropy rate (~1 bit/char) because of massive structure at every scale: letter frequencies, bigram patterns, grammar, discourse.
  • Random text has high entropy rate (~4.7 bits/char) because symbols are nearly independent.
  • Compression ratio is fundamentally limited by entropy rate: you cannot do better than 1 bit per symbol on average.
  • Hidden Markov Models extend the framework to noisy observations, essential for real-world applications.
