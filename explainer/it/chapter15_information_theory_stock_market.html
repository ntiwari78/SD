How entropy measures wealth growth. Why log-optimal portfolios beat everyone. The mathematical connection between gambling and data compression.

The Gambler Who Never Goes Broke

A Parable from the Racetrack

Imagine you're at a racetrack every day for the rest of your life. You can't predict winners, but you know the odds. Most gamblers go broke because they bet too much on single outcomes.

But there's a magical betting strategy — the Kelly criterion — that guarantees you'll grow richer than anyone else in the long run. The secret? It's identical to the math behind data compression.

The same entropy that measures information also measures the growth rate of wealth. Your portfolio IS a code, and the stock market IS an information source.

Key Insight:

Wealth compounds multiplicatively. After n periods, your wealth is the PRODUCT of daily returns. So the right objective isn't maximizing expected return — it's maximizing E[log(return)], the doubling rate.

The Mathematical Structure

The theory of log-optimal portfolios rests on elegant foundations from information theory. Each equation below reveals a deep connection between wealth and information.

1. Doubling Rate
$$W(\mathbf{b}, F) = \int \log \mathbf{b}'\mathbf{x} \, dF(\mathbf{x}) = E[\log \mathbf{b}'\mathbf{X}]$$
The doubling rate W(b, F) measures the exponential growth rate of wealth under portfolio allocation b. After n periods, wealth grows as approximately 2^{nW}. The parameter F is the distribution of stock returns X, and b is the portfolio allocation vector (weights summing to 1). This is the fundamental objective: we want to maximize the expected logarithm of portfolio return.
2. Optimal Doubling Rate
$$W^*(F) = \max_{\mathbf{b}} W(\mathbf{b}, F)$$
W* is the maximum growth rate achievable by any portfolio strategy. This is the wealth-theoretic analogue of channel capacity in information theory. Just as a channel has a limit to how much information it can transmit, a stock market has a limit to how fast wealth can grow. The log-optimal portfolio achieves this limit.
3. Log-Optimal Portfolio (Kuhn-Tucker Conditions)
$$E\left[\frac{X_i}{\mathbf{b}^{*\top} \mathbf{X}}\right] = 1 \text{ if } b_i^* > 0, \quad E\left[\frac{X_i}{\mathbf{b}^{*\top} \mathbf{X}}\right] \leq 1 \text{ if } b_i^* = 0$$
These are the first-order optimality conditions for the log-optimal portfolio. They say: for each asset held (b_i* > 0), the expected "return relative to total return" must equal 1. This creates a beautiful self-consistent condition: the log-optimal investor rationally allocates capital such that no marginal reallocation can increase growth.
4. Wealth Growth (AEP for Stock Markets)
$$\frac{1}{n} \log S_n^* \to W^* \quad \text{almost surely as } n \to \infty$$
This is the Financial AEP: the log-optimal portfolio's wealth grows asymptotically at rate W* with probability 1. After n periods, S_n* ≈ 2^{nW*}. This is a law of large numbers result for geometric averages, showing that the log-optimal strategy's growth is not just good on average, but guaranteed in the limit.
5. Asymptotic Optimality
$$\limsup_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \log\left(\frac{S_n}{S_n^*}\right) \leq 0 \quad \text{almost surely}$$
No other portfolio strategy can beat the log-optimal strategy asymptotically. Any alternative grows more slowly. By Markov's inequality, the probability that any fixed strategy S outperforms S* by a factor of t decays as 1/t. This is the information-theoretic speed limit of wealth growth.
6. Side Information Value
$$\Delta W = W(\mathbf{b}^*_f, F) - W(\mathbf{b}^*_g, F) \leq D(f \| g)$$
The gain from having side information Y is bounded by the KL divergence between the informed distribution f and uninformed distribution g. This shows quantitatively: how much is a stock tip worth? At most, the KL divergence of your updated beliefs from your prior beliefs. This connects to channel coding and the fundamental limits of information advantage.
7. Mutual Information Bound
$$\Delta W \leq I(\mathbf{X}; Y)$$
The increase in doubling rate from side information Y is bounded by the mutual information I(X; Y). This is a profound connection: the value of information in gambling is measured by how much it reduces uncertainty about returns. Perfect information (I = log m for m equally likely outcomes) allows the investor to allocate all capital to the best outcome. Partial information provides proportional gains.
8. Horse Race Connection
$$W^* = \log m - H(X)$$
In a simple horse race with m equally-likely horses, the optimal doubling rate equals log m (if you could pick the winner with certainty) minus the entropy H(X) (the uncertainty). This shows the direct link: growth rate = potential capacity minus actual uncertainty. The more unpredictable the race (high H), the lower the growth. This is why uniform distributions yield W* = 0 for fair odds.

import numpy as np from scipy.optimize import minimize def log_optimal_portfolio(returns, probs): """ Find the log-optimal portfolio b* that maximizes W(b,F). The doubling rate W(b,F) = E[log b'X] measures the exponential growth rate of wealth. By the AEP for stock markets: (1/n) log S_n* → W* with probability 1. Key insight: The portfolio IS a code. Just as Shannon's optimal code achieves expected length H(X), the log-optimal portfolio achieves growth rate W* = log m - H(X) for horse races. """ n_outcomes, n_assets = returns.shape def neg_doubling_rate(b): # W(b,F) = Σ p_i * log(b' · x_i) portfolio_returns = returns @ b portfolio_returns = np.maximum(portfolio_returns, 1e-10) return -np.sum(probs * np.log2(portfolio_returns)) # Optimize over the simplex: b_i ≥ 0, Σ b_i = 1 constraints = {'type': 'eq', 'fun': lambda b: np.sum(b) - 1} bounds = [(0, 1)] * n_assets b0 = np.ones(n_assets) / n_assets result = minimize(neg_doubling_rate, b0, method='SLSQP', bounds=bounds, constraints=constraints) return result.x, -result.fun def side_information_value(W_with, W_without): """ Theorem 15.4.2: The increase in doubling rate due to side information Y is bounded by mutual information: ΔW = W(b*_f, F) - W(b*_g, F) ≤ I(X; Y) """ return W_with - W_without def simulate_wealth(b, returns, probs, n_days=1000): """Simulate wealth: S_n = Π b'X_i ≈ 2^{nW*}""" outcomes = np.random.choice(len(probs), n_days, p=probs) daily = returns[outcomes] @ b return np.cumprod(daily) # Example: 2-stock market returns = np.array([[1.10, 0.80], # Outcome 1 [0.95, 1.30]]) # Outcome 2 probs = np.array([0.5, 0.5]) b_star, W_star = log_optimal_portfolio(returns, probs) print(f"Log-optimal: {b_star[0]:.1%} Stock A, {b_star[1]:.1%} Stock B") print(f"W* = {W_star:.4f} bits/day") print(f"Wealth after 1000 days ≈ 2^(1000·{W_star:.4f}) = {2**(1000*W_star):.2e}")
