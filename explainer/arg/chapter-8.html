Section 8.1

The Reasonable Person and Due Diligence

Despite Freud's demonstration that humans are deeply irrational, lawyers and legislators cling to a powerful fiction: the "reasonable person." If you behave as a reasonable person would, you are insulated from liability. If you fail this standard, you are negligent.

What would a reasonable person do? Engage in a risk-adjusted cost-benefit analysis: weigh (a) the probability of harm, (b) the magnitude of harm if it occurs, (c) the benefit of the risky action, and (d) the availability of less risky alternatives. A drunk driver speeding at 90 mph on a highway fails every prong. Someone who trips over their own rug — was it reasonable to have a rug?

Due diligence extends this concept to business: a reasonable investigation following the standard of care a reasonable person would apply to their own affairs. If you conduct due diligence before a securities offering and the facts turn out to be wrong, you're protected — because you acted as a reasonable person would. Due diligence is insurance against hindsight: you can't be blamed for what you couldn't have found through reasonable effort.

  Think of the reasonable person as an invisible co-pilot. Before every decision, imagine this calm, prudent, moderately risk-aware person sitting next to you asking: "Have you thought about what could go wrong? Is there a safer way? Is the benefit worth the risk?" If your co-pilot would approve, you're probably safe. If your co-pilot would grab the wheel and pull over — you're being negligent.

The beauty of the reasonable person standard is its flexibility. It doesn't demand perfection — just reasonable care. You don't have to foresee every possible harm. You have to take the precautions that a sensible, informed person would take in the same situation. The standard adapts to context: reasonable care for a surgeon is different from reasonable care for a bus driver.

The Reasonable Person Calculus: Negligence occurs when the burden of precaution is less than the probability of harm multiplied by its magnitude (the Hand Formula).
Section 8.2

Exceptional Circumstances: Force Majeure

Law is all about rules and exceptions. The most dramatic exception is force majeure — the "superior force" that makes performance of an obligation impossible, impracticable, or illegal. A sailor caught in a storm ties to a stranger's dock. The dock owner sues for trespass. But the sailor had no choice — the storm made normal behavior impossible.

The legal doctrines of impossibility (literally cannot perform), impracticability (too costly to reasonably expect performance), illegality (law now prohibits performance), and frustration (the purpose of the contract has been destroyed) all address the same problem: what happens when the world changes after an agreement is made?

The critical question is always: who bears the risk of changed circumstances? Did the contract allocate this risk? If not, whose "default" responsibility should it be? The party who could more cheaply have insured against the risk? The party who had more control over the circumstances? Force majeure clauses exist precisely to pre-allocate these risks — but they can never anticipate every possibility, which is why the law of incompleteness remains relevant.

  Imagine hiring a painter to paint your house next Saturday. On Saturday, a hurricane hits. The painter can't paint. Should they pay damages for breach of contract? Of course not — a hurricane is force majeure. But what if it just rains? What if it's a 30% chance of rain? The line between "the world changed" and "you should have planned for this" is where the real legal battles happen.

Force majeure is about the boundary between foreseeable risk (which you should plan for) and unforeseeable catastrophe (which excuses performance). COVID-19 tested this boundary everywhere: was a global pandemic foreseeable? Some contracts said yes (those with pandemic-specific force majeure clauses). Most said nothing — leaving courts to decide who bears a risk that nobody explicitly allocated.

Section 8.3

Form Versus Substance

Should law respect the form of a transaction or look through to its substance? This debate appears everywhere: in tax law (hedge fund managers structuring compensation as "carried interest" to get capital gains rates), in corporate law (shell companies obscuring beneficial ownership), and in contract law (labeling employees as "independent contractors" to avoid benefits).

The argument for respecting form: predictability, consistency, and respect for parties' ability to structure their affairs. The argument for looking through to substance: preventing evasion, ensuring rules achieve their intended purpose, and preventing sophisticated parties from exploiting formalistic loopholes.

This connects directly to interpretation: do we follow the letter of the law (form) or the spirit (substance)? The principle of effet utile cuts both ways — it can argue that we should give effect to loopholes (they exist in the text for a reason) or that we should close them (the law's purpose would be defeated otherwise).

War Room Tactic

If your client benefits from formal compliance, argue: "The law says what it says. My client structured the transaction exactly as the law permits. You cannot rewrite the rules after the game is played." If substance favors you: "This is a transparent attempt to circumvent the law's purpose through clever structuring. No amount of creative labeling changes what actually happened."

Section 8.4

Law Versus Equity

When formal law produces absurd or unjust results, equity steps in. In medieval England, the common law courts were so rigidly formalistic that citizens began petitioning the king for relief. This eventually produced a parallel court system — the Courts of Equity — empowered to do what was "fair" even when formal law said otherwise.

The law-equity distinction captures a fundamental tension: rules provide predictability but sometimes produce injustice; discretion enables justice but creates unpredictability. The Bolognian rule against "spilling blood" in the streets seemed to prevent medical care — an absurd result that equity would correct.

Today, law and equity have merged in most systems, but the tension survives. Every time a judge exercises "discretion," every time a statute has a "fairness" exception, every time a regulator grants a waiver — equity is at work, modifying the rigid letter of the law to achieve a just result. Arguments from equity appeal to a judge's conscience; arguments from law appeal to a judge's duty to follow rules.

  Think of law as a GPS system and equity as a human driver. The GPS says: "Turn right in 200 feet." But 200 feet ahead, a fallen tree blocks the road. The GPS (law) insists on the route. The human driver (equity) sees the situation and finds a reasonable detour. Both are trying to reach the same destination — justice — but one is rigid and the other adaptive.

In court, you choose your vehicle. If the formal rules favor your client, drive with the GPS: "The law is clear, the text is unambiguous, apply it as written." If the formal rules produce an unjust result for your client, become the human driver: "The law cannot have intended this absurd result. Equity demands a correction." Knowing when to argue law versus equity is one of the most important strategic choices an advocate makes.

Section 8.5

Rights Versus Duties: Noses Versus Fists

Oliver Wendell Holmes declared: "The right to swing my fist ends where the other man's nose begins." Simple enough — until you realize that noses and fists are everywhere, overlapping in complex ways. Your right to play loud music. Your neighbor's right to quiet enjoyment. Your right to build on your property. Your neighbor's right to sunlight.

Every right implies a correlative duty. If you have a right to free speech, someone has a duty not to censor you (but who — the government? your employer? a private platform?). If you have a right to health care, someone has a duty to provide it (but who — the state? your insurer? your employer?). Rights without corresponding duties are empty promises.

Trachtman's key insight: rights-talk can shut down debate prematurely. Calling something a "right" makes it seem absolute and non-negotiable. But most rights conflict with other rights, and calling both sides' positions "rights" simply restates the conflict at a higher level of abstraction without resolving it. The real question is always: whose right takes priority in this specific context?

Strategic Insight

When your opponent frames their position as a "right," respond with the correlative duty: "You claim a right to X — but who exactly has a duty to provide it? And does that duty-holder have competing rights of their own?" Decomposing rights into rights-and-duties pairs exposes the hidden complexity that "rights-talk" obscures.

Section 8.6

Conflicting Values

At the center of every legal argument lies a value conflict. We want to punish criminals (justice) but also ensure fair treatment of the accused (due process). We want free markets (efficiency) but also consumer protection (equity). We want free speech (liberty) but also protection from harm (safety).

These conflicts are inherent, not resolvable. The exclusionary rule in criminal law sacrifices truth-finding for the value of deterring government overreach. Zoning laws sacrifice property rights for community planning. Anti-discrimination laws sacrifice freedom of association for equality. Every legal rule represents a settlement between competing values — and every legal argument is an attempt to shift that settlement.

Part of legal creativity is the ability to see disputes in new ways — to invoke values that neither party initially recognized were at stake. A contract dispute might also be a consumer protection issue. A property dispute might also be an environmental one. The advocate who identifies the most compelling value framing often wins.

Every legal rule is a settlement between competing values. Every legal argument seeks to renegotiate that settlement.

  The Pandemic Concert Cancellation
A concert promoter contracted with a stadium, a headliner band, and a ticketing platform for a 50,000-seat outdoor festival in March 2020. Total investment: $8 million. When COVID-19 lockdowns were imposed, the festival was cancelled. The contract's force majeure clause covers "acts of God, war, terrorism, and natural disasters" — but does not mention pandemics or government lockdowns. The promoter paid $3M upfront to the stadium (non-refundable per the contract's form), $2M to the band (whose contract says "cancellation for any reason" requires 50% kill fee), and sold 40,000 tickets at $150 each ($6M) through a platform whose terms say "all sales final." The promoter wants all money back; the stadium, band, and ticket-holders want to keep it.
Which legal framework gives the promoter the strongest argument for recovering costs?
A Force majeure — a pandemic is clearly an "act of God" or "natural disaster" under the contract clause, so all obligations should be excused.
B Frustration of purpose combined with an equity argument — the entire purpose of the contract has been destroyed by unforeseeable government action, and enforcing "non-refundable" terms would produce an unjust windfall.
C Strict interpretation of the contract terms — the stadium's "non-refundable" clause and the band's "50% kill fee" are clear, and the promoter must honor the forms they agreed to.
D Reasonable person standard — no reasonable person would expect the promoter to absorb $8M in losses for an event cancelled by government order.
Close but legally risky. The force majeure clause is your first instinct, but it's specifically worded: "acts of God, war, terrorism, and natural disasters." A pandemic might qualify as a "natural disaster," but the clause doesn't mention pandemics or government lockdowns — and courts read force majeure clauses narrowly. The counter-parties will argue: "If the parties had intended to include pandemics, they could have said so. Their silence is intentional." This is the expressio unius canon working against you.
Strongest strategy. Frustration of purpose doesn't require a force majeure clause — it's a common-law doctrine that excuses performance when an unforeseeable event destroys the fundamental purpose of the contract. The festival's purpose (gathering 50,000 people) was made literally illegal by government order. This wasn't a mere price increase or inconvenience — the entire reason for the contract ceased to exist. The equity overlay strengthens the argument: enforcing "non-refundable" clauses when the stadium provided no event would create an unjust windfall — the stadium keeps $3M for a venue it didn't provide. Courts resist results where one party gets something for nothing. This combines doctrinal law (frustration) with equitable conscience (fairness).
This is the opposing argument, not the promoter's. If you represent the stadium or band, this is your position: the contracts are clear, the forms were freely agreed to, and the promoter accepted the risk of cancellation. But this advice was asked from the promoter's perspective. Arguing for strict enforcement of terms that destroy your client is advocacy malpractice.
Wrong doctrine for this situation. The reasonable person standard applies to negligence — did someone fail to exercise reasonable care? Nobody was negligent here. The pandemic wasn't caused by anyone's failure of care. This is a contract/excuse problem, not a tort/negligence problem. The reasonable person standard doesn't help allocate losses from unforeseeable events between contracting parties.
  Who Should Bear the Cost of Being Human?
The "reasonable person" is a fiction — no one is always reasonable. Why build an entire liability system on a character who doesn't exist?
Because the alternative is worse. A subjective standard ("did THIS person act as THEY would normally act?") would reward reckless people (their "normal" is reckless) and punish careful people (even a tiny lapse from their high standard = liability). The objective standard creates a uniform baseline that applies equally to everyone: you don't have to be perfect, but you have to be reasonable. The fiction is useful precisely because it's fiction — it provides an external benchmark that doesn't depend on anyone's individual personality. Think of it as the speed limit: it doesn't describe how anyone actually drives, but it establishes the standard everyone must meet.
If a force majeure clause doesn't mention pandemics, and a pandemic strikes, whose fault is the omission — and does it matter?
This is the problem of contractual incompleteness writ large. Both parties could have anticipated pandemics (SARS, MERS, Ebola all preceded COVID-19) — but didn't. Who bears the cost of this gap? Textualists say: what's not in the contract isn't in the contract. The parties' silence is a choice. Purposivists say: the clause was intended to address unforeseeable events; a pandemic is an unforeseeable event; the clause should cover it even if not explicitly listed. The pragmatic answer: it matters less whose "fault" the omission is than who is best positioned to bear the risk. Insurance companies exist precisely for this purpose — and the party who could have most cheaply insured against the risk arguably "should have."
In the form-versus-substance debate, who is the "good guy" — the person who follows the rules as written, or the person who follows their spirit?
Neither — and that's the point. The form-follower can be a principled rule-of-law adherent ("I structured my affairs exactly as the law permits") or a cynical manipulator ("I exploited a loophole everyone knows is unfair"). The substance-seeker can be a justice-minded reformer ("The law's purpose is being circumvented") or a retroactive rule-changer ("I want to impose my policy preferences even though the text doesn't support them"). The character of the arguer doesn't determine the validity of the argument. What matters is which approach better serves the legal system's goals in this specific context.
Holmes said "My right to swing my fist ends where your nose begins." But what if your nose is everywhere?
This is the modern problem of externalities and interconnectedness. In a world where my carbon emissions affect your climate, my data practices affect your privacy, and my social media algorithm affects your teenager's mental health — every fist hits someone's nose. The traditional Holmes framework assumed clear physical boundaries. Modern rights conflicts involve diffuse, collective, hard-to-trace harms. The legal system is still catching up: environmental law, data privacy law, and platform liability are all attempts to redraw the nose-fist boundary for a world where actions have invisible, widespread, and delayed effects. The fundamental question has evolved from "where does my fist end?" to "how do we allocate responsibility when everyone is simultaneously swinging and getting hit?"
Chapter 8 — War Room Toolbox
Reasonable Person Test
Apply the Hand Formula: was the burden of precaution less than the probability × magnitude of harm? If yes, negligence.
Due Diligence Shield
Demonstrate that your client conducted a reasonable investigation. You can't be blamed for what reasonable effort couldn't find.
Force Majeure Escape
When unforeseeable events make performance impossible, argue frustration, impracticability, or force majeure to excuse obligations.
Substance Piercer
Look through formal structures to expose the real transaction underneath. Creative labeling doesn't change economic reality.
Equity Appeal
When formal law produces unjust results, invoke equity: fairness, conscience, and the prevention of unjust enrichment.
Rights-Duty Decomposer
When your opponent claims a "right," identify the correlative duty-holder and their competing rights. Decompose rights-talk into practical obligations.
Value Reframer
Identify the competing values at stake and argue for the framing most favorable to your client. Every rule is a value settlement — propose your own.
Form Shield / Piercer
Argue for formal compliance when the text favors you. Argue for substance when the spirit does. Know which weapon to draw.
