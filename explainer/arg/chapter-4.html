Arguing About the Meaning of Texts: Interpretation

"Words are the building blocks of legal power. The ability to control what words mean is the ability to direct the force of the state. Every interpretive technique is a weapon — and for every weapon, there is a counter-weapon."

Section 4.1
Plain Meaning and the Dictionary

The most intuitive rule of interpretation is also the most deceptive: just read the words. "Plain meaning" says we should enforce the law or contract as written. It seems so simple — until you realize that words are inherently incomplete, that dictionaries contain multiple definitions, that context can transform meaning, and that the parties may have used words in private, specialized, or evolving ways.

Courts exist precisely because the words weren't clear enough to prevent a dispute. If the plain meaning were truly plain, no one would be in court. Yet "plain meaning" remains a powerful starting argument because it anchors the discussion in the text itself, foreclosing strategic manipulation of intent or context.

Legal interpretation is like being an archaeologist of language. You've found an ancient inscription (the text). Plain meaning says: "Read the words as they are." But what language is it in? What did those words mean when they were carved? What did the carver intend? What would a modern reader understand? Each question opens a different archaeological dig — and each produces different artifacts.
The legal system is, at its core, an "interpretation machine." Feed it a factual situation and an incomplete text, and it produces a ruling on what that text means in this new context. Lawyers are the operators of this machine — and they know which buttons to push to get the output they want.

Section 4.2
Original Intent and Evolution

If the plain words are ambiguous, a natural next move is to ask: what did the authors mean? This is the argument from original intent — and it comes in three powerful variants:

Subjective original intent: What did the authors specifically have in mind? This is the most intuitive approach but the most problematic — contracts have multiple parties, statutes have multiple legislators, and constitutions have multiple framers. The Chinese adage "same bed, different dreams" captures this perfectly: the parties may have agreed on the words while disagreeing about their meaning.

Objective original intent: What did the words generally mean at the time they were written? This avoids the mind-reading problem by focusing on public meaning rather than private thought.

Evolutionary intent: The authors intended for their words to evolve with changing circumstances. Under this view, "cruel and unusual punishment" in 1787 means something different in 2025 — and the framers wanted it that way.

The Originalism Debate

The debate between originalists and evolutionists is not just academic — it shapes the outcomes of landmark cases on abortion, gun rights, same-sex marriage, and executive power. The key insight is that both sides claim fidelity to the authors' intent; they just disagree about what that intent was. Even "new textualists" on the progressive side have found support for their values in original intent. The methodological lesson: always research the original intent before conceding your opponent's version of it.

Section 4.3
Context, Purpose, and Principles

Words don't exist in a vacuum. A "ton" means 2000 pounds in the U.S., 2240 pounds in the U.K., and 1000 kilograms in France. Context — the technical field, the surrounding document, related laws, the preamble — provides the interpretive environment.

Purposive interpretation asks: what is the document trying to achieve? The preamble of a statute or the "whereas" clauses of a contract express the purpose — and sophisticated negotiators know that these provisions, despite having no operative force, will be used to interpret ambiguous operative clauses.

Principles-based interpretation (common in European law) identifies the principles embedded in the text and interprets everything in light of those principles. The counterargument: the text may represent a compromise among principles, not the full expression of any one principle.

Section 4.4
Four Corners vs. Preparatory Materials: The Parol Evidence Rule

Should we look only at the final written document ("four corners"), or can we examine the negotiations, drafts, and communications that led to it? The parol evidence rule says: if the parties created a final, integrated written agreement, prior or contemporaneous oral or written statements cannot contradict it.

This rule has profound practical consequences. A salesperson's verbal promises are worthless if the written contract says "this document represents the entire agreement." A buyer who relied on oral assurances about a used car's condition has no recourse if the contract says "sold as is." The lesson: if it's not in writing, it doesn't exist.

Think of the parol evidence rule as a photograph. The final written contract is like a snapshot: it captures one moment — the moment of agreement. The "parol evidence" (prior negotiations, emails, handshake deals) is like the backstory that led to the photo. The rule says: once the snapshot is taken, the backstory can't change what's in the picture.
But like photographs, contracts can be ambiguous. If the snapshot is blurry, we might need the backstory to understand what we're looking at. Courts allow "extrinsic evidence" (parol evidence) when the written terms are genuinely ambiguous — creating strategic opportunities for the clever lawyer.
Sections 4.5 – 4.6
Extensional Pruning & Interpretation vs. Construction

Extensional pruning (§4.5): The initial use of words with their commonly accepted broad meaning, followed by a strategic retreat to a narrower definition when challenged. Lawyers identify this "wiggle room" in contracts and treaties — the gap between what words seem to mean and what they can be argued to mean under pressure.

Interpretation vs. Construction (§4.6): A crucial distinction. Interpretation determines the meaning of words that are present in the text. Construction fills in the gaps — adding terms the parties would have agreed to if they had thought about the issue. When a contract is silent on what happens if performance becomes impossible, courts may "construct" an implied term (like force majeure) based on what reasonable parties would have wanted.

Sections 4.7 – 4.9
Morality, Effet Utile, and Avoiding Absurdity

Interpretation and Morality (§4.7): Should laws be interpreted to produce morally correct outcomes? Dworkin argued yes — the law should be read through a moral lens. Others insist that the law is the law, and judicial morality amounts to judicial legislation. The deepest insight: Socrates chose to accept an unjust law (drinking hemlock) because the legal system's integrity mattered more than one wrong outcome.

Effet Utile (§4.8): The French principle of "useful effect" — every word in a legal text must be given meaning. An interpretation that renders a clause meaningless is rejected. Its corollary: consistent usage (same word = same meaning) and meaningful variation (different words = different meanings). These principles put enormous pressure on drafters to be precise.

Avoiding Absurdity (§4.9): Texts should not be interpreted to produce absurd results. The classic example: a medieval law punishing anyone who "drew blood in the streets" was held not to apply to a surgeon performing emergency aid. But who decides what is "absurd"? One person's absurdity is another's strict compliance with the text.

Sections 4.10 – 4.13
Lex Specialis, Expressio Unius, Clear Statement Rules & Reductio ad Absurdum

Lex Specialis (§4.10): When two rules conflict, the more specific one prevails. The assumption: the specific rule was more carefully tailored. But who determines which rule is "more specific"? And what if the drafters intended the general rule to dominate?

Expressio Unius (§4.11): "The expression of one thing excludes others." If a list specifies "sailboats, motorboats, rowboats, and canoes," are kayaks included? Under expressio unius, no — the list is exhaustive. But the counter: the list merely identifies common examples. Lawyers counter expressio unius by adding "including, without limitation" or "e.g." instead of "i.e."

Clear Statement Rules (§4.12): Some principles are so important that they can only be overridden by explicit, unambiguous legislative language. This forces the legislature to bear the political cost of clearly stating its intent. The rule of lenity is the criminal law version: ambiguous criminal statutes are read in the defendant's favor.

Reductio ad Absurdum (§4.13): Extend your opponent's principle to its logical extreme and show it produces absurd results. Also called the "slippery slope," "camel's nose," or "parade of horribles." The best defense: distinguish your case from the hypothetical extremes your opponent conjures.

The Autonomous Vehicle Clause
A 2018 commercial lease agreement states: "Tenant shall not park any vehicles in the building's underground garage that use combustion engines." In 2025, the tenant begins parking a fleet of autonomous electric delivery robots (each about the size of a large suitcase, moving on wheels at 5 mph) in the garage. The landlord demands they be removed, arguing they are "vehicles." The tenant says they are "robots" or "delivery devices," not vehicles. The lease contains a standard integration clause ("this agreement constitutes the entire understanding").
Which interpretive strategy gives the tenant the strongest argument?
A Argue that the lease drafters' original intent in 2018 was only to prevent exhaust fumes and noise, so the clause doesn't apply to electric robots.
B Argue plain meaning: the clause restricts "vehicles that use combustion engines." Robots are not "vehicles" under any ordinary definition, AND even if they were, they don't use combustion engines. The plain text permits them.
C Invoke the rule of lenity: since the lease is ambiguous about what counts as a "vehicle," the ambiguity should be resolved in favor of the tenant.
D Argue that the landlord waived any objection by not updating the lease when autonomous robots became common.
Weaker than it appears. Original intent is a viable argument, but here it requires you to prove what the drafters were thinking in 2018 — and the integration clause ("entire understanding") makes it harder to introduce extrinsic evidence about intent. Also, the landlord could counter that the intent was to restrict ALL vehicles, and the combustion engine clause was just the first restriction. Plain meaning is stronger here because you win without needing to prove anyone's state of mind.
Textbook plain meaning victory. You have a two-layer defense that is nearly impregnable: (1) A suitcase-sized delivery robot moving at 5 mph is not a "vehicle" under any standard dictionary definition — vehicles are designed to transport people or large goods; (2) Even if somehow classified as vehicles, they don't use combustion engines, so the clause doesn't apply by its own terms. You win on the text alone — no need for subjective intent, context, or equitable arguments. The beauty of plain meaning: when the text is clearly on your side, it's the strongest weapon in the arsenal.
Wrong doctrine. The rule of lenity applies to criminal statutes, not private commercial leases. In contract interpretation, ambiguity is typically resolved against the drafter (contra proferentem) — which might help if the landlord drafted the lease, but this is a weaker argument than plain meaning. When the text clearly supports you, don't reach for secondary doctrines.
Legally unsound. There is no general duty to "update" a lease to address new technologies. Waiver requires a knowing relinquishment of a known right. The landlord didn't know about the robots until they appeared. This argument would fail quickly and waste your credibility on a losing point. Always lead with your strongest argument — here, plain meaning.
Socratic Sandbox: "Who Controls the Meaning of Words?"
Humpty Dumpty told Alice: "When I use a word, it means just what I choose it to mean — neither more nor less." Is Humpty Dumpty an originalist, a textualist, or something else entirely? What does his position reveal about the politics of interpretation?
Humpty Dumpty is a radical subjectivist — he claims unilateral control over meaning. In legal terms, this is closer to an extreme version of original intent (the author's meaning controls), but with a key difference: Humpty Dumpty is also the interpreter. The real question Alice raises is: "Can you make words mean so many things?" The legal system says no — meaning must be intersubjective (shared between parties) to create binding obligations. Trachtman uses this passage to illustrate that the power to interpret IS the power to rule. Whoever controls meaning controls the force of the state.
If interpretation is truly indeterminate — if for every canon there is a counter-canon — then how is the legal system different from arbitrary power? What prevents it from being a "government of men, not laws"?
This is the deepest question in legal theory. The response has several layers: (1) Indeterminacy is not infinite — many cases have obvious answers that no reasonable interpreter would dispute; (2) Interpretive techniques have different persuasive power in different contexts — plain meaning is strongest when the text is clear, purpose is strongest when the text is ambiguous; (3) Institutional constraints (precedent, peer review, appellate courts) limit individual discretion; (4) The practice of giving reasons and justifying interpretive choices creates accountability. The system isn't perfectly determinate, but it's far from arbitrary. (This previews Chapter 5's discussion of precedent as a constraint on discretion.)
A contract signed in 2000 uses the word "communication." At the time, this was understood to mean letters, phone calls, and emails. In 2025, is a TikTok video a "communication" under the contract? Which interpretive technique should govern, and why?
Plain meaning in 2025 would include TikTok — it's clearly a form of communication. Subjective original intent in 2000 would not — TikTok didn't exist. Objective original intent asks: what did "communication" generally mean in 2000? Probably any method of conveying information, which could include future technologies. Evolutionary interpretation says the parties expected "communication" to encompass new technologies as they arose. The strongest argument: the word "communication" is inherently open-ended (unlike a specific list), so it naturally absorbs new instances. This is precisely the incompleteness problem from Chapter 1 — and interpretation is the machine that resolves it.
Socrates chose to drink hemlock rather than escape an unjust verdict. Was he right? When, if ever, should a judge interpret the law to produce a morally better outcome — even if the text doesn't support it?
Socrates' position is the strongest possible case for procedural justice over substantive justice: the integrity of the legal system requires accepting even unjust outcomes, because the alternative (judges rewriting law to match their morality) is worse. But this position has limits. Should a judge enforce a contract that enables human trafficking? Most would say no — some substantive injustices are so great that they override procedural fidelity. The tension is permanent: process values (Chapter 3) and interpretive fidelity (Chapter 4) must coexist with moral limits. Where exactly to draw the line is the central question of jurisprudence.
