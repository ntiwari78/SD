Section 7.1

Non Sequitur: That Doesn't Follow

A non sequitur is a conclusion that does not logically follow from the premises. It is the broadest category of rhetorical trick — and in a sense, all the other tricks in this chapter are subspecies of it. The asserted predicate does not indicate the desired conclusion, but the speaker acts as though it does.

Consider the O.J. Simpson trial. A glove was found at the crime scene, containing DNA evidence from both Simpson and the victims. The prosecutor asked Simpson to try on the glove in court, and it appeared too tight. Defense attorney Johnnie Cochran seized on this with his famous refrain. The glove's fit became the jury's focus — rather than the DNA evidence, blood evidence, and other forensic proof. This was a masterful non sequitur: the glove's courtroom fit did not logically negate the DNA evidence, but it focused attention on a fact that felt salient while not being dispositive.

Non sequiturs work because they exploit the gap between feeling and logic. Something can feel relevant — emotionally, viscerally — while having no logical bearing on the conclusion. Critical thinking requires asking: "Does this actually follow?" nine times out of ten, when you feel persuaded but can't articulate why, a non sequitur is at work.

  Imagine a magician performing a card trick. While you watch his right hand flourish dramatically, his left hand quietly palms the card. The non sequitur is the rhetorical equivalent — the flashy argument that draws your attention away from the logical gap. The audience is impressed by the flourish, never noticing the hidden move.

In legal argument, non sequiturs are the misdirection. The dramatic exhibit, the emotional testimony, the vivid anecdote — they may be true and compelling, but if they don't logically connect to the legal elements at issue, they're just the magician's flourish. Always ask: "What is the left hand doing?"

The Non Sequitur Test: If Premise P does not logically entail Conclusion C, then any argument of the form P → C is a non sequitur, regardless of how persuasive P feels.
Section 7.2

Ad Hominem Arguments: Discrediting the Source

Ad hominem attacks target the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. Even a broken clock is right twice a day — the fact that a speaker is dishonest, biased, or unlikable does not logically make their statements false.

But context matters. An ad hominem becomes appropriate when the speaker's credibility is directly relevant. If an expert witness has testified on the same side in 47 consecutive cases, that pattern of bias is legitimately relevant to evaluating their testimony. If a witness is nearsighted and claims to have seen details at great distance, challenging their eyesight isn't ad hominem — it's evidentiary.

The AstroTurf strategy is an inverse form: rather than attacking the source, you fabricate a favorable source. Creating a fake grassroots movement to simulate public support — a synthetic "grass roots" campaign funded by corporate interests — manufactures the appearance of credibility. Recognizing AstroTurf requires asking: who is really behind this seemingly independent voice?

War Room Tactic

Distinguish between appropriate and inappropriate ad hominem. Appropriate: "This expert has been paid $2M by the defense over her career — consider her financial incentive." Inappropriate: "This expert is a socialist, so her scientific testimony is unreliable." The test: does the personal characteristic have a nexus to the reliability of the specific testimony?

Section 7.3

Fallacies of Causation

Three interrelated fallacies corrupt causal reasoning: Post hoc ergo propter hoc ("after this, therefore because of this") — assuming that because B followed A, A caused B. Affirming the consequent — if A then B; B is true; therefore A is true (but other causes could produce B). And correlation versus causation — two things happening together does not mean one causes the other.

The "no peanut butter" joke illustrates post hoc beautifully: a man stomps his feet and shouts "No peanut butter!" When asked why, he says "This keeps away the elephants." There are no elephants in Manhattan, so the correlation exists — but the causation is absurd.

Omitted variable bias is the statistical cousin: a hidden third factor causes both the alleged cause and the alleged effect. Smokers get more lung cancer — but perhaps people who smoke also work in factories with air pollutants. Until you control for the omitted variable (workplace exposure), you can't confirm the smoking-cancer link. Of course, once you do control for it, the correlation holds — which is how science eventually established causation.

  Think of causation fallacies like a weather vane. The rooster on top spins to face the wind. A naive observer might conclude: "The rooster makes the wind blow." After all, every time the wind blows from the west, the rooster faces west. Perfect correlation. Zero causation. The rooster responds to the wind; it doesn't create it.

In legal argument, always ask: is this the rooster or the wind? When your opponent says "Sales dropped after the regulation was enacted, therefore the regulation killed sales" — did the regulation cause the drop, or did the same economic conditions that prompted the regulation also cause the sales decline? Separate the weather vane from the weather.

Three fallacies that corrupt causal reasoning. Each exploits the human tendency to see patterns as causes.
Section 7.4

Inappropriate Inference from Limited Data

Statistics can be weaponized through small sample sizes, misleading baselines, and selective data. A lawyer asserting that "9 out of 10 patients who took this drug developed heart disease" sounds devastating — until you learn the sample was only 10 patients, all of whom were already at extreme cardiac risk, and the drug was a last-resort treatment for people who would otherwise have died.

Without statistical literacy, both lawyers and decision-makers are vulnerable to manipulation. Key questions: How large was the sample? Was there a control group? What was the baseline rate? Were confounding variables controlled? Is the finding replicable?

As Trachtman wryly notes: "74% of statistics used in argumentation are made up on the spot." The joke itself illustrates the point — your instinct was to take that number at face value, even without any source. We are primed to trust numbers because they feel precise, even when they are fabricated.

Strategic Insight

When confronting statistical evidence, attack the denominator, not just the numerator. "9 out of 10" sounds terrifying until you know it was 9 out of 10 — not 900 out of 1,000. Small samples produce extreme results by chance alone. Demand the sample size, the confidence interval, and the methodology. Numbers without context are just decoration.

Section 7.5

Occam's Razor and the Exception That Proves the Rule

Occam's Razor says: don't seek complicated explanations when simple ones suffice. In law, it's the best tool for distinguishing ratio decidendi from obiter dictum, and for evaluating competing narratives. If the prosecution presents a simple chain of evidence and the defense constructs an elaborate theory involving stolen weapons, planted blood, and conspiring strangers — Occam's Razor cuts in the prosecution's favor.

But Occam's Razor is not infallible. The more complex explanation may be correct. It's a heuristic, not a proof. Its power is pragmatic: the simpler explanation deserves the benefit of the doubt because it requires fewer assumptions.

"The exception that proves the rule" is commonly misunderstood. Exceptions do not prove rules — they test them. When your opponent invokes this phrase, they're usually asking you to ignore the exception. Press them: does the exception disprove the rule, or does it reveal the rule's boundaries? There's a difference between "the rule is wrong" and "the rule doesn't apply here."

  Imagine two explanations for why you're locked out of your house. Explanation A: you forgot your key. Explanation B: a rival spy broke in, changed the locks, cloned your key, put the original back in your pocket, but programmed the clone to fail at exactly this hour. Both explain the locked door. Occam's Razor says: check your pockets first.

In court, when the defense offers a baroque conspiracy theory to explain away straightforward evidence, invoke Occam's Razor: "My opponent's theory requires you to believe that six independent coincidences occurred simultaneously. Our theory requires you to believe one thing: the defendant did it. Which explanation requires fewer leaps of faith?"

Section 7.6

Embedding Assumptions in Questions

The classic example: "When did you stop beating your wife?" Every possible answer — a date, "I didn't," or silence — seems to confirm the embedded assumption. This is the loaded question: a question that smuggles a premise into its structure so that answering it on its own terms forces acceptance of the hidden premise.

In legal settings, loaded questions are everywhere. "At what point did you decide to monopolize the market?" presupposes that the defendant decided to monopolize. The only proper response is to reject the question's framing: "I never decided to monopolize the market, so I cannot answer the question as posed."

In courtroom drama, this is the moment the jury gasps. In real courtroom practice, opposing counsel objects: "Assumes facts not in evidence." But outside the courtroom — in negotiations, media appearances, political debates — there is no objection mechanism. The only defense is recognizing the embedded assumption and explicitly rejecting it before it takes root.

War Room Tactic

When you spot a loaded question, never answer it on its own terms. Instead, decompose it: "Your question assumes X. I reject that assumption. Let me tell you what actually happened." This technique — reframing — is essential in depositions, cross-examinations, and media interviews. The person who controls the framing controls the narrative.

The Argument
"Ever since the new CEO took over, our stock price has dropped 30%. Clearly she is destroying the company."
Fallacy
Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc
The Trick: The stock dropped after the CEO took over, therefore she caused the drop. But correlation in time does not equal causation. The stock drop could be due to market-wide recession, sector rotation, the previous CEO's hidden problems surfacing, or seasonal trends. Counter: "The entire tech sector dropped 25% in the same period. Our CEO's performance actually outperformed the benchmark. The timeline is coincidence, not causation."
The Argument
"Why would you trust Dr. Chen's testimony? She's a vegan who once protested outside a pharmaceutical company."
Fallacy
Inappropriate Ad Hominem
The Trick: Attacking Dr. Chen's dietary choices and activism rather than her scientific methodology. Her veganism has no nexus to her expertise in toxicology. Counter: "Dr. Chen's personal dietary choices are irrelevant to her analysis of the chemical data. Her methodology is peer-reviewed, her credentials are impeccable, and opposing counsel hasn't challenged a single data point in her report. Attacking the messenger because you can't attack the message is the definition of ad hominem."
The Argument
"Our study of 8 patients showed that 7 experienced improvement after using our device. That's an 87.5% success rate — far superior to the industry standard."
Fallacy
Inference from Limited Data
The Trick: An 87.5% rate sounds impressively precise — but with only 8 patients, one different outcome changes the rate from 87.5% to 75%. The sample is far too small for statistical significance, there's no control group, and "improvement" is undefined. Counter: "With a sample of 8, the margin of error exceeds 30 percentage points. This isn't a study — it's an anecdote with a decimal point. Where is the controlled, randomized trial with hundreds of patients?"
The Argument
"At what point during negotiations did your company decide to steal our client's proprietary algorithm?"
Fallacy
Embedded Assumption
The Trick: The question embeds two unproven assumptions: (1) that theft occurred, and (2) that it was a deliberate decision made during negotiations. Any answer that provides a timeframe implicitly concedes both. Counter: "Objection — assumes facts not in evidence. My client did not steal any algorithm. The question's premise is false, and I will not answer it as framed. Let me describe what actually happened during the negotiations."
The Argument
"If the defendant is innocent, the evidence would be consistent with his story. The evidence IS consistent with his story. Therefore, the defendant is innocent."
Fallacy
Affirming the Consequent
The Trick: Classic logical fallacy: If A then B; B; therefore A. But the evidence could be consistent with the defendant's story AND with guilt — circumstantial evidence is by definition consistent with multiple explanations. A man driving a previously stolen car is "consistent with" having bought it legitimately — but also with having stolen it. Counter: "Consistency is not proof. The evidence is equally consistent with guilt. The question isn't whether the evidence could fit innocence, but whether guilt has been proven beyond reasonable doubt."
  The Dark Arts Arsenal
  The Social Media Algorithm Trial
Parents of a teenager who died by suicide are suing a social media platform. Their argument: (1) After the teen joined the platform, her mental health declined. (2) An internal company study showed that 32% of teen girls who felt bad about their bodies said Instagram made the feeling worse. (3) The company's lead researcher was previously employed by a tobacco company's research division. (4) During depositions, the company's lawyer asked the parents: "At what point did you decide to stop monitoring your daughter's social media usage?" The parents' legal team must navigate these arguments.
Which rhetorical trick is the MOST dangerous to the parents' case and hardest to counter?
A The ad hominem attack on the researcher's tobacco-industry background — it could discredit the internal study entirely.
B The post hoc fallacy in claiming the platform caused the teen's decline — "after joining" doesn't prove "because of joining."
C The loaded question in the deposition — it embeds the assumption that the parents were negligent, shifting blame to them regardless of how they answer.
D The limited data problem — 32% of a self-selected group "feeling worse" doesn't prove the platform caused the suicide.
Relevant but manageable. The researcher's tobacco background is a legitimate credibility concern, but it's actually an appropriate ad hominem — if the researcher has a pattern of producing industry-friendly results, that's fair game. More importantly, the parents can counter by noting that this was an internal study whose results were unfavorable to the company — the researcher's employer bias actually cuts in the parents' favor. The study admitted harm despite being funded by the company that would prefer to find none.
Real vulnerability but addressable. Yes, "she joined, then declined" is classic post hoc reasoning. But the parents can layer in additional evidence: the specific content she consumed, the algorithmic amplification of harmful content, dose-response relationships (more usage = worse outcomes), and the company's own internal research showing awareness of harm. Post hoc becomes more persuasive when combined with mechanism, dose-response, and the company's own admissions.
Exactly right — this is the most dangerous trick. The loaded question "When did you decide to stop monitoring?" embeds two devastating assumptions: (1) the parents were monitoring and stopped, and (2) they made a conscious decision to stop. Any answer — "I stopped when she was 15," "I never stopped," or silence — keeps the spotlight on parental negligence rather than corporate responsibility. It subtly introduces comparative fault without ever having to prove it. The only defense is reframing: "We didn't 'decide to stop monitoring.' We trusted your platform's stated safety features. The question you should answer is: when did your company decide to stop protecting children?"
Valid criticism but not the biggest threat. The 32% statistic does have limitations — it's self-reported, potentially self-selected, and "feeling worse" is subjective. But the parents don't need this study to be their entire case. It's supporting evidence — a Brandeis brief element — not the sole proof. The real power of the statistic is that it came from the company's own research, showing corporate knowledge of harm. Even if the number is imprecise, the company's awareness is the key legal fact.
  Is All Persuasion Manipulation?
If rhetorical tricks are fallacies, why are they so effective? Does their effectiveness tell us something about human reasoning?
Their effectiveness reveals that human reasoning is fundamentally dual-process: we have a fast, intuitive system (System 1) that operates on emotion, pattern recognition, and heuristics, and a slow, deliberate system (System 2) that operates on logic. Rhetorical tricks target System 1 — they feel right before we have time to analyze them. The non sequitur feels relevant. The ad hominem feels like a valid reason to distrust. The post hoc feels causal. Legal training is essentially training System 2 to override System 1 — to pause and ask "does this actually follow?" before the emotional verdict sets in.
Where is the line between legitimate persuasion and rhetorical manipulation?
The line is transparency of reasoning. Legitimate persuasion says: "Here are my premises, here is my logic, here is my conclusion — evaluate it." Manipulation says: "Here is an emotional trigger designed to bypass your evaluation." A Brandeis brief with solid data is persuasion. A cherry-picked statistic designed to create a false impression is manipulation. An emotional closing argument that ties legitimate evidence to human consequences is persuasion. An emotional argument that substitutes for evidence is manipulation. The test: if the audience could see the full logical structure of the argument, would they still be persuaded?
Occam's Razor favors simple explanations. But conspiracies do exist. How do we distinguish healthy skepticism from Occam's Razor abuse?
Occam's Razor doesn't say the simpler explanation is true — it says the simpler explanation deserves the initial benefit of the doubt. The key distinction: a conspiracy theory requires you to assume hidden coordination among multiple independent actors without evidence of that coordination. A legitimate complex explanation has evidence for each step of the complexity. If each link in the chain is independently supported by evidence, the overall explanation can be complex without violating Occam's Razor. It's only when you must assume unsupported steps that the razor cuts against you.
In an age of social media and AI-generated content, are rhetorical tricks becoming more dangerous or easier to detect?
Both — and that's the crisis. AI can generate persuasive-sounding arguments that are logically empty, produce fake statistics with convincing citations, and create deepfake expert testimony. Social media amplifies non sequiturs (viral emotional content), enables industrial-scale AstroTurfing (bot networks simulating grassroots support), and rewards post hoc reasoning (every event immediately attributed to the last political action). But AI also enables detection: fact-checking tools, logical consistency analyzers, and source-verification systems. The question is whether detection can keep pace with generation. The fundamental skill — asking "does this follow?" — has never been more important.
The adversarial legal system relies on each side using persuasion to advance their client's position. Does this inherently incentivize rhetorical tricks?
Yes — and that's by design. The adversarial system assumes that the truth emerges from the collision of opposing biased advocates. Each side is supposed to present the strongest version of their case, which inevitably includes strategic framing, emphasis, and selective presentation. The safeguards are: (1) the opposing counsel, whose job is to expose fallacies; (2) the judge, who enforces rules of evidence and logic; (3) ethical rules prohibiting outright deception; and (4) the jury, who evaluates credibility. The system doesn't eliminate rhetorical tricks — it creates an ecosystem where tricks deployed by one side are challenged by the other. The risk is asymmetry: when one side has better lawyers, the trick-detection system breaks down.
Chapter 7 — War Room Toolbox
Non Sequitur Detector
Ask: "Does this conclusion actually follow from the premise?" If the logical link is missing, call it out regardless of emotional appeal.
Ad Hominem Shield
Distinguish legitimate credibility attacks (bias, financial interest) from irrelevant personal attacks (politics, lifestyle, appearance).
Post Hoc Breaker
When your opponent argues "A then B, therefore A caused B" — identify alternative causes, omitted variables, and mere coincidence.
Consequent Reverser
Expose affirming the consequent: "If A then B; B; therefore A" is invalid because other causes (C, D, E) could also produce B.
Statistics Auditor
Demand sample size, control group, confidence interval, and methodology. Numbers without context are just decoration.
Occam's Razor
Favor the explanation that requires the fewest assumptions. The more elaborate the theory, the more it needs independent evidence for each step.
Loaded Question Defuser
Never answer a loaded question on its own terms. Decompose it, reject the embedded assumption, and reframe on your terms.
AstroTurf Spotter
When "independent" support appears for your opponent's position, investigate who is funding it. Manufactured consensus is not consensus.
