Section 5.1

Consistency and Law

The beating heart of precedent is consistency — the demand that legal decision-makers treat similar situations the same way. This is not merely a bureaucratic preference; it's a foundational principle of fairness. If the court granted a tax exemption to Factory A last year, it creates an expectation — perhaps even a right — for Factory B to receive the same treatment under the same circumstances.

Consistency manifests in several forms: non-discrimination (the same rule for everyone), most-favored-nation treatment (extending the best terms offered to any one party to all), and national treatment (treating foreigners the same as locals). These concepts permeate trade law, constitutional law, and everyday adjudication.

  Think of precedent like a trail through a forest. The first hiker who cuts through the brush creates a path. Each subsequent hiker who follows that path makes it wider and easier to follow. After hundreds of hikers, the trail becomes an established route — and anyone who tries to blaze a completely new path has to justify why the existing trail won't work.

Legal precedent works exactly this way. Early decisions cut through the uncertainty of uncharted legal territory. Over time, those decisions harden into established rules. A lawyer who wants a different result must explain why the existing "trail" doesn't apply to their case — why their client's situation is genuinely different, not just inconveniently similar.

The Consistency Principle: If Case₁ yielded Outcome A under Rule R, then Case₂ (with same material facts) should yield the same outcome — unless a legally relevant distinction exists.
Section 5.2

The Salience of Precedent

Why should past decisions bind the future? Trachtman identifies four key justifications for the authority of precedent:

Efficiency — relitigating the same question every time is costly and wasteful. Settled law reduces transaction costs. Conservatism — stability in the law allows people to plan their lives around predictable rules. Experience — past decisions represent accumulated wisdom; judges who came before may have considered angles the current judge hasn't. Acquiescence — when society doesn't react against a ruling, that silence amounts to a form of democratic ratification.

But these justifications have limits. Efficiency can become rigidity. Conservatism can preserve injustice. Experience can embed outdated assumptions. And acquiescence may simply reflect a lack of power to object.

Strategic Insight

A skilled advocate matches the justification to the audience. Before a tradition-minded judge, emphasize stability and experience. Before a reform-oriented judge, highlight the costs of blind obedience to outdated rulings. The same precedent can be framed as unshakable bedrock or crumbling foundation.

Section 5.3

Rationes Decidendi

The ratio decidendi (literally "reason for deciding") is the legal principle that actually drove the court's conclusion. It's the binding DNA of a case — the part that future courts must follow. But extracting it is an art, not a science.

Two lawyers will read the same opinion and extract different rationes. A broad reading might say "the court held that manufacturers owe a duty of care to end consumers." A narrow reading might say "the court held that a manufacturer of bottled drinks owes a duty to the consumer when the defect cannot be discovered by inspection." Both are legitimate — and which one a future court adopts determines whether the precedent applies.

This is where Occam's Razor enters legal reasoning. Courts prefer the narrowest ratio that explains the result — the most parsimonious rule. But lawyers seeking to expand or restrict a precedent's reach will frame the ratio broadly or narrowly to suit their needs.

  Imagine a scientist observing that every red ball they drop falls at 9.8 m/s². The ratio is the extracted rule: "All objects fall at 9.8 m/s²" (broad) versus "Red balls fall at 9.8 m/s²" (narrow). The redness of the ball is probably irrelevant — but maybe it isn't. Deciding which facts are "material" to the rule is exactly the art of extracting a ratio decidendi.

In law, the fight is always about scope. Did the court decide this because of the specific facts (narrow ratio), or because of a general principle that happened to appear in those facts (broad ratio)? The answer changes whether the precedent controls your case.

Extracting the ratio: From the full set of facts F, identify the material subset Fₘ that the court relied upon, then state the rule R that maps Fₘ → Outcome.
Section 5.4

Obiter Dictum and Cheap Talk

Obiter dicta (things "said in passing") are statements in a judicial opinion that are not strictly necessary to the decision. They're the judge's musings, hypotheticals, and commentary — interesting, sometimes influential, but not binding.

The distinction matters enormously. A ratio decidendi creates an obligation for lower courts to follow. An obiter dictum is merely persuasive — a suggestion, not a command. Yet the line between the two is notoriously blurry. Judges sometimes bury their most important reasoning in what appears to be a passing remark, and dictum from a Supreme Court opinion carries more practical weight than the holding of a lower court.

Trachtman draws a parallel to the economic concept of cheap talk: statements are most credible when backed by action. A court's holding is backed by the force of the judgment; its dicta are backed only by the reputation of the judge who wrote them.

War Room Tactic

If an unfavorable passage appears in a prior opinion, argue it was dictum — "not necessary to the court's decision." If a favorable passage was technically dictum, reframe it: "While styled as an aside, this passage was integral to the court's reasoning and has been relied upon by subsequent courts." The classification is surprisingly malleable.

Section 5.5

Exceptions That Swallow the Rule

Every rule generates exceptions. But sometimes the exceptions grow so large and numerous that they consume the original rule entirely. Trachtman illustrates this with the Glass-Steagall Act, which originally separated commercial banking from investment banking. Over decades, regulators carved out so many exceptions that by the time the law was formally repealed, it had already been hollowed out from within.

This phenomenon is a powerful argumentative tool. If you want to undermine a rule, don't attack it directly — catalog its exceptions. Show that so many situations are exempted that the "rule" is really just the exception to the exception. Conversely, if you want to preserve a rule, argue that exceptions should be read narrowly and that adding more would render the rule meaningless.

  Think of a dam with patches. Each time a leak springs, engineers add a patch. But after enough patches, the dam is more patch than concrete. At some point, a reasonable engineer says: "We need a new dam." Legal rules work the same way. Each exception is a patch. Eventually, the patches tell you the original rule has failed.

Smart lawyers recognize when a rule is more hole than substance. They either argue for demolishing the dam (overruling the precedent) or argue that the patches prove the dam's resilience (the rule still stands, flexibly adapting to new situations).

Section 5.6

Breaking Precedent

Sometimes a precedent must be overruled. Brown v. Board of Education overturning Plessy v. Ferguson is the paradigmatic example: the Supreme Court concluded that "separate but equal" was inherently unequal, directly repudiating decades of settled law.

Breaking precedent carries heavy costs. It undermines reliance interests — people who structured their affairs around the old rule. It signals instability, potentially reducing respect for the legal system. And it requires a court to admit that a prior court got it wrong.

But the costs of not breaking bad precedent can be worse: perpetuating injustice, ignoring changed circumstances, or clinging to rules built on factual premises that science or society has since disproven. The question is always: do the costs of continued adherence outweigh the costs of disruption?

The Overruling Calculus: Precedent P should be overruled when the social cost of continued adherence exceeds the combined costs of disruption and lost reliance.
Section 5.7

Passive Virtues

Sometimes the most powerful judicial move is not deciding. Trachtman discusses the concept of "passive virtues" — the strategic decision by courts to avoid ruling on certain issues. Through doctrines like standing, ripeness, mootness, and the political question doctrine, courts can decline to reach the merits.

This creates a peculiar kind of precedent: the precedent of silence. When a court refuses to decide, it leaves the existing law undisturbed while signaling — perhaps — that the issue is too politically sensitive, insufficiently developed, or better left to the legislature. Not deciding is deciding: it decides that the status quo will continue.

Lawyers can use passive virtues both offensively and defensively. Ask the court to abstain if the current law favors your client. Push the court to decide if silence preserves an unjust status quo.

Strategic Insight

Courts sometimes decline jurisdiction precisely because they are unwilling to set a precedent in either direction. Recognizing this allows advocates to frame their case as narrow and fact-specific — reducing the court's fear of broad, unintended consequences — or alternatively, to frame the issue as so urgent that silence is itself a harm.

Section 5.8

Specificity and Delegation

Rules exist on a spectrum from highly specific ("the speed limit is 65 mph") to highly general ("drive at a reasonable speed"). Trachtman compares this to the difference between Ultimate Frisbee rules (where players self-referee and rely on general principles of sportsmanship) and basketball rules (precise, with certified officials enforcing specific codes).

More specific rules create more predictable precedent but less flexibility. More general standards create flexible precedent but less predictability. The level of specificity also determines delegation: vague standards delegate enormous interpretive power to future decision-makers, while precise rules constrain them.

In precedent-based argument, this matters because a precedent stated as a specific rule ("manufacturers of carbonated beverages in opaque bottles owe a duty") is easier to distinguish than one stated as a broad standard ("manufacturers owe a duty of reasonable care"). The broader the standard, the harder it is to escape.

Section 5.9

Citations, Footnotes, and the Pedigree of Information

Not all precedents carry equal weight. A Supreme Court decision outranks a circuit court opinion. A unanimous ruling carries more authority than a 5-4 split. An oft-cited case is more "proven" than an obscure one. Trachtman discusses the pedigree of legal authority — the hierarchy, reputation, and citation history that determine how much persuasive force a precedent commands.

Citations function as a legal currency of credibility. Citing a well-known, frequently followed case signals that your argument stands on solid ground. Citing an obscure, rarely mentioned case may signal either brilliance (discovering an overlooked gem) or desperation (scraping the bottom of the barrel).

Footnotes, too, carry strategic weight. Some of the most important legal innovations in history have been planted in footnotes — notably footnote 4 of Carolene Products, which laid the groundwork for heightened scrutiny of laws affecting "discrete and insular minorities." The footnote is where bold ideas can hide while the main text maintains a conservative posture.

War Room Tactic

When researching precedent, don't just find cases that support you — assess their pedigree. How often have they been cited? By which courts? Have they been questioned or distinguished? A single Supreme Court paragraph is worth more than a dozen unreported trial court opinions. Build your citation chain from the strongest links.

  The Autonomous Delivery Robot
A city's 2019 ordinance bans "vehicles" from operating on public sidewalks without a human operator present. In 2024, the city fines a company $50,000 for deploying autonomous delivery robots on sidewalks. The company appeals, citing two precedents: (1) City v. Segway Tours (2020), where the court held that Segways are "vehicles" under the ordinance; and (2) City v. Remote Control Hobbyists (2021), where the court held that RC cars are not "vehicles" because they are "toys with no transportation function." The delivery robot weighs 40 lbs, moves at walking speed, carries packages, and has no passengers.
As counsel for the delivery robot company, which precedent strategy gives you the strongest argument?
A Follow Segway Tours — argue that since Segways are vehicles, and delivery robots are similar to Segways, robots are also vehicles (and therefore the fine is proper, helping us negotiate a lower penalty).
B Argue Segway Tours should be overruled because the ordinance was never intended to cover autonomous technology.
C Distinguish Segway Tours (human rider, transportation purpose) and follow RC Hobbyists by arguing the delivery robot is closer to an RC car (no passenger, no transportation function for humans) than to a Segway.
D Argue both precedents are obiter dictum because neither court specifically addressed autonomous delivery robots.
Not quite. Following Segway Tours concedes that your robot is a "vehicle," which is exactly what the city wants to prove. Why would you adopt the precedent that hurts your client? Defense counsel should be trying to escape this precedent, not embrace it. Accepting liability to "negotiate down" is a surrender strategy, not a legal argument.
Too aggressive. Asking a court to overrule a recent precedent (only 4 years old) is a high bar. You'd need to show the decision was clearly wrong or that circumstances have fundamentally changed. More importantly, you don't need to overrule it — you can simply distinguish it. Overruling is the nuclear option; distinction is the scalpel. Use the scalpel first.
Excellent. This is the textbook precedent strategy: distinguish the unfavorable case and align with the favorable one. The key material differences are: (1) Segways carry human riders; robots don't — so the safety concerns differ. (2) Segways serve a transportation function; robots serve a delivery function — different purpose. (3) The RC car precedent established that devices without a transportation function aren't "vehicles." Your robot, like the RC car, doesn't transport people. You're narrowing the ratio of Segway Tours (vehicles = devices that transport humans on sidewalks) while broadening the ratio of RC Hobbyists (non-vehicles = autonomous devices without human transportation purpose).
Misapplying the concept. Obiter dictum refers to statements in an opinion that weren't necessary to the court's decision — not to the absence of a specific technology from the case facts. Both courts' holdings about what constitutes a "vehicle" were their rationes decidendi. The fact that they didn't mention delivery robots doesn't make their holdings dictum; it means those holdings must be applied by analogy (or distinguished). Dictum is about the structure of reasoning, not the scope of facts.
  When Should the Dead Govern the Living?
If consistency is the core value of precedent, why do we also have mechanisms for breaking it?
Consider that consistency is a means, not an end. The end is justice. When consistency produces unjust results — as in Plessy v. Ferguson's "separate but equal" — the system must have a pressure valve. The mechanisms for breaking precedent (overruling, distinguishing, legislative override) exist precisely because blind consistency can perpetuate error. The question is always about the threshold: how bad must the old rule be before the costs of disruption are justified?
Two lawyers read the same opinion and extract completely different rationes decidendi. Is this a bug or a feature of the legal system?
It's both — and that's the point. The ambiguity is a bug from the perspective of predictability: if reasonable people can disagree about what a case stands for, how can anyone plan around it? But it's a feature from the perspective of adaptability: the flexibility to read rationes broadly or narrowly allows the law to evolve without constant legislative intervention. Each new case refines and clarifies the precedent, much like scientific replication gradually narrows the interpretation of an experimental result.
If a Supreme Court footnote can reshape constitutional law (as footnote 4 of Carolene Products did), what does that say about the hierarchy of legal authority?
It reveals that the formal hierarchy (holding > dictum > footnote) doesn't always match the practical hierarchy. A footnote from the highest court, if intellectually compelling and strategically placed, can have more real-world impact than the holding of a lower court. This suggests that legal authority is partly about institutional position (which court spoke) and partly about intellectual persuasiveness (what was actually said). The best legal arguments leverage both dimensions.
In an age of AI-generated legal research, where algorithms can find thousands of relevant precedents instantly, does the "pedigree" of a citation still matter?
More than ever. When AI can surface every tangentially relevant case, the lawyer's value shifts from finding precedent to curating it. A brief that cites 200 cases is not more persuasive than one that cites the 5 most authoritative ones. Pedigree — the court level, the citation frequency, the reputation of the authoring judge — becomes the filter that separates signal from noise. Paradoxically, the abundance of accessible precedent makes the art of selection more important, not less.
Can a court's decision to NOT decide (passive virtues) ever be more powerful than an actual ruling?
Absolutely. Consider a politically charged issue where any ruling would alienate half the population. By declining to decide — on standing grounds, ripeness, or the political question doctrine — the court preserves its legitimacy for future battles while allowing political processes to work. Alexander Bickel argued this was the Supreme Court's greatest power: the power to say "not yet." But there's a dark side: silence can entrench injustice. If the court refuses to rule on voting rights violations, the violations continue. The passive virtue becomes an active vice when delay serves the powerful at the expense of the powerless.
Chapter 5 — War Room Toolbox
Consistency Demand
Argue that like cases must be treated alike — the court must follow its own prior rulings unless material facts differ.
Ratio Extraction
Extract the binding principle from a case — then frame it broadly (to capture your case) or narrowly (to escape it).
Distinguishing
Identify material factual differences between the precedent and your case to argue it shouldn't apply.
Dictum Demotion
Relabel an unfavorable passage as obiter dictum — a non-binding aside rather than a controlling holding.
Precedent Breaking
When all else fails, argue that the prior decision was wrong and should be overruled — the nuclear option.
Exception Cataloging
List all the exceptions to a rule to show it's been hollowed out — or argue exceptions must be read narrowly.
Passive Virtues
Invoke jurisdictional doctrines to encourage the court not to decide — when silence serves your client.
Citation Pedigree
Build your argument on the highest-authority, most-cited precedents. Curate quality over quantity.
Specificity Framing
Cast the precedent as a narrow rule (easier to distinguish) or broad standard (harder to escape) depending on your goal.
