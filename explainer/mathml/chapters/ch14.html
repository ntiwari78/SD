Integration

Riemann integrals, the Fundamental Theorem, integration techniques, numerical methods, and the trapezoidal rule.

45 min read Part II · Calculus
Mental Model

Adding Up Infinitely Many Slices

If differentiation answers "how fast is this changing?", integration answers "how much has accumulated?" Imagine filling a swimming pool with a hose whose flow rate varies over time. The integral of the flow-rate function gives the total volume of water. In machine learning, integrals appear everywhere you see "total" or "average": the expected value of a random variable is an integral, every probability is an integral of a density, and the area under a ROC curve is literally an integral. Whenever you sum over something continuous, you are integrating.

Section 14.1

The Riemann Integral

The Riemann integral formalises the idea of "area under a curve" by partitioning the domain into thin rectangles and taking a limit as the rectangles become infinitely narrow.

Definition — Partition and Riemann Sum

A partition \(P\) of \([a, b]\) is a finite set of points \(a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b\). The mesh of \(P\) is \(\|P\| = \max_i (x_i - x_{i-1})\). For any choice of sample points \(c_i \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]\), the Riemann sum is:

\[S(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(c_i)\,(x_i - x_{i-1}).\]

Definition — Riemann Integral

The function \(f\) is Riemann integrable on \([a, b]\) if there exists \(I \in \mathbb{R}\) such that for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists \(\delta > 0\) with

\[|S(f, P) - I| < \varepsilon\]

for every partition \(P\) with \(\|P\| < \delta\) and every choice of sample points. We write \(I = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\).

Theorem — Continuous Functions Are Integrable

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\), then \(f\) is Riemann integrable on \([a, b]\).

This is a direct consequence of the fact that continuous functions on compact sets are uniformly continuous (Chapter 11). Uniform continuity ensures that the oscillation of \(f\) on each sub-interval can be made uniformly small, which forces Riemann sums to converge.

Properties of the Integral

Proposition — Basic Properties

Linearity: \(\int_a^b [\alpha f(x) + \beta g(x)]\,dx = \alpha\int_a^b f\,dx + \beta\int_a^b g\,dx\).

Additivity: \(\int_a^c f\,dx = \int_a^b f\,dx + \int_b^c f\,dx\) for \(a \leq b \leq c\).

Monotonicity: If \(f(x) \leq g(x)\) on \([a, b]\), then \(\int_a^b f\,dx \leq \int_a^b g\,dx\).

Triangle inequality: \(\left|\int_a^b f\,dx\right| \leq \int_a^b |f|\,dx\).

Section 14.2

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) is one of the most important results in all of mathematics. It establishes that differentiation and integration are inverse operations.

Theorem — FTC Part I

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and we define \(F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt\), then \(F\) is differentiable on \((a, b)\) and

\[F'(x) = f(x).\]

For \(h > 0\):

\[\frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h} = \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt.\]

By the Mean Value Theorem for integrals, there exists \(c_h \in [x, x+h]\) with \(\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt = f(c_h)\). As \(h \to 0^+\), \(c_h \to x\), and by continuity of \(f\), \(f(c_h) \to f(x)\). The same argument works for \(h \to 0^-\). \(\blacksquare\)

Theorem — FTC Part II (Newton–Leibniz Formula)

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and \(F\) is any antiderivative of \(f\) (i.e., \(F' = f\)), then

\[\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a).\]

This is what makes integration practical: instead of computing limits of Riemann sums, we find an antiderivative and evaluate it at the endpoints. The notation \(F(x)\Big|_a^b = F(b) - F(a)\) is standard.

Key Antiderivatives

\(\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C\) for \(n \neq -1\), \(\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C\), \(\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C\), \(\int \cos x\,dx = \sin x + C\), \(\int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x + C\). These are the "building blocks" — the integration techniques below show how to handle more complex expressions.

Section 14.3

Integration Techniques

Substitution (Change of Variables)

Theorem — Substitution Rule

If \(g\) is differentiable on \([a, b]\) with continuous derivative and \(f\) is continuous on the range of \(g\), then

\[\int_a^b f(g(x))\,g'(x)\,dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u)\,du.\]

Substitution "undoes" the chain rule. Whenever you see a composite function multiplied by the derivative of its inner function, substitution simplifies the integral. Set \(u = g(x)\), then \(du = g'(x)\,dx\).

Example — Substitution

Compute \(\int_0^1 2x \, e^{x^2}\,dx\). Let \(u = x^2\), so \(du = 2x\,dx\). When \(x=0\), \(u=0\); when \(x=1\), \(u=1\). The integral becomes \(\int_0^1 e^u\,du = e^1 - e^0 = e - 1\).

Integration by Parts

Theorem — Integration by Parts

If \(u\) and \(v\) are differentiable functions with continuous derivatives on \([a, b]\), then

\[\int_a^b u(x)\,v'(x)\,dx = \bigl[u(x)\,v(x)\bigr]_a^b - \int_a^b u'(x)\,v(x)\,dx.\]

Integration by parts is the inverse of the product rule. The mnemonic: choose \(u\) to be the function that simplifies when differentiated, and \(v'\) to be the function that is easy to integrate. The LIATE rule (Logarithmic, Inverse-trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential) suggests which function to choose as \(u\).

Example — Integration by Parts

Compute \(\int_0^1 x\,e^x\,dx\). Let \(u = x\) (so \(u' = 1\)) and \(v' = e^x\) (so \(v = e^x\)). Then \(\int_0^1 x\,e^x\,dx = [x\,e^x]_0^1 - \int_0^1 e^x\,dx = e - (e - 1) = 1\).

Section 14.4

Improper Integrals

Many integrals in probability and ML involve infinite domains or unbounded integrands. These require a limiting process.

Definition — Improper Integral (Infinite Domain)

\[\int_a^\infty f(x)\,dx = \lim_{R \to \infty} \int_a^R f(x)\,dx,\]

provided the limit exists. If it does, the integral converges; otherwise it diverges.

The Gaussian integral is the most important improper integral in probability and ML:

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}.\]

This result underpins the normal distribution. The density of \(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) is \(f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}\), and the normalisation constant \(\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\) comes precisely from this integral.

Proposition — p-Test for Improper Integrals

\(\displaystyle\int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^p}\,dx\) converges if and only if \(p > 1\). Compare with the p-series test for sums (Chapter 10) — the integral test connects the two.

Section 14.5

Numerical Integration

When no closed-form antiderivative exists — which is the common case in ML — we fall back on numerical methods (quadrature).

The Trapezoidal Rule

Instead of rectangles (Riemann sums), use trapezoids connecting consecutive function values:

\[\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \approx T_n = \frac{h}{2}\left[f(x_0) + 2\sum_{i=1}^{n-1} f(x_i) + f(x_n)\right],\]

where \(h = (b-a)/n\) and \(x_i = a + ih\). The error is \(O(h^2)\): \(|I - T_n| \leq \frac{(b-a)^3}{12n^2}\max|f''|\).

Simpson's Rule

Fit parabolas through consecutive triples of points for even better accuracy:

\[\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \approx S_n = \frac{h}{3}\left[f(x_0) + 4\sum_{\text{odd } i} f(x_i) + 2\sum_{\text{even } i} f(x_i) + f(x_n)\right],\]

where \(n\) must be even. The error is \(O(h^4)\): dramatically better than trapezoidal for smooth functions.

Performance Deep-Dive

Comparing Quadrature Methods

Implementation and error comparison of the three main quadrature rules.

import numpy as np

def trapezoidal(f, a, b, n):
    """Trapezoidal rule with n sub-intervals."""
    x = np.linspace(a, b, n + 1)
    y = f(x)
    h = (b - a) / n
    return h * (y[0]/2 + np.sum(y[1:-1]) + y[-1]/2)

def simpsons(f, a, b, n):
    """Simpson's rule (n must be even)."""
    assert n % 2 == 0
    x = np.linspace(a, b, n + 1)
    y = f(x)
    h = (b - a) / n
    return h/3 * (y[0] + 4*np.sum(y[1::2]) + 2*np.sum(y[2:-1:2]) + y[-1])

def midpoint(f, a, b, n):
    """Midpoint rule."""
    h = (b - a) / n
    mids = a + h * (np.arange(n) + 0.5)
    return h * np.sum(f(mids))

# Test: ∫₀¹ e^x dx = e - 1
f = np.exp
exact = np.e - 1

print(f"{'n':>6}  {'Midpoint err':>14}  {'Trapez. err':>14}  {'Simpson err':>14}")
for n in [4, 16, 64, 256, 1024]:
    n_even = n if n % 2 == 0 else n + 1
    err_m = abs(midpoint(f, 0, 1, n) - exact)
    err_t = abs(trapezoidal(f, 0, 1, n) - exact)
    err_s = abs(simpsons(f, 0, 1, n_even) - exact)
    print(f"{n:6d}  {err_m:14.2e}  {err_t:14.2e}  {err_s:14.2e}")
# Simpson's error shrinks as O(n⁻⁴) — much faster

Gaussian Quadrature

For maximum accuracy with \(n\) function evaluations, Gaussian quadrature chooses both the positions and weights optimally. An \(n\)-point Gauss–Legendre rule exactly integrates polynomials of degree up to \(2n - 1\). SciPy provides this via scipy.integrate.quad, which uses adaptive Gaussian quadrature internally.

Python
from scipy import integrate
import numpy as np

# scipy.integrate.quad — adaptive Gaussian quadrature
result, error = integrate.quad(np.exp, 0, 1)
print(f"∫₀¹ eˣ dx = {result:.15f} (error ≈ {error:.2e})")

# Gaussian integral: ∫₋∞^∞ e^{-x²} dx = √π
result2, error2 = integrate.quad(lambda x: np.exp(-x**2), -np.inf, np.inf)
print(f"∫₋∞^∞ e^(-x²) dx = {result2:.15f}")
print(f"√π               = {np.sqrt(np.pi):.15f}")
Section 14.6

Monte Carlo Integration

Deterministic quadrature rules (trapezoidal, Simpson's, Gauss) suffer from the curse of dimensionality: in \(d\) dimensions, a grid with \(n\) points per axis needs \(n^d\) evaluations. For the high-dimensional integrals in ML (expectations over latent variables, marginal likelihoods), Monte Carlo methods are essential.

Definition — Monte Carlo Estimator

To estimate \(I = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\), draw \(N\) samples \(x_1, \ldots, x_N\) uniformly from \([a, b]\). The Monte Carlo estimator is:

\[\hat{I}_N = \frac{b - a}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i).\]

By the Law of Large Numbers, \(\hat{I}_N \to I\) as \(N \to \infty\). The standard error is \(O(1/\sqrt{N})\), independent of dimension.

The \(O(1/\sqrt{N})\) rate seems slow, but crucially it does not depend on dimension. For a 100-dimensional integral, Monte Carlo needs the same order of samples as for a 1-dimensional integral — whereas a grid method would need \(n^{100}\) points.

Python
import numpy as np

def monte_carlo_integrate(f, a, b, N, d=1):
    """Monte Carlo integration over [a,b]^d."""
    samples = np.random.uniform(a, b, size=(N, d))
    values = np.array([f(s) for s in samples])
    volume = (b - a)**d
    estimate = volume * np.mean(values)
    std_err = volume * np.std(values) / np.sqrt(N)
    return estimate, std_err

# 1D: ∫₀¹ e^x dx = e - 1
est, se = monte_carlo_integrate(lambda x: np.exp(x[0]), 0, 1, N=100000, d=1)
print(f"MC estimate: {est:.6f} ± {se:.6f}  (exact: {np.e-1:.6f})")

# 5D: ∫_{[0,1]^5} (x₁² + x₂² + ... + x₅²) dx = 5/3
f_5d = lambda x: np.sum(x**2)
est5, se5 = monte_carlo_integrate(f_5d, 0, 1, N=100000, d=5)
print(f"5D MC estimate: {est5:.6f} ± {se5:.6f}  (exact: {5/3:.6f})")

Importance Sampling

Vanilla Monte Carlo can be inefficient if \(f\) has most of its mass in a small region. Importance sampling draws from a proposal distribution \(q(x)\) that concentrates samples where \(f\) is large:

\[\int f(x)\,dx = \int \frac{f(x)}{q(x)}\,q(x)\,dx \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{f(x_i)}{q(x_i)}, \quad x_i \sim q.\]

The optimal proposal is \(q^*(x) \propto |f(x)|\), which minimises variance. In practice, importance sampling is the foundation of techniques like variational inference and particle filtering in Bayesian ML.

Section 14.7

Integration in ML

Why This Matters for AI/ML

Integration Is Everywhere in Machine Learning

  • Probability densities: A continuous random variable has density \(f(x)\) where \(P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\). Every probability statement is an integral.
  • Expected values: \(\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int g(x)\,f(x)\,dx\). Loss functions, risk, bias-variance decompositions — all are expectations, hence integrals.
  • Marginalisation: Computing \(p(x) = \int p(x, z)\,dz\) (integrating out latent variables) is central to Bayesian inference, VAEs, and mixture models. These integrals are almost never tractable analytically.
  • Normalisation constants: The partition function \(Z = \int e^{-E(\mathbf{x})}\,d\mathbf{x}\) normalises energy-based models and Boltzmann machines. Computing or estimating \(Z\) is a core challenge in probabilistic ML.
  • AUC-ROC: The area under the ROC curve is literally \(\int_0^1 \text{TPR}(t)\,dt\), computed numerically via the trapezoidal rule in sklearn.metrics.auc.
  • Monte Carlo is the workhorse: Nearly every non-trivial integral in modern ML is estimated via Monte Carlo sampling. MCMC, variational inference, and reparameterisation tricks are all integration methods at their core.
Performance Deep-Dive

AUC-ROC via the Trapezoidal Rule

The industry-standard way to compute AUC uses the trapezoidal rule on discrete (FPR, TPR) points.

import numpy as np

def auc_trapezoidal(y_true, y_scores):
    """Compute AUC-ROC using the trapezoidal rule."""
    # Sort by descending score
    order = np.argsort(-y_scores)
    y_true = y_true[order]

    # Compute TPR and FPR at each threshold
    tps = np.cumsum(y_true)
    fps = np.cumsum(1 - y_true)
    tpr = tps / tps[-1]
    fpr = fps / fps[-1]

    # Prepend origin
    tpr = np.concatenate([[0], tpr])
    fpr = np.concatenate([[0], fpr])

    # Trapezoidal rule: AUC = ∫ TPR d(FPR)
    auc = np.trapz(tpr, fpr)
    return auc

# Example: simulate a classifier
np.random.seed(42)
y_true = np.random.randint(0, 2, 1000)
y_scores = y_true * 0.6 + np.random.randn(1000) * 0.3

auc = auc_trapezoidal(y_true, y_scores)
print(f"AUC-ROC = {auc:.4f}")

# Verify against sklearn
from sklearn.metrics import roc_auc_score
print(f"sklearn  = {roc_auc_score(y_true, y_scores):.4f}")

Socratic Knowledge Check

You are implementing a Bayesian model and need to compute the marginal likelihood \(p(\mathbf{x}) = \int p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})\,p(\mathbf{z})\,d\mathbf{z}\) where \(\mathbf{z}\) is a 50-dimensional latent variable. Your colleague asks: "Why can't we just use Simpson's rule?"
1
Compute \(\int_0^2 (3x^2 + 2x)\,dx\) using the FTC. Verify with the trapezoidal rule using \(n = 1000\).
Antiderivative: \(F(x) = x^3 + x^2\). So \(\int_0^2 (3x^2 + 2x)\,dx = F(2) - F(0) = (8 + 4) - 0 = 12\). Trapezoidal with \(n=1000\): \(h = 0.002\), and the sum evaluates to \(\approx 12.000004\), matching to 5 decimal places. The small error is \(O(h^2) = O(4 \times 10^{-6})\).
2
Why does the FTC Part I require \(f\) to be continuous? What goes wrong for a discontinuous function?
The proof uses the Mean Value Theorem for integrals, which requires continuity to guarantee \(c_h\) exists with \(f(c_h) = \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt\). For a discontinuous \(f\), the accumulation function \(F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt\) is still continuous (and even differentiable almost everywhere), but \(F'(x)\) may not equal \(f(x)\) at points of discontinuity. The step function \(f(t) = \mathbf{1}_{[0.5, 1]}\) has \(F(x) = \max(0, x - 0.5)\), which is continuous but not differentiable at \(x = 0.5\).
3
Simpson's rule has error \(O(n^{-4})\) while the trapezoidal rule has \(O(n^{-2})\). If you need 12 digits of accuracy for a 1D integral, roughly how many points does each need?
For error \(\leq 10^{-12}\): Trapezoidal needs \(n^{-2} \approx 10^{-12}\), so \(n \approx 10^6\) (one million points). Simpson's needs \(n^{-4} \approx 10^{-12}\), so \(n \approx 10^3\) (one thousand points). Simpson's wins by a factor of 1000 in computational cost for the same accuracy. This is why higher-order quadrature is preferred for smooth 1D integrands.
4
To your colleague's question: why can't we use Simpson's rule for a 50-dimensional integral? How many function evaluations would it require?
Simpson's rule with even a modest \(n = 10\) points per dimension in 50 dimensions requires \(10^{50}\) function evaluations — more than the number of atoms in the observable universe (\(\approx 10^{80}\)). This is the curse of dimensionality. Monte Carlo integration avoids this: its error \(O(1/\sqrt{N})\) does not depend on dimension. With \(N = 10^6\) samples, Monte Carlo gives \(\sim 10^{-3}\) relative error regardless of whether the integral is 1D or 50D. This is why MCMC, variational inference, and importance sampling are the tools of choice for Bayesian ML.
5
The normalisation constant for the Gaussian distribution involves \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2}\,dx\). Using the substitution \(u = x/\sqrt{2}\), relate this to the standard Gaussian integral \(\int e^{-u^2}\,du = \sqrt{\pi}\).
Let \(u = x/\sqrt{2}\), so \(x = \sqrt{2}\,u\) and \(dx = \sqrt{2}\,du\). Then \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2}\,dx = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-u^2}\,\sqrt{2}\,du = \sqrt{2}\sqrt{\pi} = \sqrt{2\pi}\). This is why the standard normal density is \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}\) — the factor \(1/\sqrt{2\pi}\) makes the density integrate to 1.
