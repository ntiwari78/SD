The Structure of Mathematics

Understanding the tools of proof: definitions, axioms, theorems, and the most powerful techniques for establishing mathematical truth.

45 min read
Foundation
The Big Picture

How mathematicians build certainty

Mathematics isn't a collection of facts to memorise — it's a logical structure built on foundations. Every theorem you've seen (Pythagorean theorem, laws of algebra, calculus theorems) was proven using the methods in this appendix. Understanding proof techniques is essential for understanding ML theory: convergence guarantees, correctness proofs for algorithms, and PAC learning theory all rely on these tools. This appendix teaches you the vocabulary and strategies mathematicians use to establish truth with absolute certainty.

Section B.1

Definitions and Axioms

Every mathematical structure begins with definitions and axioms. Definitions clarify what we're talking about. Axioms are foundational assumptions we don't prove — they're the starting points.

Definition — Well-Formed Definition

A good mathematical definition is:

  • Precise: No ambiguity. Every element either satisfies the definition or doesn't.
  • Non-circular: Doesn't use the term being defined in its own definition.
  • Complete: Covers all cases the term applies to, with necessary and sufficient conditions.
  • Consistent: Doesn't contradict existing definitions or axioms.

Axioms: The foundation we assume

Axioms are statements we accept without proof. They're not "obvious truths" — rather, they're the starting assumptions a mathematical theory is built on. Different axiom systems lead to different mathematics. For example:

Property — Common Axiom Systems
  • Peano axioms: Foundations of natural numbers (0, 1, 2, ...).
  • ZFC (Zermelo-Fraenkel-Choice): Set theory axioms, foundation of most modern mathematics.
  • Euclidean axioms: Geometry (including the parallel postulate). Drop the parallel postulate and you get non-Euclidean geometry.
  • Real number axioms: Field axioms, order axioms, and completeness axiom.

In practice, you rarely need to think about axioms — they're background. But recognising that mathematics rests on chosen foundations is important for understanding why certain results hold in some contexts but not others.

Section B.2

Theorems, Lemmas, and Corollaries

Once you have definitions and axioms, you can prove new statements. These come in a hierarchy:

Theorem

A significant, proven statement. Theorems are the "main results" — the facts mathematicians care about proving.

Lemma

A helper theorem. Lemmas are stepping stones toward larger theorems. They're proved first because they're useful for the proof of something more important.

Corollary

A consequence of a theorem. A corollary follows "easily" from an existing theorem without much additional work.

The distinction is about importance and dependency, not mathematical substance. A "lemma" could be profound; a "theorem" might be simple. The label reflects the role in the proof structure.

Anatomy of a theorem statement

Theorems have a standard form: If [assumptions], then [conclusion].

Example: "If \(f\) is differentiable on \([a,b]\) and continuous on \([a,b]\), then there exists \(c \in (a,b)\) such that \(f'(c) = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\)." This is the Mean Value Theorem. Assumptions: differentiable on the interval, continuous on the interval. Conclusion: specific point with the stated property exists.

A theorem is only as strong as its assumptions. Weaken them, and the conclusion might fail. Strengthen the conclusion, and you might need stronger assumptions. Understanding both is crucial.

Section B.3

Direct Proof

The most straightforward proof strategy: assume the hypothesis is true, then logically derive the conclusion.

Direct Proof Strategy

To prove "If \(P\), then \(Q\)":

  1. Assume \(P\) is true.
  2. Use definitions, axioms, and previously proven theorems to derive \(Q\).
  3. Conclude: \(P \implies Q\).

Example: Even and odd numbers

Definition — Even and Odd

An integer \(n\) is even if \(n = 2k\) for some integer \(k\). It is odd if \(n = 2k+1\) for some integer \(k\).

Theorem

If \(n\) is even, then \(n^2\) is even.

Proof: Assume \(n\) is even. By definition, \(n = 2k\) for some integer \(k\). Then \(n^2 = (2k)^2 = 4k^2 = 2(2k^2)\). Since \(2k^2\) is an integer, we can write \(n^2 = 2m\) where \(m = 2k^2\). By definition, \(n^2\) is even. \(\square\)

This proof is "direct": we assumed the hypothesis (n is even) and mechanically applied definitions to reach the conclusion (n² is even).

Section B.4

Proof by Contradiction

Sometimes assuming the conclusion is false and deriving a contradiction is easier than direct proof.

Proof by Contradiction Strategy

To prove "If \(P\), then \(Q\)":

  1. Assume \(P\) is true.
  2. Assume \(Q\) is false (assume the negation).
  3. Use definitions and theorems to derive a contradiction (something that's false).
  4. Conclude: \(Q\) cannot be false, so \(Q\) is true.

Classic example: Square root of 2 is irrational

Theorem

\(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational (cannot be written as \(\frac{a}{b}\) for integers \(a, b\)).

Proof by contradiction: Assume \(\sqrt{2}\) is rational. Then \(\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}\) where \(a, b\) are integers with \(\gcd(a,b) = 1\) (the fraction is in lowest terms). Squaring both sides: \(2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2}\), so \(a^2 = 2b^2\). This means \(a^2\) is even, so \(a\) is even (we can prove: if \(a\) is odd, then \(a^2\) is odd). Write \(a = 2k\). Then \((2k)^2 = 2b^2\), so \(4k^2 = 2b^2\), giving \(b^2 = 2k^2\). This means \(b^2\) is even, so \(b\) is even. But now both \(a\) and \(b\) are even, contradicting \(\gcd(a,b) = 1\). Therefore, our assumption was false: \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational. \(\square\)

This is one of the most famous proofs in mathematics. Notice: we couldn't directly construct the proof. Instead, assuming the opposite and finding a contradiction forced the truth of our claim.

Section B.5

Proof by Induction

Induction proves statements about all natural numbers by breaking it into a base case and a recursive step.

Mathematical Induction

To prove a statement \(P(n)\) is true for all positive integers \(n\):

  1. Base case: Prove \(P(1)\) (or \(P(0)\), depending on domain) is true.
  2. Inductive step: Prove that if \(P(k)\) is true for some \(k\), then \(P(k+1)\) is true.
  3. Conclusion: By induction, \(P(n)\) is true for all \(n \geq 1\).

Example: Sum of first n integers

Theorem

For all positive integers \(n\): \(\sum_{i=1}^{n} i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}\)

Proof by induction: Base case: For \(n = 1\): \(\sum_{i=1}^{1} i = 1\), and \(\frac{1(1+1)}{2} = 1\). Check. Inductive step: Assume \(\sum_{i=1}^{k} i = \frac{k(k+1)}{2}\) for some \(k \geq 1\) (this is the inductive hypothesis). We need to show \(\sum_{i=1}^{k+1} i = \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}\). \begin{align} \sum_{i=1}^{k+1} i &= \left(\sum_{i=1}^{k} i\right) + (k+1) \\ &= \frac{k(k+1)}{2} + (k+1) \quad \text{(by inductive hypothesis)} \\ &= \frac{k(k+1) + 2(k+1)}{2} \\ &= \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2} \end{align} By induction, the formula holds for all positive integers \(n\). \(\square\)

Strong induction

Sometimes you need to assume \(P(j)\) is true for all \(j \leq k\), not just \(P(k)\) alone. This is strong induction, and it's logically equivalent to regular induction but sometimes more convenient.

Implementation

Verifying induction with Python

# Verify the sum formula: 1 + 2 + ... + n = n(n+1)/2
def verify_induction_sum(n):
    """Check if the sum formula holds for 1, 2, ..., n."""
    for k in range(1, n+1):
        empirical_sum = sum(range(1, k+1))
        formula_value = k * (k + 1) // 2
        assert empirical_sum == formula_value, \
            f"Failed for k={k}: sum={empirical_sum}, formula={formula_value}"
    print(f"Sum formula verified for all k from 1 to {n}.")

verify_induction_sum(100)

# Verify divisibility by induction: 3 divides n³ - n for all n ≥ 1
def verify_divisibility(n):
    """Check if 3 divides n³ - n for all n."""
    for k in range(1, n+1):
        value = k**3 - k
        assert value % 3 == 0, \
            f"Failed for k={k}: {k}³ - {k} = {value}, not divisible by 3"
    print(f"Divisibility verified: 3 divides (n³ - n) for all n from 1 to {n}.")

verify_divisibility(100)

# Verify Fibonacci induction: F(n) < 2^n
def fib(n):
    if n <= 1:
        return n
    return fib(n-1) + fib(n-2)

def verify_fib_bound(n):
    """Check if F(n) < 2^n for all n."""
    for k in range(n+1):
        f_k = fib(k)
        bound = 2**k
        assert f_k < bound, f"Failed for k={k}: F({k})={f_k}, 2^{k}={bound}"
    print(f"Fibonacci bound verified: F(n) < 2^n for all n from 0 to {n}.")

verify_fib_bound(20)
Section B.6

Proof by Contraposition

Instead of proving "If \(P\), then \(Q\)", prove the logically equivalent statement: "If not \(Q\), then not \(P\)."

Contraposition

\(P \implies Q\) is logically equivalent to \(\neg Q \implies \neg P\)

(If \(P\) implies \(Q\), then \(Q\) not happening implies \(P\) didn't happen.)

Example: Contrapositive of evenness

Theorem

If \(n^2\) is even, then \(n\) is even.

Direct proof is hard: how do we go from "n² is even" to "n is even"? Instead, use contraposition:

Proof by contraposition: We prove the contrapositive: "If \(n\) is odd, then \(n^2\) is odd." Assume \(n\) is odd. Then \(n = 2k+1\) for some integer \(k\). \(n^2 = (2k+1)^2 = 4k^2 + 4k + 1 = 2(2k^2 + 2k) + 1 = 2m + 1\) where \(m = 2k^2 + 2k\). So \(n^2\) is odd. By contraposition, if \(n^2\) is even, then \(n\) must be even. \(\square\)

Contraposition is logically equivalent to the original statement — proving one proves the other. Choose whichever direction is easier to work with.

Section B.7

Proofs in Machine Learning

Why This Matters for ML

Proof techniques in practice

  • Convergence proofs: Gradient descent converges to a local minimum. These proofs use induction on iterations and contradiction arguments. You prove: if the gradient is large, the step decreases loss; by induction, the sequence eventually converges.
  • Correctness guarantees: Does an algorithm solve the problem it's designed for? Proofs verify termination (strong induction on problem size) and correctness (induction on problem decomposition).
  • PAC learning theory: Probably Approximately Correct learning bounds use concentration inequalities and proof by contradiction: assume the algorithm fails, derive that this violates probabilistic bounds, contradiction.
  • Complexity analysis: Why does quicksort average \(O(n \log n)\)? Induction on recursion depth and linearity of expectation from probability theory.
  • Formal verification: Proving neural networks never misclassify certain inputs. Uses constraint satisfaction, proof by contradiction, and sometimes induction on the number of layers.
Unifying Insight

Every ML guarantee — whether convergence, correctness, or sample complexity — is ultimately a proof. Direct proofs show algorithms compute what they should. Induction proves recursive algorithms work on all sizes. Contradiction proves algorithms don't fail under stated assumptions. Understanding the structure of proofs helps you read ML papers, debug convergence issues, and design new algorithms with theoretical guarantees.

🤔

Test Your Understanding

You're reading a paper on neural network convergence. The main theorem states: "Under assumptions A, B, and C, Algorithm X converges to an epsilon-optimal solution in at most T steps." The proof uses induction. You need to understand: Are the assumptions necessary? Is the bound tight? Which proof technique was the right choice?
1
The paper proves by induction that after k steps, the loss decreases by at least ε per step. Why is induction the right technique here? What would the base case and inductive step look like?
Induction is ideal because the statement depends on a natural number (number of steps). The base case proves loss decreases in step 1 (often by direct computation). The inductive step: assume loss decreases by ε in steps 1 through k; prove it also decreases in step k+1 (often using the fact that the algorithm's rule applies uniformly at each step). By induction, the property holds for all steps. This is natural for iterative algorithms.
2
Why are the assumptions A, B, C stated upfront? What goes wrong if you drop one of them?
Theorems are conditional: "If assumptions, then conclusion." Stating assumptions clearly is essential — it tells you when the result applies. If you drop assumption A (say, the loss is convex), the algorithm might not converge, or the bound might not hold. The proof relies on each assumption at a specific step. Missing one breaks the chain of logic. This is why practitioners read assumptions carefully: a convergence guarantee only applies if your problem satisfies all stated assumptions.
3
The paper proves by contradiction that the loss cannot remain above some threshold indefinitely. How does contradiction apply here? What's the negation you'd assume?
The statement is: "Loss eventually drops below the threshold." To prove by contradiction, assume the negation: "Loss stays above the threshold forever." Then the paper shows this contradicts the decrease property (loss decreases by ε per step; if it decreases indefinitely by ε, it cannot stay above a fixed threshold forever). So the assumption is false, and loss must drop. Contradiction is useful when direct proof is hard — sometimes showing something cannot happen is easier than directly showing what does happen.
4
If the convergence proof uses contraposition, what would the contrapositive of "If assumptions A, B, C, then convergence" be? When is contraposition cleaner than direct proof?
The contrapositive: "If the algorithm doesn't converge, then at least one of A, B, C is violated." Proving contraposition is cleaner when the negative (things failing) is easier to characterise than the positive (things succeeding). For instance, it might be easier to say "if convergence failed, the learning rate would be too large (violating assumption B)" than to directly show "under assumption B, convergence happens." Choose the direction where the logic flows most naturally.
5
How would you check if the paper's assumptions are realistic for your specific problem? What does it mean if one assumption doesn't hold?
Check if each assumption is satisfied in your setting (e.g., "Is the loss convex?", "Is the learning rate small enough?"). If all assumptions hold, the paper's guarantee applies — your algorithm will converge as promised. If one doesn't hold, the guarantee is void. The algorithm might still work (the bound might not be tight), but you can't rely on the proof. This is why practitioners validate assumptions empirically: run the algorithm, monitor convergence, and check if real behaviour matches theoretical predictions.
