The Geometric Structure of Vector Spaces

Norms, distances, inner products, and why geometry is the soul of linear algebra

~45 min read
6 code blocks
Intermediate
The Feynman Bridge — Mental Model

Vectors with a ruler, protractor, and similarity meter

Imagine you have a pile of arrows on a map (Chapter 1 taught us we can add and scale them). But now you want to measure them. How long is the arrow? How far apart are two arrows? What angle do they form? Which two arrows point almost the same direction?

Think of it like upgrading from a plain street map to Google Maps: instead of just knowing how to combine routes, you now have a distance ruler to measure how far apart two cities are, an angle protractor to see the direction difference, and a similarity tool that tells you if two routes are almost parallel. That's what norms (length), metrics (distance), and inner products (angle and similarity) do for vectors.

The beautiful part? All of this flows from one simple idea: the dot product — multiplying the components of two vectors and adding them up. Everything else in this chapter unfolds from that single operation. It's like a seed that grows into length, distance, angles, orthogonality, and the Gram-Schmidt algorithm that powers linear algebra libraries worldwide.

Section 2.1

Norms and Distances

Vectors represent data: images, sounds, text embeddings, sensor readings. To compare them, optimize models, and measure error, we need a notion of length and distance. Norms give us both.

Magnitude in the Euclidean Plane

In \(\mathbb{R}^2\), a vector \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2)\) has length given by the Pythagorean theorem:

$$|\mathbf{x}| = \sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2}$$

The distance between two vectors is just the length of their difference:

$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \sqrt{(x_1 - y_1)^2 + (x_2 - y_2)^2}$$

This generalizes to \(\mathbb{R}^n\). But are there other ways to measure distance? Absolutely.

Different Distance Metrics

Manhattan distance (taxicab distance) measures distance along grid lines:

$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = |x_1 - y_1| + |x_2 - y_2| + \cdots + |x_n - y_n|$$

In a city grid, it's how far you'd actually walk. In deep learning, L1 distance is used in robustness and adversarial examples.

Now let's formalize "measure of length" abstractly:

Definition 2.1.1
Norms

A function \(|\cdot| : V \to [0, \infty)\) is a norm if for all \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in V\) and \(c \in \mathbb{R}\):

  • Positive definiteness: \(|\mathbf{x}| \geq 0\) and \(|\mathbf{x}| = 0\) if and only if \(\mathbf{x} = 0\).
  • Positive homogeneity: \(|c\mathbf{x}| = |c| \cdot |\mathbf{x}|\).
  • Triangle inequality: \(|\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}| \leq |\mathbf{x}| + |\mathbf{y}|\).

The p-norm Family

The most important family is the p-norm:

$$|\mathbf{x}|_p = \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_i|^p\right)^{1/p}, \quad p \geq 1$$

L2 norm (Euclidean): \(p = 2\) gives the familiar Euclidean norm.

L1 norm (Manhattan): \(p = 1\) gives the Manhattan distance.

Infinity norm: Taking \(p \to \infty\) gives:

$$|\mathbf{x}|_\infty = \max\{|x_1|, |x_2|, \ldots, |x_n|\}$$

The infinity norm asks: "What's the largest component?" It's used in adversarial robustness and numerical stability.

Connection to Machine Learning

Mean Squared Error is just a scaled L2 norm:

$$\text{MSE}(\mathbf{y}, \hat{\mathbf{y}}) = \frac{1}{n}|\mathbf{y} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}|_2^2$$

Regularization penalizes large weights:

$$\text{Loss} = \text{MSE}(\mathbf{y}, \hat{\mathbf{y}}) + \lambda|\mathbf{w}|_p$$

L1 regularization (\(p=1\)) encourages sparsity (many zeros); L2 regularization (\(p=2\)) encourages small but non-zero weights.

Defining Distances from Norms

Every norm induces a metric (distance function):

$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) := |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}|$$

The distance satisfies key properties:

Definition 2.1.2
Metrics

A function \(d: V \times V \to [0, \infty)\) is a metric if:

  • \(d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}\)
  • \(d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = d(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x})\) (symmetry)
  • \(d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z}) \leq d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) + d(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z})\) (triangle inequality)

Norm-induced metrics have an extra property: translation invariance. Shifting both vectors by the same amount doesn't change distance:

$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = d(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{z}, \mathbf{y} + \mathbf{z})$$
Python
import numpy as np

# Create two sample vectors
x = np.array([3.0, 4.0])          # 2D vector: (3, 4)
y = np.array([1.0, 2.0])          # 2D vector: (1, 2)

# L2 norm (Euclidean): sqrt(3^2 + 4^2) = 5
l2_norm_x = np.linalg.norm(x, ord=2)    # 5.0

# L1 norm (Manhattan): |3| + |4| = 7
l1_norm_x = np.linalg.norm(x, ord=1)    # 7.0

# Infinity norm: max(|3|, |4|) = 4
linf_norm_x = np.linalg.norm(x, ord=np.inf)  # 4.0

# Distance between x and y using L2 metric
distance_l2 = np.linalg.norm(x - y, ord=2)   # sqrt(4+4) = 2.828...

# Distance using L1 metric (sum of absolute differences)
distance_l1 = np.linalg.norm(x - y, ord=1)   # |2| + |2| = 4

print(f"L2 norm of x: {l2_norm_x}")
print(f"L1 norm of x: {l1_norm_x}")
print(f"L∞ norm of x: {linf_norm_x}")
print(f"L2 distance: {distance_l2}")
print(f"L1 distance: {distance_l1}")
Section 2.2

Inner Products, Angles, and Why They Matter

Now we ask: given two vectors, what's the angle between them? Are they parallel? Perpendicular? This requires the dot product, or more generally, an inner product.

In \(\mathbb{R}^2\), the dot product is:

$$\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = x_1 y_1 + x_2 y_2$$

The angle \(\alpha\) between them satisfies:

$$\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = |\mathbf{x}| \cdot |\mathbf{y}| \cdot \cos(\alpha)$$

This is the key formula: inner product links geometry (angles) to algebra (multiplication).

Definition 2.2.1
Inner Products

An inner product is a function \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle : V \times V \to \mathbb{R}\) satisfying:

  • Linearity in the first argument: \(\langle a\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z} \rangle = a\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z} \rangle + \langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z} \rangle\)
  • Symmetry: \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x} \rangle\)
  • Positive definiteness: \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x} \rangle > 0\) for \(\mathbf{x} \neq 0\)

The Generated Norm

Every inner product gives rise to a norm via the square root of self-inner-product. But first, we need Cauchy-Schwarz:

Theorem 2.2.1
Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

For any vectors \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\) in an inner product space,

$$|\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle|^2 \leq \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x} \rangle \langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{y} \rangle$$

Proof. For any scalar \(\lambda\), the quadratic polynomial \(p(t) = \langle \mathbf{x} + t\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x} + t\mathbf{y} \rangle \geq 0\). Expanding:

$$p(t) = \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x} \rangle + 2t\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle + t^2\langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{y} \rangle$$

This quadratic is always non-negative, so its discriminant must be non-positive:

$$4\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle^2 - 4\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x} \rangle\langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{y} \rangle \leq 0$$

Dividing by 4 gives the result. ∎

Theorem 2.2.2
Norm Generated by Inner Product

The function \(|\mathbf{x}| := \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x} \rangle}\) is a norm.

Proof. Positive definiteness and homogeneity follow from the inner product. For the triangle inequality:

$$|\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}|^2 = \langle \mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y} \rangle = |\mathbf{x}|^2 + 2\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle + |\mathbf{y}|^2$$

By Cauchy-Schwarz, \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle \leq |\mathbf{x}||\mathbf{y}|\), so:

$$|\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}|^2 \leq |\mathbf{x}|^2 + 2|\mathbf{x}||\mathbf{y}| + |\mathbf{y}|^2 = (|\mathbf{x}| + |\mathbf{y}|)^2$$

Taking square roots: \(|\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}| \leq |\mathbf{x}| + |\mathbf{y}|\). ∎

Theorem 2.2.3
Polarization Identity

In an inner product space,

$$\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = \frac{1}{2}\left(|\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y}|^2 - |\mathbf{x}|^2 - |\mathbf{y}|^2\right)$$

Orthogonality

Definition 2.2.2
Orthogonality

Two vectors \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\) are orthogonal, written \(\mathbf{x} \perp \mathbf{y}\), if \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = 0\).

Theorem 2.2.4
Pythagorean Theorem (Inner Product Version)

If \(\mathbf{x} \perp \mathbf{y}\), then

$$|\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}|^2 = |\mathbf{x}|^2 + |\mathbf{y}|^2$$

Geometric Interpretation: Projections and Angles

Given \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\), we can decompose \(\mathbf{x}\) into a component parallel to \(\mathbf{y}\) and a component orthogonal to it:

$$\mathbf{x} = \underbrace{\text{proj}_\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{x})}_{\text{parallel}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{x}_\perp}_{\text{orthogonal}}$$

The orthogonal projection is:

$$\text{proj}_\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle}{\langle \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{y} \rangle}\mathbf{y}$$

This is the "shadow" of \(\mathbf{x}\) cast onto \(\mathbf{y}\). From the law of cosines and the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

$$\alpha = \arccos\left(\frac{\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle}{|\mathbf{x}||\mathbf{y}|}\right)$$

where \(\alpha\) is the angle between them. When \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle\) is large, they point in similar directions.

Cosine similarity normalizes the dot product:

$$\cos(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \left\langle \frac{\mathbf{x}}{|\mathbf{x}|}, \frac{\mathbf{y}}{|\mathbf{y}|} \right\rangle$$

This ranges from -1 (opposite) to +1 (identical), and is the backbone of recommendation systems and NLP.

Python
import numpy as np

# Two vectors in R^3
x = np.array([1.0, 2.0, 3.0])
y = np.array([4.0, 5.0, 6.0])

# Dot product (inner product in R^n)
dot_prod = np.dot(x, y)                # 1*4 + 2*5 + 3*6 = 32

# Norms (lengths)
norm_x = np.linalg.norm(x)             # sqrt(1 + 4 + 9) = 3.742...
norm_y = np.linalg.norm(y)             # sqrt(16 + 25 + 36) = 8.774...

# Angle between vectors: arccos(dot_product / (|x| * |y|))
cos_angle = dot_prod / (norm_x * norm_y)   # ~0.9746
angle_rad = np.arccos(cos_angle)           # ~0.2527 radians (~14.5 degrees)

# Cosine similarity (for normalized vectors)
cosine_sim = dot_prod / (norm_x * norm_y)  # Same as cos(angle)

# Check orthogonality: if dot product = 0, vectors are perpendicular
z = np.array([1.0, -2.0, 1.0])         # Different vector
dot_x_z = np.dot(x, z)                 # 1 - 4 + 3 = 0 → x ⊥ z!

print(f"Dot product x·y: {dot_prod}")
print(f"||x||: {norm_x:.3f}, ||y||: {norm_y:.3f}")
print(f"Angle (radians): {angle_rad:.4f}")
print(f"Cosine similarity: {cosine_sim:.4f}")
print(f"x · z = {dot_x_z} → x ⊥ z")

Orthogonal and Orthonormal Bases

Definition 2.2.3
Orthogonal and Orthonormal Bases

A basis \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n\}\) is orthogonal if \(\langle \mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j \rangle = 0\) for all \(i \neq j\). It is orthonormal if it is orthogonal and each \(\mathbf{v}_i\) has norm 1 (i.e., \(|\mathbf{v}_i| = 1\)).

Theorem 2.2.5

If \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n\}\) is an orthonormal basis, then for any \(\mathbf{x}\),

$$\mathbf{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{v}_i \rangle \mathbf{v}_i$$

In other words, the coefficients are just dot products with the basis vectors.

The Gram-Schmidt Orthogonalization Process

Given any linearly independent set \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n\}\), we can construct an orthonormal basis using Gram-Schmidt:

Theorem 2.2.6
Gram-Schmidt Orthogonalization

Algorithm: For each \(i = 1, \ldots, n\):

1. \(\mathbf{u}_i := \mathbf{v}_i - \sum_{j=1}^{i-1} \langle \mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{e}_j \rangle \mathbf{e}_j\)

2. \(\mathbf{e}_i := \mathbf{u}_i / |\mathbf{u}_i|\)

This produces an orthonormal basis \(\{\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_n\}\).

Python
import numpy as np

def gram_schmidt(vectors):
    """
    Apply Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization to a list of vectors.
    Input: list of linearly independent vectors (as NumPy arrays)
    Output: list of orthonormal vectors
    """
    orthonormal = []

    for v in vectors:
        # Step 1: Subtract projections onto all previous orthonormal vectors
        u = v.copy()  # Start with the original vector
        for e in orthonormal:
            # Subtract the projection of v onto e
            u = u - np.dot(v, e) * e

        # Step 2: Normalize to unit length
        norm_u = np.linalg.norm(u)
        if norm_u > 1e-10:  # Avoid division by zero
            e = u / norm_u
            orthonormal.append(e)

    return orthonormal

# Example: orthogonalize these three linearly independent vectors
v1 = np.array([1.0, 0.0, 0.0])
v2 = np.array([1.0, 1.0, 0.0])
v3 = np.array([1.0, 1.0, 1.0])

# Apply Gram-Schmidt
e1, e2, e3 = gram_schmidt([v1, v2, v3])

# Verify orthonormality: dot products should be 0 or 1
print("Orthonormality check:")
print(f"e1 · e1 = {np.dot(e1, e1):.6f} (should be 1)")
print(f"e1 · e2 = {np.dot(e1, e2):.6f} (should be 0)")
print(f"e2 · e3 = {np.dot(e2, e3):.6f} (should be 0)")
print(f"||e1|| = {np.linalg.norm(e1):.6f} (should be 1)")
print(f"||e2|| = {np.linalg.norm(e2):.6f} (should be 1)")
print(f"||e3|| = {np.linalg.norm(e3):.6f} (should be 1)")
Corollary 2.2.1

Every finite-dimensional inner product space has an orthonormal basis.

Remark: If the input vectors are linearly dependent, Gram-Schmidt produces zero vectors at some step. We simply skip those steps.

The Orthogonal Complement

Definition 2.2.4
Orthogonal Subspaces

Two subspaces \(S_1, S_2\) of \(V\) are orthogonal, written \(S_1 \perp S_2\), if \(\mathbf{x} \perp \mathbf{y}\) for all \(\mathbf{x} \in S_1, \mathbf{y} \in S_2\).

Definition 2.2.5
Orthogonal Complement

The orthogonal complement of a subspace \(S \subseteq V\) is

$$S^\perp := \{\mathbf{x} \in V : \mathbf{x} \perp \mathbf{s} \text{ for all } \mathbf{s} \in S\}$$
Theorem 2.2.7

\(S^\perp\) is a subspace of \(V\), and \(S \cap S^\perp = \{\mathbf{0}\}\).

Theorem 2.2.8
Direct Sum Decomposition

For any finite-dimensional subspace \(S\) of an inner product space \(V\),

$$V = S \oplus S^\perp$$

Every vector \(\mathbf{x} \in V\) can be uniquely written as \(\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{s} + \mathbf{s}^\perp\) where \(\mathbf{s} \in S\) and \(\mathbf{s}^\perp \in S^\perp\).

Section 2.3

Summary

Geometry is not a luxury—it is the foundation of linear algebra and machine learning. Norms measure length, metrics measure distance, and inner products reveal angles and similarity. All three are intertwined.

The Euclidean structure (L2 norm, dot product) is the most common in ML, but other norms (L1, infinity) have special roles. Orthogonality simplifies computation: orthonormal bases make coordinate representation trivial. Gram-Schmidt is the algorithm that turns any basis into an orthonormal one, and the orthogonal complement decomposes spaces into perpendicular subspaces.

These ideas scale: from 2D geometric intuition to infinite-dimensional function spaces, the principles remain unchanged. This is why geometry works.

Python
import numpy as np

def orthogonal_projection(x, y):
    """
    Project vector x onto vector y.
    Returns: proj_y(x) = ( / ) * y
    """
    coeff = np.dot(x, y) / np.dot(y, y)  # Scalar:  / ||y||^2
    return coeff * y

def orthogonal_component(x, y):
    """
    Get the component of x orthogonal to y.
    Returns: x - proj_y(x)
    """
    return x - orthogonal_projection(x, y)

# Example: project (3, 4) onto (1, 0)
x = np.array([3.0, 4.0])
y = np.array([1.0, 0.0])

proj = orthogonal_projection(x, y)      # (3, 0) — shadow on x-axis
orthog = orthogonal_component(x, y)     # (0, 4) — perpendicular part

print(f"x = {x}")
print(f"proj_y(x) = {proj} (parallel component)")
print(f"x - proj_y(x) = {orthog} (orthogonal component)")
print(f"Verification: proj + orthog = {proj + orthog} (should equal x)")
Why This Matters for AI

Geometry powers machine learning

  • Norms → Loss functions & Regularization: MSE is L2 norm squared; L1/L2 regularization controls overfitting by penalizing weight magnitude.
  • Inner products → Similarity: Cosine similarity finds similar documents, recommendations, and embeddings. Attention mechanisms in transformers use inner products to compute relevance.
  • Orthogonality → Feature Independence: PCA (Chapter 7) uses orthogonal projections to find independent directions of maximum variance. Decorrelated features improve model stability.
  • Gram-Schmidt → Numerical Stability: QR decomposition (built on Gram-Schmidt) is the foundation of least-squares solving, matrix inversion, and eigendecomposition in libraries like NumPy and PyTorch.
  • Orthogonal complement → Constraint handling: Projecting onto subspaces is how we enforce constraints, handle underdetermined systems, and design fair ML models.

Socratic Knowledge Check

You're building a recommendation engine. Documents are represented as TF-IDF vectors in a high-dimensional space.

The Scenario: You have a user query \(\mathbf{q}\) and 1 million documents \(\{\mathbf{d}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{d}_{10^6}\}\), each represented as a sparse TF-IDF vector in \(\mathbb{R}^{50000}\) (words in the vocabulary). You need to find the top-10 most similar documents to the query.
1
Would you use Euclidean distance \(|\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{d}_i|_2\) or cosine similarity \(\cos(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{d}_i)\) to rank documents? Why?
Cosine similarity. TF-IDF vectors have arbitrary scale (longer documents have larger norms). Euclidean distance would favor short documents. Cosine similarity is scale-invariant: it only cares about direction, not magnitude. Two documents with the same topic but different lengths should have high similarity.
2
If \(\langle \mathbf{d}_i, \mathbf{d}_j \rangle = 0\), what does that tell you about the two documents?
They are orthogonal — they share no common words (in the TF-IDF space). They represent completely different topics. Their cosine similarity is 0.
3
Your ranking algorithm uses a weighted combination: \(\text{score}_i = 0.7 \cdot \cos(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{d}_i) + 0.3 \cdot |\mathbf{d}_i|_\infty\). The infinity norm \(|\mathbf{d}_i|_\infty\) gives the most important term in the document. Why might using \(|\mathbf{d}_i|_\infty\) (max term weight) be better than \(|\mathbf{d}_i|_2\) (Euclidean norm)?
The infinity norm ignores document length and focuses on the strongest single signal. If a query matches the single most important word in a document, the \(L^\infty\) norm captures that. The Euclidean norm spreads credit across all words, so a long document with weak matches might score higher than a short document with one perfect match. It depends on your ranking strategy.
4
Suppose you decide to compress documents from 50,000 dimensions to 100 dimensions using PCA (Principal Component Analysis). At a high level, what does PCA do geometrically?
PCA finds an orthonormal basis of 100 vectors that capture the directions of maximum variance in your document collection. It projects each document onto this lower-dimensional subspace. High-variance directions capture important distinctions between documents; low-variance directions are noise. PCA discards the low-variance directions, keeping the high-signal ones.
5
BONUS: After PCA compression, the cosine similarities between documents should be approximately preserved (though not exactly). Why does projecting onto an orthonormal basis preserve angles and similarities?
Orthonormal bases preserve inner products (and hence angles and cosine similarities). If \(\{\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_k\}\) is orthonormal and \(\mathbf{x}' = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{e}_i \rangle \mathbf{e}_i\) (the projection), then the angle between two projected vectors is the same as the angle between their original shadows on the subspace. That's why Gram-Schmidt and QR decomposition preserve geometric relationships—orthonormal transformations are isometries.
