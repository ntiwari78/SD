Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

The characteristic polynomial, finding eigenvalue–eigenvector pairs, eigenspaces, and their geometric meaning

Part I — Linear Algebra ~40 min read
Mental Model

The "Stubborn Directions" of a Transformation

Imagine pushing a rubber sheet through a machine that stretches and shears it in complicated ways. Most points end up somewhere unexpected. But a few special directions survive the transformation unchanged — they may get longer or shorter, even flip, but they stay on the same line. These are the eigenvectors, and the scale factor along each direction is the eigenvalue. In ML, these stubborn directions reveal the intrinsic structure of your data: the principal components in PCA, the steady-state distribution of a Markov chain, and the spectral modes that graph neural networks rely on.

Section 6.1

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Definition

We begin with the fundamental definition that drives almost everything in spectral analysis.

Definition 6.1 — Eigenvalue and Eigenvector
Eigen-pair of a Square Matrix

Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\). A scalar \(\lambda \in \mathbb{C}\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) if there exists a non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{C}^{n}\), called the corresponding eigenvector, such that

$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}.$$

Equivalently, \(\mathbf{v}\) lies in the null space of \(A - \lambda I\):

$$(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}, \quad \mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}.$$

The requirement \(\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}\) is essential — the zero vector trivially satisfies \(A\mathbf{0} = \lambda \mathbf{0}\) for any \(\lambda\), so it carries no information. Notice also that if \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector, so is \(\alpha \mathbf{v}\) for every non-zero scalar \(\alpha\). Eigenvectors are therefore defined only up to a scalar multiple.

Geometric Intuition

When a matrix \(A\) acts on an eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\), it produces a vector that points in the same direction (or exactly opposite if \(\lambda < 0\)). The eigenvalue \(\lambda\) tells you by how much the vector is scaled: \(\lambda > 1\) means stretching, \(0 < \lambda < 1\) means compression, and \(\lambda = 0\) means collapse onto a lower-dimensional subspace.

The Characteristic Polynomial

How do we find eigenvalues? The equation \((A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) has a non-trivial solution if and only if the matrix \(A - \lambda I\) is singular, which is equivalent to requiring its determinant to be zero.

Theorem 6.1 — Characteristic Polynomial

The characteristic polynomial of \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is

$$p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I).$$

This is a polynomial of degree \(n\) in \(\lambda\). The eigenvalues of \(A\) are exactly the roots of \(p(\lambda) = 0\). By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, \(A\) has exactly \(n\) eigenvalues counted with multiplicity (possibly complex).

Proof. The equation \(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}\) can be rewritten as \((A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\). A non-trivial solution exists iff \(\ker(A - \lambda I) \neq \{\mathbf{0}\}\), which happens iff \(A - \lambda I\) is singular, i.e., \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\).

Each entry of \(A - \lambda I\) is either constant or linear in \(\lambda\) (the diagonal entries are \(a_{ii} - \lambda\)). The Leibniz formula for the determinant shows that \(\det(A - \lambda I)\) is a sum of products of \(n\) such entries, each product containing at most \(n\) factors of the form \((a_{ii} - \lambda)\). The unique term with the highest power of \(\lambda\) comes from the product of all diagonal entries:

$$\prod_{i=1}^{n}(a_{ii} - \lambda) = (-\lambda)^n + \cdots$$

so \(p(\lambda)\) is a polynomial of degree \(n\) with leading coefficient \((-1)^n\). By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, \(p\) has exactly \(n\) roots in \(\mathbb{C}\) (with multiplicity). \(\blacksquare\)

Definition 6.2 — Algebraic Multiplicity
Multiplicity of an Eigenvalue

The algebraic multiplicity \(\mu_A(\lambda)\) of an eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is its multiplicity as a root of the characteristic polynomial. If \(p(\lambda_0) = 0\) and \((\lambda - \lambda_0)^k\) divides \(p(\lambda)\) but \((\lambda - \lambda_0)^{k+1}\) does not, then \(\mu_A(\lambda_0) = k\).

For a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\), the characteristic polynomial is straightforward:

$$p(\lambda) = \det\begin{pmatrix} a - \lambda & b \\ c & d - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = \lambda^2 - (a + d)\lambda + (ad - bc) = \lambda^2 - \text{tr}(A)\lambda + \det(A).$$

This reveals an elegant connection: the sum of the eigenvalues equals the trace, and the product of the eigenvalues equals the determinant.

Proposition 6.1 — Trace-Determinant Relations

For \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\) (counted with algebraic multiplicity):

$$\text{tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i, \qquad \det(A) = \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda_i.$$

Proof. Since \(p(\lambda) = (-1)^n(\lambda - \lambda_1)(\lambda - \lambda_2)\cdots(\lambda - \lambda_n)\), expanding gives:

$$p(\lambda) = (-1)^n \lambda^n + (-1)^{n-1}(\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n)\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + \lambda_1\lambda_2\cdots\lambda_n.$$

Comparing the coefficient of \(\lambda^{n-1}\) with that from the Leibniz expansion yields \(\text{tr}(A) = \sum_i \lambda_i\). Setting \(\lambda = 0\) gives \(p(0) = \det(A) = \prod_i \lambda_i\). \(\blacksquare\)

Section 6.2

Computing Eigenvalues

Small Matrices by Hand

For \(2 \times 2\) matrices, the quadratic formula gives eigenvalues directly. Let's work through a concrete example.

Consider \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\). The characteristic polynomial is:

$$p(\lambda) = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2).$$

So \(\lambda_1 = 5\) and \(\lambda_2 = 2\). To find the eigenvectors, we solve \((A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) for each:

For \(\lambda_1 = 5\): \((A - 5I)\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 2 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\), giving \(v_1 = 2v_2\), so \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).

For \(\lambda_2 = 2\): \((A - 2I)\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\), giving \(v_1 = -v_2\), so \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).

For \(3 \times 3\) matrices we must factor a cubic, which is tedious but doable. For larger matrices, analytic approaches are impractical — this is where numerical algorithms take over.

NumPy in Practice

In practice we never compute eigenvalues by forming the characteristic polynomial and finding its roots. Root-finding for high-degree polynomials is notoriously ill-conditioned — Wilkinson's polynomial is the classic cautionary tale. Instead, modern libraries use iterative algorithms (the QR algorithm or divide-and-conquer methods) that work directly on the matrix. 

Python
import numpy as np

# Example: eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a 2x2 matrix
A = np.array([[4, 2],
              [1, 3]])

eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eig(A)

print("Eigenvalues:", eigenvalues)
print("Eigenvectors (columns):")
print(eigenvectors)
print()

# Verify: A @ v = lambda * v
for i in range(len(eigenvalues)):
    lam = eigenvalues[i]
    v = eigenvectors[:, i]
    residual = np.linalg.norm(A @ v - lam * v)
    print(f"λ_{i+1} = {lam:.4f}, residual = {residual:.2e}")
Important Caveat

np.linalg.eig returns eigenvectors as columns of the output matrix. The \(i\)-th eigenvector is eigenvectors[:, i], not eigenvectors[i]. This is a frequent source of bugs. Also, eigenvectors are normalised to unit length — they may differ from the "simplest" form you'd write by hand.

For real symmetric matrices, a specialised routine exists that is both faster and more numerically stable: 

Python
import numpy as np

# Symmetric matrix — always has real eigenvalues
S = np.array([[5, 2, 1],
              [2, 6, 3],
              [1, 3, 7]])

# eigh is for Hermitian (or real symmetric) matrices
eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eigh(S)

print("Eigenvalues (sorted ascending):", eigenvalues)
print()

# Verify orthogonality: Q^T Q = I
Q = eigenvectors
print("Q^T Q (should be identity):")
print(np.round(Q.T @ Q, 12))
print()

# Verify spectral decomposition: A = Q Λ Q^T
Lambda = np.diag(eigenvalues)
reconstructed = Q @ Lambda @ Q.T
print("Max reconstruction error:", np.max(np.abs(S - reconstructed)))
Performance Deep-Dive

eig vs eigh: When Symmetry Matters

For an \(n \times n\) symmetric matrix, eigh runs roughly 3–5× faster than eig and guarantees real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors. Under the hood, eigh uses a tridiagonal reduction followed by divide-and-conquer, while eig uses the full QR algorithm. Always use eigh when you know your matrix is symmetric — which is the case for covariance matrices, graph Laplacians, and kernel matrices in ML. 

Python
import numpy as np
import time

for n in [100, 500, 1000, 2000]:
    M = np.random.randn(n, n)
    S = M.T @ M  # symmetric positive definite

    t0 = time.perf_counter()
    np.linalg.eig(S)
    t_eig = time.perf_counter() - t0

    t0 = time.perf_counter()
    np.linalg.eigh(S)
    t_eigh = time.perf_counter() - t0

    print(f"n={n:4d}  eig: {t_eig:.4f}s  eigh: {t_eigh:.4f}s  "
          f"speedup: {t_eig / t_eigh:.1f}×")
Section 6.3

Eigenspaces and Diagonalisation

Eigenspaces and Geometric Multiplicity

Given an eigenvalue \(\lambda\), the set of all eigenvectors corresponding to \(\lambda\) (together with the zero vector) forms a subspace.

Definition 6.3 — Eigenspace
Eigenspace

The eigenspace associated with eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is the null space of \(A - \lambda I\):

$$E_\lambda = \ker(A - \lambda I) = \{\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n : A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}\}.$$

The dimension of this eigenspace, \(\dim(E_\lambda)\), is called the geometric multiplicity \(\mu_G(\lambda)\) of the eigenvalue.

Proposition 6.2 — Multiplicity Inequality

For every eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of a matrix \(A\):

$$1 \leq \mu_G(\lambda) \leq \mu_A(\lambda).$$

The geometric multiplicity is at least 1 (there's at least one eigenvector) and at most the algebraic multiplicity.

When \(\mu_G(\lambda) < \mu_A(\lambda)\), we say the eigenvalue is defective. This gap has real consequences: a defective matrix cannot be diagonalised.

Consider a classic defective example:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The characteristic polynomial is \(p(\lambda) = (\lambda - 2)^2\), so \(\lambda = 2\) has algebraic multiplicity 2. But \(A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\), whose null space is spanned by \(\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\) alone — geometric multiplicity 1. We are "missing" an eigenvector. 

Python
import numpy as np

# Defective matrix
A = np.array([[2, 1],
              [0, 2]])

eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eig(A)
print("Eigenvalues:", eigenvalues)
print("Eigenvectors (columns):")
print(eigenvectors)
print()

# Check if eigenvectors are linearly independent
det_V = np.linalg.det(eigenvectors)
print(f"det(V) = {det_V:.6f}")
print("Eigenvectors are", "independent" if abs(det_V) > 1e-10 else "DEPENDENT (defective)")
print()

# Rank of eigenspace for lambda = 2
null_dim = 2 - np.linalg.matrix_rank(A - 2 * np.eye(2))
print(f"Geometric multiplicity of λ=2: {null_dim}")
print(f"Algebraic multiplicity of λ=2: 2")

Diagonalisation

The eigenvector decomposition is one of the most powerful tools in linear algebra. If a matrix has "enough" linearly independent eigenvectors, we can decompose it into a simple diagonal form.

Theorem 6.2 — Diagonalisation

An \(n \times n\) matrix \(A\) is diagonalisable if and only if it has \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors. In this case,

$$A = P \Lambda P^{-1}$$

where \(P = [\mathbf{v}_1 \mid \mathbf{v}_2 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{v}_n]\) is the matrix whose columns are the eigenvectors, and \(\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)\).

Proof. If \(A\mathbf{v}_i = \lambda_i \mathbf{v}_i\) for \(i = 1, \ldots, n\) and the \(\mathbf{v}_i\) are linearly independent, then \(P\) is invertible and:

$$AP = A[\mathbf{v}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{v}_n] = [\lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \lambda_n\mathbf{v}_n] = P\Lambda.$$

Multiplying on the right by \(P^{-1}\) gives \(A = P\Lambda P^{-1}\).

Conversely, if \(A = P\Lambda P^{-1}\) with \(P\) invertible and \(\Lambda\) diagonal, then \(AP = P\Lambda\), so the columns of \(P\) are eigenvectors and the diagonal entries of \(\Lambda\) are the eigenvalues. \(\blacksquare\)

The practical power of diagonalisation is that it makes matrix powers trivial: \(A^k = P\Lambda^k P^{-1}\), and \(\Lambda^k = \text{diag}(\lambda_1^k, \ldots, \lambda_n^k)\). This is what makes eigendecomposition essential for Markov chains, recurrence relations, and dynamical systems. 

Python
import numpy as np

A = np.array([[4, 2],
              [1, 3]])

eigenvalues, P = np.linalg.eig(A)
Lambda = np.diag(eigenvalues)
P_inv = np.linalg.inv(P)

# Verify decomposition
print("A = P Λ P⁻¹ ?")
print("Max error:", np.max(np.abs(A - P @ Lambda @ P_inv)))
print()

# Matrix powers via eigendecomposition
k = 10
A_k_direct = np.linalg.matrix_power(A, k)
A_k_eigen = P @ np.diag(eigenvalues**k) @ P_inv

print(f"A^{k} via direct multiplication:")
print(np.round(A_k_direct, 2))
print(f"\nA^{k} via eigendecomposition:")
print(np.round(A_k_eigen.real, 2))
print(f"\nMax difference: {np.max(np.abs(A_k_direct - A_k_eigen.real)):.2e}")
Sufficient Condition

A matrix with \(n\) distinct eigenvalues is always diagonalisable (distinct eigenvalues produce linearly independent eigenvectors). But the converse is false: the \(n \times n\) identity matrix has only the eigenvalue 1 (with multiplicity \(n\)), yet it is trivially diagonalisable.

Section 6.4

Special Eigenvalue Properties

Certain classes of matrices have eigenvalue structures that are especially nice. These show up constantly in ML.

Theorem 6.3 — Spectral Theorem (Real Symmetric Case)

If \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is symmetric (\(A = A^\top\)), then:

(a) All eigenvalues of \(A\) are real.

(b) Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.

(c) \(A\) is always diagonalisable, and there exists an orthogonal matrix \(Q\) such that \(A = Q\Lambda Q^\top\).

Proof of (a). Let \(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}\) with \(\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}\). Taking the conjugate transpose of both sides:

$$\bar{\mathbf{v}}^\top A^\top = \bar{\lambda}\,\bar{\mathbf{v}}^\top.$$

Since \(A = A^\top\), multiply on the right by \(\mathbf{v}\):

$$\bar{\mathbf{v}}^\top A \mathbf{v} = \bar{\lambda}\,\bar{\mathbf{v}}^\top \mathbf{v}.$$

But also \(\bar{\mathbf{v}}^\top A \mathbf{v} = \bar{\mathbf{v}}^\top (\lambda \mathbf{v}) = \lambda\,\bar{\mathbf{v}}^\top \mathbf{v}\). Since \(\bar{\mathbf{v}}^\top \mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 > 0\), we get \(\lambda = \bar{\lambda}\), so \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\).

Proof of (b). If \(A\mathbf{v}_1 = \lambda_1 \mathbf{v}_1\) and \(A\mathbf{v}_2 = \lambda_2 \mathbf{v}_2\) with \(\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2\), then:

$$\lambda_1 \mathbf{v}_1^\top \mathbf{v}_2 = (A\mathbf{v}_1)^\top \mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{v}_1^\top A^\top \mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{v}_1^\top A \mathbf{v}_2 = \lambda_2 \mathbf{v}_1^\top \mathbf{v}_2.$$

So \((\lambda_1 - \lambda_2)\mathbf{v}_1^\top \mathbf{v}_2 = 0\). Since \(\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2\), we have \(\mathbf{v}_1^\top \mathbf{v}_2 = 0\). \(\blacksquare\)

The Spectral Theorem is arguably the single most important result for machine learning mathematics. PCA, spectral clustering, kernel methods, and graph neural networks all depend on it.

Proposition 6.3 — Eigenvalues of Special Matrices

Several matrix types encountered in ML have constrained eigenvalue structures:

  • Positive definite: all eigenvalues are strictly positive (\(\lambda_i > 0\)).
  • Positive semi-definite: all eigenvalues are non-negative (\(\lambda_i \geq 0\)).
  • Orthogonal matrices: all eigenvalues have \(|\lambda_i| = 1\).
  • Idempotent (\(A^2 = A\)): eigenvalues are either 0 or 1.
  • Nilpotent (\(A^k = 0\) for some \(k\)): all eigenvalues are 0.

The relationship between eigenvalues and matrix invertibility is fundamental:

Theorem 6.4 — Invertibility via Eigenvalues

A square matrix \(A\) is invertible if and only if \(0\) is not an eigenvalue. Moreover, if \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) with eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\), then \(\lambda^{-1}\) is an eigenvalue of \(A^{-1}\) with the same eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\).

Let's verify these properties computationally:

Python
import numpy as np

np.random.seed(42)

# --- Positive definite matrix ---
M = np.random.randn(4, 4)
S = M.T @ M  # S = M^T M is positive semi-definite; add εI for definite
S += 0.1 * np.eye(4)
eigs_S = np.linalg.eigvalsh(S)
print("Positive definite eigenvalues:", np.round(eigs_S, 4))
print("All positive?", np.all(eigs_S > 0))
print()

# --- Orthogonal matrix ---
Q, _ = np.linalg.qr(np.random.randn(4, 4))
eigs_Q = np.linalg.eigvals(Q)
print("Orthogonal matrix eigenvalues:", np.round(eigs_Q, 4))
print("|λ| values:", np.round(np.abs(eigs_Q), 10))
print()

# --- Idempotent (projection) matrix ---
v = np.array([1, 2, 3, 4], dtype=float)
v /= np.linalg.norm(v)
P = np.outer(v, v)  # rank-1 projection
eigs_P = np.linalg.eigvalsh(P)
print("Projection matrix eigenvalues:", np.round(eigs_P, 10))
print("All 0 or 1?", np.allclose(eigs_P * (1 - eigs_P), 0))
print()

# --- Eigenvalues of inverse ---
A = np.random.randn(3, 3) + 3 * np.eye(3)  # shift to ensure invertibility
eigs_A = np.linalg.eigvals(A)
eigs_Ainv = np.linalg.eigvals(np.linalg.inv(A))
print("λ(A):", np.sort(np.round(eigs_A, 6)))
print("λ(A⁻¹):", np.sort(np.round(eigs_Ainv, 6)))
print("1/λ(A):", np.sort(np.round(1 / eigs_A, 6)))

One last eigenvalue identity that proves essential for matrix calculus and optimization:

Proposition 6.4 — Eigenvalues under Shift and Scale

If \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\), then:

  • \(\alpha\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(\alpha A\) for any scalar \(\alpha\).
  • \(\lambda + \alpha\) is an eigenvalue of \(A + \alpha I\).
  • \(\lambda^k\) is an eigenvalue of \(A^k\) for any positive integer \(k\).

All three share the same eigenvector as the original pair \((\lambda, \mathbf{v})\).

The shift property \(A + \alpha I\) is the mathematical foundation of ridge regularization. Adding \(\alpha I\) to \(X^\top X\) shifts every eigenvalue by \(\alpha\), ensuring the matrix is invertible even when \(X^\top X\) is singular or near-singular.

Section 6.5

Summary

This chapter introduced the eigen-decomposition — the factorisation of a matrix into its fundamental directions and scale factors. We defined eigenvalues via the characteristic polynomial, distinguished algebraic from geometric multiplicity, and showed when and how a matrix can be diagonalised. The Spectral Theorem for symmetric matrices ensures that the most important matrices in ML (covariance matrices, kernels, graph Laplacians) always have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.

Why This Matters for AI

Eigenvalues Are Everywhere in Machine Learning

  • PCA (Principal Component Analysis) — finds the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix to identify the directions of maximum variance. The eigenvalues quantify the explained variance along each component.
  • Spectral clustering — embeds data points using eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian, then clusters in this lower-dimensional space. The smallest eigenvalues reveal the connected components.
  • Markov chains and PageRank — the stationary distribution is the eigenvector of the transition matrix corresponding to eigenvalue 1. Convergence speed is governed by the second-largest eigenvalue.
  • Ridge regression — regularization adds \(\alpha I\) to the Gram matrix, shifting all eigenvalues by \(\alpha\). This controls ill-conditioning and prevents overfitting.
  • Condition numbers — the ratio \(\lambda_{\max} / \lambda_{\min}\) of a positive definite matrix determines how sensitive the solution to \(Ax = b\) is to perturbations, and how fast gradient descent converges.

Socratic Knowledge Check

You are building a recommendation system. The user-item interaction matrix is large and sparse, and you plan to use spectral methods (eigendecomposition) to extract latent factors. Think through these questions to solidify your understanding.
1
Your covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) has eigenvalues \(\{50, 12, 3, 0.5, 0.01, 0.001\}\). How many principal components would you keep, and what fraction of total variance do they explain?
The total variance is the sum of all eigenvalues: \(50 + 12 + 3 + 0.5 + 0.01 + 0.001 = 65.511\). The top 3 components capture \((50 + 12 + 3)/65.511 \approx 99.2\%\) of the variance, which is well above the conventional 95% threshold. Keeping 3 out of 6 components reduces dimensionality by half while preserving essentially all the signal. The remaining eigenvalues (0.5, 0.01, 0.001) represent noise.
2
The Gram matrix \(X^\top X\) has a condition number of \(10^{12}\). What does this tell you about fitting a linear regression, and how would you fix it?
A condition number of \(10^{12}\) means \(\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min} = 10^{12}\), so the smallest eigenvalue is about \(10^{12}\) times smaller than the largest. This makes the normal equations \(X^\top X \boldsymbol{\beta} = X^\top \mathbf{y}\) catastrophically ill-conditioned: small perturbations in the data will cause huge swings in the solution. Ridge regression fixes this by adding \(\alpha I\), changing the condition number to \((\lambda_{\max} + \alpha)/(\lambda_{\min} + \alpha)\). Even a small \(\alpha\) (say \(10^{-4}\)) can dramatically improve conditioning.
3
You're told a matrix is symmetric and all its eigenvalues are non-negative. Can you guarantee it's a valid covariance matrix? What additional condition (if any) would you check?
A covariance matrix must be symmetric and positive semi-definite, which is exactly what "symmetric with non-negative eigenvalues" means. So yes, mathematically it could serve as a covariance matrix. In practice you'd also check that the diagonal entries are non-negative (they represent variances) and that the matrix is consistent with observed data. Numerical issues can create tiny negative eigenvalues in computed covariance matrices — in that case, you can project onto the positive semi-definite cone by clamping negative eigenvalues to zero.
4
A graph Laplacian \(L\) of a network has eigenvalues \(\{0, 0, 0.3, 1.2, 2.5, 3.8\}\). What can you infer about the graph's structure from the multiplicity of the zero eigenvalue?
The number of zero eigenvalues of the graph Laplacian equals the number of connected components. With two zero eigenvalues, the graph has exactly 2 connected components. The corresponding eigenvectors are indicator vectors for each component. The smallest non-zero eigenvalue (0.3, called the Fiedler value or algebraic connectivity) measures how well-connected the graph is — a small Fiedler value suggests a near-disconnection or "bottleneck" in the graph, which spectral clustering exploits to find communities.
5
Why does np.linalg.eigh return eigenvalues in ascending order, while np.linalg.eig returns them in no particular order? When does the ordering matter for your ML pipeline?
eigh uses a specialised algorithm (tridiagonal reduction + divide-and-conquer) that naturally produces eigenvalues in sorted order because it exploits the symmetric structure. eig uses the general QR algorithm, which converges to eigenvalues in an order determined by their magnitude and the matrix structure, not by value. Ordering matters in PCA (you want the largest eigenvalues first), spectral clustering (you want the smallest non-zero eigenvalues), and truncated eigendecompositions. With eigh you can simply slice from the end for PCA: eigvecs[:, -k:].
