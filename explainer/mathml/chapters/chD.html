D

Complex Numbers

Extending the real numbers to solve polynomial equations and unlock rotations, oscillations, and the elegance of Euler's formula — essential tools that appear throughout machine learning.

45 min read
Appendix
The Big Picture

When real numbers are not enough

The equation \(x^2 + 1 = 0\) has no real solution. For centuries, mathematicians considered this a problem — until they realised these "imaginary" numbers were hiding deep truths about rotations, oscillations, and the structure of linear systems. Complex numbers aren't just mathematical curiosities: they're intrinsic to Fourier analysis (signal processing), eigenvalues of real matrices that encode rotations, positional encodings in transformers, and even complex-valued neural networks. This appendix builds the foundations: from the definition of the imaginary unit through arithmetic operations, polar form, and their role in machine learning.

Section D.1

The Imaginary Unit

We begin with a simple question: what is the square root of \(-1\)?

In the real numbers, no number squared equals \(-1\). But if we extend our number system to allow a solution, we can define:

Definition — The Imaginary Unit

The imaginary unit \(i\) is defined by the property:

\[i^2 = -1\]

This solves the equation \(x^2 + 1 = 0\), which has no real solutions. By introducing \(i\), we gain the ability to express all polynomial equations' solutions.

Why introduce imaginary numbers?

This seems like a artificial extension, but it unlocks profound mathematics:

  • Fundamental Theorem of Algebra: Every polynomial of degree \(n\) has exactly \(n\) solutions (counting multiplicity) in the complex numbers. In the reals, polynomials can have fewer solutions.
  • Rotations and oscillations: Complex numbers encode rotations naturally. Multiplying by \(i\) rotates by \(90°\).
  • Fourier analysis: The Fourier transform, essential in signal processing and deep learning, fundamentally relies on complex exponentials.

Key insight: The "imaginary" unit isn't less real than \(\pi\) or \(\sqrt{2}\) — it's simply a different dimension we add to our number system. Calling it "imaginary" is historical baggage.

Section D.2

Complex Numbers

A complex number is a combination of a real part and an imaginary part.

Definition — Complex Number (Rectangular Form)

A complex number \(z\) is written as:

\[z = a + bi\]

where \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(i\) is the imaginary unit. We call:

  • \(\text{Re}(z) = a\) the real part
  • \(\text{Im}(z) = b\) the imaginary part

Note: \(b\) itself is real; the "imaginary" part refers to the coefficient of \(i\).

The complex plane (Argand diagram)

Just as real numbers live on a line, complex numbers live in a 2D plane:

  • Horizontal axis: real part \(a\)
  • Vertical axis: imaginary part \(b\)

The complex number \(z = 3 + 2i\) is plotted as the point \((3, 2)\) in the complex plane. This geometric representation reveals why complex numbers encode rotations.

Conjugate and Modulus

Definition — Conjugate and Modulus

For a complex number \(z = a + bi\):

  • Conjugate: \(\bar{z} = a - bi\) (flip the sign of the imaginary part)
  • Modulus (absolute value): \(|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\) (distance from origin)

Key property: \(z \cdot \bar{z} = |z|^2 = a^2 + b^2\)

Section D.3

Arithmetic Operations

Complex numbers follow familiar arithmetic rules, with the key constraint: \(i^2 = -1\).

Addition and subtraction

\[(a + bi) + (c + di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i\] \[(a + bi) - (c + di) = (a-c) + (b-d)i\]

Example: \((3 + 2i) + (1 - i) = 4 + i\)

Multiplication

Use FOIL (First, Outer, Inner, Last) and remember \(i^2 = -1\):

\[(a + bi)(c + di) = ac + adi + bci + bd i^2 = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i\]

Example: \((2 + 3i)(1 + 2i) = 2 + 4i + 3i + 6i^2 = 2 + 7i - 6 = -4 + 7i\)

Division

To divide by a complex number, multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate:

\[\frac{a+bi}{c+di} = \frac{(a+bi)(c-di)}{(c+di)(c-di)} = \frac{(ac+bd) + (bc-ad)i}{c^2+d^2}\]

Example: \(\frac{1+i}{1-i} = \frac{(1+i)(1+i)}{(1-i)(1+i)} = \frac{1 + 2i - 1}{1 - (-1)} = \frac{2i}{2} = i\)

Implementation

Complex arithmetic in Python

import cmath

# Define complex numbers (Python uses 'j' for the imaginary unit)
z1 = 3 + 2j
z2 = 1 - 1j

# Arithmetic operations
print(z1 + z2)  # (4+1j)
print(z1 * z2)  # (5+(-1j))
print(z1 / z2)  # (1+2j)

# Properties
print(z1.real, z1.imag)  # 3.0 2.0
print(abs(z1))           # 3.605... (modulus)
print(z1.conjugate())    # (3-2j)

# Using cmath for advanced operations
print(cmath.sqrt(-1))    # 1j
print(cmath.exp(1j * cmath.pi))  # ≈ -1 (Euler's formula!)
Section D.4

Polar Form and Euler's Formula

While rectangular form \(a + bi\) is convenient for addition, multiplying complex numbers becomes elegant in polar form.

Definition — Polar Form

A complex number can be written as:

\[z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) = r e^{i\theta}\]

where:

  • \(r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\) is the modulus (distance from origin)
  • \(\theta = \arg(z)\) is the argument (angle from the positive real axis)

The conversion: \(a = r\cos\theta\) and \(b = r\sin\theta\)

Euler's formula

The most beautiful equation in mathematics:

Theorem — Euler's Formula
\[e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\]

Consequence: \(e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0\) (Euler's identity), which elegantly connects \(e\), \(i\), \(\pi\), \(1\), and \(0\).

Why polar form?

Multiplication becomes trivial in polar form: multiply the moduli and add the arguments.

\[z_1 z_2 = r_1 e^{i\theta_1} \cdot r_2 e^{i\theta_2} = r_1 r_2 e^{i(\theta_1 + \theta_2)}\]

Geometric insight: Multiplying by \(e^{i\theta}\) rotates by angle \(\theta\). This is why complex numbers naturally encode rotations.

Implementation

Euler's formula verification and polar form

import cmath
import numpy as np

# Euler's formula: e^(iθ) = cos(θ) + i*sin(θ)
theta = np.pi / 4  # 45 degrees

# Left side: exponential form
euler_exp = cmath.exp(1j * theta)
print(f"e^(iπ/4) = {euler_exp}")

# Right side: trig form
euler_trig = np.cos(theta) + 1j * np.sin(theta)
print(f"cos(π/4) + i*sin(π/4) = {euler_trig}")
print(f"Are they equal? {np.allclose(euler_exp, euler_trig)}")

# Convert rectangular to polar
z = 3 + 4j
r = abs(z)
theta = cmath.phase(z)  # angle in radians
print(f"\nz = {z}")
print(f"Polar: r = {r:.3f}, θ = {theta:.3f} rad")

# Convert back
z_recovered = r * cmath.exp(1j * theta)
print(f"Recovered: {z_recovered}")

# Rotation: multiply by e^(iπ/2) to rotate 90 degrees
z_rotated = z * cmath.exp(1j * np.pi / 2)
print(f"\n{z} rotated by 90° = {z_rotated}")  # Should be -4 + 3j
Section D.5

Complex Numbers in Linear Algebra

Complex numbers naturally appear in linear algebra, especially through eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

Complex eigenvalues

A real matrix can have complex eigenvalues. For example, a rotation matrix:

\[\text{Rotation by } \theta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}\]

This matrix has eigenvalues \(\lambda = e^{i\theta}\) and \(\lambda = e^{-i\theta}\), which are complex conjugates. The eigenvalues encode the rotation angle.

Spectral theorem for symmetric matrices

Theorem — Real Symmetric Matrices Have Real Eigenvalues

If \(A\) is a real symmetric matrix (i.e., \(A^T = A\)), then all its eigenvalues are real. Even though we work in complex numbers, symmetry guarantees real eigenvalues, and eigenvectors can be chosen to be real.

Importance for ML: Covariance matrices are symmetric, so their eigenvalues are always real. This is why PCA and spectral clustering work on covariance matrices — we avoid complex eigenvalues.

Section D.6

Complex Numbers in Machine Learning

Complex numbers are not decorative in ML — they solve real problems in signal processing, positional encodings, and system analysis.

Why This Matters for ML

Five critical applications

  • Fourier Transforms: The FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) decomposes signals into complex frequency components. Used in audio processing, spectrogram computation, and convolutional architectures that operate on frequency domain.
  • Complex-Valued Neural Networks: Some architectures (e.g., PhaseNet) operate directly on complex numbers, gaining expressive power for phase-sensitive tasks like signal recovery and quantum-inspired learning.
  • Eigenvalue Analysis: Graph neural networks use eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix (often complex) for spectral graph convolution. Complex eigenvalues reveal the rotational dynamics of the graph.
  • Positional Encodings in Transformers: The sinusoidal positional encodings \(\text{PE}(pos, 2i) = \sin(pos / 10000^{2i/d_{model}})\) and \(\text{PE}(pos, 2i+1) = \cos(\ldots)\) come directly from Euler's formula: \(e^{i\cdot pos/10000^{2i/d}} = \cos(\ldots) + i\sin(\ldots)\). This connection reveals why transformers can represent relative positions.
  • Control Theory & RNNs: Eigenvalues of the recurrence matrix determine stability and learning dynamics. Complex eigenvalues with modulus 1 (on the unit circle) enable long-range dependencies without exploding/vanishing gradients.

Fourier Transform example

Implementation

FFT and frequency analysis

import numpy as np
from scipy.fft import fft, fftfreq
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Create a signal: combination of two sine waves
t = np.linspace(0, 1, 256)
signal = np.sin(2 * np.pi * 5 * t) + 0.5 * np.sin(2 * np.pi * 20 * t)

# Compute FFT (decompose into complex frequency components)
fft_result = fft(signal)
frequencies = fftfreq(len(signal), t[1] - t[0])

# The FFT result is complex: magnitude tells intensity, phase tells shift
magnitude = np.abs(fft_result)
phase = np.angle(fft_result)

print(f"FFT result (first 5): {fft_result[:5]}")
print(f"Magnitudes at freq: {magnitude[:10]}")
print(f"Phases at freq: {phase[:10]}")

# Peak frequencies (where magnitude is large)
peak_indices = np.argsort(magnitude)[-3:]  # Top 3
for idx in peak_indices:
    print(f"Peak at {frequencies[idx]:.1f} Hz, magnitude {magnitude[idx]:.1f}")

Positional encoding connection

In transformers, position encoding uses sine and cosine for different dimensions. This is Euler's formula in disguise:

\[\text{PE}(pos, 2d) = \sin\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2d/d_{model}}}\right)\] \[\text{PE}(pos, 2d+1) = \cos\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2d/d_{model}}}\right)\]

These come from the real and imaginary parts of \(e^{i \cdot pos / 10000^{2d/d_{model}}}\). The transformer thus embeds positions as complex numbers (split into real and imaginary parts across dimensions).

❓

Reflect: Complex Numbers and Your Models

You're designing a signal processing pipeline for an audio classification task. You'll apply Fourier transforms to convert audio into frequency domain, train a neural network on spectrograms (which contain magnitude and phase information from complex FFT outputs), and use attention mechanisms with sinusoidal positional encodings. At each step, complex numbers are silently at work. Do you see how?
1
Why does the FFT produce complex numbers when we input real audio signals? What information does the complex phase carry that we'd lose if we only kept the magnitude?
The FFT decomposes the signal into sums of \(e^{i2\pi f t}\) (complex exponentials at frequency \(f\)). Each component's amplitude and phase are encoded in a complex number. The magnitude \(|e^{i\theta}|\) tells you the intensity at that frequency; the phase \(\theta\) tells you the time shift. If you discard the phase (keep only magnitude), you lose information about whether a frequency component is delayed or advanced relative to the input. This matters: two signals with identical frequency content but different phase can sound very different. Phase is also crucial for reconstructing the original signal via inverse FFT.
2
In a transformer's positional encoding, why do we use both sine and cosine (or equivalently, real and imaginary parts of \(e^{i\theta}\))? Could we just use sine alone?
Using both sine and cosine creates 2D rotations in the embedding space. The pair \((\sin\theta, \cos\theta)\) spans a circle, encoding the angle \(\theta\) (position in this case) in 2D. If you used sine alone, you'd lose one degree of freedom: sin and sin-shifted don't distinguish all positions uniquely. Additionally, sine and cosine are orthogonal, so they carry independent information. Together, they form the real and imaginary components of \(e^{i\theta}\), which naturally represents a rotation. This allows the model to extract relative position information (via relative angle differences) without explicitly computing relative distances.
3
A rotation matrix (used in computer vision and some graph neural networks) has complex eigenvalues \(e^{i\theta}\) and \(e^{-i\theta}\). What does the angle \(\theta\) represent, and why are complex conjugate eigenvalues guaranteed for real rotation matrices?
The angle \(\theta\) is the rotation angle itself. A rotation matrix rotates all vectors by \(\theta\). Complex eigenvalues appear because rotation is a 2D operation (you're rotating in a plane), and the characteristic polynomial of a 2D rotation matrix has complex roots. For real matrices, if \(\lambda = a + bi\) is an eigenvalue, then its complex conjugate \(\bar{\lambda} = a - bi\) must also be an eigenvalue (complex roots come in conjugate pairs for polynomials with real coefficients). This is why rotations have paired complex eigenvalues: they represent the same rotation in two dimensions orthogonal to each other.
4
In RNN training, the recurrence matrix \(W_h\) (hidden state weights) has eigenvalues \(\lambda_i\). If these eigenvalues have magnitude \(|\lambda_i| > 1\), gradients explode; if \(|\lambda_i| < 1\), they vanish. Why does the modulus (magnitude) of the eigenvalue matter, and what role do complex eigenvalues play?
Repeated multiplication by \(W_h\) applies the eigenvalue decomposition: gradients get scaled by \(\lambda_i^T\) across time. If \(|\lambda_i| > 1\), then \(\lambda_i^T\) grows exponentially in \(T\), causing explosion. If \(|\lambda_i| < 1\), it decays exponentially, causing vanishing gradients. For complex eigenvalues \(\lambda = re^{i\theta}\), the modulus is \(r\). If \(r = 1\) (eigenvalue on the unit circle in the complex plane), the magnitude stays constant across time steps, neither exploding nor vanishing. This is why spectral normalization (keeping max eigenvalue modulus near 1) stabilises RNNs, and why gated mechanisms (LSTM, GRU) are designed to maintain \(|\lambda_i| \approx 1\) implicitly.
5
Why are the eigenvalues of a covariance matrix (used in PCA) guaranteed to be real, even though we studied complex eigenvalues? How does this simplify machine learning?
Covariance matrices are symmetric: \(\Sigma = \Sigma^T\). By the spectral theorem, symmetric real matrices have only real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors (also real). This is profound: we can decompose any data via PCA without entering the complex numbers. The eigenvalues are the variances explained by each principal component, which must be non-negative (variance can't be negative). This simplification is why PCA is so elegant: we only need real arithmetic and can interpret results directly as variance. Without symmetry, we'd need to handle complex eigenvalues, making interpretation and computation harder. Many ML algorithms (PCA, spectral clustering, etc.) rely on this property of covariance matrices.
