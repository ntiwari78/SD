Chapter 9: Mechanical Properties of Fluids

Explore pressure, viscosity, surface tension, and Bernoulli's principle—the physics that governs liquid and gas behavior in nature and engineering.

Why Does This Matter?

Real-World Context: Fluids—liquids and gases—dominate our world. Water flows through pipes at different pressures, blood circulates through your veins at precise velocities, aeroplanes fly because of pressure differences, and hydraulic systems lift entire cars with minimal effort. Understanding mechanical properties of fluids is essential for designing pumps, turbines, medical devices, aircraft wings, and countless everyday technologies.

Key Question: How can a small force applied to a hydraulic lift move a 2000-ton car? How does a plane wing generate lift? Why does blood flow faster through narrow arteries? The answers lie in the elegant principles of fluid mechanics.

Core Concepts

1. Pressure: Force Per Unit Area

Pressure is the perpendicular force exerted by a fluid per unit area. It's a scalar quantity (direction doesn't matter for its definition, though it acts perpendicular to surfaces).

Definition & Formula

$$P = \frac{F}{A}$$

SI Unit: Pascal (Pa) = N/m² = 1 kg/(m·s²)

Atmospheric Pressure: 1 atm = 101,325 Pa ≈ 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa (at sea level, 0°C)

Absolute vs. Gauge Pressure

  • Absolute Pressure: Total pressure from all sources (atmospheric + gauge)
  • Gauge Pressure: Pressure above atmospheric. P_gauge = P_absolute - P_atm
  • Vacuum: When P_absolute approaches zero
Example 1: Pressure from a Book

Problem: A book with mass 1 kg rests on a table. The contact area is 0.02 m². Calculate pressure exerted. (g = 10 m/s²)

Force: F = mg = 1 × 10 = 10 N
Pressure: P = F/A = 10/0.02 = 500 Pa
Example 2: Atmospheric Pressure Calculation

Problem: Calculate the total force due to atmospheric pressure on a window pane of area 1.5 m².

Force: F = P × A = 1.013 × 10⁵ × 1.5 = 1.52 × 10⁵ N ≈ 152 kN
Insight: This huge force is balanced by equal pressure inside, so no net force on the window!

2. Pascal's Law: Pressure Transmission

Statement: An external pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every part of the fluid and to the walls of the container.

Hydraulic Lift (Hydraulic Press)

A practical application of Pascal's Law. Two cylinders of different areas connected by an incompressible fluid (usually oil).

$$\frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{F_2}{A_2}$$

Where: F₁ and A₁ are input force and area; F₂ and A₂ are output force and area.

Mechanical Advantage: M = F₂/F₁ = A₂/A₁ (can be very large if A₂ >> A₁)

Example 3: Car Lift Problem

Problem: A hydraulic lift has input piston area 0.01 m² and output piston area 1 m². A force of 500 N is applied to the input piston. Calculate the output force and the car's mass that can be lifted.

Output Force: F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁) = 500 × (1/0.01) = 500 × 100 = 50,000 N
Mass of Car: m = F₂/g = 50,000/10 = 5,000 kg = 5 tons
Mechanical Advantage: M = 100 (a 100× force amplification!)

Hydraulic Brakes

When you press the brake pedal, a small force is amplified by Pascal's Law. The master cylinder (small area) transmits pressure to the wheel cylinders (larger combined area), creating large braking forces at all four wheels simultaneously.

Example 4: Hydraulic Brakes

Problem: Master cylinder (pedal): A = 1 cm² = 10⁻⁴ m². Wheel cylinders (per wheel): A = 5 cm² = 5 × 10⁻⁴ m². Applied pedal force = 100 N. Calculate force on each wheel if 4 wheels share the output pressure.

Pressure in System: P = F₁/A₁ = 100/(10⁻⁴) = 10⁶ Pa
Force per Wheel: F = P × A = 10⁶ × 5 × 10⁻⁴ = 500 N
Total Braking Force: 4 × 500 = 2000 N (20× amplification)

3. Pressure Variation with Depth

In a static fluid (at rest), pressure increases with depth due to the weight of the fluid above.

Hydrostatic Pressure Formula

$$P = P_0 + \rho g h$$

Where:

  • P₀ = atmospheric pressure at surface
  • ρ = density of fluid (kg/m³)
  • g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
  • h = depth below surface (m)

Hydrostatic Paradox

The pressure at a given depth depends only on the depth and fluid density—not on the shape or volume of the container! A tall, thin tube and a wide basin at the same depth have the same pressure.

Manometers and Barometers

Manometer: A U-tube filled with mercury (or oil) used to measure pressure differences. The height difference h gives the pressure difference: ΔP = ρgh.

Barometer: A mercury column in a tube (closed at one end, open at the other) that measures atmospheric pressure. At sea level, mercury rises ~760 mm (0.76 m), confirming P_atm = ρ_mercury × g × 0.76 ≈ 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa.

Example 5: Deep Sea Pressure

Problem: Calculate absolute pressure at a depth of 100 m in the ocean. ρ_water = 1000 kg/m³, P_atm = 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa, g = 10 m/s².

Gauge Pressure: P_gauge = ρgh = 1000 × 10 × 100 = 10⁶ Pa
Absolute Pressure: P = P₀ + ρgh = 1.01 × 10⁵ + 10⁶ = 1.101 × 10⁶ Pa ≈ 11 atm
Insight: Every 10 m depth adds ~1 atm of pressure (for water). Submarine hulls must withstand immense forces!
Example 6: Barometer Calculation

Problem: A mercury barometer reads 760 mm. Verify that this corresponds to atmospheric pressure of ~1.01 × 10⁵ Pa. ρ_Hg = 13,600 kg/m³.

Pressure: P = ρ_Hg × g × h = 13,600 × 10 × 0.76 = 103,360 Pa ≈ 1.03 × 10⁵ Pa
Note: This is approximately 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa (small differences due to rounding and temperature effects).

4. Bernoulli's Principle: Energy in Flowing Fluids

Statement: In a streamline flow of an ideal, incompressible fluid, the sum of pressure energy, kinetic energy, and potential energy per unit volume remains constant.

Bernoulli's Equation

$$P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant}$$

Or, comparing two points along a streamline:

$$P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g h_1 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g h_2$$

Where: P is pressure, ρv²/2 is kinetic energy per unit volume, ρgh is gravitational potential energy per unit volume.

Derivation (from Work-Energy Theorem)

Consider a fluid element moving through a pipe. The net work done by pressure and gravity equals the change in kinetic energy. This leads directly to Bernoulli's equation.

Key Insight

Higher velocity → Lower pressure. Lower velocity → Higher pressure. This is why aeroplanes fly, why water is sucked up a drinking straw, and why tornados have low central pressure.

Applications of Bernoulli's Principle

a) Venturi Meter: Measures fluid velocity in pipes. When fluid flows through a constriction (smaller area), it speeds up and pressure drops. The pressure difference indicates velocity.

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2(P_1 - P_2)}{\rho(1 - (A_2/A_1)^2)}}$$

b) Atomizer/Spray Bottle: When you blow air through a tube, the moving air has low pressure. It draws up liquid from a reservoir because surrounding pressure is higher.

c) Aeroplane Lift: The wing is shaped (airfoil) so that air flows faster over the curved top surface than the flatter bottom surface. By Bernoulli's principle, pressure is lower above, creating an upward lift force.

d) Magnus Effect: A spinning ball in a moving fluid (like a cricket ball or baseball) curves because the spin creates asymmetric flow patterns around the ball, producing sideways lift.

Example 7: Bernoulli in Horizontal Pipe

Problem: Water flows through a horizontal pipe. At point 1: P₁ = 2 × 10⁵ Pa, v₁ = 1 m/s. At point 2 (narrower section): v₂ = 4 m/s. Find P₂. ρ = 1000 kg/m³.

Bernoulli's Equation (h₁ = h₂): P₁ + ½ρv₁² = P₂ + ½ρv₂²
P₂ = P₁ + ½ρ(v₁² - v₂²) = 2 × 10⁵ + ½ × 1000 × (1 - 16)
P₂ = 2 × 10⁵ - 7500 = 1.925 × 10⁵ Pa
Observation: Velocity quadrupled, pressure dropped by 3.75% (higher velocity → lower pressure!)
Example 8: Water from a Tap

Problem: Water flows from a faucet held at height h = 1 m. At the tap opening: v₁ ≈ 0 (approximately), P₁ = P_atm. After falling height h, the water accelerates. Find v₂.

Bernoulli's Equation: P₁ + ½ρv₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + ½ρv₂² + ρgh₂
At both points: P = P_atm. So: ½ρv₁² + ρgh = ½ρv₂²
v₂ = √(2gh) = √(2 × 10 × 1) = √20 ≈ 4.47 m/s

5. Viscosity: Resistance to Flow

Viscosity is the measure of a fluid's resistance to flow due to internal friction between fluid layers (no relative motion in the direction perpendicular to flow, but layers slide past each other).

Newton's Law of Viscosity

$$F = \eta A \frac{dv}{dx}$$

Where:

  • F = viscous force (N)
  • η = coefficient of viscosity (Pa·s or poise, 1 Pa·s = 10 poise)
  • A = area of contact between layers (m²)
  • dv/dx = velocity gradient (rate of change of velocity with position, s⁻¹)

SI Unit of Viscosity: Pascal-second (Pa·s) = N·s/m² = kg/(m·s)

Examples of Viscosity at 20°C

  • Water: ~0.001 Pa·s
  • Olive Oil: ~0.08 Pa·s
  • Honey: ~10 Pa·s
  • Air: ~1.8 × 10⁻⁵ Pa·s

Stokes' Law (Terminal Velocity)

A spherical object falling through a viscous fluid experiences drag.

$$F_{\text{drag}} = 6\pi \eta r v$$

Where: r is radius of sphere (m), v is velocity (m/s).

Terminal Velocity: When drag force equals gravitational force, acceleration stops and the object falls at constant velocity.

$$v_t = \frac{2r^2(\rho - \sigma)g}{9\eta}$$

Where:

  • ρ = density of sphere material
  • σ = density of surrounding fluid (for buoyancy)
  • g = gravitational acceleration

Reynolds Number

A dimensionless quantity that predicts whether flow is laminar (smooth) or turbulent (chaotic).

$$Re = \frac{\rho v d}{\eta}$$

Where: d is characteristic dimension (pipe diameter, etc.), v is fluid velocity, ρ is density.

  • Re < 1000: Laminar flow (layers slide smoothly)
  • Re > 2000: Turbulent flow (chaotic, eddies)
  • 1000 < Re < 2000: Transitional
Example 9: Stokes' Law and Terminal Velocity

Problem: A steel ball (r = 0.001 m, ρ = 7800 kg/m³) falls through glycerin (η = 1.5 Pa·s, σ = 1260 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s²). Calculate terminal velocity.

Formula: v_t = [2r²(ρ - σ)g] / (9η)
Numerator: 2 × (0.001)² × (7800 - 1260) × 10 = 2 × 10⁻⁶ × 6540 × 10 = 0.13
Denominator: 9 × 1.5 = 13.5
v_t = 0.13 / 13.5 ≈ 0.0096 m/s ≈ 1 cm/s
Example 10: Reynolds Number in Pipe Flow

Problem: Water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³, η = 0.001 Pa·s) flows through a pipe of diameter d = 0.1 m at velocity v = 1 m/s. Calculate Reynolds number and determine if flow is laminar.

Re = ρvd/η = (1000 × 1 × 0.1) / 0.001 = 100,000
Since Re = 100,000 >> 2000, the flow is highly turbulent (fast, chaotic).

6. Surface Tension: Molecules at the Boundary

Surface tension is the property of a liquid surface that behaves like a stretched elastic membrane. It arises because molecules at the surface experience unbalanced intermolecular forces.

Definition & Formula

$$S = \frac{F}{l}$$

Where: F is force (N), l is length of the line along which the force acts (m).

SI Unit: N/m (or J/m², since surface energy = surface tension)

Examples at 20°C:

  • Water-air: 0.072 N/m
  • Mercury-air: 0.487 N/m (high surface tension!)
  • Soap solution: 0.025 N/m (much lower—detergents reduce surface tension)

Surface Energy

Surface tension equals the energy per unit area required to create a new surface. High surface energy means high surface tension.

Excess Pressure in Curved Surfaces

For a spherical droplet:

$$\Delta P = \frac{2S}{r}$$

For a soap bubble (two surfaces):

$$\Delta P = \frac{4S}{r}$$

This pressure difference explains why small droplets are harder to form and why bubbles collapse in a warm environment (higher internal pressure gradient).

Capillarity (Capillary Rise)

When a capillary tube (thin tube) is dipped in a liquid, the liquid either rises (wetting) or falls (non-wetting) depending on the angle of contact θ.

$$h = \frac{2S \cos \theta}{\rho g r}$$

Where:

  • h = height of liquid column (m)
  • S = surface tension (N/m)
  • θ = contact angle (0° to 180°; θ < 90° means wetting, θ > 90° means non-wetting)
  • ρ = density of liquid
  • r = radius of capillary tube (m)

Angle of Contact

θ < 90°: Wetting liquid (e.g., water on glass). Liquid climbs the tube.

θ > 90°: Non-wetting liquid (e.g., mercury on glass). Liquid depresses in the tube.

θ = 0°: Perfect wetting. θ = 180°: perfect non-wetting.

Detergents and Surface-Active Agents

Detergent molecules have one end that loves water (hydrophilic) and one end that hates water (hydrophobic). They accumulate at the air-water interface, reducing surface tension and allowing water to wet surfaces and penetrate dirt more effectively.

Example 11: Excess Pressure in a Water Droplet

Problem: A water droplet has radius r = 0.001 m. Calculate the excess pressure inside. S_water = 0.072 N/m.

Formula: ΔP = 2S/r (one surface)
ΔP = (2 × 0.072) / 0.001 = 144 Pa
This excess pressure is about 0.0014 atm—small but measurable!
Example 12: Capillary Rise in Water

Problem: A glass capillary tube (r = 0.0005 m) is inserted in water. Calculate the height to which water rises. Assume θ = 0° (perfect wetting), S = 0.072 N/m, ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s².

Formula: h = 2S cos θ / (ρgr)
h = (2 × 0.072 × 1) / (1000 × 10 × 0.0005) = 0.144 / 5 = 0.0288 m ≈ 2.88 cm
Very thin tubes show dramatic capillary effects—water rises several centimeters!
Example 13: Mercury in Capillary (Non-wetting)

Problem: A glass capillary (r = 0.001 m) is placed in mercury. Calculate the depression. θ = 140°, S_Hg = 0.487 N/m, ρ_Hg = 13,600 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s².

h = 2S cos θ / (ρgr) = (2 × 0.487 × cos(140°)) / (13,600 × 10 × 0.001)
cos(140°) ≈ -0.766 (negative because θ > 90°)
h = (0.974 × -0.766) / 136 ≈ -0.0055 m = -0.55 cm
Mercury depresses ~0.55 cm (negative height = depression for non-wetting)

7. Equation of Continuity: Conservation of Mass

When an incompressible fluid flows through a pipe or channel of varying cross-section, the mass flow rate must be conserved (no fluid is created or destroyed).

Continuity Equation

$$A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2 = \text{constant}$$

Or in terms of mass flow rate:

$$\frac{dm}{dt} = \rho A v = \text{constant}$$

Where: A is cross-sectional area, v is speed.

Physical Interpretation

If the pipe narrows, the fluid must speed up to maintain the same volume flow rate (or mass flow rate for incompressible fluids). If the pipe widens, the fluid slows down.

Combined with Bernoulli's Principle

When fluid accelerates (due to narrowing, from continuity equation), its kinetic energy increases. By Bernoulli's principle, this comes at the expense of pressure energy—the pressure decreases!

Example 14: Water Hose Narrowing

Problem: A water hose (initial diameter 2 cm, velocity 2 m/s) is constricted to 1 cm diameter. Find the new velocity.

A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ (continuity)
Radius ratio: r₁/r₂ = 2/1, so A₁/A₂ = 4
v₂ = v₁ × (A₁/A₂) = 2 × 4 = 8 m/s
Narrowing by half (diameter) → velocity quadruples! (This is why water from a hose shoots farther when you pinch it.)

8. Torricelli's Theorem: Speed of Efflux

When a fluid flows out of an orifice (opening) in a container, the speed at which it exits depends on the depth of the orifice below the free surface.

Torricelli's Theorem

$$v = \sqrt{2gh}$$

Where: h is the vertical height of the fluid above the orifice.

Derivation (using Bernoulli's Principle)

At the top surface (point 1): P₁ = P_atm, v₁ ≈ 0 (assuming large container), h₁ = h

At the orifice (point 2): P₂ = P_atm, v₂ = v (to find), h₂ = 0

By Bernoulli: P_atm + 0 + ρgh = P_atm + ½ρv² + 0

Simplifying: ρgh = ½ρv², so v = √(2gh)

Interesting Observation

The speed of efflux from a tank is identical to the speed a freely falling object would have after falling height h! This makes intuitive sense: the fluid falls from the surface to the orifice, converting potential energy to kinetic energy.

Example 15: Water from a Tank

Problem: A water tank (open top) has an orifice at the bottom. The water level is 5 m high. What is the speed of water exiting the orifice? g = 10 m/s².

v = √(2gh) = √(2 × 10 × 5) = √100 = 10 m/s
This is the same speed as an object in free fall for 5 m!
Example 16: Range of Water Jet

Problem: A tank with water level 1 m high has an orifice 0.5 m above the ground. Water flows horizontally. How far (horizontal range) from the tank does the water hit the ground? g = 10 m/s².

Speed of efflux: v = √(2 × 10 × 1) = √20 ≈ 4.47 m/s (horizontal component)
Time to fall 0.5 m: h = ½gt² → 0.5 = ½ × 10 × t² → t = √0.1 ≈ 0.316 s
Horizontal Range: R = v × t = 4.47 × 0.316 ≈ 1.41 m

Higher-Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS 1: Submarine Depth Design

Scenario: A submarine's hull must withstand extreme pressures at ocean depths. At 300 m depth, engineers must design a hull that can handle the pressure without collapsing. Using the hydrostatic pressure formula, calculate the total force on a circular viewport (radius 0.1 m) and explain what materials and design features would be necessary.

Analysis: This problem integrates pressure concepts, material strength, and engineering design. Students must calculate absolute pressure at depth, convert to force, and reason about material properties (yield strength, thickness ratios). It also touches on safety factors and real-world constraints.

Follow-up: How would this design change if the submarine could only reach 100 m? What if it needed to reach 500 m? What is the maximum depth a submarine could theoretically reach with current materials?

HOTS 2: Venturi Meter Design for Water Pipeline

Scenario: A water treatment plant uses a Venturi meter to measure flow rate in a 20 cm diameter main pipeline. The meter reduces the pipe diameter to 10 cm at the throat. Manometer readings show a pressure difference of 5000 Pa. Using Bernoulli's principle and the continuity equation, calculate the flow rate and explain how changes in pipe geometry affect measurement accuracy.

Analysis: This integrates Bernoulli's principle, continuity equation, and practical fluid measurement. Students must work through multi-step calculations, understand how pressure and velocity are coupled, and reason about measurement error in real systems.

Follow-up: Would the measurement be more accurate or less accurate if we made the throat even narrower? What happens if the fluid isn't perfectly incompressible (e.g., contains air bubbles)?

HOTS 3: Terminal Velocity and Sedimentation

Scenario: In a water treatment facility, fine clay particles (radius 10 μm, density 2600 kg/m³) are allowed to settle in a tank. Using Stokes' law and terminal velocity formulas, determine how long particles take to settle from the surface to the bottom of a 2-meter-deep tank. How would the settling time change if the water were heated (viscosity decreases)? What industrial application could benefit from understanding this?

Analysis: This explores viscosity effects on particle motion, connects to real environmental engineering, and requires students to reason about how changing one variable (temperature) affects the system through viscosity dependence.

Follow-up: Why do chemical plants add flocculants (which increase particle size) to speed up sedimentation? If we wanted to speed up settling without changing the fluid, what could we do?

Case-Based Questions

Case Study 1: Blood Flow in Arteries (Hagen-Poiseuille)

Medical Context: A patient has narrowed coronary arteries due to plaque buildup. Normal artery diameter is 3 mm; the narrowed section is only 1.5 mm. Blood viscosity η = 0.004 Pa·s, density ρ = 1050 kg/m³. The pressure difference driving flow is typically 100 mmHg ≈ 13,300 Pa.

Part A: Using the Hagen-Poiseuille equation (volume flow rate Q = πΔP r⁴ / 8ηL), explain why reducing artery diameter dramatically reduces blood flow. Calculate the flow reduction.

Part B: Calculate the average velocity in the narrowed artery using A·v = Q. How does this compare to velocity in the normal artery?

Part C: Using Bernoulli's principle, explain why constricted arteries create turbulent flow and potential clot formation risk (high velocity → lower pressure locally).

Clinical Implication: Even a 50% diameter reduction causes a ~94% flow reduction (since Q ∝ r⁴). This explains why angioplasty (reopening narrowed arteries) is critical.

Case Study 2: Surface Tension in Lung Surfactant

Biological Context: Lungs are lined with a thin fluid layer (surfactant) that has abnormally low surface tension (~0.025 N/m for surfactant vs. ~0.072 N/m for pure water). When surfactant is absent (premature infants), surface tension causes alveoli (tiny air sacs) to collapse.

Part A: Calculate the excess pressure inside an alveolus (radius ~50 μm) with normal water surface tension. Then recalculate with surfactant. Explain the difference.

Part B: Why do smaller alveoli (radius ~20 μm) tend to collapse into larger ones without surfactant? Use excess pressure calculations to explain.

Part C: Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) in premature infants: explain why they struggle to breathe and why artificial surfactant replacement therapy works.

Biological Insight: Evolution solved this problem by producing pulmonary surfactant—a lipid-protein mixture that reduces surface tension, allowing efficient breathing even at small lung volumes.

Case Study 3: Oil Spill Spreading and Viscosity

Environmental Context: An oil tanker ruptures, spilling 100,000 tons of crude oil into the ocean. Crude oil viscosity varies greatly with temperature: η ≈ 0.1 Pa·s at 20°C but η ≈ 0.01 Pa·s at 40°C. Ocean temperature and wave action affect spreading and evaporation.

Part A: Explain (using viscosity concepts) why crude oil spreads rapidly initially but can be contained if cold weather sets in. How does the Reynolds number change with temperature?

Part B: Surface tension of oil is lower than water (S_oil ≈ 0.03 N/m). Explain why oil naturally spreads across water surfaces and forms thin films. Use capillary pressure concepts.

Part C: Dispersants (like Corexit) are surfactants that further reduce oil-water surface tension, promoting emulsification. Why is this beneficial for cleanup? Why is it controversial environmentally?

Real-World Impact: The 2010 Deepwater Horizon spill involved ~5 million barrels of oil. Understanding fluid mechanics was crucial for predicting spread, designing containment, and planning cleanup efforts.
