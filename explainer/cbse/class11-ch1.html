Units and Measurement

Units and Measurement

Class 11 · Chapter 1 · CBSE Physics

How do you measure the universe? From the tiniest atom to the most distant galaxy, science rests on measurement. Without a standard ruler, a standard scale, and a standard clock, we cannot communicate our discoveries. This chapter is about building the language of measurement—the foundation upon which all of physics is built.

1. Need for Measurement and Standard Units

Why Measurement Matters

Physics is fundamentally a quantitative science. Unlike philosophy, which asks "why does the universe exist?", physics asks "how much, how fast, and how heavy?" Every law of physics—Newton's laws, Einstein's relativity, quantum mechanics—is expressed through numbers and equations. To state that an object falls with an acceleration of \(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\) means nothing without a universally accepted definition of the metre and the second.

Imagine a world where each town has its own definition of "length." New York's metre might be 1.2 metres long, while Delhi's metre might be 0.8 metres long. Science would crumble instantly. We couldn't compare experiments, predict phenomena, or build bridges across borders. Standard units are not merely convenient—they are essential to civilization.

Historical Journey of Measurement Systems

Humans have always measured. Ancient traders weighed grain. Architects measured fields. But before the 18th century, every region had its own standards. The French "pied du roi" (king's foot) was different from the English foot. This chaos lasted until the French Revolution, when scientists proposed something radical: a measurement system based not on the king's body or arbitrary tradition, but on nature itself.

The CGS System (Centimetre-Gram-Second)

In 1873, the first metric system based entirely on nature's constants was established. Length was measured in centimetres (1/100th of a metre, defined as 1/10,000,000th of the distance from Earth's pole to equator), mass in grams, and time in seconds. This system, called CGS, was revolutionary—it was coherent, decimal-based, and universal.

Limitation: The base unit (the metre) was defined by Earth's dimensions, which are neither convenient nor unchanging. The system also predated modern physics and lacked units for electric current and other phenomena.

The MKS System (Metre-Kilogram-Second)

As physics advanced, especially electricity and magnetism, scientists realized they needed a base unit for electric current. The metre-kilogram-second system emerged as a more practical variant, adding the ampere as the unit of electric current. This system proved far more useful for practical engineering and industrial applications.

The FPS System (Foot-Pound-Second)

Primarily used in Anglo-Saxon countries, this system measures length in feet, force in pounds, and time in seconds. While still used in some engineering contexts, it is increasingly obsolete due to its lack of scientific coherence and the global adoption of SI units.

Did You Know?
The original prototype of the kilogram was kept in a vault in Paris from 1889 to 2019. Scientists discovered it was gaining or losing mass due to dust and oxidation! In 2019, the world officially redefined the kilogram using a fundamental constant (Planck's constant) rather than a physical object. This was a historic moment—for the first time, all seven SI base units are defined by fundamental physical constants, not by artifacts.

What Makes a Good Standard?

A standard unit must satisfy four criteria:

  • Invariability: It must not change with time or place. A kilogram measured today must equal a kilogram measured 100 years ago.
  • Accessibility: Scientists worldwide must be able to realize or verify the standard independently. A standard locked in one vault is useless.
  • Reproducibility: The standard must be reproducible in any laboratory with reasonable precision.
  • Convenience: It must be practical for use in commerce, science, and engineering.

The SI units satisfy all four criteria by being defined through fundamental physical constants like the speed of light, Planck's constant, and the elementary charge.

2. The International System of Units (SI)

The Seven Base Units

The International System of Units (Système International d'unités), adopted in 1960, consists of seven base units from which all other units are derived. These are chosen such that:

  • They are independent (one cannot be expressed in terms of others)
  • They cover all domains of physics
  • They can be defined with extreme precision using fundamental constants
Physical Quantity SI Base Unit Symbol Definition (2019 onwards)
Length Metre m Distance light travels in vacuum in 1/299,792,458 seconds
Mass Kilogram kg Defined using Planck's constant (\(h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34}\) J·s)
Time Second s Duration of 9,192,631,770 periods of radiation from Cs-133 atoms
Electric Current Ampere A Flow of 1/(1.602176634 × 10^19) elementary charges per second
Absolute Temperature Kelvin K Defined using Boltzmann's constant (\(k_B = 1.380649 \times 10^{-23}\) J/K)
Amount of Substance Mole mol Exactly \(6.02214076 \times 10^{23}\) elementary entities (Avogadro's number)
Luminous Intensity Candela cd Luminous flux of 1/683 watt per steradian at frequency 540 × 10^12 Hz

Understanding Each Base Unit

1. Metre (m) — Length

The metre is the unit of length. Historically, it was defined as 1/10,000,000 of the distance from the North Pole to the equator along the meridian passing through Paris. Today, it's defined through the speed of light: the metre is the distance light travels in vacuum in 1/299,792,458 seconds.

Why this definition? The speed of light is a fundamental constant of nature that is the same everywhere and is now known to extraordinarily high precision. This makes the metre equally precise and universal.

2. Kilogram (kg) — Mass

The kilogram is the unit of mass (the measure of the amount of matter in an object). Originally, it was the mass of one litre of pure water at 4°C—a convenient reference. From 1889 to 2019, it was defined as the mass of the International Prototype Kilogram, a platinum-iridium cylinder kept in Paris.

In 2019, scientists realized that this physical artifact was gradually changing due to oxidation and dust, making it an unreliable standard. The kilogram was redefined using Planck's constant, a fundamental constant from quantum mechanics. This ensures that the kilogram is now defined by nature itself, not by a decaying artifact.

3. Second (s) — Time

The second is the unit of time. It was originally defined as 1/86,400 of a solar day, but this is variable—a day is not constant due to tidal friction and other effects. Today, it's defined using atomic clocks: one second is the duration of 9,192,631,770 periods of oscillation of the cesium-133 atom.

Why this precision? Atomic clocks can measure the second to within one part in \(10^{14}\), making them more precise than any mechanical device. GPS satellites, for example, must account for relativistic time dilation, and they use atomic clocks.

4. Ampere (A) — Electric Current

The ampere is the unit of electric current—the flow of charge. It's defined as a fixed number of elementary charges passing through a point per second: \(1/(1.602176634 \times 10^{-19})\) charges per second.

Intuition: When you turn on a light bulb rated at 60 W at 120 V, the current flowing is 0.5 A. This means about 3 × 10^18 electrons are flowing through the filament every second.

5. Kelvin (K) — Absolute Temperature

The kelvin is the unit of absolute temperature—temperature measured from absolute zero. It's defined using Boltzmann's constant, which relates energy and temperature at the atomic scale.

Why "absolute"? Celsius and Fahrenheit are arbitrary—they were historically set based on ice and steam points of water. But zero kelvin has deep physical meaning: it's the temperature at which all molecular motion stops (according to classical physics; quantum mechanics says zero-point energy remains).

Relationship to Celsius: \(T(\text{K}) = T(\text{°C}) + 273.15\)

6. Mole (mol) — Amount of Substance

The mole is the unit of "amount of substance"—how many atoms or molecules you have. One mole contains exactly \(6.02214076 \times 10^{23}\) elementary entities (this number is called Avogadro's number).

Why this number? Historically, one mole was defined as the number of carbon-12 atoms in exactly 12 grams of pure carbon-12. This made chemistry and physics elegantly connected: the atomic mass of an element in u (atomic mass units) is numerically equal to the molar mass in g/mol.

Example: One mole of hydrogen atoms has a mass of approximately 1 gram. One mole of carbon atoms has a mass of approximately 12 grams. This makes chemical calculations intuitive.

7. Candela (cd) — Luminous Intensity

The candela is the unit of luminous intensity—the brightness of light as perceived by the human eye. It's defined using a specific frequency of light (540 × 10^12 Hz, which is green light) because the human eye is most sensitive to green.

Note: This is the only SI unit explicitly based on human perception. It's less fundamental than the others, which is why scientists often prefer the "lumen" (total light flux) or "watt per steradian" (physical power) for scientific work.

Supplementary Units

Beyond the seven base units, SI recognizes two supplementary units for measuring angles:

Quantity Unit Symbol Definition
Plane Angle Radian rad Angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc equal to the radius
Solid Angle Steradian sr Solid angle subtended at the center of a sphere by an area equal to the square of the radius

Radian: If a circle has radius \(r\) and the arc length is \(s\), then the angle in radians is \(\theta = s/r\). Since the circumference of a circle is \(2\pi r\), a full circle is \(2\pi\) radians.

\[\text{Angle in radians} = \frac{\text{Arc length}}{\text{Radius}} = \frac{s}{r}\]

Rules for Writing SI Units

The SI system has strict conventions for how units are written. These rules ensure international clarity:

  • Unit symbols are written in roman (upright) type: m, kg, s (not m, kg, s)
  • Unit symbols follow the numerical value with a space: 5 m, NOT 5m
  • Compound units are written with spaces or dots: m·s⁻¹ or m/s, NOT ms⁻¹
  • Plural forms are not used: 5 metre should be written as 5 m, NOT 5 metres
  • Prefixes are directly attached to the unit, with NO space: 5 nm, NOT 5 n m
  • Capital and lowercase letters matter: M = mega (10⁶), m = milli (10⁻³); they are different!
  • Product of units uses a dot or space: N·m or N m (not Nm, which is not standard)
  • Division in compound units uses a slash or negative exponent: m/s or m·s⁻¹
Did You Know?
Scientists once made a mistake that cost a Mars mission! The NASA Mars Climate Orbiter failed in 1999 because one team used SI units (meters, kilograms) while another used Imperial units (feet, pounds). The lack of unit conversion caused the spacecraft to burn up in the Martian atmosphere, destroying a $327 million mission. This is a real-world reminder of why standardized units matter.

3. Significant Figures

What Are Significant Figures?

When you measure something in the lab, your answer is only as good as your instrument. If you use a metre stick marked in centimetres, you can measure to the nearest centimetre, not the nearest millimetre. If you use a voltmeter that displays two decimal places, you cannot report three decimal places.

Significant figures (or significant digits) are the digits in a number that carry meaningful information about the precision of that measurement. They include all the digits you know with certainty, plus one estimated digit.

Example: If you measure a length using a ruler and get 4.75 cm:

  • The "4" is known with certainty (full centimetres)
  • The "7" is known with certainty (tenths of centimetre)
  • The "5" is estimated (you're saying it's closer to 5 mm than 4 mm or 6 mm)

So 4.75 cm has three significant figures. This means you're confident in your measurement to about 1 part in 100 (since the uncertainty is about ±0.01 cm).

Rules for Counting Significant Figures

Rule 1: All Non-Zero Digits Are Significant

In the number 4.75 cm, all three digits (4, 7, 5) are significant because they are all non-zero.

Rule 2: Zeros Between Non-Zero Digits Are Significant

In the number 4.05 cm, the zero is significant because it's between the 4 and the 5. The number has three significant figures.

Rule 3: Leading Zeros Are NOT Significant

Leading zeros (zeros before the first non-zero digit) are not significant. They just show where the decimal point is.

  • 0.045 m has TWO significant figures (4 and 5). The leading zeros don't count.
  • We can write this as 4.5 × 10⁻² m to make it clear there are only two significant figures.
  • 0.0000345 kg has THREE significant figures (3, 4, 5), same as 3.45 × 10⁻⁵ kg.

Rule 4: Trailing Zeros in a Decimal Number Are Significant

Trailing zeros (zeros after the last non-zero digit) are significant IF the number contains a decimal point.

  • 4.50 m has THREE significant figures (4, 5, 0). The trailing zero shows the measurement was made to the nearest 0.01 m.
  • 4.5 m has TWO significant figures. The trailing zero is not written, suggesting less precision.
  • 4.500 m has FOUR significant figures, indicating measurement to the nearest 0.001 m.

Rule 5: Trailing Zeros in a Whole Number Are Ambiguous (Use Scientific Notation)

This is the trickiest rule. In the number 4500, it's unclear if the trailing zeros are significant.

  • Does 4500 m have 2, 3, or 4 significant figures?
  • To be clear, use scientific notation:
  • 4.5 × 10³ m has 2 sig figs
  • 4.50 × 10³ m has 3 sig figs
  • 4.500 × 10³ m has 4 sig figs

Rules for Arithmetic with Significant Figures

Addition and Subtraction: Use Decimal Places

When adding or subtracting, the result should have no more decimal places than the least precise number.

Example: Add 4.75 cm + 0.8 cm + 13.234 cm

  • 4.75 cm has 2 decimal places
  • 0.8 cm has 1 decimal place
  • 13.234 cm has 3 decimal places
  • Sum = 18.784 cm
  • Result should have 1 decimal place (limited by 0.8 cm)
  • Answer: 18.8 cm

Multiplication and Division: Use Significant Figures

When multiplying or dividing, the result should have the same number of significant figures as the least precise number.

Example: Calculate the area of a rectangle with sides 4.5 m and 12.34 m

  • 4.5 m has 2 significant figures
  • 12.34 m has 4 significant figures
  • Area = 4.5 × 12.34 = 55.53 m²
  • Result should have 2 significant figures (limited by 4.5 m)
  • Answer: 55 m² or 5.5 × 10¹ m²

Rounding Rules

When you have more digits than needed, round to the correct number of significant figures using these rules:

  • If the digit to be dropped is <5, round down
  • If the digit to be dropped is >5, round up
  • If the digit to be dropped is exactly 5, round to the nearest even number (banker's rounding)

Examples:

  • 5.34 rounded to 2 sig figs → 5.3 (drop 4, which is <5)
  • 5.37 rounded to 2 sig figs → 5.4 (drop 7, which is >5)
  • 5.35 rounded to 2 sig figs → 5.4 (drop 5, round to nearest even, which is 4, so we round up to 5.4)
  • 5.45 rounded to 2 sig figs → 5.4 (drop 5, round to nearest even, which is 4)

Exact Numbers vs. Measured Quantities

Some numbers in physics are exact—they have infinite significant figures. These include:

  • Counting numbers: "3 apples" has infinite precision
  • Defined constants: The charge of an electron is now defined as exactly -e
  • Conversion factors: 1 inch is defined as exactly 2.54 cm
  • Pure geometry: A circle has exactly 360 degrees

When multiplying or dividing, exact numbers don't limit the precision of the result. For example:

\[\text{Area} = 2 \times (3.45 \, \text{m})^2 = 2 \times 11.90 \, \text{m}^2 = 23.8 \, \text{m}^2\]

The "2" doesn't limit precision; the result has 3 sig figs from 3.45.

Did You Know?
In experimental physics, reporting significant figures correctly is crucial for credibility. If you measure something with high precision but report it with too many significant figures, you look like you don't understand your instrument. Conversely, if you report too few sig figs, you're hiding information. The best scientists are precise about their precision!

4. Dimensions of Physical Quantities

What Are Dimensions?

The dimensionThe expression of a quantity in terms of its base physical quantities of a physical quantity describes its nature in terms of base quantities like length, mass, and time. Dimensions are expressed using square brackets.

For example, "force" has dimensions of mass × length / (time²), written as [M L T⁻²]. "Velocity" has dimensions [L T⁻¹]. "Density" has dimensions [M L⁻³].

Key insight: Two quantities with the same dimensions can be added or subtracted. Quantities with different dimensions cannot—that would be like adding apples and oranges.

The Seven Base Dimensions

Physical Quantity Dimension Symbol Definition
Length [L] Space or distance
Mass [M] Amount of substance
Time [T] Interval between events
Electric Current [A] Flow of charge
Thermodynamic Temperature [Θ] or [K] Measure of thermal energy
Amount of Substance [N] Number of moles
Luminous Intensity [J] Brightness as perceived

Dimensional Formulae of Common Quantities

Here's how to derive the dimensional formula for any physical quantity. Start with its definition or the equation defining it, then express each term in terms of base dimensions.

Mechanical Quantities

Quantity Definition / Equation Dimensional Formula
Area Length × Width [L²]
Volume Length × Width × Height [L³]
Density Mass / Volume [M L⁻³]
Velocity Displacement / Time [L T⁻¹]
Acceleration Change in velocity / Time [L T⁻²]
Force Mass × Acceleration [M L T⁻²]
Momentum Mass × Velocity [M L T⁻¹]
Pressure Force / Area [M L⁻¹ T⁻²]
Energy / Work Force × Distance [M L² T⁻²]
Power Energy / Time [M L² T⁻³]

Example: Deriving Dimensional Formula for Force

Newton's second law states: \(F = ma\)

Dimensions of force = Dimensions of (mass × acceleration)

\[[F] = [M] \times [L T^{-2}] = [M L T^{-2}]\]

Example: Deriving Dimensional Formula for Work

Work is defined as: \(W = F \cdot d\) (Force applied over a distance)

\[[W] = [M L T^{-2}] \times [L] = [M L^2 T^{-2}]\]

This is the same as the dimensions of energy—they're actually the same physical quantity!

Dimensionless Quantities

Some quantities have no dimensions—they're pure numbers. These include:

  • Ratios: Efficiency = Output / Input has dimensions [anything]/[same thing] = dimensionless
  • Angles: Radian = Arc length / Radius has dimensions [L]/[L] = dimensionless
  • Refractive index: n = c / v (speed of light / speed in medium) is dimensionless
  • Relative density: Density of substance / Density of water is dimensionless
  • Mathematical functions: sin(θ), cos(θ), log(x) are dimensionless when the argument is dimensionless

Important: You cannot take the sine of something with dimensions. \(\sin(5 \text{ m})\) makes no sense. But \(\sin(5 \text{ rad})\) makes sense because radians are dimensionless.

Did You Know?
The fine structure constant, denoted α and equal to approximately 1/137, is a dimensionless quantity. It determines how strongly electrons interact with photons. Its value is mysterious—no one knows why it's exactly 1/137. Finding the origin of this number is one of the deepest questions in physics!

5. Dimensional Analysis and Its Applications

What Is Dimensional Analysis?

Dimensional analysisTechnique of analyzing relationships between physical quantities using their dimensions is a powerful technique that uses the dimensions of quantities to:

  • Check whether equations are dimensionally consistent
  • Derive unknown relationships between quantities
  • Convert between unit systems
  • Predict how physical quantities depend on other quantities

Principle of Homogeneity of Dimensions

An equation is dimensionally consistent if both sides have the same dimensions. This is the principle of homogeneitySameness in kind; here, same dimensions on both sides.

Example: Is the equation \(v = u + at\) dimensionally correct?

  • Left side: \([v] = [L T^{-1}]\)
  • Right side: \([u] + [at] = [L T^{-1}] + [L T^{-2}] \times [T] = [L T^{-1}] + [L T^{-1}]\)
  • Both sides equal \([L T^{-1}]\), so the equation is dimensionally correct ✓

Application 1: Checking Dimensional Consistency of Equations

This is the simplest and most practical application. If you make an error while deriving an equation, dimensional analysis will catch it.

Example: A student derives that kinetic energy is \(KE = \frac{1}{2} m v^2\). Is this correct?

  • Left side: \([KE] = [M L^2 T^{-2}]\) (energy has these dimensions)
  • Right side: \([m v^2] = [M] \times [L T^{-1}]^2 = [M] \times [L^2 T^{-2}] = [M L^2 T^{-2}]\)
  • Both sides match, so the equation could be correct ✓

Note: Dimensional consistency is necessary but not sufficient. An equation can be dimensionally correct but physically wrong. For example, \(KE = m v^2\) is dimensionally correct but physically incorrect (missing the factor of 1/2). Dimensional analysis tells you whether an equation might be right, not whether it is right.

Application 2: Deriving Relationships Using Dimensional Analysis

If you know which quantities affect a physical process but not the exact relationship, dimensional analysis can help you find it.

Example: Deriving the Period of a Pendulum

A simple pendulum's period \(T\) (time for one swing) depends on:

  • Length of the string: \(L\) with dimension \([L]\)
  • Acceleration due to gravity: \(g\) with dimension \([L T^{-2}]\)
  • Mass of the bob: \(m\) with dimension \([M]\)

We assume \(T\) depends on these as: \(T = k \cdot L^a \cdot g^b \cdot m^c\) where \(k\) is a dimensionless constant.

Dimensionally:

\[[T] = [L]^a \cdot [L T^{-2}]^b \cdot [M]^c\]

\[[T] = [L^a] \cdot [L^b T^{-2b}] \cdot [M^c]\]

\[[T] = [M^c L^{a+b} T^{-2b}]\]

For dimensional consistency:

  • Power of M: \(0 = c\) → \(c = 0\) (period doesn't depend on mass!)
  • Power of T: \(1 = -2b\) → \(b = -1/2\)
  • Power of L: \(0 = a + b\) → \(a - 1/2 = 0\) → \(a = 1/2\)

Therefore:

\[T = k \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}\]

Experimental and theoretical analysis shows that \(k = 2\pi\), so:

\[T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}\]

Remarkable result: Dimensional analysis predicted that the period doesn't depend on the mass! This is correct and reflects a deep truth: all objects fall at the same rate in a gravitational field.

Example: Terminal Velocity of a Falling Object

A sphere falling through air eventually reaches a terminal velocity \(v_t\) where air resistance equals gravity. This depends on:

  • Radius: \(r\) with dimension \([L]\)
  • Density of air: \(\rho\) with dimension \([M L^{-3}]\)
  • Viscosity of air: \(\eta\) with dimension \([M L^{-1} T^{-1}]\)

Assume: \(v_t = k \cdot r^a \cdot \rho^b \cdot \eta^c\)

\[[L T^{-1}] = [L]^a \cdot [M L^{-3}]^b \cdot [M L^{-1} T^{-1}]^c\]

\[[L T^{-1}] = [L^a M^b L^{-3b} M^c L^{-c} T^{-c}]\]

\[[L T^{-1}] = [M^{b+c} L^{a-3b-c} T^{-c}]\]

For dimensional consistency:

  • Power of M: \(0 = b + c\)
  • Power of T: \(-1 = -c\) → \(c = 1\)
  • Power of L: \(1 = a - 3b - c\)

From these: \(b = -1\), so:

\[1 = a - 3(-1) - 1 = a + 3 - 1 = a + 2\]

\[a = -1\]

Therefore:

\[v_t = k \cdot r^{-1} \cdot \rho^{-1} \cdot \eta^1 = k \frac{\eta}{\rho \cdot r}\]

This is incorrect! The correct formula (from fluid mechanics) is \(v_t = \frac{2r^2 g (\rho_{\text{sphere}} - \rho_{\text{air}})}{9\eta}\), which depends on gravity. The reason: gravity wasn't included in the initial list of variables. This shows that dimensional analysis can only find relationships among the variables you provide.

Application 3: Unit Conversion

You can convert between unit systems by using the dimensions and conversion factors.

Example: Convert 60 km/h to m/s

\[60 \, \frac{\text{km}}{\text{h}} = 60 \, \frac{1000 \, \text{m}}{3600 \, \text{s}} = \frac{60 \times 1000}{3600} \, \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} = \frac{60}{3.6} \, \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} = 16.67 \, \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}\]

Example: Convert 5 g/cm³ to kg/m³

\[5 \, \frac{\text{g}}{\text{cm}^3} = 5 \times \frac{10^{-3} \, \text{kg}}{10^{-6} \, \text{m}^3} = 5 \times 10^{3} \, \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{m}^3} = 5000 \, \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{m}^3}\]

Limitations of Dimensional Analysis

While powerful, dimensional analysis has important limitations:

Limitation 1: Cannot Determine Dimensionless Constants

Dimensional analysis gave us \(T = k\sqrt{L/g}\), but it couldn't determine that \(k = 2\pi\). Numbers like \(2\pi\), \(1/2\), \(e\) are dimensionless and must be found by experiment or theoretical derivation.

Limitation 2: Cannot Handle Logarithmic or Trigonometric Functions

The equation \(N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}\) (radioactive decay) involves an exponential. You cannot derive this from dimensional analysis because you can't take e to a power with dimensions—the exponent must be dimensionless.

Limitation 3: Requires Knowing All Relevant Variables

As we saw with terminal velocity, if you omit a crucial variable (gravity), you get the wrong answer.

Limitation 4: Multiple Valid Forms

Sometimes multiple dimensionally consistent equations exist. For example, both \(F = ma\) and \(F^2 = (ma)^2\) are dimensionally consistent, but only the first is correct.

Did You Know?
In 1974, physicist Geoffrey Chew proposed that dimensional analysis could reconstruct physics without assuming any fundamental laws—that the consistency of dimensions alone could determine the universe! While this "bootstrap" approach didn't fully work out, dimensional analysis remains powerful enough to derive many physical laws. Physicist Richard Feynman called dimensional analysis "one of the most useful techniques in physics."

6. Errors in Measurement

Why Do Measurements Have Errors?

No measurement is perfect. Every time you measure something, your answer differs slightly from the true value. Errors arise from:

  • Limitations of instruments
  • Environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, vibrations)
  • Human judgment and skill
  • Fundamental uncertainty at quantum scales

Understanding and quantifying errors is as important as making measurements. A measurement without an error estimate is useless. A physicist who reports "The charge of an electron is \(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\) C" without saying how precisely they know this has told you nothing—it could be precise to the nearest 10⁻¹⁹ C or might be off by a factor of 2.

Types of Errors

Systematic Errors

Systematic errors are consistent, predictable errors that bias all measurements in the same direction. They arise from:

  • Instrumental errors: A ruler that's shorter than 30 cm but labeled "30 cm" will make all your length measurements too large. A spring balance whose zero mark is offset will bias all mass measurements.
  • Personal errors: If you consistently read a thermometer 1°C higher than the true value due to your viewing angle, this introduces a systematic error.
  • Environmental errors: If you measure the length of a metal rod in a warm lab and then in a cold lab, thermal expansion will cause systematic differences.

How to identify and reduce systematic errors:

  • Compare your instrument with a calibrated standard (a "reference" ruler, thermometer, etc.)
  • Use multiple methods to measure the same quantity. If they agree, systematic errors are likely small
  • Identify and correct known biases (e.g., if a scale reads 0.5 kg when empty, subtract 0.5 kg from all readings)
  • Control environmental conditions

Random Errors

Random errors are unpredictable fluctuations that scatter measurements above and below the true value. They arise from:

  • Observational uncertainty: When measuring a moving pointer on a scale, different observers read it slightly differently.
  • Environmental noise: Vibrations in the lab, electrical noise in sensors, thermal fluctuations
  • Quantum uncertainty: At the atomic scale, there's inherent uncertainty (Heisenberg's uncertainty principle)

How to reduce random errors:

  • Repeat measurements multiple times. Random errors cancel out when you average
  • Use more precise instruments (e.g., a micrometer instead of a ruler)
  • Reduce environmental noise (use vibration isolation, shield from electromagnetic interference)
  • Improve technique (careful handling, proper alignment, etc.)

Quantifying Errors

Absolute Error

The absolute errorThe magnitude of the difference between measured and true value is the magnitude of the difference between your measured value and the true value:

\[\Delta a = |a_{\text{measured}} - a_{\text{true}}|\]

If you measure a length as 4.75 cm and the true length is 4.80 cm, the absolute error is:

\[\Delta L = |4.75 - 4.80| = 0.05 \, \text{cm}\]

Mean Absolute Error (Average Error)

If you make multiple measurements \(a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots, a_n\), calculate their mean:

\[\bar{a} = \frac{a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n}{n}\]

Then calculate the absolute error for each measurement (deviation from the mean):

\[\Delta a_i = |a_i - \bar{a}|\]

The mean absolute error is:

\[\Delta \bar{a} = \frac{\Delta a_1 + \Delta a_2 + \cdots + \Delta a_n}{n} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} |a_i - \bar{a}|\]

Example: You measure the mass of a ball five times: 105 g, 103 g, 107 g, 104 g, 106 g

  • Mean: \(\bar{m} = (105 + 103 + 107 + 104 + 106)/5 = 525/5 = 105 \, \text{g}\)
  • Deviations: |105-105| = 0, |103-105| = 2, |107-105| = 2, |104-105| = 1, |106-105| = 1
  • Mean absolute error: \(\Delta \bar{m} = (0 + 2 + 2 + 1 + 1)/5 = 6/5 = 1.2 \, \text{g}\)
  • Result: \(m = 105 \pm 1.2 \, \text{g}\)

Relative Error

The relative errorRatio of absolute error to the measured value, showing fractional uncertainty is the ratio of absolute error to the measured value:

\[\text{Relative Error} = \frac{\Delta a}{a}\]

This is dimensionless and tells you the fractional uncertainty. A relative error of 0.01 means you're uncertain by 1%, whereas a relative error of 0.1 means you're uncertain by 10%.

Percentage Error

The percentage error is simply the relative error expressed as a percentage:

\[\text{Percentage Error} = \frac{\Delta a}{a} \times 100\%\]

Example (continued): For the mass measurement above:

  • Relative error: \(\Delta m / m = 1.2 / 105 = 0.0114\)
  • Percentage error: \(0.0114 \times 100\% = 1.14\%\)

Combination of Errors

When you use measured quantities in calculations, how do errors propagate? The rules differ for addition/subtraction vs. multiplication/division.

For Addition and Subtraction

Absolute errors add:

\[\text{If } Z = A + B - C, \text{ then } \Delta Z = \Delta A + \Delta B + \Delta C\]

Example: You measure two lengths: \(L_1 = 10.0 \pm 0.1 \, \text{cm}\) and \(L_2 = 5.0 \pm 0.2 \, \text{cm}\)

If you add them: \(L_1 + L_2 = 15.0 \pm (0.1 + 0.2) = 15.0 \pm 0.3 \, \text{cm}\)

For Multiplication and Division

Relative errors add:

\[\text{If } Z = \frac{A \cdot B}{C}, \text{ then } \frac{\Delta Z}{Z} = \frac{\Delta A}{A} + \frac{\Delta B}{B} + \frac{\Delta C}{C}\]

Example: You measure the dimensions of a rectangular block:

  • Length: \(L = 10.0 \pm 0.1 \, \text{cm}\), relative error = 0.01 or 1%
  • Width: \(W = 5.0 \pm 0.2 \, \text{cm}\), relative error = 0.04 or 4%
  • Height: \(H = 2.0 \pm 0.1 \, \text{cm}\), relative error = 0.05 or 5%

Volume \(V = L \times W \times H = 10.0 \times 5.0 \times 2.0 = 100 \, \text{cm}^3\)

Relative error in volume: \(\frac{\Delta V}{V} = \frac{0.1}{10.0} + \frac{0.2}{5.0} + \frac{0.1}{2.0} = 0.01 + 0.04 + 0.05 = 0.10\) or 10%

Absolute error: \(\Delta V = 0.10 \times 100 = 10 \, \text{cm}^3\)

Result: \(V = 100 \pm 10 \, \text{cm}^3\)

For Powers

If \(Z = A^n\), then:

\[\frac{\Delta Z}{Z} = n \cdot \frac{\Delta A}{A}\]

Example: If you measure the radius of a sphere as \(r = 5.0 \pm 0.2 \, \text{cm}\), what is the error in the volume?

Volume: \(V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\)

The relative error depends only on \(r^3\):

\[\frac{\Delta V}{V} = 3 \cdot \frac{\Delta r}{r} = 3 \times \frac{0.2}{5.0} = 3 \times 0.04 = 0.12 \text{ or } 12\%\]

Notice how the error in volume (12%) is much larger than the error in radius (4%). Squaring or cubing measurements amplifies errors!

General Formula for Error Propagation

For a general function \(Z = f(A, B, C, \ldots)\), the absolute error is:

\[\Delta Z = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial A}\Delta A\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial B}\Delta B\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial C}\Delta C\right)^2 + \cdots}\]

This formula (using partial derivatives) is more accurate when errors are small and random. For Class 11, you won't need this level of detail, but it's good to know it exists.

Did You Know?
The most precise measurements in the world are made by physicists building gravitational wave detectors. The LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory) can detect changes in distance of about 10⁻¹⁸ meters—that's a millionth of a billionth of a meter! To achieve this, physicists had to account for errors from thermal vibrations, electromagnetic noise, and even the quantum uncertainty principle itself. When they detected gravitational waves in 2015, the signal was only about 10 times the measurement noise—a triumph of error analysis and instrumentation!

8. Exam Preparation — HOTS & Case-Based Questions

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

HOTS Q1: Why did scientists redefine the kilogram using Planck's constant rather than keeping the International Prototype Kilogram?
Answer: The International Prototype Kilogram was a physical artifact—a platinum-iridium cylinder kept in a vault in Paris. This definition had several critical flaws:
  • Non-invariability: The artifact was changing over time due to oxidation and dust accumulation. Scientists discovered it was losing mass at a rate of about 1 microgram per 100 years. This violated the fundamental requirement of a standard unit.
  • Non-accessibility: The kilogram was defined by a single object in Paris. Scientists worldwide couldn't access or reproduce the standard independently.
  • Non-reproducibility: You couldn't create another "kilogram" in your lab with arbitrary precision without referring back to the original artifact.

In 2019, the kilogram was redefined using Planck's constant (\(h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34}\) J·s), which is:

  • Invariable: A fundamental constant of nature that never changes
  • Accessible: Any physicist anywhere can measure it using Planck's constant experiments (like the kibble balance)
  • Reproducible: The definition is now based on nature, not artifacts

This was a historic achievement—for the first time in human history, all seven SI base units are defined by fundamental physical constants.

HOTS Q2: Derive the dimensional formula for the constant of proportionality in Coulomb's law: F = k(q₁q₂)/r²
Answer: Coulomb's law states that the electrostatic force between two charges is:

\[F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\]

Rearranging for k:

\[k = \frac{F r^2}{q_1 q_2}\]

Now express each quantity dimensionally:

  • [F] = [M L T⁻²] (force)
  • [r²] = [L²] (distance squared)
  • [q₁ q₂] = [A T] × [A T] = [A² T²] (charge = current × time)

Therefore:

\[[k] = \frac{[M L T^{-2}] \times [L^2]}{[A^2 T^2]} = \frac{[M L^3 T^{-2}]}{[A^2 T^2]} = [M L^3 T^{-4} A^{-2}]\]

The SI unit of k is N·m²/C², where 1 N·m²/C² = 1 kg·m³·s⁻⁴·A⁻² (which matches our dimensional formula).

Note: Coulomb's constant in SI is k ≈ 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C². Often, it's written as k = 1/(4πε₀) where ε₀ is the permittivity of free space.

HOTS Q3: A student measures the density of an object as (5.0 ± 0.3) kg/m³. Another student measures it as (5.00 ± 0.03) kg/m³. Which measurement is better? Why?
Answer: At first glance, the second measurement (5.00 ± 0.03) appears more precise because the error is smaller. But we must consider the relative errorFirst measurement: Relative error = 0.3/5.0 = 0.06 = 6%

Second measurement: Relative error = 0.03/5.00 = 0.006 = 0.6%

The second measurement has a much smaller relative error (0.6% vs 6%), so it's significantly better! The student who made the second measurement used a more precise instrument or technique.

Key lesson: Absolute error alone is not enough to judge measurement quality. You must consider relative error. A measurement with larger absolute error might still be better if the measured quantity is much larger.

For example: "The distance to the Moon is 384,400 ± 1000 km" (relative error 0.26%) is an excellent measurement, while "The distance to the door is 5.0 ± 2.0 m" (relative error 40%) is poor, even though the absolute error is much smaller in the second case.

Case-Based Questions

Case Q1: Measuring the Acceleration Due to Gravity Using a Pendulum

Scenario: A physics student performs an experiment to measure the acceleration due to gravity (g) using a simple pendulum. They use a string of length L = 1.00 ± 0.01 m and measure the time for 10 oscillations as T₁₀ = 20.0 ± 0.2 s, repeated 5 times.

(i) Calculate the period of the pendulum and find the error in T:

Solution:

Period T = T₁₀ / 10 = 20.0 / 10 = 2.00 s

Error: ΔT = ΔT₁₀ / 10 = 0.2 / 10 = 0.02 s

Result: T = 2.00 ± 0.02 s

(ii) Using T = 2π√(L/g), derive an expression for g and its error:

Solution:

From T = 2π√(L/g), squaring both sides:

\[T^2 = 4\pi^2 \frac{L}{g}\]

\[g = \frac{4\pi^2 L}{T^2}\]

For error in g, using the formula for products/quotients:

\[\frac{\Delta g}{g} = \frac{\Delta L}{L} + 2 \frac{\Delta T}{T}\]

Substituting values:

\[\frac{\Delta g}{g} = \frac{0.01}{1.00} + 2 \times \frac{0.02}{2.00} = 0.01 + 0.02 = 0.03\]

Calculate g:

\[g = \frac{4\pi^2 \times 1.00}{(2.00)^2} = \frac{4 \times 9.87 \times 1.00}{4.00} = 9.87 \text{ m/s}^2\]

Calculate Δg:

\[\Delta g = 0.03 \times 9.87 = 0.30 \text{ m/s}^2\]

Result: g = 9.87 ± 0.30 m/s² or g = (9.9 ± 0.3) m/s²

Case Q2: Measuring the Density of a Metal Cube

Scenario: A student measures the side of a metal cube three times: 2.45 cm, 2.43 cm, 2.46 cm. The mass is measured as 41.7 g (assuming ±0.1 g error). Calculate the density and its uncertainty.

Solution:

Step 1: Find the mean side length and its error

Mean side: s = (2.45 + 2.43 + 2.46)/3 = 7.34/3 = 2.45 cm

Deviations: |2.45 - 2.45| = 0, |2.43 - 2.45| = 0.02, |2.46 - 2.45| = 0.01

Mean absolute error: Δs = (0 + 0.02 + 0.01)/3 = 0.01 cm

Result: s = 2.45 ± 0.01 cm

Step 2: Calculate volume and its error

Volume: V = s³ = (2.45)³ = 14.7 cm³

For V = s³:

\[\frac{\Delta V}{V} = 3 \frac{\Delta s}{s} = 3 \times \frac{0.01}{2.45} = 0.0122\]

ΔV = 0.0122 × 14.7 = 0.18 cm³

Result: V = 14.7 ± 0.2 cm³

Step 3: Calculate density and its error

Density: ρ = m/V = 41.7 g / 14.7 cm³ = 2.84 g/cm³

Relative error:

\[\frac{\Delta \rho}{\rho} = \frac{\Delta m}{m} + \frac{\Delta V}{V} = \frac{0.1}{41.7} + 0.0122 = 0.0024 + 0.0122 = 0.0146\]

Δρ = 0.0146 × 2.84 = 0.04 g/cm³

Result: ρ = 2.84 ± 0.04 g/cm³

Note: This density is close to aluminum (2.70 g/cm³), so the metal could be aluminum or an aluminum alloy.

Case Q3: Dimensional Analysis of Projectile Motion

Scenario: A ball is thrown horizontally with initial velocity v₀ from a height h. Using only dimensional analysis, determine the form of the range R (horizontal distance traveled).

Solution:

The range R depends on:

  • Initial horizontal velocity: v₀ with dimension [L T⁻¹]
  • Height: h with dimension [L]
  • Acceleration due to gravity: g with dimension [L T⁻²]

Assume: R = k · v₀ᵃ · hᵇ · gᶜ where k is dimensionless

Dimensionally:

\[[L] = [L T^{-1}]^a \cdot [L]^b \cdot [L T^{-2}]^c\]

\[[L] = [L^a T^{-a}] \cdot [L^b] \cdot [L^c T^{-2c}]\]

\[[L] = [L^{a+b+c} T^{-a-2c}]\]

For dimensional consistency:

  • Power of L: 1 = a + b + c
  • Power of T: 0 = -a - 2c

From the second equation: a = -2c

Substituting into the first: 1 = -2c + b + c → b = 1 + c

We have one free parameter (c). Physically, gravity shouldn't increase the range (that would require c > 0), so c = 1/2 is reasonable. Then:

  • c = 1/2
  • a = -2(1/2) = -1... wait, this should be positive for range!

Let me reconsider. We need c = 1/2 (g¹/²) to get the time of flight term. Then a = -2(1/2) = -1 is wrong. Actually, from a + 2c = 0: a = -2c. If c = 1/2, then a = -1, which is negative!

The issue is my assumption. The correct analysis: a = 1 (range is proportional to initial velocity), b = 1/2 (range depends on √h because time of flight ∝ √h), c = -1/2 (to balance dimensions). But this doesn't make physical sense either (g in denominator).

Physical Result: Time of flight: \(t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}\)

Range: \(R = v_0 \cdot t = v_0 \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}\)

So: a = 1, b = 1/2, c = -1/2, and the constant k depends on the angle of projection.

Lesson: Dimensional analysis gives you the form, but you need physics reasoning to determine the signs and exact relationships.
