Exploring Forces

Class 8 Science | Chapter 5 | Physics

Every time you kick a ball, lift a book, or simply stand on the ground — forces are at play. But what exactly is a force, and why does everything in the universe obey its rules? In this chapter, we'll uncover the invisible power that shapes our world, from the gentle gravity pulling you toward Earth to the friction that lets you grip your pencil. Let's explore the fundamental concept of force and how it changes everything around us.

1. What is a Force?

A force is fundamentally a push or pull acting on an object. Whenever you interact with the world — kicking a ball, lifting a book, or even just standing — you're applying or experiencing forces.

Effects of Force

Force can produce remarkable changes in objects:

  • Change in speed: A force can accelerate an object (speed up) or decelerate it (slow down). When you press the brakes on a bicycle, friction force slows you down.
  • Change in direction: Even without changing speed, a force can alter direction. A fielder's hand catches a cricket ball, redirecting its motion.
  • Change in shape: Force can deform an object. Squeezing a sponge changes its shape; when you release it, the spring force pushes it back.

Nature of Force

Force is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude (how strong) and direction (which way). This is why saying "10 Newton force" is incomplete; you must also specify "10 Newton force to the right."

SI Unit of Force: Newton (N)

Named after Sir Isaac Newton, the legendary physicist. 1 Newton is the force needed to accelerate a 1 kg mass by 1 m/s². So the weight of a 1 kg object on Earth is approximately 10 N.

Balanced vs. Unbalanced Forces

Net Force

When multiple forces act on an object simultaneously, the overall effect is called the net force. If two forces cancel out, net force is zero. If they don't, the object experiences unbalanced force.

Balanced Forces Unbalanced Forces
Equal and opposite forces Net force ≠ 0
No change in motion Change in motion occurs
Example: Book resting on a table (weight = normal force) Example: Ball kicked by a foot
The Conversation Analogy

Think of force like a conversation between objects. A push force says, "Move away from me!" A pull force says, "Come closer to me!" When two objects push equally hard, they're saying the same thing to each other — so nothing moves. But when one pushes harder, the conversation becomes unbalanced, and motion follows.

2. Contact Forces

Contact forces require physical contact between two objects. You must touch something to apply a contact force.

Muscular Force

This is the force applied by muscles. You use muscular force constantly:

  • Pushing: Moving a chair across the room
  • Pulling: Opening a door or drawing water from a well
  • Lifting: Carrying a book or holding your friend
  • Kicking: Scoring a goal in football
  • Throwing: Tossing a ball to a friend

Friction: The Hidden Force

Friction is the force that opposes relative motion between surfaces in contact. It's one of the most important forces in daily life.

Key Property of Friction

Friction always acts opposite to the direction of motion. If you push a block to the right, friction pushes it to the left. If you slide down a slope, friction acts upward along the slope.

Factors Affecting Friction

  • Nature of surfaces: Rough surfaces produce more friction than smooth surfaces. Sandpaper on wood creates more friction than ice on ice.
  • Weight of the object: Heavier objects experience more friction. A heavy box is harder to slide than a light box on the same surface.

Advantages of Friction

Friction enables walking — without friction between shoes and ground, you'd slip like on ice.
Friction enables writing — pencil lead doesn't glide freely; friction holds it against paper.
Friction enables braking — brake pads use friction to stop vehicles.
Friction helps grip objects — you can hold a pen because friction grips it.

Disadvantages of Friction

Friction wears surfaces — constant rubbing erodes materials over time.
Friction produces heat — rub your hands together; they get warm. This is wasted energy.
Friction wastes energy — cars consume fuel partly to overcome friction.

Managing Friction

Reducing friction: Use lubricants (oil, grease), ball bearings in machines, streamlining (smooth shapes for air/water), polishing surfaces.

Increasing friction: Add treads to tires, use rough-soled shoes, apply textured brake pads, sand surfaces.

Normal Force

When an object rests on a surface, the surface pushes back on it perpendicular (at 90°) to the surface. This is the normal force. For a book on a table, the table's normal force balances the book's weight, keeping it from falling through.

3. Non-Contact Forces

Non-contact forces act at a distance without physical contact. The objects don't need to touch; the force reaches across space.

Gravitational Force

Gravity is the most dominant force in the universe. Every object attracts every other object, according to Newton's law of universal gravitation.

Newton's Law of Gravitation

$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

Where F is gravitational force, G is gravitational constant, m₁ and m₂ are masses, r is distance between them

The Earth's gravity pulls everything toward its center. This is why:

  • Objects fall downward when dropped
  • The moon orbits Earth
  • Earth orbits the sun
Weight vs. Mass

Mass is the amount of matter in an object (measured in kg). Weight is the gravitational force on that object. Weight = mg, where g ≈ 10 m/s² on Earth. So a 5 kg object weighs about 50 N.

Magnetic Force

Magnets attract certain metals across a distance. Magnetic force acts on ferromagnetic materials: iron, cobalt, nickel.

Key Properties:

  • Like poles repel: North pole of one magnet repels north pole of another
  • Unlike poles attract: North pole attracts south pole
  • Magnetic field: Invisible region around a magnet where its force acts

Magnetic forces power electric motors, hold refrigerator magnets, and guide compasses. Earth itself is a giant magnet with a magnetic field protecting us from solar radiation.

Electrostatic Force

Charged objects exert forces on each other. You create charge by rubbing:

  • Rubbing a comb creates negative charge; it then attracts small bits of paper
  • Rubbing a balloon on hair makes it stick to walls
  • Shuffling feet on carpet builds static charge; touching a metal surface causes a shock

Electrostatic Rules:

  • Like charges repel: Two negative charges push away from each other
  • Unlike charges attract: Positive and negative charges pull toward each other
Lightning: A Massive Electrostatic Discharge

During thunderstorms, clouds become heavily charged. When charge builds up enough, a massive electrostatic discharge occurs between cloud and ground — we see this as lightning, a giant spark of electricity.

The Personality Analogy

Think of charges like personalities. Two extroverts (like charges) clash and push apart. An extrovert and introvert (unlike charges) find each other attractive. Gravity, magnetism, and electrostatic forces all follow this pattern of attraction and repulsion.

4. Pressure: Force Per Unit Area

Pressure is a crucial concept: the same force on a smaller area creates more pressure. This is why sharp knives cut better than dull ones, and why camels don't sink in sand.

Pressure Formula

$$P = \frac{F}{A}$$

Where P is pressure (Pa), F is force (N), A is area (m²)

SI Unit: Pascal (Pa)

1 Pascal = 1 Newton per square meter (N/m²). It's a small unit; atmospheric pressure is about 101,325 Pa!

Pressure and Area: The Sharp Advantage

The Snowshoe Analogy

Imagine stepping on snow in winter. With regular shoes (small area), your weight concentrates on a tiny area — high pressure — so you sink. With snowshoes (large area), the same weight spreads over more area — low pressure — so you float on top.

Why Nails are Pointed

A nail's point has a tiny area. When you hit it with a hammer, the same force concentrates on that tiny point, creating enormous pressure — enough to break through resistance and penetrate material.

Why Knives are Sharp

A sharp blade has an incredibly thin edge. Pressing with force creates huge pressure at that edge, allowing it to slice through material easily. A dull blade spreads the same force over more area — less pressure — so it won't cut well.

Why Camels Have Wide Feet

Camels weigh hundreds of kilograms, but their wide feet spread that weight over a large area, creating low pressure on sand. This prevents them from sinking into the desert.

Why School Bags Have Wide Straps

The weight of books creates pressure on your shoulders. Wide straps distribute this weight over more area, reducing pressure and preventing shoulder pain. Thin straps concentrate pressure, causing discomfort.

Why Tanks Have Tracks

Military tanks weigh tons, but their tracks distribute weight over a long area. This reduces pressure and prevents sinking into mud. A person's sharp heels would create higher pressure and sink in the same mud.

Liquid Pressure

Pressure in liquids increases with depth. The deeper you go in water, the more water above you presses down. This is why:

  • Dam walls are thicker at the bottom to withstand higher pressure
  • Deep-sea creatures have special adaptations to survive crushing pressure
  • Submarines must be incredibly strong to withstand water pressure at depth

Atmospheric Pressure

The air above us has weight. The entire atmosphere presses down on Earth's surface, creating atmospheric pressure:

Atmospheric Pressure at Sea Level ≈ 101,325 Pa (1 atm)

That's like having a 10-ton weight sitting on every 1 m² of surface! You don't feel crushed because your body's internal pressure balances it.

Why we don't collapse: Your body contains air at the same pressure as outside. Internal pressure balances external pressure, so you stay intact.

Atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude: Higher up, there's less air above you. Mountaineers at Everest summit experience only about 34 kPa — one-third of sea-level pressure. This is why oxygen is scarce at high altitudes.

Atmospheric Pressure Demonstrations:

  • The Collapsing Can: Heat an aluminum can with a little water. Quickly flip it upside down into cold water. The sudden cooling causes internal pressure to drop, and atmospheric pressure crushes the can.
  • Drinking Through a Straw: When you suck on a straw, you reduce pressure inside. Atmospheric pressure on the drink's surface pushes it up the straw into your mouth.
  • Suction Cups: A suction cup sticks to walls because you remove air, creating low pressure inside. Atmospheric pressure pushes the cup against the wall.

5. Pressure in Fluids

Fluids include both liquids and gases. Both can transmit pressure, leading to remarkable applications.

Pascal's Law

Pascal's Law (Blaise Pascal, 1653)

When pressure is applied to an enclosed fluid, it is transmitted equally in all directions. This law revolutionized engineering.

Real-World Applications of Pascal's Law

Hydraulic Brakes

Your car's braking system uses Pascal's law. When you press the brake pedal:

  1. Your foot applies force to the pedal, increasing pressure in the master cylinder
  2. This pressure spreads through brake fluid in all directions
  3. It reaches the brake cylinders at all four wheels
  4. Increased pressure pushes brake pads against wheels, creating friction that stops the car

Genius: One small push at the pedal becomes four powerful braking forces!

Hydraulic Presses

Industrial hydraulic presses use Pascal's law to apply enormous force. A small piston with small area receives pressure, which transmits through fluid to a large piston with large area. The large piston experiences large force = small force × (large area / small area). Thus, modest effort becomes crushing power.

Hydraulic Jacks

These portable machines lift cars. Pump a small lever multiple times to gradually increase pressure in the hydraulic fluid. This pressure lifts the large piston, raising tons of weight with minimal human effort.

Hydraulic systems are everywhere: construction equipment, dentist chairs, hospital beds, airplane control surfaces — all leveraging Pascal's law to convert small forces into large useful forces.

7. Exam Prep: Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

These questions require deeper thinking beyond memorization. They build critical reasoning for competitive exams and board exams.

HOTS Q1: Why do heavy vehicles like trucks have wider tires than cars?

Hint: Consider pressure, weight distribution, and surface damage.

Answer: Trucks are much heavier than cars. If they had the same tire width, all that weight would concentrate on a small area of road, creating extremely high pressure. This would:

  • Damage the road surface (creating potholes and cracks)
  • Wear tire rubber quickly
  • Reduce traction and stability
Wider tires increase the contact area. The same weight is now distributed over more area, reducing pressure on the road. This protects both the road and the tires. Math: If truck weight is 10× that of a car, but tire area is 4× larger, pressure is only 2.5× higher — manageable.

HOTS Q2: If you push a heavy box on a rough floor and it doesn't move, what can you say about the forces?

Hint: Think about balanced forces, static friction, and Newton's first law.

Answer: The box experiences balanced forces — your push force is exactly balanced by static friction. The box remains stationary because:

  • Your push force (action) is ≤ maximum static friction force (reaction)
  • Since forces are balanced, net force = 0
  • With zero net force, the box won't accelerate (Newton's 1st law)
To move the box, you must apply force greater than maximum static friction. Once moving, kinetic friction (which is usually less) takes over, making it easier to keep it moving.

HOTS Q3: Astronauts float in space. Does this mean there is no gravity in space? Explain.

Hint: Recall Newton's law of gravitation: F ∝ 1/r². Consider falling, weightlessness, and orbits.

Answer: No! There IS gravity in space. Astronauts float not because gravity vanishes, but because they're in free fall.

  • Earth's gravity reaches far into space (it never truly ends, just gets weaker)
  • Gravity holds the International Space Station in orbit
  • The station and astronauts are falling together toward Earth, continuously missing it because they have sideways velocity
  • Since both astronaut and station fall at the same rate, the astronaut experiences no relative pressure force — hence floating
Analogy: It's like being inside an elevator in free fall. You'd float inside, not because gravity is gone, but because everything falls together. This is why NASA calls it "microgravity," not "zero gravity."

8. Case-Based Questions (CBSE Format 2025-26)

These questions present a scenario and ask you to analyze using concepts learned.

Case Study 1: The Friction Experiment

Scenario: A physics teacher conducted an experiment with a wooden block being pulled across different surfaces using a force meter (spring scale).

  • Polished marble surface: Force needed = 2 N
  • Rough wooden surface: Force needed = 5 N
  • Sandpaper surface: Force needed = 8 N

Questions:

a) Which surface has the most friction? Why?

Answer: Sandpaper has the most friction (8 N needed). Friction depends on surface roughness. Sandpaper is extremely rough with many irregularities that catch and resist the block's motion.

b) If the teacher used a heavier block (double the mass), what would you expect?

Answer: The force needed would approximately double. Heavier objects press harder on the surface, increasing friction force proportionally.

c) How could you reduce friction on the rough wooden surface?

Answer: Apply lubricant (oil, grease) or polish the surface. Lubrication fills microscopic gaps, making surfaces smoother. Alternatively, use ball bearings to replace sliding friction with rolling friction.

Case Study 2: The Pressure Calculation Challenge

Scenario: A student has a nail and a flat piece of metal, both made from the same material. Both weigh 100 grams. The nail's point has area 1 mm², and the flat piece's base has area 100 mm². The student presses both with the same force against a wooden board.

Questions:

a) Calculate the pressure exerted by each. (Assume force = weight ≈ 1 N)

Answer:

Nail: P = F/A = 1 N / (1 × 10⁻⁶ m²) = 1,000,000 Pa (1 MPa)

Flat piece: P = F/A = 1 N / (100 × 10⁻⁶ m²) = 10,000 Pa

b) Which one penetrates the wood deeper? Why?

Answer: The nail penetrates much deeper because its pressure is 100 times greater. High pressure can overcome material strength and create permanent deformation.

c) How does this relate to sharp vs. dull tools?

Answer: A sharp tool has a smaller contact area, creating higher pressure from the same force. This makes cutting and piercing much more efficient. A dull tool spreads the same force over more area, reducing pressure below the material's cutting threshold.

Case Study 3: Hydraulic Systems in Everyday Life

Scenario: A dentist's chair uses a hydraulic system. The foot pump (small piston) has area 5 cm², while the chair lift (large piston) has area 250 cm². A patient weighing 600 N sits on the chair.

Questions:

a) By Pascal's law, if you pump the pedal with 50 N force, what pressure is created in the fluid?

Answer: P = F/A = 50 N / (5 × 10⁻⁴ m²) = 100,000 Pa

b) This pressure acts on the large piston. What force is generated there?

Answer: F = P × A = 100,000 Pa × (250 × 10⁻⁴ m²) = 2,500 N

c) Can this system lift the patient? Why or why not?

Answer: Yes! The force generated (2,500 N) is much greater than the patient's weight (600 N). The system multiplies your modest pump force into a large lifting force, demonstrating the mechanical advantage of hydraulic systems. Pump multiple times to build pressure gradually.

d) Give two other examples of hydraulic systems you encounter daily.

Answer: Car brakes, excavators, airplane landing gear, hospital beds, construction equipment, forklift trucks.

Glossary of Key Terms

Force
A push or pull that can change an object's motion, direction, or shape. Vector quantity with magnitude and direction.
Newton (N)
SI unit of force. The force needed to accelerate 1 kg mass by 1 m/s².
Friction
Contact force opposing relative motion between surfaces. Acts opposite to direction of motion.
Gravity
Non-contact force where every object attracts every other object. Strongest at Earth's surface, decreases with altitude.
Pressure
Force per unit area. P = F/A. Same force on smaller area produces more pressure.
Pascal (Pa)
SI unit of pressure. 1 Pa = 1 N/m². Atmospheric pressure ≈ 101,325 Pa at sea level.
Contact Force
Force requiring physical contact between objects. Examples: friction, muscular force, normal force.
Non-Contact Force
Force acting at a distance without physical contact. Examples: gravity, magnetism, electrostatic force.
Balanced Forces
Equal and opposite forces resulting in zero net force. Object remains at rest or moves at constant velocity.
Unbalanced Forces
Forces where net force ≠ 0. Results in acceleration or change in motion.
Magnetic Force
Non-contact force between magnets and ferromagnetic materials. Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.
Electrostatic Force
Non-contact force between charged objects. Like charges repel, unlike charges attract.
Atmospheric Pressure
Pressure exerted by weight of air column above. ~101,325 Pa at sea level, decreases with altitude.
Pascal's Law
Pressure applied to enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions. Basis for hydraulic systems.

Remediation Guide: If You're Struggling...

Struggling with friction?

Remember: friction opposes motion. Rub your hands together — they get warm because friction converts motion into heat. More rough surfaces = more friction. Friction is your friend when you need grip (walking, writing) but an enemy when you need movement (sliding, skating).

Struggling with balanced vs. unbalanced forces?

Balanced = no change in motion. Think of a book on a table: weight pushes down, table pushes up — they balance, so book stays still. Unbalanced = motion changes. Push a book across the table: your push overcomes friction, forces unbalance, book accelerates.

Struggling with pressure calculations?

Remember P = F/A. Bigger area = smaller pressure. Smaller area = bigger pressure. A nail's tiny point creates huge pressure. A camel's wide feet create small pressure. Use this intuition: if you're the force, would you rather push on a pointy pin (ouch!) or a pillow? That's pressure!

Struggling with non-contact forces?

These act at a distance: gravity (objects fall), magnets (attract/repel without touching), electricity (charges push/pull). The key: no contact needed. Imagine invisible hands pushing or pulling — that's non-contact force.

Struggling with why astronauts float?

Gravity is still there! The space station orbits Earth — it's constantly falling, just missing Earth because it has sideways velocity. Astronauts inside fall at the same rate as the station, so they float relative to it. Like an elevator falling with the button stuck down — you'd float inside, not because gravity disappeared, but because everything falls together.

10. Key Takeaways: The Big Picture

  • Force is fundamental: It's what makes things move, stop, or change direction. Newton's laws explain all motion in the universe.
  • Two types of forces: Contact forces (friction, muscular, normal) require touching. Non-contact forces (gravity, magnetism, electricity) act at distance.
  • Friction is everywhere: It enables walking, writing, braking. It also wastes energy and wears surfaces. Understanding when to reduce or increase friction is crucial.
  • Pressure = Force/Area: Sharp knives cut well, nails penetrate, camels don't sink — all because of pressure. Same force on smaller area = more pressure.
  • Gravity is universal: Every object attracts every other object. It keeps us on Earth, holds planets in orbits, and shapes the cosmos.
  • Balanced vs. unbalanced forces determine motion: When forces balance, nothing accelerates. When they don't, motion changes.
  • Fluids transmit pressure: Pascal's law makes hydraulic systems work — small pumps move heavy loads. Your car's brakes use this principle.
  • Atmospheric pressure is real: We live at the bottom of an ocean of air. This pressure is usually invisible but becomes obvious in mountains or when air pressure changes suddenly.

11. Additional Practice Problems

Problem 1: Friction Calculation

A 50 kg crate is pulled across a concrete floor with a rope. The force of friction opposing motion is 150 N. How much force must you apply to pull at constant speed? (Hint: At constant speed, net force = 0)

Solution: For constant speed, applied force = friction force = 150 N.

Problem 2: Pressure Comparison

A 70 kg person stands on the ground. Their shoe sole area is 200 cm². What pressure do they exert? (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution: F = mg = 70 × 10 = 700 N. P = F/A = 700 N / (200 × 10⁻⁴ m²) = 700 / 0.02 = 35,000 Pa

Problem 3: Hydraulic System

A hydraulic jack has input piston area 10 cm² and output piston area 100 cm². If you apply 50 N to the input, what force is exerted by the output piston?

Solution: By Pascal's law, pressure is the same throughout. P = 50 N / (10 × 10⁻⁴ m²) = 50,000 Pa. Force at output = P × A = 50,000 × (100 × 10⁻⁴) = 500 N. Mechanical advantage = 500/50 = 10×

Problem 4: Gravity and Weight

An astronaut weighs 800 N on Earth (g = 10 m/s²). What is their mass? What would they weigh on the Moon (g = 1.6 m/s²)?

Solution: Mass = W/g = 800/10 = 80 kg. On Moon: W = 80 × 1.6 = 128 N. (Mass stays same, weight changes!)
