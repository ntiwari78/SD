Bar Magnet: Magnetic Field and Properties

Magnetic Field Lines

Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines that represent the direction and strength of a magnetic field. They originate from the North pole and terminate at the South pole. The density of field lines indicates the strength of the magnetic field.

Key Characteristics:
  • Field lines never intersect each other
  • They form closed loops through the magnet
  • Closer spacing indicates stronger field
  • Field lines are continuous
[Magnetic field lines around a bar magnet - visualization showing N and S poles with field lines]

Magnetic Dipole Moment

The magnetic dipole moment (m) is a quantitative measure of the strength of a magnetic source. It is the product of the pole strength and the distance between the poles.

m = q_m × 2l where: q_m = pole strength, 2l = distance between poles Unit: A·m² (Ampere-meter squared)

The magnetic dipole moment is a vector quantity pointing from South to North pole inside the magnet.

Magnetic Field on Axial Line

The magnetic field at a point on the axial line (passing through both poles) at distance r from the center of a bar magnet:

B_axial = (μ₀/4π) × (2m/r³) where: μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A

The field is maximum at the poles and decreases with the cube of distance. The field direction is along the axis, pointing away from South pole.

Magnetic Field on Equatorial Line

The magnetic field at a point on the equatorial line (perpendicular bisector) at distance r from the center:

B_equatorial = -(μ₀/4π) × (m/r³) (negative sign indicates field opposes dipole moment)
Important Relations:
  • B_axial = -2 × B_equatorial (at same distance)
  • Magnitude ratio: |B_axial|/|B_equatorial| = 2

Gauss's Law for Magnetism

Gauss's law for magnetism states that the total magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero. This is because magnetic monopoles do not exist—magnetic field lines always form closed loops.

∮ B · dA = 0 (Gauss's law for magnetism)
Physical Significance:
  • No magnetic monopoles exist in nature
  • Every magnetic field line that enters a surface must exit it
  • Net magnetic flux through any closed surface is zero
  • Magnetic poles always come in pairs

Earth's Magnetism

Introduction

Earth acts as a huge magnetic dipole with the magnetic South pole near the geographic North pole. The Earth's magnetic field is believed to be generated by convection currents of molten iron in the outer core.

Properties of Earth's Magnetic Field:
  • Field strength varies from 25 to 65 μT
  • Varies with geographic location
  • Slowly changes with time
  • Approximately dipole-like far from Earth

Magnetic Declination

Magnetic declination (θ) is the angle between the true geographic north and the magnetic north direction at a location. It varies with geographic location and time.

Important Points:
  • Declination is positive (east) or negative (west)
  • Varies from -20° to +20° in most inhabited regions
  • Changes with time at a rate of 0.01-0.1° per year
  • Zero declination locations form agonic lines

Angle of Dip (Inclination)

The angle of dip (δ) is the angle between the magnetic field direction and the horizontal at a location. It is also called magnetic inclination or inclination.

Characteristics of Dip:
  • δ = 0° at the magnetic equator (field horizontal)
  • δ = 90° at the magnetic poles (field vertical)
  • Positive in the Northern hemisphere (downward)
  • Negative in the Southern hemisphere (upward)

Components of Earth's Magnetic Field

The Earth's magnetic field at any location can be resolved into two perpendicular components:

Horizontal component: B_h = B cos(δ) Vertical component: B_v = B sin(δ) where: B = total magnetic field strength, δ = angle of dip
[Vector diagram showing B, B_h, B_v and angle δ]
Key Relations:
  • B² = B_h² + B_v²
  • tan(δ) = B_v / B_h
  • Horizontal component is independent of latitude for navigation

Magnetisation, Magnetic Intensity & Susceptibility

Magnetisation (M)

Magnetisation is the magnetic dipole moment per unit volume of a material. It represents the degree of alignment of magnetic dipoles within the material.

M = m_total / V = Σm / V Unit: A/m (Amperes per meter)

Magnetisation is a vector quantity pointing in the direction of the net magnetic dipole moment. It increases with applied magnetic field and decreases with temperature.

Magnetic Intensity (H)

Magnetic intensity (or magnetic field strength) is defined as the magnetic field produced by external sources, excluding the field due to magnetisation of the material.

H = B₀/μ₀ = F/(I × L) where: B₀ = field in vacuum, F = magnetomotive force (MMF) Unit: A/m
Important Distinction:
  • H is the applied field (independent of material)
  • B is the total field (includes material effects)
  • H determines how much M is induced

Magnetic Susceptibility (χ)

Magnetic susceptibility is a dimensionless quantity that measures how easily a material gets magnetised. It is defined as the ratio of magnetisation to magnetic intensity.

χ = M / H (dimensionless quantity)
Physical Meaning:
  • χ > 0: Paramagnetic and ferromagnetic (attracted)
  • χ < 0: Diamagnetic (repelled)
  • Larger |χ| means easier magnetisation
  • Typical values: χ ~ 10⁻⁶ to 10³

Magnetic Permeability (μ)

Magnetic permeability is a measure of how easily a material can be magnetised. Relative permeability (μᵣ) is the ratio of permeability of the material to that of free space.

B = μ₀(H + M) [Fundamental relationship] μ = μ₀(1 + χ) [Absolute permeability] μᵣ = μ/μ₀ = 1 + χ [Relative permeability]
Key Relations:
  • B = μH = μ₀μᵣH
  • M = χH = (μᵣ - 1)H
  • For diamagnetic: μᵣ slightly less than 1 (0.99-0.999)
  • For paramagnetic: μᵣ slightly greater than 1 (1.001-1.01)
  • For ferromagnetic: μᵣ >> 1 (100 to 100000)

Relationship Between B, H, and M

The fundamental relationship connecting magnetic field, intensity, and magnetisation:

B = μ₀(H + M) This is the constitutive relation in magnetism
[Vector diagram showing B, H, M relationship in a material]

This equation shows that the total magnetic field B is the sum of the applied field (μ₀H) and the contribution from magnetisation (μ₀M).

Classification of Magnetic Materials

Diamagnetism

Diamagnetism is the property of materials to develop a weak magnetic moment in the opposite direction to an applied magnetic field, resulting in repulsion from the field.

Characteristics:
  • χ is negative (typically -10⁻⁵ to -10⁻³)
  • μᵣ < 1 (slightly less than 1)
  • Occurs in all materials (weak effect masked in paramagnetic/ferromagnetic)
  • Independent of temperature
  • Due to Lenz's law effect on electron orbits

Examples: Copper, Bismuth, Gold, Water, Graphite

Diamagnetic force: Causes repulsion from magnetic field Application: Magnetic levitation (diamagnetic levitation)

Paramagnetism

Paramagnetism is the property of materials having permanent magnetic dipoles that align with an applied magnetic field, resulting in attraction to the field.

Characteristics:
  • χ is positive (typically 10⁻⁶ to 10⁻³)
  • μᵣ > 1 (slightly greater than 1)
  • Obeys Curie's law
  • Magnetisation is proportional to applied field
  • Due to unpaired electrons in atoms/ions

Examples: Aluminum, Manganese, Chromium, Oxygen, Platinum

Curie's Law: χ = C/T where: C = Curie's constant, T = absolute temperature (K)

Ferromagnetism

Ferromagnetism is the strong, permanent magnetic property of materials that retain magnetisation even after removal of applied field due to aligned electron spins.

Characteristics:
  • χ is large and positive (100 to 100000)
  • μᵣ >> 1 (very large)
  • Shows hysteresis (non-linear B-H relationship)
  • Magnetic domains exist (regions of aligned spins)
  • Has a Curie temperature above which ferromagnetism disappears

Examples: Iron, Cobalt, Nickel, and their alloys

Curie Temperature

The Curie temperature (Tc) is the temperature above which a ferromagnetic material loses its permanent magnetic properties and becomes paramagnetic.

χ = C/(T - Tc) [Modified Curie's law for ferromagnetics] where: Tc = Curie temperature
Material Curie Temperature (°C)
Iron (Fe) 770
Cobalt (Co) 1115
Nickel (Ni) 358
Gadolinium (Gd) 16
Physical Reason: At T > Tc, thermal energy exceeds exchange interaction energy, disrupting magnetic domain alignment.

Magnetic Hysteresis

Magnetic hysteresis is the phenomenon where the magnetisation of a ferromagnetic material lags behind the applied magnetic field due to domain resistance.

Hysteresis Curve Properties:
  • Non-linear B-H relationship
  • Closed loop shape showing energy loss
  • Area of loop = energy loss per cycle
  • Curve is wider for hard magnets, narrower for soft magnets
[Hysteresis curve showing B vs H relationship with Br and Hc marked]

Retentivity and Coercivity

Two important parameters describing ferromagnetic materials:

Retentivity (Remanence): Br = Magnetisation remaining when H = 0 (Residual/remanent magnetic induction)
Coercivity: Hc = Reverse field needed to reduce B to zero (Coercive force needed to demagnetise)
Physical Meaning:
  • High Br: Material retains strong magnetisation
  • High Hc: Difficult to demagnetise (hard magnet)
  • Low Hc: Easy to demagnetise (soft magnet)

Permanent Magnets vs Soft Magnets

Property Permanent Magnets (Hard) Soft Magnets
Retentivity (Br) High (large remaining magnetisation) Low (little remaining magnetisation)
Coercivity (Hc) High (difficult to demagnetise) Low (easy to demagnetise)
Hysteresis Loop Wide loop (large area) Narrow loop (small area)
Energy Loss Large (high energy dissipation) Small (low energy dissipation)
Examples Steel, Alnico, Neodymium alloys Iron, Silicon steel, Mu-metal
Applications Permanent magnets, loudspeakers Electromagnets, transformer cores

Magnetic Domains

Magnetic domains are regions within a ferromagnetic material where atomic magnetic moments are aligned in the same direction, contributing to net magnetisation.

Domain Properties:
  • Typical size: 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻² mm
  • Each domain has uniform magnetisation direction
  • Separated by domain walls (thin transition regions)
  • In unmagnetised material: random domain orientation
  • In magnetised material: preferential domain alignment

When applied field increases, domains aligned with field grow (wall movement), while other domains shrink. This explains the non-linear hysteresis behavior.

Case-Based Study Questions

Case Study 1: Magnetic Levitation Technology

A magnetic levitation train uses diamagnetic properties of pyrolytic graphite. The material experiences repulsion from permanent magnets below the track. Given the negative susceptibility of graphite (χ ≈ -0.2), explain why this creates levitation and discuss the stability conditions required for practical implementation.

Case Study 2: Transformer Core Design

Transformer cores use silicon steel (soft magnetic material) rather than permanent magnet steel. Analyze the hysteresis curves of both materials and explain why soft magnets are superior for energy efficiency in transformers. Calculate the energy loss difference if hysteresis loop areas are A₁ = 100 J/m³ and A₂ = 5000 J/m³ for 1000 cycles/second.

Case Study 3: Compass Declination Correction

A surveyor uses a compass at a location where the magnetic declination is 8°W (magnetic north is 8° west of true north). If a surveying instrument is aligned along what appears to be true north using a compass, how much angular error occurs? What corrections would be needed for accurate measurements covering large distances?

Problem-Solving Tips:
  • Always identify the type of magnetic material (diamagnetic/paramagnetic/ferromagnetic)
  • Use B = μ₀(H + M) to relate field quantities
  • Remember that axial field is twice the equatorial field (magnitude) at same distance
  • For Earth's magnetism: resolve B into horizontal and vertical components using δ
  • Hysteresis curves show energy dissipation as area enclosed in B-H loop
  • Curie temperature marks transition from ferromagnetic to paramagnetic behavior
  • Magnetic susceptibility directly indicates ease of magnetisation
