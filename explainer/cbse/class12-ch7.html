1. Introduction to Alternating Current

Alternating current (AC) is an electric current that periodically reverses direction. It is the form of electric power delivered to businesses and residences, and is the form of electrical energy that consumers typically use when they plug in domestic appliances.

AC is generated by electromagnetic induction and is continuously changing in magnitude and direction.

Historical Context

The development of AC circuits was crucial for long-distance power transmission. Nikola Tesla's work on polyphase AC systems revolutionized electrical engineering in the late 19th century.

Advantages of AC over DC

  • Easy to transform using transformers
  • Efficient long-distance transmission
  • Lower power losses in transmission
  • Can be easily converted to DC using rectifiers
  • Simpler and more economical generation
  • Easier distribution and control

2. AC Voltage and Current

Instantaneous AC Voltage and Current

An alternating voltage or current can be represented by sinusoidal functions:

AC Voltage: v(t) = V₀ sin(ωt)
AC Current: i(t) = I₀ sin(ωt + φ)

Where:
• V₀ = Peak (maximum) voltage
• I₀ = Peak (maximum) current
• ω = Angular frequency (rad/s) = 2πf
• f = Frequency (Hz)
• T = Time period = 1/f = 2π/ω
• φ = Phase difference between V and I

RMS Values (Root Mean Square)

The RMS value is the effective value of an AC quantity. For a sinusoidal AC, the RMS value is:

RMS Voltage: Vrms = V₀/√2 ≈ 0.707V₀
RMS Current: Irms = I₀/√2 ≈ 0.707I₀

The RMS value produces the same heating effect as an equivalent DC current.
Household AC voltage in India is 230V RMS at 50 Hz frequency. The peak voltage is 230√2 ≈ 325V.

Peak-to-Peak Value

The peak-to-peak value is the difference between the maximum positive and maximum negative values:

Peak-to-Peak Value: Vpp = 2V₀ = 2√2 × Vrms
Quantity Peak Value RMS Value Average Value (Half Cycle)
Sinusoidal AC V₀ V₀/√2 2V₀/π

3. AC Through Individual Components

AC Through Pure Resistance (R)

When an AC voltage is applied across a pure resistor, the current through it is given by Ohm's law at every instant:

Instantaneous Current: i = V₀/R × sin(ωt)
Voltage and Current: v = V₀ sin(ωt), i = I₀ sin(ωt)

The voltage and current are in phase (φ = 0)
In a resistor, voltage and current oscillate together. The phase difference is zero.

Power Dissipation in Resistor

Instantaneous Power: P = vi = I₀²R sin²(ωt)
Average Power: Pavg = (1/2)I₀²R = Irms²R = Vrms²/R

Phasor Diagram for Resistor:

V and I are in phase (same direction)

AC Through Pure Inductance (L)

When an AC voltage is applied across a pure inductor (no resistance), the induced back EMF opposes the change in current:

Voltage across Inductor: v = V₀ sin(ωt)
Current through Inductor: i = I₀ sin(ωt - π/2)

Inductive Reactance: XL = ωL

Where: I₀ = V₀/XL = V₀/(ωL)
In an inductor, current lags voltage by π/2 (90°). The inductor opposes changes in current.

Reactance and Phase Relationship

Inductive Reactance: XL = ωL = 2πfL (in Ohms)
Phase Difference: φ = +π/2 (current lags voltage)
Impedance: Z = XL
Power: P = 0 (No real power dissipated, only reactive power)
An ideal inductor stores energy in its magnetic field and does not dissipate power (no resistance).

Energy Storage in Inductor

Energy is alternately stored in the magnetic field and returned to the circuit. The maximum energy stored is:

Maximum Energy: U = (1/2)LI₀²

AC Through Pure Capacitance (C)

When an AC voltage is applied across a pure capacitor, the charging current leads the voltage:

Voltage across Capacitor: v = V₀ sin(ωt)
Current through Capacitor: i = I₀ sin(ωt + π/2)

Capacitive Reactance: XC = 1/(ωC)

Where: I₀ = V₀ × XC = V₀ × ωC
In a capacitor, current leads voltage by π/2 (90°). The capacitor opposes changes in voltage.

Reactance and Phase Relationship

Capacitive Reactance: XC = 1/(ωC) = 1/(2πfC) (in Ohms)
Phase Difference: φ = -π/2 (current leads voltage)
Impedance: Z = XC
Power: P = 0 (No real power dissipated)
An ideal capacitor stores energy in its electric field and does not dissipate power (no resistance).

Energy Storage in Capacitor

Energy is alternately stored in the electric field and returned to the circuit:

Maximum Energy: U = (1/2)CV₀²
Component Impedance Phase Difference Power Dissipated
Resistor (R) R (Independent of ω) 0° (In phase) Pavg = I²R
Inductor (L) XL = ωL (Increases with ω) +90° (Current lags) 0 (Ideal inductor)
Capacitor (C) XC = 1/(ωC) (Decreases with ω) -90° (Current leads) 0 (Ideal capacitor)

4. Series LCR Circuit

Circuit Analysis

When R, L, and C are connected in series with an AC source, the total opposition to current flow is the impedance Z.

Total Impedance: Z = √[R² + (XL - XC)²]

Where:
• R = Resistance
• XL = Inductive Reactance = ωL
• XC = Capacitive Reactance = 1/(ωC)

Phase Difference

Phase Difference (φ): tan φ = (XL - XC)/R

• If XL > XC: Circuit is inductive, φ > 0 (current lags voltage)
• If XL < XC: Circuit is capacitive, φ < 0 (current leads voltage)
• If XL = XC: Circuit is resistive, φ = 0 (voltage and current in phase)
The impedance varies with frequency. At low frequencies, capacitive reactance dominates; at high frequencies, inductive reactance dominates.

Current in Series LCR Circuit

Peak Current: I₀ = V₀/Z
RMS Current: Irms = Vrms/Z
Instantaneous Current: i = I₀ sin(ωt - φ)

Voltage Distribution

The voltages across individual components can be represented as phasor quantities:

Voltage across R: VR = I₀R (in phase with current)
Voltage across L: VL = I₀XL (leads current by 90°)
Voltage across C: VC = I₀XC (lags current by 90°)

Total Voltage: V = √[VR² + (VL - VC)²]

Phasor Diagram

A phasor diagram represents the phase relationships between voltage and current. The following relationships hold:

• Current is the reference phasor (horizontal)
• VR is in phase with current (horizontal)
• VL leads current by 90° (vertical, upward)
• VC lags current by 90° (vertical, downward)
• Net voltage VL - VC acts vertically

Phasor Diagram Components:

The resultant voltage V is obtained by vector addition.

Quality Factor (Q-factor)

The quality factor measures the sharpness of resonance in an LCR circuit:

Q-factor: Q = ω₀L/R = 1/(ω₀RC) = (1/R)√(L/C)

Where ω₀ is the resonant angular frequency

• High Q: Narrow resonance peak (selective circuit)
• Low Q: Broad resonance peak (less selective)
A higher Q-factor indicates less resistance and sharper resonance. Q-factor also equals the voltage magnification at resonance.

5. Resonance in AC Circuits

Resonance Condition

Resonance occurs when the inductive and capacitive reactances are equal, making the impedance minimum and current maximum:

Resonance Condition: XL = XC
ωL = 1/(ωC)
ω₀² = 1/(LC)

Resonant Frequency: ω₀ = 1/√(LC) (in rad/s)
Resonant Frequency: f₀ = 1/(2π√(LC)) (in Hz)
At resonance, the circuit behaves as purely resistive with minimum impedance Z = R and maximum current I = V/R.

Impedance at Resonance

At Resonance:
Z = R (Minimum)
φ = 0 (Voltage and current in phase)
I = V/R (Maximum)
Power = V²/R (Maximum)

Frequency Response

The impedance and current vary with frequency:

Bandwidth (BW): BW = f₀/Q = R/(ω₀L)

The bandwidth is the range of frequencies where the power is greater than half the maximum power.
The bandwidth decreases with increasing Q-factor. A high-Q circuit is more selective and has a narrower bandwidth.

Applications of Resonance

  • Radio Tuning: Selection of specific radio frequencies
  • Wireless Communication: Frequency selection and filtering
  • Power Transmission: Compensation of reactive power
  • Medical Applications: MRI and other diagnostic equipment
  • Vibration Control: Isolation of vibrations at specific frequencies

Resonance Curves

The response of an LCR circuit at different frequencies shows a peak at resonance. The sharpness of this peak depends on the Q-factor:

Higher Q-factor leads to a sharper peak and narrower bandwidth. Lower Q-factor leads to a broader peak and wider bandwidth.

6. Power in AC Circuits

Instantaneous Power

The instantaneous power in an AC circuit is the product of instantaneous voltage and current:

Instantaneous Power: P(t) = v(t) × i(t) = V₀I₀ sin(ωt) sin(ωt + φ)

Using trigonometric identity:
P(t) = (V₀I₀/2)[cos(φ) - cos(2ωt + φ)]

Average Power (Real Power)

The average power over one complete cycle is:

Average Power: Pavg = (1/2)V₀I₀ cos(φ)
Or: Pavg = Vrms × Irms × cos(φ)

Where cos(φ) is the power factor
Only the component of current that is in phase with voltage contributes to average power. The component perpendicular to voltage produces no average power.

Power Factor

The power factor determines what fraction of the apparent power is actually delivered to the load:

Power Factor: cos(φ) = R/Z

Where:
• cos(φ) = 1 (φ = 0°): All power is real (purely resistive)
• cos(φ) = 0 (φ = 90°): All power is reactive (purely reactive)
• 0 < cos(φ) < 1: Mixed load

Types of Power

Power Type Formula Unit Description
Real Power (P) P = Vrms Irms cos(φ) Watt (W) Power actually consumed and dissipated
Reactive Power (Q) Q = Vrms Irms sin(φ) VAR (Volt-Ampere Reactive) Power stored and returned by reactive components
Apparent Power (S) S = Vrms Irms VA (Volt-Ampere) Total power supplied to the circuit

Power Triangle

The power relationships form a right triangle:

Power Triangle Relationship:
S² = P² + Q²
(Vrms Irms)² = (Vrms Irms cos φ)² + (Vrms Irms sin φ)²

Wattless Current

In reactive circuits, part of the current is perpendicular to voltage and produces no average power:

Wattless Current Component: Iwattless = I₀ sin(φ) or Irms sin(φ)

This component is responsible for reactive power and oscillates back and forth without being consumed.
In pure reactive circuits (L or C alone), all current is wattless. In LCR circuits, only the component perpendicular to voltage contributes to wattless current.

Power Factor Improvement

In inductive loads, the power factor can be improved by adding capacitance in parallel:

Power Factor Improvement:
• Capacitive current leads voltage
• Inductive current lags voltage
• These currents can cancel each other partially
• Result: Reduced reactive power and improved power factor
Industries use power factor correction by adding capacitor banks in parallel with inductive loads to reduce VAR charges and improve efficiency.

7. Transformers

Basic Principle

A transformer is a device that changes the voltage level of AC current. It operates on the principle of electromagnetic induction and is based on Faraday's law.

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction:
ε = -N(dΦ/dt)

Where:
• ε = Induced EMF
• N = Number of turns
• Φ = Magnetic flux through the coil

Transformer Equation

For an ideal transformer with no losses:

Voltage Transformation Ratio:
Vs/Vp = Ns/Np

Current Transformation Ratio:
Is/Ip = Np/Ns

Where:
• Vp, Ip = Primary voltage and current
• Vs, Is = Secondary voltage and current
• Np, Ns = Number of turns in primary and secondary coils

Power Relationship

Power Conservation (Ideal Transformer):
Pp = Ps
Vp × Ip = Vs × Is

The power input equals the power output (no losses)
An ideal transformer has 100% efficiency. In practice, transformers are 95-99% efficient depending on design and materials.

Impedance Transformation

A transformer can match impedances between circuits:

Impedance Transformation:
Zs/Zp = (Ns/Np)²

Where Zs and Zp are the secondary and primary impedances.

Types of Transformers

Type Turns Ratio Voltage Effect Current Effect Application
Step-Up Ns > Np Vs > Vp Is < Ip Power transmission (high voltage)
Step-Down Ns < Np Vs < Vp Is > Ip Distribution and household use
Isolation Ns = Np Vs = Vp Is = Ip Electrical isolation, safety

Transformer Losses

Real transformers experience energy losses due to several factors:

1. Copper Loss (I²R Loss)

Copper Loss: Pcu = I²R

Heat generated due to resistance of the coil windings. Increases with current and coil resistance.

2. Iron Loss (Core Loss)

Iron Loss Components:
• Hysteresis loss: Due to magnetic property variation in core material
• Eddy current loss: Due to circulating currents in the core

Hysteresis loss: Ph = ηBmax^n × f
(η = hysteresis constant, n = Steinmetz index ≈ 1.6-2.0)

3. Flux Leakage Loss

Flux Leakage: Not all magnetic flux from primary couples to secondary, resulting in leakage inductance and voltage drop.

Transformer Efficiency

Efficiency: η = (Output Power / Input Power) × 100%
η = [Ps / (Ps + Losses)] × 100%
η = [Po / (Po + Pcu + Pfe)] × 100%

Where:
• Po = Output power
• Pcu = Copper loss
• Pfe = Iron loss
Typical transformer efficiencies range from 95-99%. Larger transformers have higher efficiency due to lower resistance-to-power ratio.

Practical Considerations

  • Cooling: Oil-filled or air-cooled designs dissipate heat
  • Core Material: Soft iron or silicon steel minimizes core losses
  • Wire Gauge: Thicker wires reduce copper loss
  • Laminated Core: Reduces eddy current losses
  • Shielding: Reduces electromagnetic interference
  • Load Testing: Ensures proper operation and efficiency

Applications of Transformers

  • Power Transmission: Step-up transformers at generation stations, step-down at distribution
  • Home Appliances: Chargers, adaptors, power supplies
  • Voltage Regulation: Automatic voltage regulators
  • Impedance Matching: Audio systems, communication circuits
  • Isolation: Patient safety in medical equipment
  • Measurement: Current and voltage transformers for meters

HOTS Questions (Higher Order Thinking Skills)

HOTS Question 1: Power Factor Correction

Question: A factory has an inductive load with power factor 0.6 lagging. The factory consumes 100 kW of real power. Calculate:

  1. The apparent power (S)
  2. The reactive power (Q)
  3. The value of capacitance needed to improve power factor to 0.9 (assume voltage 400V, 50 Hz)

Solution Approach:

Given: P = 100 kW, cos φ₁ = 0.6

Step 1: S = P/cos φ = 100/0.6 = 166.67 kVA

Step 2: Q₁ = √(S² - P²) = √(166.67² - 100²) = 133.33 kVAR

Step 3: For cos φ₂ = 0.9
φ₂ = cos⁻¹(0.9) = 25.84°
Q₂ = P tan(φ₂) = 100 × tan(25.84°) = 48.43 kVAR

Reactive power to be corrected: ΔQ = Q₁ - Q₂ = 133.33 - 48.43 = 84.9 kVAR

For capacitor: Q = V²/(ω × Xc) = ω × C × V²
C = Q/(ω × V²) = 84,900/(2π × 50 × 400²) ≈ 84.3 μF

HOTS Question 2: Transformer Design and Losses

Question: A 1000 VA transformer has copper loss of 20 W and iron loss of 15 W at rated load. If it operates at 75% load, calculate:

  1. The copper loss at 75% load
  2. The total loss at 75% load
  3. The efficiency at 75% load

Solution Approach:

Copper loss is proportional to current squared (I²R loss)

At rated load (100%): Pcu = 20 W
At 75% load: Pcu = 20 × (0.75)² = 20 × 0.5625 = 11.25 W

Iron loss remains approximately constant (depends on flux, not load):
Pfe = 15 W (constant)

Total loss at 75% load: Ptotal = 11.25 + 15 = 26.25 W

Output power at 75% load: Pout = 1000 × 0.75 = 750 W

Efficiency: η = Pout/(Pout + Ptotal) × 100%
η = 750/(750 + 26.25) × 100% = 96.6%

HOTS Question 3: LCR Circuit Analysis

Question: An LCR series circuit has R = 10 Ω, L = 0.1 H, C = 10 μF, and is connected to a 230V, 50 Hz AC source. Calculate:

  1. The impedance of the circuit
  2. The current through the circuit
  3. The phase difference
  4. The power factor and real power consumed
  5. Is the circuit resonant at this frequency?

Solution Approach:

Given: R = 10 Ω, L = 0.1 H, C = 10 μF = 10×10⁻⁶ F
V = 230 V (RMS), f = 50 Hz, ω = 2πf = 314.16 rad/s

Step 1: XL = ωL = 314.16 × 0.1 = 31.42 Ω
XC = 1/(ωC) = 1/(314.16 × 10×10⁻⁶) = 318.3 Ω

Step 2: Z = √[R² + (XL - XC)²] = √[10² + (31.42 - 318.3)²]
Z = √[100 + 82,406] = √82,506 ≈ 287.2 Ω

Step 3: I = V/Z = 230/287.2 = 0.801 A

Step 4: tan φ = (XL - XC)/R = (31.42 - 318.3)/10 = -28.69
φ = tan⁻¹(-28.69) ≈ -88.0°
Power factor: cos φ = cos(-88.0°) ≈ 0.0349 (capacitive)

Real power: P = VI cos φ = 230 × 0.801 × 0.0349 ≈ 6.43 W

Step 5: Resonant frequency: f₀ = 1/(2π√LC) = 1/(2π√(0.1 × 10×10⁻⁶))
f₀ ≈ 159.2 Hz
Since 50 Hz ≠ 159.2 Hz, the circuit is NOT at resonance at 50 Hz.

Case-Based Questions

Case Study 1: Power Transmission System

Background: A power generation station produces 10 MW of power at 11 kV. This power needs to be transmitted to a city 100 km away with transmission line resistance of 10 Ω. A step-up transformer increases the voltage to 110 kV for transmission.

Question A: Calculate the transmission loss if power is transmitted at 11 kV without step-up transformer.

At 11 kV: I = P/V = 10×10⁶/11×10³ = 909.1 A
Loss = I²R = (909.1)² × 10 = 8.26 MW
Loss percentage = (8.26/10) × 100% = 82.6%

Question B: Calculate the transmission loss with step-up transformer at 110 kV.

At 110 kV: I = P/V = 10×10⁶/110×10³ = 90.91 A
Loss = I²R = (90.91)² × 10 = 0.826 MW
Loss percentage = (0.826/10) × 100% = 8.26%

Question C: What is the voltage after step-down at the receiving end (assuming similar step-down ratio)?

Voltage drop in transmission line = I × R = 90.91 × 10 = 909.1 V
Voltage at receiving end of line = 110,000 - 909.1 = 109,090.9 V
After step-down transformer (110 kV → 11 kV): ≈ 10,909 V ≈ 10.9 kV

This shows the importance of step-up transformers in reducing transmission losses!

Case Study 2: Household AC Circuit Analysis

Background: A typical Indian household has:

  • Voltage supply: 230V, 50 Hz
  • Various loads: refrigerator (mostly capacitive), fan motor (mostly inductive), heater (resistive)

Question A: If a fan motor draws 5A at 0.8 power factor lagging, what is the real power consumed?

P = V × I × cos φ = 230 × 5 × 0.8 = 920 W

Question B: If a heater of 1000W runs for 5 hours daily, what is the monthly energy consumption (30 days)?

Energy = Power × Time = 1000 W × 5 hours × 30 days
= 150,000 Wh = 150 kWh = 0.15 MWh

Question C: If the electricity rate is Rs. 5 per kWh, what is the monthly bill for just the heater?

Cost = Energy × Rate = 150 kWh × Rs. 5/kWh = Rs. 750

Question D: Can we use a simple capacitor to completely correct the power factor of an AC circuit? Explain with reasoning.

No, we cannot completely correct the power factor if the load is mixed (R-L combination).

Reason: A capacitor provides leading current that opposes inductive current, but it cannot provide resistance. It can only neutralize the reactive component (XL). A purely resistive load always has unity power factor, but practical loads always have some resistance, so perfect correction is not possible with just a capacitor.

We can improve (but not perfect) the power factor to values close to unity by choosing the appropriate capacitance.

Case Study 3: Resonance in Radio Tuning

Background: A radio receiver contains an LCR tuning circuit with L = 10 μH and variable capacitor C. The circuit is tuned to receive different radio stations.

Question A: What should be the capacitance to tune to a station broadcasting at 1000 kHz?

At resonance: f₀ = 1/(2π√LC)
C = 1/(4π²f₀²L)
C = 1/(4π² × (10⁶)² × 10×10⁻⁶)
C = 1/(4π² × 10¹² × 10×10⁻⁶)
C = 1/(4π² × 10⁷) ≈ 253 pF

Question B: If the resistance of the circuit is 50 Ω, what is the Q-factor at resonance?

Q = ω₀L/R = 2πf₀L/R
Q = 2π × 10⁶ × 10×10⁻⁶ / 50
Q = 2π × 10 / 50 = 20π/50 = 0.4π ≈ 1.26

This relatively low Q-factor means the tuning circuit has a broader bandwidth, allowing reception of a range of frequencies around 1000 kHz.

Question C: What is the bandwidth of the receiver?

Bandwidth BW = f₀/Q = 10⁶/1.26 ≈ 793.7 kHz

This is actually quite broad. In practice, better selectivity is achieved with higher Q circuits.
