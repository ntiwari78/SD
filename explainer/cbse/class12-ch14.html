1. Energy Bands in Solids

Introduction to Band Theory

In solids, electrons occupy energy levels. When atoms come together, these discrete levels split and form continuous bands of energies. The two most important bands are:

Valence Band: The highest energy band that contains valence electrons at absolute zero temperature (T = 0 K).

Conduction Band: The next higher energy band, which is empty at T = 0 K. Electrons here are free to move and conduct electricity.

Band Gap (E_g)

The energy difference between the conduction band and valence band is called the band gap:

$$E_g = E_c - E_v$$

where E_c = conduction band edge, E_v = valence band edge

Classification of Materials by Band Gap

Material Type Band Gap (E_g) Characteristics Examples
Conductors (Metals) E_g ≈ 0 eV Valence and conduction bands overlap; electrons are always mobile Cu, Al, Ag, Fe
Semiconductors E_g ~ 1 eV Small gap; electrons can be promoted to conduction band with thermal energy or doping Si (E_g = 1.1 eV), Ge (E_g = 0.7 eV)
Insulators E_g > 3 eV Large gap; very few electrons in conduction band at room temperature Glass, Rubber, Porcelain
Key Insight: The conductivity of a material depends critically on how many free electrons are available. Metals have the highest conductivity (E_g = 0), followed by semiconductors, then insulators.

Energy Band Diagram

METALS: [Conduction Band] [Valence Band] ← Overlapping bands SEMICONDUCTORS: [Conduction Band] ↑ E_g ~ 1 eV [Valence Band] INSULATORS: [Conduction Band] ↑ E_g > 3 eV [Valence Band]

2. Intrinsic and Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic semiconductors are pure semiconductors without any dopant atoms. The most common are silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge).

Properties of Intrinsic Semiconductors:

  • At T = 0 K, valence band is completely filled; conduction band is empty
  • At room temperature, thermal energy creates electron-hole pairs
  • Number of free electrons = Number of holes: n_e = n_h = n_i
  • Conductivity increases with temperature (unlike metals)
$$n_i = \sqrt{N_c N_v} \exp\left(-\frac{E_g}{2k_B T}\right)$$

where n_i = intrinsic carrier concentration, N_c, N_v = effective densities of states, k_B = Boltzmann constant

Extrinsic Semiconductors (Doped Semiconductors)

When we add small amounts of impurities to a pure semiconductor, we create an extrinsic semiconductor. The process is called doping.

n-type Semiconductor (Negative type)

Dopant: Pentavalent atoms (5 valence electrons)
Examples: Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb)

Donor Atom: The extra electron (not needed for bonding) is loosely bound and easily excited to the conduction band, making it a donor.

Major Carrier: Electrons (n > p)
Minor Carrier: Holes

Fermi Level: Moves closer to the conduction band
$$n \gg p \quad \text{and} \quad n \approx N_D$$

where N_D = donor concentration

p-type Semiconductor (Positive type)

Dopant: Trivalent atoms (3 valence electrons)
Examples: Boron (B), Indium (In), Aluminum (Al)

Acceptor Atom: Creates an empty orbital (hole) in the valence band. The trivalent atom readily accepts an electron from the valence band, making it an acceptor.

Major Carrier: Holes (p > n)
Minor Carrier: Electrons

Fermi Level: Moves closer to the valence band
$$p \gg n \quad \text{and} \quad p \approx N_A$$

where N_A = acceptor concentration

Comparison Table

Property Intrinsic n-type p-type
Dopant None Pentavalent (P, As) Trivalent (B, In)
Majority Carrier Electrons & Holes (equal) Electrons (n_e) Holes (p_h)
Majority Carrier Concentration n_e = n_h = n_i n >> p p >> n
Conductivity (σ) Low High High
Temperature Dependence Increases (thermal excitation) Decreases (ionization freezes out) Decreases (ionization freezes out)

Remember: In an n-type semiconductor, electrons are the majority carriers (responsible for most current), even though holes also exist. Similarly, in p-type, holes are the majority carriers.

3. The p-n Junction and Diode

Formation of p-n Junction

A p-n junction is formed when p-type and n-type semiconductors are joined together. At the junction, several important phenomena occur:

Diffusion and Drift

  • Diffusion: Due to concentration gradient, electrons diffuse from n-region to p-region, and holes diffuse from p-region to n-region.
  • Recombination: When electrons and holes meet, they recombine, creating an electric field.
  • Drift: The electric field causes electrons and holes to drift in opposite directions, opposing further diffusion.

Depletion Region and Barrier Potential

Depletion Region: The region near the junction where mobile charge carriers (electrons and holes) have been removed due to recombination. It contains only fixed ions (positively charged donor ions in n-region, negatively charged acceptor ions in p-region).

Barrier Potential (V_B): The potential difference across the depletion region that opposes further diffusion of carriers.
$$V_B = \frac{k_B T}{e} \ln\left(\frac{N_A N_D}{n_i^2}\right)$$

At room temperature (T ≈ 300 K): V_B ≈ 0.7 V for Si, ≈ 0.3 V for Ge

p-type region Depletion Region n-type region (holes) (no mobile carriers) (electrons) p p p p |↔↔↔↔↔↔↔↔↔↔↔| n n n n | E-field | | Barrier | | V = 0.7V |

Forward Bias

Forward bias is applied by connecting the positive terminal of a battery to the p-region and the negative terminal to the n-region.

Effects of Forward Bias:

  • Applied voltage opposes the barrier potential
  • Depletion region narrows
  • Resistance decreases dramatically
  • Large current flows when applied voltage exceeds barrier potential (V > V_B)
  • Current increases exponentially with voltage
$$I_f = I_0 \left[\exp\left(\frac{eV_f}{k_B T}\right) - 1\right]$$

where I_0 = saturation current, V_f = forward bias voltage

Reverse Bias

Reverse bias is applied by connecting the negative terminal to the p-region and positive terminal to the n-region.

Effects of Reverse Bias:

  • Applied voltage adds to the barrier potential
  • Depletion region widens
  • Resistance increases enormously
  • Very small current flows (reverse saturation current I_0, typically nanoamperes)
  • This small current is due to minority carriers (thermally generated)
  • Current remains nearly constant with reverse voltage (until breakdown)
Diode Behavior: A p-n junction acts like a one-way valve for current—it conducts easily in forward direction but blocks current in reverse direction (until breakdown voltage is reached).

4. Diode as Rectifier

One of the most important applications of a diode is rectification—converting alternating current (AC) to direct current (DC).

Half-Wave Rectifier

Half-Wave Rectifier: Uses a single diode to allow only one half of the AC wave to pass through.

How It Works:

  • During the positive half-cycle of AC input: Diode is forward biased → Conducts → Output appears
  • During the negative half-cycle: Diode is reverse biased → Blocks → No output
  • Result: Only positive half-waves appear in output

Characteristics:

  • Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV): Peak value of the negative half-cycle that appears across the diode
  • Average Output Voltage: $$V_{dc} = \frac{V_m}{\pi}$$ (where V_m = peak input voltage)
  • Ripple Factor: r ≈ 1.21 (high ripple—not smooth DC)
  • Efficiency: η ≈ 40.6%

Full-Wave Rectifier

Full-Wave Rectifier (Bridge Rectifier): Uses 4 diodes to convert both positive and negative half-cycles to positive output.

How It Works (Bridge Configuration):

During positive half-cycle: D1 and D3 conduct During negative half-cycle: D2 and D4 conduct ┌─ D1 ─ D2 ─┐ AC ─┤ ├─ DC (always positive) └─ D4 ─ D3 ─┘ Output current always flows in same direction through load

Characteristics:

  • Average Output Voltage: $$V_{dc} = \frac{2V_m}{\pi}$$ (twice that of half-wave)
  • Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV): V_m (each diode sees only half the AC voltage)
  • Ripple Factor: r ≈ 0.48 (much less ripple—smoother DC)
  • Efficiency: η ≈ 81.2%

Comparison: Half-Wave vs Full-Wave

Parameter Half-Wave Full-Wave
Number of Diodes 1 4 (bridge) or 2 (center-tap)
DC Output Voltage V_m/π ≈ 0.318 V_m 2V_m/π ≈ 0.636 V_m
Ripple Factor 1.21 (121%) 0.48 (48%)

Ripple: The variation in the DC output voltage. A lower ripple factor means smoother, more stable DC output. Full-wave rectifiers are superior for most practical applications.

5. Special Diodes

Zener Diode

Zener Diode: A special p-n junction diode doped heavily to have a sharp reverse-bias breakdown characteristic. It is designed to operate in the reverse-bias breakdown region.

Characteristics:

  • In forward bias: Acts like a normal diode
  • In reverse bias: Allows sharp increase in current above Zener voltage (V_Z)
  • The voltage across the diode remains nearly constant (≈ V_Z) even as current increases
  • Maintains constant voltage over a wide range of reverse currents

Applications:

  • Voltage Regulator: Main application—keeps output voltage constant despite variations in input voltage or load current
  • Overvoltage protection
  • Reference voltage generation
ZENER REGULATOR CIRCUIT ────R────┐ │ │ AC/DC [≈] Zener Diode │ │ ────┬─────┘ │ Load │ GND V_out ≈ V_Z (regulated, nearly constant)

Photodiode

Photodiode: A semiconductor diode that is reverse-biased and conducts current when illuminated. The incident photons create electron-hole pairs.

Principle:

  • When light hits the junction, photons with energy greater than band gap (hf > E_g) create electron-hole pairs
  • These carriers are swept across the depletion region by the electric field
  • Photocurrent I_ph is proportional to light intensity: I_ph ∝ Light Intensity

Applications:

  • Light detection and measurement
  • Optical communication systems
  • Light sensors in cameras
  • Pulse counting

Light Emitting Diode (LED)

LED: A p-n junction diode that emits light when forward-biased. Operates on the principle of electroluminescence.

Principle:

  • When forward-biased, electrons and holes are injected into the junction
  • Electrons recombine with holes, releasing energy in the form of photons
  • Color of light depends on band gap: E_g = hf = hc/λ
  • Different materials emit different colors: GaAs (infrared), GaP (red/yellow), GaN (blue/violet)

Characteristics:

  • Threshold Voltage: 1.4-2.0 V (depends on material)
  • Small forward voltage: ~1.5-2 V for most colors
  • Long lifetime: 10,000+ hours typical
  • Highly efficient: Most power goes to light emission

Applications:

  • Display devices (screens, indicators, digital displays)
  • Illumination (LED lights, street lights)
  • Traffic signals
  • Optical communication
  • Backlighting

Solar Cell (Photovoltaic Cell)

Solar Cell: A large-area p-n junction designed to absorb maximum light and convert it to electrical energy.

Principle:

  • Functions as a photodiode in reverse (generates EMF instead of conducting when illuminated)
  • Photons create electron-hole pairs near the junction
  • Built-in electric field separates charges → EMF is generated
  • Open-circuit voltage ≈ 0.5-0.7 V (independent of light intensity)
  • Short-circuit current is proportional to light intensity: I_sc ∝ Light Intensity

Specifications:

  • Efficiency (η): Ratio of electrical power output to incident light power (typically 10-20% for commercial cells)
  • Fill Factor (FF): Ratio of maximum power output to theoretical maximum power (V_oc × I_sc)
  • Band Gap: Si (1.1 eV) absorbs well for sunlight spectrum

Applications:

  • Solar power generation for homes and utilities
  • Space satellites and spacecraft
  • Calculators and watches
  • Remote power systems
Comparison: Photodiode generates current when illuminated (photoelectric effect). Solar cell generates voltage and power. Both use the photovoltaic effect but optimized differently.

6. Junction Transistor

Introduction to Transistors

A transistor is a three-terminal semiconductor device that can amplify electrical signals and act as a switch. The most common type is the bipolar junction transistor (BJT).

Types of BJT

1. npn Transistor

  • Structure: n-type region (emitter) / p-type region (base) / n-type region (collector)
  • Majority carriers: Electrons flow from emitter to collector
  • Base-emitter junction: Forward-biased
  • Base-collector junction: Reverse-biased

2. pnp Transistor

  • Structure: p-type region (emitter) / n-type region (base) / p-type region (collector)
  • Majority carriers: Holes flow from emitter to collector
  • Base-emitter junction: Forward-biased
  • Base-collector junction: Reverse-biased

Transistor Regions and Terminals

Emitter (E): Heavily doped, injects majority carriers into the base. Always forward-biased relative to base.

Base (B): Lightly doped, thin region. Controls the flow of majority carriers. Receives input signal.

Collector (C): Moderately doped, collects the carriers injected by emitter. Reverse-biased relative to base. Takes output signal.

Common Emitter (CE) Configuration

The most commonly used configuration has emitter grounded (common), input applied to base, output taken from collector.

Input Characteristics:

  • Plots base current (I_B) vs base-emitter voltage (V_BE) for constant collector-emitter voltage (V_CE)
  • Shows transistor acts as forward-biased p-n junction at input
  • Base current increases sharply when V_BE > 0.6-0.7 V (Si)
  • Input resistance r_i typically 1-10 kΩ

Output Characteristics:

  • Plots collector current (I_C) vs collector-emitter voltage (V_CE) for different base currents
  • Three regions:
    • Cutoff Region: Base current ≈ 0, collector current ≈ 0 (transistor OFF)
    • Active Region: Base current > 0, collector current ∝ base current (transistor amplifies)
    • Saturation Region: Base current large, collector current maximum (transistor fully ON)

Current Gain (β)

$$\beta = \frac{I_C}{I_B}$$

where I_C = collector current, I_B = base current

  • Typical values: 50 to 500 (depends on transistor type and operating point)
  • A small base current is amplified by β times to give collector current
  • Allows weak signals to control large currents

Transistor as Switch

Operation Modes:

  • Cutoff State: Base current = 0 → Collector current ≈ 0 → Output voltage ≈ V_CC (LED off)
  • Saturation State: Base current is large → Collector current is maximum → Output voltage ≈ 0 (LED on)
  • Acts as an electronic switch: Input signal controls whether transistor conducts or blocks

Applications:

  • LED drivers
  • Motor control
  • Relay drivers
  • Digital circuits

Transistor as Amplifier

Principle:

  • Small input signal causes changes in base current
  • Due to β, these changes are amplified in collector current
  • Voltage swing at collector output is much larger than at base input

Voltage Amplification (A_v):

$$A_v = -\beta \frac{R_c}{r_i}$$

where R_c = collector load resistance, r_i = input resistance, negative sign indicates phase reversal

Key Points:

  • Input signal applied to base causes I_B to vary
  • I_C varies by β × ΔI_B
  • Voltage drop across R_c varies: ΔV_out = β × ΔI_B × R_c
  • Larger R_c → Larger voltage gain
  • Output voltage is inverted with respect to input (180° phase shift)

Applications:

  • Audio amplifiers
  • Radio frequency amplifiers
  • Microphone preamplifiers
  • Signal conditioning circuits
Key Relationship: The transistor acts as a current-controlled current source. A small change in base current produces a large change in collector current. This is the basis for both switching and amplification.

7. Digital Electronics: Logic Gates

Introduction to Logic Gates

Logic gates are fundamental building blocks of digital circuits. They perform logical operations on binary inputs (0 = LOW/FALSE, 1 = HIGH/TRUE) and produce binary outputs.

NOT Gate (Inverter)

NOT Gate: Has one input and one output. Output is the logical opposite (complement) of input.

OR Gate: Has two or more inputs. Output is HIGH if at least one input is HIGH.

AND Gate: Has two or more inputs. Output is HIGH only if all inputs are HIGH.

NAND Gate: Combination of AND gate followed by NOT gate. Output is LOW only if all inputs are HIGH.

NOR Gate (NOT OR)

NOR Gate: Combination of OR gate followed by NOT gate. Output is HIGH only if all inputs are LOW.
Truth Table (2-input):
A B Y = NOR (A,B)
0 0 1

1 0 0
1 1 0
$$Y = \overline{A + B} = \overline{A} \cdot \overline{B}$$

Universal Gates: NAND and NOR

Universal Gates: Gates that can be used to implement any boolean function. NAND and NOR are universal gates.

Why are NAND and NOR Universal?

  • NOT: Can be implemented using NAND or NOR (by connecting both inputs together)
  • AND: Can be implemented using NAND (by adding NOT gate to output, or using double negation)
  • OR: Can be implemented using NOR (by adding NOT gate to output, or using De Morgan's law)

Implementation using NAND Gates:

  • NOT A: NAND(A, A) = A' ✓
  • AND: NOT(NAND(A,B)) = A·B ✓
  • OR: NAND(A', B') = A + B (by De Morgan's law) ✓

Implementation using NOR Gates:

  • NOT A: NOR(A, A) = A' ✓
  • OR: NOT(NOR(A,B)) = A + B ✓
  • AND: NOR(A', B') = A·B (by De Morgan's law) ✓
Practical Importance: Since NAND and NOR gates are easier to manufacture and are universal, most digital circuits are built using only NAND or NOR gates, reducing component count and cost.

Higher-Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS-1: Explain why the conductivity of semiconductors increases with temperature, while that of metals decreases. How does this difference relate to the band gap concept?

Answer: In semiconductors, the band gap is small (~1 eV). Increasing temperature provides thermal energy that excites electrons from the valence band to the conduction band, creating more free carriers. The number of carriers increases exponentially: σ ∝ exp(-E_g/2k_BT). In metals, there is no band gap (E_g = 0), and all valence electrons are already in the conduction band. Increasing temperature causes lattice vibrations (increased phonon scattering), which impede electron motion, reducing conductivity. Thus, semiconductors show positive temperature coefficient of conductivity, while metals show negative temperature coefficient.

HOTS-2: A forward-biased p-n diode has an exponential I-V relationship: I_f = I_0[exp(eV/k_BT) - 1]. Why does the diode not burn out even with very large forward currents?

Answer: The diode itself has internal resistance (bulk resistance of the semiconductor material and contact resistance). As forward current increases, the voltage drop across this internal resistance increases, limiting further current increase. Additionally, the forward bias voltage needed to drive large currents is limited by the resistance of the external circuit (series resistance). At high currents, most of the applied voltage drops across the external resistance, not across the diode junction itself. This self-limiting behavior prevents runaway current. However, excessive current can cause Joule heating and permanent damage if a current-limiting resistor is not used in the circuit.

HOTS-3: Why is the full-wave rectifier preferred over the half-wave rectifier in practical applications despite being more complex?

Answer: The full-wave rectifier is preferred for several reasons: (1) Higher DC output: V_dc = 2V_m/π vs V_m/π, providing twice the average voltage and better power utilization. (2) Lower ripple: Ripple factor r = 0.48 vs 1.21. The output is much smoother, reducing the need for expensive filtering capacitors. (3) Better efficiency: η = 81.2% vs 40.6%. More input power is converted to useful DC power. (4) Lower transformer rating: Each diode only needs to handle half the AC voltage. (5) Reduced EMI: Smoother output reduces electromagnetic interference. The extra cost of 4 diodes and bridge configuration is justified by these advantages in most practical power supplies.

Case-Based Questions

Case-Based Question 1: Mobile Phone Charger Design

A mobile phone charger converts AC (from wall outlet) to stable DC (5V for charging). The circuit uses a transformer to step down voltage, a bridge rectifier with 4 diodes, a filter capacitor, and a voltage regulator IC.

Questions:

  1. Why is a full-wave rectifier used instead of a half-wave rectifier?
  2. What is the function of the filter capacitor?
  3. Why is a voltage regulator (like a Zener diode or LM7805 IC) necessary even after rectification?

Answers:

  1. Full-wave rectifier provides higher average DC voltage (2V_m/π vs V_m/π), better efficiency, and lower ripple (r = 0.48 vs 1.21). This means less filtering is needed and less power is wasted as heat.
  2. The filter capacitor stores charge during the positive half-cycles and releases it during the gaps between rectified pulses. This significantly reduces the ripple voltage, providing a smoother DC waveform closer to ideal DC.
  3. The rectifier output voltage depends on AC input voltage and is not constant. The regulator (Zener or IC) maintains a fixed output voltage (5V) despite variations in input voltage (due to different countries' supply standards) or load current (different charging rates). It protects the phone from overvoltage.

Case-Based Question 2: Microcontroller I/O Interface with LED

A microcontroller (logic level: 5V for HIGH, 0V for LOW) needs to control a high-brightness red LED (requires 20 mA at 2V for full brightness). The microcontroller I/O pin can provide maximum 5 mA at 5V. Direct connection would damage the LED or the microcontroller.

Questions:

  1. Design a circuit to solve this problem. Which component would you use and why?
  2. Calculate the base current needed if the transistor has β = 100.
  3. Design the base resistor to provide sufficient base current with 5V input.

Answers:

  1. Use an NPN transistor (like 2N2222 or BC547) as a switch. The microcontroller output drives the transistor base, and the LED is in the collector circuit powered by a separate 5V or higher supply. The transistor acts as a current amplifier, allowing a small base current to control a large collector current.
  2. For LED current I_C = 20 mA: Required base current = I_C / β = 20 mA / 100 = 0.2 mA = 200 µA. For saturation (fully ON), we typically want base current ≈ 10% of this, so I_B ≈ 20-30 µA minimum. Use 100 µA for safe operation.
  3. Using Ohm's law: R_B = (V_in - V_BE) / I_B = (5 - 0.7) / 0.0001 = 4.3 / 0.0001 = 43 kΩ. Use a standard resistor value of 47 kΩ. This provides ample base current for switching while not exceeding microcontroller I/O rating.

Case-Based Question 3: Designing a Logic Circuit for Alarm System

An alarm system needs to trigger an alarm if: (A) Door sensor activated AND (B) Motion sensor activated, but NOT if (C) Bypass switch is ON. Express this as a logic function and simplify using boolean algebra.

Questions:

  1. Write the boolean expression for the alarm output.
  2. Simplify the expression using De Morgan's laws if needed.
  3. Draw the logic gate circuit (use AND, OR, NOT, NAND, NOR as appropriate).

Answers:

  1. Boolean expression: Y = (A · B) · C' (Alarm = Door AND Motion AND NOT Bypass) where A = Door sensor, B = Motion sensor, C = Bypass switch.
  2. Using De Morgan's laws: Y = (A · B · C') This is already simplified. Alternatively: Y = (A · B) · C' means if door and motion are detected, but bypass is not active, trigger alarm.
  3. Circuit implementation: (1) Simple gate version: AND gate for (A · B), then another AND gate with the first output and NOT C. (2) NAND-only version: Can be implemented using NAND gates by applying De Morgan's law transformations. (3) The gate circuit has 3 inputs and 1 output (the alarm signal).
