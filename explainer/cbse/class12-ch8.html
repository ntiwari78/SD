Electromagnetic Waves

NCERT Class 12 Physics - Chapter 8

1. Maxwell's Equations and Displacement Current

1.1 Displacement Current

Maxwell identified a fundamental limitation in Ampere's law and introduced the concept of displacement current. While a capacitor is charging or discharging, no conduction current flows between the plates, yet a magnetic field is observed around the region between the plates.

Displacement Current:
Id = ε₀ dΦE/dt

where:

  • Id = displacement current (Amperes)
  • ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m
  • dΦE/dt = rate of change of electric flux (Wb/s or V⋅m/s)
Key Insight: Displacement current was Maxwell's crucial contribution that unified electricity, magnetism, and optics. It predicts the existence of electromagnetic waves traveling through space.

1.2 Maxwell's Equations (Qualitative)

Maxwell's four equations form the foundation of electromagnetism:

1. Gauss's Law (Electric Charges)

Electric field lines originate from positive charges and terminate at negative charges. The total electric flux through a closed surface equals the enclosed charge divided by ε₀.

2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism (No Magnetic Monopoles)

Magnetic field lines form closed loops. There are no isolated magnetic charges (monopoles). The net magnetic flux through any closed surface is zero.

3. Faraday's Law (Electromagnetic Induction)

A changing magnetic flux through a circuit induces an electric field. This law explains how generators work and demonstrates that magnetism can create electricity.

4. Ampere-Maxwell Law (Modified Ampere's Law)

A current and a changing electric flux both produce magnetic fields. Maxwell's addition of the displacement current term completed the symmetry of electromagnetic laws.

Profound Symmetry: Changing electric fields produce magnetic fields (Faraday), and changing magnetic fields produce electric fields (Maxwell). This mutual generation of fields allows electromagnetic waves to propagate through empty space.

2. Electromagnetic Wave Properties

2.1 Nature of EM Waves

Electromagnetic waves are transverse waves composed of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation.

Characteristics of EM Waves:
  • Both E and B fields oscillate in phase
  • E ⊥ B (perpendicular to each other)
  • Both E and B perpendicular to direction of propagation
  • Transverse nature allows polarization
  • No medium required for propagation

2.2 Speed of Electromagnetic Waves

The speed of electromagnetic waves in vacuum depends only on the fundamental constants of electromagnetism.

Speed of Light:
c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) = 3 × 10⁸ m/s

where:

  • c = speed of light in vacuum
  • μ₀ = permeability of free space = 4π × 10⁻⁷ H/m
  • ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m
Historical Significance: This prediction that light is an electromagnetic wave came as a dramatic revelation. All electromagnetic waves, regardless of frequency or wavelength, travel at this speed in vacuum.

2.3 Relationship Between E and B Fields

The magnitudes of the electric and magnetic field amplitudes are related:

Field Amplitude Relationship:
E₀/B₀ = c

This relationship indicates that the electric field amplitude is always c times larger than the magnetic field amplitude in an electromagnetic wave.

2.4 Energy in EM Waves

Electromagnetic waves carry energy in both their electric and magnetic fields. The energy densities are equal:

Energy Density:
uE = uB = ½ε₀E₀² = B₀²/(2μ₀)

The Poynting vector S = (1/μ₀)(E × B) represents the direction and magnitude of energy flow.

2.5 Intensity and Power

The intensity (average power per unit area) of an electromagnetic wave is given by:

Average Intensity:
I = ½ε₀cE₀² = B₀²/(2μ₀c)

This shows that intensity is proportional to the square of the field amplitudes.

3. Electromagnetic Spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum encompasses all types of electromagnetic radiation, arranged in order of increasing frequency (or decreasing wavelength). All these radiations have the fundamental property that they travel at the speed of light in vacuum.

Microwaves 0.1 mm - 1 mm 3 × 10⁹ - 3 × 10¹¹ Hz Klystron, magnetron Bolometer, crystal diode
Infrared 700 nm - 0.1 mm 3 × 10¹¹ - 4.3 × 10¹⁴ Hz Hot objects, thermal sources Thermopile, bolometer
Visible Light 400 nm - 700 nm 4.3 × 10¹⁴ - 7.5 × 10¹⁴ Hz Sun, incandescent bulbs, lasers Human eye, photodiode, CCD
Ultraviolet 10 nm - 400 nm 7.5 × 10¹⁴ - 3 × 10¹⁶ Hz Sun, mercury lamps, ionized atoms Photographic plate, Geiger counter

Gamma Rays < 0.01 nm > 3 × 10¹⁹ Hz Radioactive nuclei, nuclear reactions Geiger counter, scintillation counter

3.2 Detailed Spectrum Analysis

Radio Waves

Wavelength Range: > 0.1 mm (or > 1 mm for conventional radio)

Frequency Range: < 3 × 10¹¹ Hz

Main Sources:

  • AC circuits with varying currents
  • Oscillators and transmitters
  • Accelerating charges

Detection Methods:

  • Tuned LC circuits (resonance)
  • Antennas (dipole, parabolic)
  • Receivers with amplifiers

Applications:

  • AM/FM broadcasting
  • Television transmission
  • Wireless communication
  • Radar systems

Microwaves

Wavelength Range: 0.1 mm - 1 mm

Frequency Range: 3 × 10⁹ - 3 × 10¹¹ Hz

Main Sources:

  • Klystron (vacuum tube)
  • Magnetron (vacuum tube)
  • Solid-state oscillators

Detection Methods:

  • Bolometer (temperature-sensitive resistor)
  • Crystal diode
  • Microwave antennas

Applications:

  • Microwave ovens (heating food)
  • Cellular/mobile communication
  • WiFi and wireless networks
  • Radar and navigation

Infrared Radiation

Wavelength Range: 700 nm - 0.1 mm (also 1 μm - 100 μm)

Frequency Range: 3 × 10¹¹ - 4.3 × 10¹⁴ Hz

Main Sources:

  • Hot objects and thermal radiation
  • Incandescent lamps
  • Thermal imaging sources

Detection Methods:

  • Thermopile
  • Bolometer
  • Infrared cameras
  • Photodiodes

Applications:

  • Thermal imaging and night vision
  • Heat sensing and detection
  • Remote controls
  • Fiber optic communications

Visible Light

Wavelength Range: 400 nm - 700 nm (violet to red)

Frequency Range: 4.3 × 10¹⁴ - 7.5 × 10¹⁴ Hz

Main Sources:

  • Sun (primary natural source)
  • Incandescent bulbs
  • Lasers and LEDs
  • Fluorescent lamps

Detection Methods:

  • Human eye (most sensitive)
  • Photodiodes and phototransistors
  • CCD and CMOS sensors
  • Photomultiplier tubes

Applications:

  • Vision and illumination
  • Optical fiber communication
  • Photography and cinematography
  • Spectroscopy and microscopy

Ultraviolet (UV) Radiation

Wavelength Range: 10 nm - 400 nm

Frequency Range: 7.5 × 10¹⁴ - 3 × 10¹⁶ Hz

Main Sources:

  • Sun (UV portion)
  • Mercury lamps
  • Ionized atoms and plasmas

Detection Methods:

  • Photographic plates
  • Geiger counter
  • Fluorescent screens
  • UV sensors

Applications:

  • Sterilization (kills bacteria)
  • Fluorescence and phosphorescence
  • Vitamin D production
  • Chemical and biological analysis

X-rays

Wavelength Range: 0.01 nm - 10 nm

Frequency Range: 3 × 10¹⁶ - 3 × 10¹⁹ Hz

Main Sources:

  • X-ray tubes (accelerated electrons)
  • High-energy atomic transitions
  • Bremsstrahlung radiation

Detection Methods:

  • Ionization chambers
  • Photographic film
  • Scintillation counters
  • CCD detectors

Applications:

  • Medical imaging (radiography)
  • Crystallography
  • Security scanning
  • Astronomical observations

Gamma Rays

Wavelength Range: < 0.01 nm

Frequency Range: > 3 × 10¹⁹ Hz

Main Sources:

  • Radioactive nuclei (decay)
  • Nuclear reactions
  • Cosmic sources (pulsars, quasars)

Detection Methods:

  • Geiger counter
  • Scintillation counter
  • Ionization chamber
  • Gamma camera

Applications:

  • Medical imaging (PET scans)
  • Cancer treatment (radiotherapy)
  • Nuclear power generation
  • Sterilization of medical instruments

3.3 Key Features of the Spectrum

Important Points:
  • All EM radiations travel at speed c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum
  • Spectrum is continuous with no gaps
  • Wavelength and frequency are inversely related: λν = c
  • Energy of photons: E = hf = hc/λ (higher frequency = higher energy)
  • Visible light is a tiny portion of the complete spectrum
  • Each type has distinct sources and applications

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS Q1: Maxwell's Revolutionary Insight

Question: Maxwell predicted the existence of electromagnetic waves by recognizing that changing electric fields produce magnetic fields. Why was this prediction so revolutionary for physics and explain how it unified three apparently different phenomena (electricity, magnetism, and light)?

Key Points for Answer:

  • Maxwell showed that EM waves must exist from theoretical predictions alone
  • The speed of EM waves (c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀)) exactly matched the known speed of light
  • This provided overwhelming evidence that light itself is an EM wave
  • Unified all three phenomena under one framework: Maxwell's equations
  • Predicted unknown types of radiation (radio waves, X-rays, gamma rays)

HOTS Q2: Energy Transport in EM Waves

Question: The energy density in both electric and magnetic fields of an EM wave are equal (u_E = u_B). What does this tell us about how energy is distributed in the wave? How is energy transported through space by an EM wave?

Key Points for Answer:

  • Equal energy densities indicate a perfect balance between E and B field contributions
  • Energy flows in the direction of propagation via the Poynting vector: S = (1/μ₀)(E × B)
  • EM waves are self-propagating—no medium needed because changing fields create each other
  • Intensity is proportional to the square of field amplitudes: I ∝ E₀²
  • This mechanism allows energy to reach Earth from the Sun and distant stars

HOTS Q3: Application of EM Spectrum Knowledge

Question: Why are different types of radiation in the EM spectrum suitable for different applications? For example, why use radio waves for communication, infrared for thermal imaging, and gamma rays for cancer treatment? Explain in terms of wavelength, frequency, and energy properties.

Key Points for Answer:

  • Radio waves (long wavelength, low frequency) can diffract around obstacles—ideal for long-distance communication
  • Infrared (medium energy) can detect heat signatures without being harmful—used in thermal imaging
  • Gamma rays (very high energy, high frequency) can ionize and damage cancer cells while energy is absorbed by protective equipment
  • Shorter wavelength = better spatial resolution; used in microscopy and medical imaging
  • Higher frequency = higher photon energy; capable of ionizing atoms and breaking molecular bonds
  • Different detectors suited to each type based on interaction mechanisms with matter

Case-Based Questions (CBQ)

CBQ 1: Mobile Phone Communication

Context: Mobile phones use electromagnetic waves in the microwave and radio frequency range to communicate. The frequency bands are highly regulated and different countries use different frequencies. Modern 5G technology uses frequencies up to 60 GHz.

Questions:

  1. Why are frequencies in the GHz range preferred for mobile communication rather than optical frequencies?
  2. Calculate the wavelength corresponding to a 5G signal at 30 GHz.
  3. Why can radio signals penetrate buildings while visible light cannot?
  4. What is the relationship between increased frequency and penetration ability in real scenarios?

Expected Answers:

  • 1. Easier to generate, transmit, and modulate; can be produced by electronic circuits; less equipment needed than optical
  • 2. λ = c/f = (3 × 10⁸)/(30 × 10⁹) = 0.01 m = 1 cm
  • 3. Longer wavelengths (centimeter scale) diffract around building materials; shorter wavelengths absorbed more
  • 4. Higher frequencies actually penetrate less in real materials; frequency selection is a compromise between range and data rate

CBQ 2: Medical Imaging Techniques

Context: Different medical imaging techniques use different parts of the EM spectrum. X-rays penetrate soft tissue to create bone images, while MRI uses radio waves and magnetic fields, and thermal imaging detects infrared radiation from the body.

Questions:

  1. Why can X-rays pass through soft tissue but are mostly absorbed by bone?
  2. Compare the energy of a typical X-ray photon (λ = 0.1 nm) with a microwave photon (λ = 1 cm)
  3. Why is thermal imaging useful for detecting tumors?
  4. Why is it safe to use radio waves for MRI while X-rays require lead protection?

Expected Answers:

  • 1. High atomic number elements (Ca in bone) interact strongly with X-rays through photoelectric effect
  • 2. E = hc/λ. X-ray: E = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 3 × 10⁸)/(0.1 × 10⁻⁹) ≈ 2 × 10⁻¹⁴ J; Microwave: ≈ 2 × 10⁻²⁶ J (10¹² times less)
  • 3. Tumors have higher metabolic rate and blood flow, generating more heat; detected by thermal sensitivity
  • 4. Radio waves have low energy (don't ionize atoms); X-rays are ionizing and can damage DNA

CBQ 3: Solar Radiation and Climate

Context: The Sun emits EM radiation across a wide spectrum. Most reaches Earth's surface as visible and infrared. Greenhouse gases absorb infrared radiation, causing climate change. Understanding the EM spectrum is crucial for climate science.

Questions:

  1. Why does the Sun emit primarily in the visible and infrared region?
  2. Why are infrared (not visible light) wavelengths responsible for the greenhouse effect?
  3. Calculate the wavelength of peak solar radiation if the Sun's surface temperature is ~5800 K (using Wien's law: λ_max = 2.9 × 10⁻³/T)
  4. How would increased atmospheric CO₂ (which absorbs infrared) affect Earth's temperature?

Expected Answers:

  • 1. As a hot thermal source, the Sun emits according to Planck's law; peak in visible range due to surface temperature
  • 2. Earth re-radiates absorbed solar energy as infrared; greenhouse gases trap this, creating warming
  • 3. λ_max = 2.9 × 10⁻³/5800 ≈ 500 nm (visible, greenish-yellow light)
  • 4. More CO₂ → more infrared trapped → increased surface temperature → global warming
