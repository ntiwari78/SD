🪞 1. Reflection: Mirrors and Mirror Equation

Mirror Laws of Reflection

Light reflects from a smooth surface following two fundamental laws:

Law 1: Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection: θᵢ = θᵣ
Law 2: Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal all lie in the same plane

Concave Mirror (Converging Mirror)

A concave mirror has a reflecting surface that curves inward, converging parallel rays to a focus.

Properties:
  • Center of curvature (C) is in front of mirror
  • Focal length f = R/2 (where R is radius of curvature)
  • Forms real, virtual, magnified, or diminished images
  • Used in searchlights, shaving mirrors, astronomical telescopes

Convex Mirror (Diverging Mirror)

A convex mirror has a reflecting surface that curves outward, diverging light rays.

Properties:
  • Center of curvature (C) is behind mirror
  • Focal length f = R/2 (behind mirror, taken as negative)
  • Always forms virtual, erect, and diminished images
  • Used as side mirrors, security mirrors, vehicle reflectors

Mirror Equation and Magnification

Mirror Equation: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f

Where:
  • u = object distance (measured from mirror)
  • v = image distance (measured from mirror)
  • f = focal length (f = R/2)
  • Sign Convention: distances measured opposite to light direction are negative
Magnification: m = -v/u = h_image/h_object

  • m > 0: virtual, erect image
  • m < 0: real, inverted image
  • |m| > 1: magnified image
  • |m| < 1: diminished image

Example Problem

An object of height 2 cm is placed 15 cm in front of a concave mirror with focal length 10 cm. Find the position, nature, and magnification of the image.

Solution: Using 1/v + 1/u = 1/f with u = -15 cm, f = -10 cm

1/v + 1/(-15) = 1/(-10) → 1/v = -1/10 + 1/15 = -1/30 → v = -30 cm

Magnification: m = -v/u = -(-30)/(-15) = -2

Result: Real, inverted, magnified image 30 cm in front of mirror

🌊 2. Refraction: Snell's Law and Total Internal Reflection

Refraction at Plane Surfaces

When light travels from one medium to another, it bends due to change in speed of light.

Refractive Index (n): n = c/v, where c is speed in vacuum (3×10⁸ m/s) and v is speed in medium

Snell's Law of Refraction

Snell's Law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

Where:
  • n₁ = refractive index of medium 1
  • n₂ = refractive index of medium 2
  • θ₁ = angle of incidence
  • θ₂ = angle of refraction
  • Angles measured from normal to surface
Key Points:
  • Light bends toward normal when entering denser medium (n₁ < n₂)
  • Light bends away from normal when entering rarer medium (n₁ > n₂)
  • Incident ray, refracted ray, and normal are coplanar

Real and Apparent Depth

Objects underwater appear closer to the surface than they actually are due to refraction.

Apparent Depth Relation: n = real depth / apparent depth

For small angles: apparent depth = real depth / n

Example

A fish in water appears at 1.5 m depth when actually at 2 m. Find refractive index of water.

Solution: n = real depth / apparent depth = 2 / 1.5 = 1.33

Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

When light travels from denser to rarer medium, at a certain angle, refraction is no longer possible and total reflection occurs.

Critical Angle: sin C = n₂/n₁ (where n₁ > n₂)

For light traveling from denser medium to air:
  • sin C = 1/n (refractive index)
  • C = critical angle
Conditions for TIR:
  • Light must travel from denser to rarer medium
  • Angle of incidence must exceed critical angle (θᵢ > C)
  • 100% reflection occurs (no refraction)
Applications of TIR:
  • Optical fibers: light guided through fibers via TIR
  • Prisms: used for beam deviation and dispersion
  • Diamond sparkle: high refractive index (2.42) causes TIR
  • Mirages: mirage due to TIR in atmosphere
Medium Refractive Index (n) Critical Angle (from glass)
Vacuum 1.00 -
Air 1.00 -
Water 1.33 48.6°
Glass (Crown) 1.52 41.1°
Glass (Flint) 1.65 36.7°
Diamond 2.42 24.4°

🔍 3. Lenses: Thin Lens Equation and Power

Types of Lenses

Converging Lenses (Convex):
  • Thicker at center, thinner at edges
  • Converge parallel rays to focal point
  • Positive focal length (f > 0)
  • Can form real and virtual images
Diverging Lenses (Concave):
  • Thinner at center, thicker at edges
  • Diverge parallel rays from focal point
  • Negative focal length (f < 0)
  • Always form virtual, erect, diminished images

Thin Lens Equation

Thin Lens Formula: 1/f = 1/v - 1/u

Or equivalently: 1/v = 1/f + 1/u

Where:
  • f = focal length of lens
  • u = object distance (from lens)
  • v = image distance (from lens)
  • Sign Convention: distances on same side as object are negative

Lensmaker's Formula

Lensmaker's Formula: 1/f = (n - 1)[1/R₁ - 1/R₂]

Where:
  • n = refractive index of lens material
  • R₁ = radius of curvature of first surface
  • R₂ = radius of curvature of second surface
  • Sign Convention: R is positive if center is on right side of surface
Alternative Form (for lens in medium):
1/f = (n_lens/n_medium - 1)[1/R₁ - 1/R₂]

Power of Lens

Lens Power: P = 1/f (in diopters, D)

Where f is in meters

  • Converging lens: P > 0 (positive power)
  • Diverging lens: P < 0 (negative power)
  • 1 diopter = power of lens with focal length 1 m

Combination of Lenses

When two or more lenses are placed in contact (separated by negligible distance):

Power of Combination: P = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ...

Focal Length: 1/f = 1/f₁ + 1/f₂ + 1/f₃ + ...
Applications:
  • Eyepieces in optical instruments
  • Correcting vision defects
  • Achromaticity: reducing chromatic aberration

Magnification

Linear Magnification: m = v/u = h_image/h_object

  • m > 0: virtual, erect image
  • m < 0: real, inverted image

Example Problem

A convex lens of focal length 20 cm forms a real image at 60 cm from the lens. Find object distance and magnification.

Solution: Using 1/f = 1/v - 1/u with f = 20 cm, v = -60 cm (real image)

1/20 = 1/(-60) - 1/u → 1/u = -1/60 - 1/20 = -4/60 = -1/15 → u = -15 cm

Magnification: m = v/u = (-60)/(-15) = 4

Result: Object 15 cm from lens, magnification = 4 (4 times magnified)

⬠ 4. Prism: Deviation and Dispersion

Light through Prism

A prism is a transparent optical element with flat, polished surfaces that refract light.

Angle of Prism (A): angle between two refracting surfaces
Angle of Deviation (δ): angle between incident and emergent rays

Prism Formula

Deviation Formula: δ = i₁ + i₂ - A

Where:
  • δ = angle of deviation
  • i₁ = angle of incidence at first surface
  • i₂ = angle of emergence at second surface
  • A = angle of prism

Minimum Deviation

Deviation is minimum when ray passes symmetrically through the prism (angle of incidence = angle of emergence).

Refractive Index at Minimum Deviation:
n = sin[(A + δ_min)/2] / sin(A/2)

At minimum deviation:
  • i₁ = i₂
  • r₁ = r₂ = A/2
  • δ is minimum and independent of angle of incidence

Dispersion

Different wavelengths of light have different refractive indices in a material, causing them to deviate by different amounts.

Chromatic Dispersion:
  • Red light has lowest refractive index, deviates least
  • Violet light has highest refractive index, deviates most
  • Angular dispersion = δ_violet - δ_red
Dispersive Power: ω = (n_v - n_r) / (n_y - 1)
  • where n_v = refractive index for violet
  • n_r = refractive index for red
  • n_y = refractive index for yellow (mean)
Applications of Dispersion:
  • Spectroscopy: analyzing composition of light
  • Rainbow formation
  • Achromaticity: designing lenses free from color defects

Example Problem

A prism of angle 60° has a refractive index of 1.5. Find the minimum deviation.

Solution: At minimum deviation, n = sin[(A + δ_min)/2] / sin(A/2)

1.5 = sin[(60 + δ_min)/2] / sin(30°)

1.5 × 0.5 = sin[(60 + δ_min)/2]

0.75 = sin[(60 + δ_min)/2]

(60 + δ_min)/2 = 48.6°

δ_min = 37.2°

🔬 5. Optical Instruments: Human Eye, Microscope, and Telescope

Human Eye

The human eye is a remarkable optical instrument that forms real images on the retina.

Parts of the Eye:
  • Cornea: refracts light entering eye
  • Lens: fine-tunes focus, changes shape (accommodation)
  • Iris: controls pupil diameter for light intensity
  • Retina: light-sensitive layer, contains photoreceptors
  • Optic nerve: transmits signals to brain
Vision Defects and Corrections:
  • Myopia (short-sightedness): corrected with concave lens
  • Hyperopia (long-sightedness): corrected with convex lens
  • Presbyopia: loss of accommodation with age
  • Astigmatism: blurred vision at all distances
Eye Parameters:
  • Near point: minimum distance for clear vision (D = 25 cm typically)
  • Far point: maximum distance for clear vision (∞ for normal eye)
  • Least distance of distinct vision: 25 cm

Microscope

A microscope magnifies small objects using two converging lenses: objective and eyepiece.

Structure:
  • Objective lens: forms real, inverted, magnified image
  • Eyepiece lens: acts as magnifying glass for intermediate image
  • Tube length L: distance between focal points of lenses
Microscope Magnification: m = m_obj × m_eye

For normal adjustment (final image at ∞):
m = (L/f_obj) × (D/f_eye)

Where:
  • L = tube length
  • f_obj = focal length of objective
  • D = least distance of distinct vision (25 cm)
  • f_eye = focal length of eyepiece
Resolving Power: ability to distinguish between two closely spaced objects
Resolving power = 1 / (minimum distance separable)
Improves with shorter wavelength and larger objective lens

Telescope (Refracting)

A telescope magnifies distant celestial objects using objective and eyepiece lenses.

Structure:
  • Objective lens: long focal length, gathers light
  • Eyepiece lens: short focal length, magnifies image
  • Much longer than microscope
Telescope Magnification: m = f_obj / f_eye

Tube Length: L = f_obj + f_eye

Where:
  • f_obj = focal length of objective
  • f_eye = focal length of eyepiece
Properties:
  • Magnifies distant objects
  • Has limited field of view
  • Gathering power depends on objective diameter
  • Resolving power: minimum separable angle = 1.22λ/D

Reflecting Telescope

Uses curved mirrors instead of lenses to avoid chromatic aberration and allow large apertures.

Advantages:
  • No chromatic aberration
  • Can be made much larger
  • Better light gathering power
  • Used for professional astronomy
Types:
  • Newtonian: primary mirror and plane mirror
  • Cassegrain: primary mirror and secondary convex mirror
  • Gregorian: primary mirror and secondary concave mirror
Instrument Magnification Formula Application
Simple Magnifier m = D/f (when image at ∞) Viewing small details
Microscope m = (L/f_obj) × (D/f_eye) Viewing microscopic objects
Telescope m = f_obj/f_eye Viewing distant celestial objects
Projection Lantern m = v/u Projecting images on screen
Camera m = f/∞ → 0 (approx) Recording images

Summary of Key Concepts

Reflection

Light reflects from smooth surfaces following the law that angle of incidence equals angle of reflection. Concave mirrors converge light and can form real images, while convex mirrors diverge light and form only virtual images. The mirror equation 1/v + 1/u = 1/f relates object distance, image distance, and focal length.

Refraction

Light bends when traveling between media with different refractive indices, following Snell's law. When light travels from denser to rarer medium at angles exceeding the critical angle, total internal reflection occurs, trapping light within the denser medium. This principle is essential for optical fibers and explains phenomena like mirages.

Lenses

Convex lenses converge light while concave lenses diverge it. The thin lens equation 1/f = 1/v - 1/u describes their behavior. Lens power (P = 1/f in diopters) is additive when lenses are in contact, allowing design of complex optical systems. The lensmaker's formula connects focal length to radius of curvature and refractive index.

Prism

Prisms deviate light according to δ = i₁ + i₂ - A. At minimum deviation, the refractive index can be determined from n = sin[(A+δₘ)/2]/sin(A/2). Different colors deviate by different amounts, enabling spectroscopy and explaining dispersion phenomena.

Optical Instruments

The human eye focuses light on the retina through corneal and lens refraction. Microscopes use two converging lenses to magnify nearby objects, while telescopes magnify distant objects. Magnification formulas and resolving power are crucial for understanding instrument performance. Modern reflecting telescopes avoid chromatic aberration inherent in refracting telescopes.
