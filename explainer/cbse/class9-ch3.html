Atoms and Molecules

Class 9 · Chapter 3 · Chemistry · CBSE 2025-26

Everything around you — the air you breathe, the food you eat, even your own body — is made of tiny particles called atoms. But how small are they really? And how do they combine to create the millions of different substances in our world?

1. Laws of Chemical Combination

Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1774)

Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. The total mass of reactants always equals the total mass of products.

💡 Think of it like this...

Imagine you have a pile of LEGO bricks. You rearrange them to build different structures — a tower, a house, a car. No matter what you build, you still have the same number and type of bricks. You haven't created or destroyed any bricks; you've just rearranged them. That's exactly what happens in chemical reactions!

Real-World Example: Burning Wood

When wood burns, it seems to disappear, and the ash that remains weighs much less than the original wood. Where did the mass go? The answer: invisible gases! The burning wood combines with oxygen from the air to produce carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water vapor (H₂O). These gases escape into the atmosphere. The "missing" mass is the mass of these gases. If you could capture all the gases and weigh them, the total mass of ash + gases would equal the original mass of wood + oxygen.

Law of Constant Proportions (Proust)

A chemical compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass, regardless of the source or method of preparation.

🌍 Did You Know?

Water is the most universal example of this law. Whether you collect water from a river in India, rain from the Amazon rainforest, or melt ice from Antarctica, water ALWAYS contains hydrogen and oxygen in a 1:8 mass ratio. 2g of hydrogen always combines with 16g of oxygen to form 18g of water — always, every time!

Examples:

  • Water (H₂O): H:O always in 1:8 mass ratio
  • Carbon Dioxide (CO₂): C:O always in 3:8 mass ratio
  • Ammonia (NH₃): N:H always in 14:3 mass ratio
🔬 Did You Know?

These two laws are the foundation of stoichiometry — the branch of chemistry that deals with the quantitative relationships of substances in chemical reactions. Without these laws, chemistry as we know it wouldn't exist!

2. Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808)

John Dalton proposed a revolutionary theory to explain the laws of chemical combination. His five postulates were:

1. All matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms

Matter cannot be endlessly divided; there's a limit to how small you can go.

2. Atoms of the same element are identical in mass, size, and properties

All hydrogen atoms are identical to each other, all oxygen atoms are identical to each other, etc.

3. Atoms of different elements have different properties and masses

A hydrogen atom is fundamentally different from an oxygen atom.

4. Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds

Water is H₂O (2:1), not H₂.₅O. Carbon dioxide is CO₂ (1:2), not C₀.₅O. Always whole numbers!

5. Atoms cannot be created, destroyed, or converted into atoms of another element in a chemical reaction

Chemical reactions only rearrange atoms; they don't destroy or create them.

What Was WRONG with Dalton's Theory?

⚠️ Later Discoveries
Atoms ARE divisible: In 1897, J.J. Thomson discovered the electron. We now know atoms contain protons, neutrons, and electrons.
Atoms of the same element are NOT always identical: In 1913, Frederick Soddy discovered isotopes — atoms of the same element with different masses (e.g., Carbon-12 and Carbon-14).

Historical Context: Ancient Indian Philosophy

Maharishi Kanad and the Concept of Paramanu

Long before Dalton, around 600 BCE, an ancient Indian philosopher named Maharishi Kanad proposed that all matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles called "Paramanu" (meaning "smallest particle" in Sanskrit). This is a striking parallel to modern atomic theory! India's contribution to atomic science is often overlooked in Western textbooks, but the philosophical foundation was laid centuries before Dalton.

📜 Did You Know?

Despite its flaws, Dalton's theory was so powerful that it became the foundation of modern chemistry. His postulate that atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios (Postulate 4) is STILL valid and is one of the most important concepts in chemistry!

3. What Is an Atom?

An atom is the smallest particle of an element that retains its chemical properties. It is the fundamental building block of matter.

Size of an Atom

Atoms are incredibly small. Their size is measured in nanometers (nm).

1 \text{ nm} = 10^{-9} \text{ m} = 0.000000001 \text{ m}

Some atomic radii (in nanometers):

  • Hydrogen: ≈ 0.037 nm
  • Carbon: ≈ 0.070 nm
  • Oxygen: ≈ 0.060 nm
  • Sodium: ≈ 0.186 nm
Size Perspective: An Incredible Comparison

Imagine if an atom were the size of a cricket ball (about 4 cm diameter). To maintain the same proportion, the cricket ball would need to be the size of EARTH! That's how incredibly tiny atoms are. A grain of sand contains trillions of atoms. A single drop of water contains about 1.67 × 10²¹ water molecules (and each molecule contains 3 atoms)!

Atomic Symbols

Each element has a unique one or two-letter symbol:

Modern System (Berzelius, 1814)

Symbols are derived from the element's English name OR its Latin name:

  • From English: H (Hydrogen), O (Oxygen), C (Carbon), N (Nitrogen), S (Sulphur)
  • From Latin: Fe (Ferrum = Iron), Au (Aurum = Gold), Ag (Argentum = Silver), Na (Natrium = Sodium), K (Kalium = Potassium), Cu (Cuprum = Copper)
🔤 Did You Know?

Why is the symbol for iron "Fe" and not "I"? Because in Latin, iron is called "Ferrum." When Berzelius created the modern symbolic system, he used Latin names to avoid confusion (since "I" could be confused with the number 1 or the letter l). This is why many symbols seem strange — they're derived from ancient Latin names!

Atomic Mass

The mass of an atom is measured in atomic mass units (u), also called unified atomic mass units.

1 \text{ u} = 1.66 \times 10^{-24} \text{ g}

Atomic masses (in u) of common elements:

  • Hydrogen (H): 1 u
  • Carbon (C): 12 u
  • Nitrogen (N): 14 u
  • Oxygen (O): 16 u
  • Sodium (Na): 23 u
  • Magnesium (Mg): 24 u
  • Aluminium (Al): 27 u
  • Sulphur (S): 32 u
  • Potassium (K): 39 u
  • Calcium (Ca): 40 u
  • Iron (Fe): 56 u
⚛️ Did You Know?

The atomic mass unit was specifically chosen as 1/12 the mass of carbon-12 because carbon is abundant and stable. This makes hydrogen (the lightest element) have an atomic mass very close to 1, which simplifies calculations. Before 1961, different sciences used different standards (chemists used oxygen-16 as the reference, physicists used oxygen-16 as well but with slightly different values). Now, everyone uses the same international standard!

4. What Is a Molecule?

A molecule is the smallest particle of a substance (either an element or a compound) that can exist independently and retain its chemical properties.

Molecules of Elements

Monoatomic Molecules (Single Atoms)

Noble gases naturally exist as single atoms because they are extremely stable:

  • He (Helium)
  • Ne (Neon)
  • Ar (Argon)
  • Kr (Krypton)
Diatomic Molecules (Two Atoms)

Most non-metallic elements exist as pairs of atoms bonded together:

  • H₂ (Hydrogen)
  • O₂ (Oxygen)
  • N₂ (Nitrogen)
  • F₂ (Fluorine)
  • Cl₂ (Chlorine)
  • Br₂ (Bromine)
  • I₂ (Iodine)
Polyatomic Molecules (Multiple Atoms)

Some elements form molecules with more than two atoms:

  • O₃ (Ozone) — 3 oxygen atoms
  • P₄ (Phosphorus) — 4 phosphorus atoms
  • S₈ (Sulphur) — 8 sulphur atoms

Molecules of Compounds

Compounds are formed when atoms of different elements combine. Each compound has a fixed, specific number of each type of atom:

\text{H}_2\text{O (Water)}: 2 \text{ hydrogen atoms} + 1 \text{ oxygen atom}
\text{CO}_2 \text{(Carbon Dioxide)}: 1 \text{ carbon atom} + 2 \text{ oxygen atoms}
\text{NH}_3 \text{(Ammonia)}: 1 \text{ nitrogen atom} + 3 \text{ hydrogen atoms}
\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 \text{(Glucose)}: 6 \text{ C atoms} + 12 \text{ H atoms} + 6 \text{ O atoms}

Atomicity

Atomicity is the number of atoms in a molecule.

Examples of Atomicity
  • He, Ne, Ar: Atomicity = 1 (monoatomic)
  • H₂, O₂, N₂: Atomicity = 2 (diatomic)
  • O₃: Atomicity = 3 (triatomic)
  • P₄: Atomicity = 4 (tetratomic)
  • S₈: Atomicity = 8
  • H₂O: Atomicity = 3
  • CO₂: Atomicity = 3
  • C₆H₁₂O₆: Atomicity = 24
🌬️ Did You Know?

Ozone (O₃) is a form of oxygen that exists in the upper atmosphere and protects us from harmful ultraviolet radiation. Even though it's made of pure oxygen atoms, ozone is chemically and physically different from the oxygen (O₂) we breathe. Different arrangements of the same atoms can create completely different substances! This is called allotropy.

5. Ions

An ion is an atom or group of atoms that carries an electric charge.

Formation of Ions

Ions form when atoms lose or gain electrons:

Cations (Positive Ions)

Formed when an atom LOSES one or more electrons. Since electrons are negatively charged, losing them makes the atom positively charged.

  • Na atom → loses 1 electron → Na⁺ ion
  • K atom → loses 1 electron → K⁺ ion
  • Ca atom → loses 2 electrons → Ca²⁺ ion
  • Al atom → loses 3 electrons → Al³⁺ ion
  • Fe atom → loses 2 or 3 electrons → Fe²⁺ or Fe³⁺ ion
Anions (Negative Ions)

Formed when an atom GAINS one or more electrons. Since electrons are negatively charged, gaining them makes the atom negatively charged.

  • Cl atom → gains 1 electron → Cl⁻ ion
  • O atom → gains 2 electrons → O²⁻ ion
  • S atom → gains 2 electrons → S²⁻ ion
  • N atom → gains 3 electrons → N³⁻ ion

Polyatomic Ions

A group of atoms bonded together can also carry a charge. These are called polyatomic ions:

Common Polyatomic Ions
  • Negative: OH⁻ (hydroxide), NO₃⁻ (nitrate), SO₄²⁻ (sulphate), CO₃²⁻ (carbonate), PO₄³⁻ (phosphate), ClO₄⁻ (perchlorate)
  • Positive: NH₄⁺ (ammonium), H₃O⁺ (hydronium)

Ionic vs. Covalent Compounds

Ionic Compounds: Built from Ions, NOT Molecules

When we write NaCl (sodium chloride), we're NOT describing a single molecule with one sodium and one chlorine atom. Instead, NaCl is an ionic compound made up of Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions arranged in a three-dimensional crystalline lattice. There are no "NaCl molecules" — it's a whole crystal of alternating Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions.

The Structure of Sodium Chloride (Table Salt)

Imagine a 3D chess board. Every white square contains a Na⁺ ion, and every black square contains a Cl⁻ ion. Each Na⁺ is surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ ions, and each Cl⁻ is surrounded by 6 Na⁺ ions. This arrangement extends in all directions, forming a giant crystal. When we say "NaCl," we're describing the 1:1 ratio of sodium to chlorine in this vast crystal lattice.

💎 Did You Know?

Ionic compounds are often crystalline solids because of this regular, repeating arrangement of ions. When you look at a salt crystal under a microscope, the geometric shapes you see (cubes, rectangles) are the result of this orderly ionic arrangement. The same is true for other ionic compounds like sugar (though its lattice is more complex), potassium permanganate (which forms beautiful purple crystals), and copper sulphate (which forms beautiful blue crystals).

6. Writing Chemical Formulae

A chemical formula tells us the type and number of atoms (or ions) in a substance. Writing correct formulae is essential in chemistry.

Valency: The Combining Capacity

Valency is the combining capacity of an atom — essentially, "how many bonds" an atom can form.

Valencies of common elements:

  • H (Hydrogen): 1
  • C (Carbon): 4
  • N (Nitrogen): 3
  • O (Oxygen): 2
  • Na (Sodium): 1
  • Mg (Magnesium): 2
  • Al (Aluminium): 3
  • Si (Silicon): 4
  • P (Phosphorus): 3
  • S (Sulphur): 2
  • Cl (Chlorine): 1
  • Fe (Iron): 2 or 3
  • Cu (Copper): 1 or 2

Criss-Cross Method

The simplest way to write the formula of an ionic compound:

Steps of the Criss-Cross Method

Step 1: Write the symbols of the cation (positive ion) and anion (negative ion) side by side.

Step 2: Write the valency of each ion above its symbol.

Step 3: "Criss-cross" the valencies — swap them and place them as subscripts below each element.

Step 4: Simplify the subscripts to the lowest whole-number ratio (just like simplifying fractions).

Example 1: Aluminium Oxide

\text{Al}^{3+} \quad \text{and} \quad \text{O}^{2-} \\ \text{Cross the valencies: } 3 \text{ and } 2 \\ \text{Result: } \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3

Example 2: Calcium Chloride

\text{Ca}^{2+} \quad \text{and} \quad \text{Cl}^{1-} \\ \text{Cross the valencies: } 2 \text{ and } 1 \\ \text{Result: } \text{Ca}_1\text{Cl}_2 = \text{CaCl}_2

Example 3: Magnesium Oxide

\text{Mg}^{2+} \quad \text{and} \quad \text{O}^{2-} \\ \text{Cross the valencies: } 2 \text{ and } 2 \\ \text{Result: } \text{Mg}_2\text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{Simplify: MgO}

General Rules for Writing Formulae

  • Write the symbol of the cation (positive ion) FIRST, then the anion (negative ion)
  • Use subscripts to show the number of each atom; omit subscripts if the number is 1
  • Simplify to the lowest whole-number ratio
  • For polyatomic ions, enclose the ion in parentheses if you need more than one of them: Ca(OH)₂, not CaOH₂

Formulae of Common Compounds

  • Sodium chloride: NaCl
  • Magnesium chloride: MgCl₂
  • Calcium carbonate: CaCO₃
  • Sodium hydroxide: NaOH
  • Sulphuric acid: H₂SO₄
  • Nitric acid: HNO₃
  • Sodium carbonate: Na₂CO₃
  • Potassium nitrate: KNO₃
  • Ammonium sulphate: (NH₄)₂SO₄
  • Calcium hydroxide: Ca(OH)₂
✍️ Did You Know?

The criss-cross method works because the total positive charge must equal the total negative charge in a neutral compound. If you have Al³⁺ (three positive charges) and O²⁻ (two negative charges), you need 2 Al³⁺ ions (total 6+ charge) and 3 O²⁻ ions (total 6- charge) to balance. That's why the formula is Al₂O₃!

7. Molecular Mass and Mole Concept

Molecular Mass (Molar Mass)

Molecular mass is calculated by adding up the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule. It's measured in atomic mass units (u).

\text{Molecular Mass} = \sum (\text{number of atoms} \times \text{atomic mass})

Example 1: Water (H₂O)

\text{H}_2\text{O} = 2(1) + 1(16) = 2 + 16 = 18 \text{ u}

Example 2: Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

\text{CO}_2 = 1(12) + 2(16) = 12 + 32 = 44 \text{ u}

Example 3: Sulphuric Acid (H₂SO₄)

\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 = 2(1) + 1(32) + 4(16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 \text{ u}

Example 4: Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)

\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 = 6(12) + 12(1) + 6(16) = 72 + 12 + 96 = 180 \text{ u}

Formula Unit Mass (For Ionic Compounds)

For ionic compounds (which don't exist as discrete molecules), we use the term formula unit mass instead of molecular mass.

Example: Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

\text{NaCl} = 1(23) + 1(35.5) = 58.5 \text{ u}

The Mole Concept (THE MOST IMPORTANT CONCEPT!)

What Is a Mole?

A mole is a unit of measurement, just like a "dozen" (12) or a "gross" (144). But instead of 12 or 144 items, 1 mole contains 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, ions, etc.).

💡 Think of it like this...

Just as we say "a dozen eggs" (12 eggs), we can say "a mole of water" (6.022 × 10²³ water molecules). The word "mole" is simply a convenient way to express a very large, specific number of particles.

Avogadro's Number

Avogadro's number (Nₐ) = 6.022 × 10²³

🔬 Did You Know?

6.022 × 10²³ is an enormous number. To put it in perspective: if every person on Earth (about 8 billion) counted one particle per second, 24 hours a day, it would take roughly 2.4 billion years to count to Avogadro's number! It's a number almost as large as the number of stars in the observable universe.

The Magic Relationship: Molar Mass

Here's the beautiful connection: The molar mass (molecular mass in grams) of any substance equals its molecular mass in atomic mass units (u).

\begin{align} \text{Water: Molecular mass} &= 18 \text{ u} \\ \text{Molar mass of water} &= 18 \text{ g/mol} \end{align}

Key principle:

1 \text{ mole of any substance} = \text{Its molar mass in grams}

Examples:

  • 1 mole of H atoms = 1 g
  • 1 mole of O atoms = 16 g
  • 1 mole of H₂O molecules = 18 g
  • 1 mole of CO₂ molecules = 44 g
  • 1 mole of H₂SO₄ molecules = 98 g
  • 1 mole of NaCl ions = 58.5 g

Calculations with the Mole Concept

Formula 1: Number of Moles
n = \frac{m}{M}

Where:

  • n = number of moles
  • m = given mass (in grams)
  • M = molar mass (in g/mol)

Example: How many moles are in 36 g of water (H₂O)?

n = \frac{36}{18} = 2 \text{ moles}
Formula 2: Number of Particles
N = n \times N_A

Where:

  • N = total number of particles
  • n = number of moles
  • Nₐ = Avogadro's number (6.022 × 10²³)

Example: How many molecules are in 2 moles of water?

N = 2 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 1.2044 \times 10^{24} \text{ molecules}
Combined Formula: From Mass to Particles
N = \frac{m}{M} \times N_A

Example: How many molecules are in 36 g of water?

N = \frac{36}{18} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 2 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 1.2044 \times 10^{24} \text{ molecules}

Counting Atoms in a Compound

Once you know the number of molecules, you can find the number of specific atoms:

N_{\text{atoms}} = N_{\text{molecules}} \times \text{(number of atoms of that type per molecule)}

Example: How many hydrogen atoms are in 36 g of water?

\begin{align} N_{\text{molecules}} &= 1.2044 \times 10^{24} \text{ molecules} \\ \text{(Each H}_2\text{O has 2 H atoms)} \\ N_{\text{H atoms}} &= 1.2044 \times 10^{24} \times 2 = 2.4088 \times 10^{24} \text{ atoms} \end{align}
⚛️ Did You Know?

The mole concept is so fundamental to chemistry that it's sometimes called "the bridge between the microscopic world of atoms and the macroscopic world we observe." Without the mole concept, it would be impossible to predict the quantities of products in chemical reactions or to balance equations quantitatively. It's one of the most elegant and powerful ideas in all of science!

Q1: Which postulate of Dalton's theory is STILL valid today?
Correct! Option (c) is the best answer. While (a) was also Dalton's postulate, it's now known to be false — atoms ARE divisible into electrons, protons, and neutrons. However, postulate (c) about whole-number ratios remains valid.
Q2: The molecular mass of H₂SO₄ is:
Correct! H₂SO₄ = 2(1) + 32 + 4(16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 u. This is molar mass is 98 g/mol.
Q3: What is the formula of aluminium oxide?
Correct! Al has valency 3⁺ and O has valency 2⁻. Using the criss-cross method: Al₃ and O₂ → Al₂O₃ (simplified).
Q4: 1 mole of water molecules contains:
Correct! By definition, 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles. Note: 1 mole of H₂O contains 6.022 × 10²³ molecules, but 3 × 6.022 × 10²³ atoms (2 H + 1 O per molecule).
Q5: The chemical symbol for iron is Fe because:
Correct! Berzelius adopted Latin names to avoid confusion. Iron = Ferrum in Latin, so Fe. Similarly, Gold = Aurum (Au), Silver = Argentum (Ag), Sodium = Natrium (Na), etc.
Q6: Which of these is a polyatomic ion?
Correct! SO₄²⁻ (sulphate) is a polyatomic ion — it's made of one S atom and four O atoms bonded together with a net charge of 2-. The others are monatomic ions (single atoms with charges).

Exam Preparation

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

Q1:
If atoms of carbon from a diamond and atoms of carbon from charcoal are the same, why do they look and behave so differently?
Expected Answer

Although the individual carbon atoms are identical, the way they are ARRANGED is completely different. In diamond, carbon atoms are bonded in a strong, three-dimensional crystalline lattice with each carbon bonded to four others in a tetrahedral arrangement. In charcoal, the carbon atoms are much more loosely arranged in layers. This difference in atomic arrangement (called "allotropy") gives them completely different properties: diamond is hard and transparent, while charcoal is soft and black. This demonstrates that the properties of a substance depend not just on what atoms it contains, but on how those atoms are arranged.

Q2:
Water from any source — river, rain, or even ancient ice — always has hydrogen and oxygen in a 1:8 mass ratio. Which law does this observation verify, and what does it tell us about the nature of chemical compounds?
Expected Answer

This observation verifies the Law of Constant Proportions (Proust's Law). It tells us that a chemical compound has a definite composition — the types and ratios of atoms in a compound are fixed and unchanging. Water will always be H₂O, no matter where it comes from or how it's prepared. This constancy of composition is fundamental to the definition of a chemical compound. It also implies that compounds are formed by atoms combining in specific, fixed ratios (supporting Dalton's postulate about whole-number ratios).

Q3:
Calculate the number of molecules and the number of atoms in 36 grams of water (H₂O).
Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Calculate molar mass of H₂O

M_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} = 2(1) + 1(16) = 18 \text{ g/mol}

Step 2: Calculate number of moles

n = \frac{m}{M} = \frac{36}{18} = 2 \text{ moles}

Step 3: Calculate number of molecules

N_{\text{molecules}} = n \times N_A = 2 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 1.2044 \times 10^{24} \text{ molecules}

Step 4: Calculate total number of atoms

Each H₂O molecule contains 3 atoms (2 H + 1 O)

N_{\text{atoms}} = 1.2044 \times 10^{24} \times 3 = 3.6132 \times 10^{24} \text{ atoms}

Final Answer:

  • Number of molecules = 1.2044 × 10²⁴
  • Number of atoms = 3.6132 × 10²⁴

Case-Based Questions (CBSE 2025-26 Pattern)

Case 1: Thermal Decomposition and Conservation of Mass

A student heats 10 g of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) in a test tube and observes a white powder forming. The white powder (calcium oxide, CaO) is weighed and found to be 5.6 g. A colourless gas (carbon dioxide, CO₂) is released and escapes.

Reaction: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂

Questions:

  • Which law of chemical combination explains why the mass of products equals the mass of reactants?
  • Calculate the mass of CO₂ released.
  • If the molar masses are Ca=40, C=12, O=16, and Molar mass of CaCO₃ = 100, CaO = 56, CO₂ = 44, calculate the number of moles of each substance.
Solution

Answer 1: Law of Conservation of Mass

Answer 2: Mass of CO₂ = Mass of CaCO₃ - Mass of CaO = 10 - 5.6 = 4.4 g

Answer 3:

  • Moles of CaCO₃ = 10 ÷ 100 = 0.1 mol
  • Moles of CaO = 5.6 ÷ 56 = 0.1 mol
  • Moles of CO₂ = 4.4 ÷ 44 = 0.1 mol
Case 2: Ancient Atomic Theory vs Modern Understanding

Kanad, an ancient Indian philosopher, proposed around 600 BCE that matter could be divided and subdivided until an indivisible particle was reached. He called this particle "Paramanu." This concept predates Dalton's atomic theory by over 2200 years!

Questions:

  • How does Kanad's "Paramanu" concept compare to Dalton's "atom"?
  • What is the major difference between ancient atomic theory and modern atomic theory?
  • How were Dalton's postulates improved by later scientists like Thomson and Rutherford?
Expected Answers

Answer 1: Kanad's "Paramanu" and Dalton's "atom" are conceptually very similar — both describe an indivisible, fundamental particle of matter. Both philosophers proposed that matter is not continuous but made of discrete particles.

Answer 2: Ancient atomic theory was philosophical and speculative, without experimental evidence. Modern atomic theory is based on rigorous experimental evidence and mathematical relationships.

Answer 3: Dalton proposed atoms are indivisible, but Thomson discovered electrons (1897), showing atoms have internal structure. Rutherford discovered the nucleus (1911), revealing the atom is mostly empty space with a dense central nucleus. This led to the Bohr model and eventually the modern electron cloud model.

Case 3: Mole Calculations and Atomic Counting

A chemistry class has 9 grams of pure water. The teacher asks students to determine how many molecules and how many total atoms are present in this sample, and to calculate the number of hydrogen and oxygen atoms separately.

Given: Molar mass of water = 18 g/mol, Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³

Questions:

  • Calculate the number of moles of water.
  • Calculate the total number of water molecules.
  • Calculate the total number of atoms.
  • Calculate the number of hydrogen atoms and oxygen atoms separately.
Complete Solution

Answer 1: Moles of water

n = \frac{9}{18} = 0.5 \text{ moles}

Answer 2: Number of molecules

N_{\text{molecules}} = 0.5 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 3.011 \times 10^{23} \text{ molecules}

Answer 3: Total atoms

Each water molecule has 3 atoms (2H + 1O)

N_{\text{atoms}} = 3.011 \times 10^{23} \times 3 = 9.033 \times 10^{23} \text{ atoms}

Answer 4: Individual atoms

Hydrogen atoms: Each molecule has 2 H atoms

N_{\text{H}} = 3.011 \times 10^{23} \times 2 = 6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ atoms}

Oxygen atoms: Each molecule has 1 O atom

N_{\text{O}} = 3.011 \times 10^{23} \times 1 = 3.011 \times 10^{23} \text{ atoms}

An atom or group of atoms carrying an electric charge due to loss or gain of electrons
Cation
A positively charged ion formed by loss of electrons
Anion
A negatively charged ion formed by gain of electrons
Valency
The combining capacity of an atom, representing the number of bonds it can form
Atomic Mass Unit (u)
A unit of mass equal to 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom; 1 u = 1.66 × 10⁻²⁴ g
Molecular Mass
The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule, measured in atomic mass units (u)
Molar Mass
The mass of one mole of a substance in grams per mole (g/mol); numerically equal to its molecular mass in u
Mole
A unit of measurement containing 6.022 × 10²³ particles; used to count very large numbers of atoms, molecules, or ions
Avogadro's Number (Nₐ)
The number of particles in one mole of a substance; 6.022 × 10²³
Atomicity
The number of atoms present in a molecule of an element or compound
Law of Conservation of Mass
Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction; total mass of reactants = total mass of products
Law of Constant Proportions
A compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass, regardless of source or preparation method

Remediation Guide: Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Confusing atoms and molecules
Wrong: "One oxygen molecule contains one oxygen atom"
Right: Oxygen exists as O₂ (two atoms bonded together). One oxygen molecule contains two oxygen atoms.
Mistake 2: Forgetting to simplify in the criss-cross method
Wrong: Mg₂O₂ is the formula of magnesium oxide
Right: Simplify to MgO (divide by 2). The lowest whole-number ratio is required.
Mistake 3: Incorrect calculation of molecular mass
Wrong: H₂O = 1 + 1 + 16 = 18 (forgetting to multiply H by 2)
Right: H₂O = 2(1) + 16 = 2 + 16 = 18
Mistake 4: Confusing moles with grams
Wrong: "18 grams of water = 18 moles of water"
Right: 18 grams of water = 1 mole of water (molar mass of H₂O = 18 g/mol)
Mistake 5: Forgetting Avogadro's number is a coefficient
Wrong: Calculating particles without multiplying moles by Nₐ
Right: Always use: Number of particles = n × 6.022 × 10²³
Mistake 6: Writing ions inside compounds
Wrong: Magnesium has valency 2⁺ when writing MgO
Right: In compounds, atoms combine with valencies, but we don't write the charges in the formula. MgO (not Mg²⁺O²⁻)
