Chapter 11: Thermodynamics

"Can you build a perfect engine?"

Understanding heat, work, and the fundamental laws that govern energy transformation in the universe.

1. Thermal Equilibrium & Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

Core Concepts

Thermodynamics deals with the study of heat and work and how they relate to energy and the properties of matter. A thermodynamic system consists of a large number of particles (atoms and molecules) whose behavior we need to analyze.

System and Surroundings

  • System: The portion of the universe we are studying (e.g., a gas in a container, a block of metal)
  • Surroundings: Everything else in the universe external to the system
  • Boundary: The real or imaginary surface separating the system from surroundings
  • Isolated System: No exchange of matter or energy with surroundings (e.g., ideal thermos)
  • Closed System: Exchange of energy but not matter with surroundings
  • Open System: Exchange of both matter and energy with surroundings

Thermal Equilibrium

Two systems are in thermal equilibrium when there is no net flow of heat between them even when they are brought into contact. This occurs when both systems are at the same temperature.

Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

Statement: If two systems A and B are separately in thermal equilibrium with a third system C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

This fundamental law establishes the concept of temperature. Temperature is a property that determines whether a system is in thermal equilibrium with other systems. It is the parameter that is the same for all systems in thermal equilibrium with one another.

Key Insight
Temperature is defined as the physical quantity that is equal for systems in thermal equilibrium. This allows us to define the concept of temperature based on the empirical property of thermal equilibrium rather than molecular motion.

Temperature Scales

  • Celsius (°C): Water freezes at 0°C, boils at 100°C (at 1 atm)
  • Fahrenheit (°F): T(°F) = 1.8 × T(°C) + 32
  • Kelvin (K): Absolute temperature scale; T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15
  • Absolute Zero: 0 K = -273.15°C (lowest possible temperature; no molecular motion)

2. Internal Energy, Heat, and Work

Internal Energy (U)

The internal energy of a system is the total kinetic and potential energy of all the particles (atoms and molecules) in the system. It includes:

  • Kinetic energy of translational motion of molecules
  • Kinetic energy of rotational motion of molecules
  • Kinetic energy of vibrational motion within molecules
  • Potential energy due to intermolecular forces

Important Properties of Internal Energy

  • State Function: U depends only on the current state of the system (temperature, pressure, volume), not on how the system reached that state
  • Extensive Property: U is proportional to the amount of substance in the system
  • For Ideal Gases: U depends only on temperature: U = nCᵥT (where Cᵥ is molar heat capacity at constant volume)
Change in Internal Energy
$$\Delta U = U_{\text{final}} - U_{\text{initial}}$$ For an ideal gas: $$\Delta U = nC_v \Delta T$$

Heat (Q)

Heat is the transfer of thermal energy from one system to another due to a temperature difference. It is the energy flowing from a hotter body to a cooler body.

Heat as a Path Function

  • Heat is NOT a state function; it depends on the path taken by the system
  • Different processes between the same initial and final states result in different amounts of heat transfer
  • Heat is process-dependent

Sign Convention for Heat

  • Q > 0: Heat is absorbed by the system (endothermic process)
  • Q < 0: Heat is released by the system (exothermic process)
  • Q = 0: No heat transfer (adiabatic process)

Work (W)

Work is the energy transfer that is not due to heat. In thermodynamics, we primarily consider work done during volume changes (P-V work).

Work Done BY and ON the System

Work Expression
$$W = \int P_{\text{ext}} \, dV$$ For constant external pressure: $$W = P_{\text{ext}} \Delta V$$

Sign Convention for Work

  • W > 0: Work is done BY the system (system expands, pushes surroundings)
  • W < 0: Work is done ON the system (system is compressed)
  • W = 0: No volume change (isochoric process)

Work as a Path Function

  • Like heat, work is NOT a state function
  • Work depends on the process path
  • Different processes between the same initial and final states result in different amounts of work
PV Diagram Visualization: A PV diagram plots pressure (vertical axis) against volume (horizontal axis). The area under a curve in a PV diagram represents the work done. For the same initial and final states, different paths (isothermal, adiabatic, isobaric) have different areas, representing different work values.

3. First Law of Thermodynamics

Statement

The First Law of Thermodynamics is a statement of conservation of energy. It states that the change in internal energy of a system equals the heat supplied to the system minus the work done by the system.

First Law of Thermodynamics
$$\Delta U = Q - W$$
Alternative form: $$Q = \Delta U + W$$
Where:
ΔU = change in internal energy
Q = heat absorbed by the system
W = work done by the system

Physical Interpretation

  • When heat Q is added to a system, part of it increases the internal energy (ΔU), and the rest is used to do work on the surroundings (W)
  • When work is done on a system and no heat is removed, the internal energy increases
  • The first law is equivalent to the conservation of energy principle

For Ideal Gases

Internal Energy Change for Ideal Gas
$$\Delta U = nC_v \Delta T$$
Where:
n = number of moles
Cᵥ = molar heat capacity at constant volume
ΔT = change in temperature

Molar Heat Capacities

  • Cᵥ: Heat capacity at constant volume (no work done)
  • Cₚ: Heat capacity at constant pressure (work done against external pressure)
  • Mayer's Relation: Cₚ - Cᵥ = R (where R = 8.314 J/(mol·K))

Heat Capacities for Ideal Gases

  • Monatomic gas (He, Ne, Ar): Cᵥ = (3/2)R, Cₚ = (5/2)R, γ = Cₚ/Cᵥ = 5/3
  • Diatomic gas (O₂, N₂, H₂): Cᵥ = (5/2)R, Cₚ = (7/2)R, γ = 7/5 = 1.4
  • Polyatomic gas (CO₂, H₂O): Cᵥ = 3R, Cₚ = 4R, γ = 4/3 ≈ 1.33

Applications of First Law

The first law can be applied to various processes by substituting appropriate expressions for Q and W for each specific process.

4. Thermodynamic Processes

A thermodynamic process is a change in the state of a system. Different processes are distinguished by which variables (P, V, T) remain constant.

Comprehensive Process Comparison Table

Process Constant Variable Work Done Heat Absorbed ΔU Relation
Isothermal T = constant W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁) Q = nRT ln(V₂/V₁) ΔU = 0 PV = constant
Adiabatic Q = 0 W = (P₁V₁ - P₂V₂)/(γ-1) Q = 0 ΔU = W PVᵞ = constant
TVᵞ⁻¹ = constant
Isochoric V = constant W = 0 Q = nCᵥΔT ΔU = nCᵥΔT P/T = constant
Isobaric P = constant W = PΔV = nRΔT Q = nCₚΔT ΔU = nCᵥΔT V/T = constant

Isothermal Process (T = constant)

In an isothermal process, the temperature of the system remains constant. For an ideal gas undergoing an isothermal process:

Isothermal Process Relations
$$PV = \text{constant} \quad \text{or} \quad P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$$ $$W = nRT \ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right) = nRT \ln\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)$$ $$\Delta U = 0 \text{ (no change in temperature)}$$ $$Q = W = nRT \ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)$$
  • If V₂ > V₁ (expansion): W > 0, Q > 0 (heat absorbed)
  • If V₂ < V₁ (compression): W < 0, Q < 0 (heat released)
  • Graphically: Isothermal process is a hyperbola on a PV diagram

Adiabatic Process (Q = 0)

In an adiabatic process, there is no heat exchange with the surroundings (Q = 0). The system is thermally insulated.

Adiabatic Process Relations
$$PV^\gamma = \text{constant}$$ $$TV^{\gamma-1} = \text{constant}$$ $$P^{1-\gamma}T^\gamma = \text{constant}$$ $$W = \frac{P_1V_1 - P_2V_2}{\gamma - 1} = \frac{nR(T_1 - T_2)}{\gamma - 1}$$ $$\Delta U = W = -Q = nC_v(T_2 - T_1)$$ Where γ = Cₚ/Cᵥ (adiabatic index or heat capacity ratio)
  • If the gas expands adiabatically (V₂ > V₁): T₂ < T₁, pressure and temperature decrease
  • If the gas is compressed adiabatically (V₂ < V₁): T₂ > T₁, pressure and temperature increase
  • Adiabatic process is steeper than isothermal on a PV diagram
  • Example: Compression stroke in a diesel engine, rapid expansion of air when opening a pressurized container

Isochoric Process (V = constant)

In an isochoric (constant volume) process, the volume of the system remains constant. No work is done during this process.

Isochoric Process Relations
$$W = 0 \text{ (no volume change)}$$ $$Q = nC_v\Delta T$$ $$\Delta U = nC_v\Delta T = Q$$ $$\frac{P}{T} = \text{constant} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2}$$
  • All heat absorbed/released goes into changing internal energy
  • Graphically: Vertical line on a PV diagram
  • Example: Heating gas in a rigid, sealed container

Isobaric Process (P = constant)

In an isobaric (constant pressure) process, the pressure remains constant. This is common in real processes where the system is in contact with the atmosphere.

Isobaric Process Relations
$$W = P\Delta V = nR\Delta T$$ $$Q = nC_p\Delta T$$ $$\Delta U = nC_v\Delta T$$ $$\frac{V}{T} = \text{constant} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}$$
  • Heat absorbed is distributed between increasing internal energy and doing work
  • Q = ΔU + W (First Law with W = PΔV)
  • Graphically: Horizontal line on a PV diagram
  • Example: Boiling water in an open container, heating air in a balloon

Mayer's Relation

Relationship Between Heat Capacities
$$C_p - C_v = R$$
This important relation shows that the difference between the two heat capacities is always equal to the gas constant R.

$$\gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_v}$$

PV Diagram Visualization

PV Diagram Features:
  • Isothermal: Hyperbolic curve, area under curve = work done
  • Adiabatic: Steeper than isothermal, no area interpretation for Q (Q=0)
  • Isochoric: Vertical straight line (no volume change)
  • Isobaric: Horizontal straight line (no pressure change)
The area enclosed between a curve and the volume axis in a PV diagram represents the work done during the process. The shape of the curve determines the work value for the same initial and final states.

5. Heat Engines

Definition and Basic Principle

A heat engine is a device that converts heat energy into mechanical work. It operates in a cyclic process, absorbing heat from a hot reservoir, performing useful work, and rejecting heat to a cold reservoir.

Components of a Heat Engine

  • Hot Reservoir: Source of heat at high temperature T₁ (or Tₕ), supplies heat Q₁
  • Cold Reservoir: Sink of heat at low temperature T₂ (or Tᶜ), receives heat Q₂
  • Working Substance: Usually a gas that undergoes cyclic changes

Efficiency of Heat Engines

Heat Engine Efficiency
$$\eta = \frac{W}{Q_1} = \frac{Q_1 - Q_2}{Q_1} = 1 - \frac{Q_2}{Q_1}$$
Where:
η = efficiency (dimensionless, 0 < η < 1)
W = useful work output
Q₁ = heat absorbed from hot reservoir
Q₂ = heat rejected to cold reservoir

Key Points about Efficiency

  • Efficiency is always less than 1 (less than 100%)
  • No heat engine can convert all input heat into work
  • Higher efficiency means more work output for the same heat input
  • Efficiency increases with increasing temperature difference between reservoirs

Carnot Engine - The Ideal Heat Engine

A Carnot engine is a hypothetical heat engine that operates on a reversible cycle and represents the maximum possible efficiency for any heat engine operating between two temperature reservoirs.

Carnot Cycle

The Carnot cycle consists of four reversible processes:

  1. Isothermal Expansion (A→B): Gas expands at constant temperature T₁ (hot reservoir), absorbs heat Q₁, does work W₁
  2. Adiabatic Expansion (B→C): Gas expands adiabatically, temperature drops from T₁ to T₂, does work W₂, Q = 0
  3. Isothermal Compression (C→D): Gas is compressed at constant temperature T₂ (cold reservoir), rejects heat Q₂, work W₃ done on gas
  4. Adiabatic Compression (D→A): Gas is compressed adiabatically, temperature rises from T₂ to T₁, work W₄ done on gas, Q = 0
Carnot Cycle PV Diagram: The cycle appears as a closed loop on a PV diagram. The two horizontal portions represent isothermal processes (hyperbolic curves), and the two curved portions represent adiabatic processes (steeper than isothermal). The area enclosed within the cycle represents the net work done by the engine in one cycle.

Carnot Efficiency

Maximum Heat Engine Efficiency
$$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1}$$
Or equivalently:
$$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_1 - T_2}{T_1}$$
Where T₁ and T₂ are absolute temperatures (in Kelvin)

Key Features of Carnot Engine

  • No real heat engine can be more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between the same temperature reservoirs
  • Carnot efficiency depends only on the absolute temperatures, not on the properties of the working substance
  • To increase efficiency: increase T₁ or decrease T₂
  • Maximum efficiency possible is less than 100% (would require T₂ = 0 K, which is impossible)
  • The Carnot engine is reversible and operates infinitely slowly (not practical)

Example: Carnot Efficiency Calculation

A Carnot engine operates between a hot reservoir at 500 K and a cold reservoir at 300 K.

$$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{300}{500} = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4 = 40\%$$

This means at most 40% of the heat absorbed can be converted to work.

Energy Balance in Heat Engines

Energy Conservation in Heat Engines
$$W = Q_1 - Q_2$$
In a complete cycle: $$\Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0$$
Therefore: $$Q_{\text{net}} = Q_1 - Q_2 = W$$

6. Refrigerators and Heat Pumps

Refrigerators

A refrigerator is a device that transfers heat from a cold reservoir (inside) to a hot reservoir (outside) using mechanical work. It operates opposite to a heat engine.

Components

  • Cold Reservoir: Inside the refrigerator at temperature T₂, where heat Q₂ is absorbed
  • Hot Reservoir: Outside the refrigerator at temperature T₁, where heat Q₁ is rejected
  • Working Substance: Usually a refrigerant fluid

Coefficient of Performance (COP) for Refrigerators

Refrigerator COP
$$\text{COP} = \frac{Q_2}{W} = \frac{Q_2}{Q_1 - Q_2}$$
Where:
COP = coefficient of performance
Q₂ = heat extracted from cold reservoir
W = work input required
Q₁ = heat delivered to hot reservoir

Significance of COP

  • COP > 1 (usually much greater, e.g., 4-6 for good refrigerators)
  • Higher COP means more efficient cooling for the same work input
  • COP can be greater than 1 (unlike engine efficiency)

Carnot Refrigerator

Carnot Refrigerator COP
$$\text{COP}_{\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_2}{T_1 - T_2}$$
This represents the maximum possible COP for a refrigerator operating between T₁ and T₂.

Heat Pumps

A heat pump is essentially a refrigerator operated in reverse. It transfers heat from a cold reservoir (outdoors) to a hot reservoir (indoors) for heating purposes.

Coefficient of Performance for Heat Pumps

Heat Pump COP
$$\text{COP} = \frac{Q_1}{W} = \frac{Q_1}{Q_1 - Q_2}$$
Alternatively: $$\text{COP} = 1 + \frac{Q_2}{W}$$
For Carnot heat pump: $$\text{COP}_{\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_1}{T_1 - T_2}$$

Heat Pump vs Refrigerator

  • Same device, different perspective
  • Refrigerator focuses on cooling (Q₂)
  • Heat pump focuses on heating (Q₁)
  • For Carnot devices: COP_heat pump = COP_refrigerator + 1

Practical Applications

  • Refrigerators and Air Conditioners: Remove heat from interior spaces
  • Heat Pumps: Provide heating for homes and buildings in winter
  • Industrial Processes: Liquefaction of gases, food preservation

7. Second Law of Thermodynamics

Statement and Significance

The Second Law of Thermodynamics addresses the directionality and feasibility of natural processes. It states that the entropy of an isolated system always increases for any spontaneous process.

Two Important Statements

Kelvin-Planck Statement

"It is impossible to construct a heat engine that operates in a complete cycle and produces no effect other than the absorption of heat from a single reservoir and the conversion of that heat entirely into work."

  • No heat engine can be 100% efficient
  • Heat must be rejected to a cold reservoir
  • Some energy is always "wasted" as heat to the surroundings

Clausius Statement

"It is impossible to construct a device that operates in a complete cycle and produces no effect other than the transfer of heat from a cooler body to a hotter body."

  • Heat does not spontaneously flow from cold to hot
  • Heat flows naturally from hot to cold
  • To reverse this flow requires work input (e.g., refrigerator)

Equivalence of the Two Statements

The Kelvin-Planck and Clausius statements are mathematically equivalent. A violation of one leads to a violation of the other. Together, they express the fundamental asymmetry of nature and the directionality of spontaneous processes.

Entropy

Entropy (S) is a measure of the disorder or randomness of a system. It is a state function that increases for spontaneous (irreversible) processes in an isolated system.

Entropy Change
$$dS = \frac{dQ_{\text{rev}}}{T}$$
For a finite process at constant temperature:
$$\Delta S = \frac{Q}{T}$$
For an isolated system (Second Law):
$$\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} > 0$$

Reversible and Irreversible Processes

Reversible Processes

  • Infinitely slow processes where the system is always in equilibrium
  • Can be reversed by an infinitesimal change in external conditions
  • No entropy generation within the system itself
  • Examples: Ideal frictionless motion, isothermal expansion/compression at equilibrium
  • No real process is perfectly reversible, but some can approximate reversibility

Irreversible Processes

  • Spontaneous, actual processes in nature
  • Cannot be reversed without external intervention
  • Generate entropy within the system
  • Examples: Heat flow from hot to cold, free expansion of gas, combustion, friction
  • For irreversible processes: ΔS > Q/T

Key Insight

The Second Law tells us that all real processes are irreversible. The universe tends towards greater disorder (higher entropy). This explains why certain processes never occur spontaneously in reverse (e.g., heat never flows from cold to hot on its own, a broken cup never reassembles itself).

Entropy and Disorder

  • High entropy: System is disordered, molecules randomly distributed
  • Low entropy: System is ordered, organized molecular arrangement
  • Natural processes tend to increase disorder (increase entropy)
  • Creating order requires work input and generates even more disorder elsewhere

8. Carnot Cycle - Complete Analysis

Four Processes in the Carnot Cycle

Process 1: Isothermal Expansion (A → B)

  • Temperature: constant at T₁ (hot reservoir)
  • Volume: increases from Vₐ to V_b
  • Heat absorbed: Q₁ = nRT₁ ln(V_b/Vₐ)
  • Work done by gas: W₁ = Q₁
  • Internal energy change: ΔU = 0

Process 2: Adiabatic Expansion (B → C)

  • No heat transfer: Q = 0
  • Temperature: decreases from T₁ to T₂
  • Volume: increases from V_b to V_c
  • Work done by gas: W₂ = nCᵥ(T₁ - T₂)
  • Relation: T₁V_b^(γ-1) = T₂V_c^(γ-1)

Process 3: Isothermal Compression (C → D)

  • Temperature: constant at T₂ (cold reservoir)
  • Volume: decreases from V_c to V_d
  • Heat rejected: Q₂ = nRT₂ ln(V_c/V_d)
  • Work done on gas: W₃ = Q₂ (negative work by gas)
  • Internal energy change: ΔU = 0

Process 4: Adiabatic Compression (D → A)

  • No heat transfer: Q = 0
  • Temperature: increases from T₂ to T₁
  • Volume: decreases from V_d to Vₐ
  • Work done on gas: W₄ = nCᵥ(T₁ - T₂)
  • Relation: T₂V_d^(γ-1) = T₁Vₐ^(γ-1)

Net Results for One Complete Cycle

Carnot Cycle Summary
Net work output: $$W_{\text{net}} = Q_1 - Q_2$$
Heat ratio: $$\frac{Q_1}{Q_2} = \frac{T_1}{T_2}$$
Efficiency: $$\eta = \frac{W_{\text{net}}}{Q_1} = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1}$$
Change in entropy: $$\Delta S_{\text{cycle}} = 0 \text{ (reversible process)}$$

PV Diagram Features

Carnot Cycle on PV Diagram:
  • A to B: Hyperbolic curve (isothermal), area under curve = Q₁ = W₁
  • B to C: Steep curve (adiabatic expansion)
  • C to D: Hyperbolic curve (isothermal), area under curve = Q₂
  • D to A: Steep curve (adiabatic compression)
The area enclosed by the cycle = net work done in one cycle. The cycle is traversed clockwise for a heat engine (net work output positive).

Why Carnot Engine is Most Efficient

  • Operates reversibly (no irreversibilities)
  • Heat absorption occurs at maximum temperature (T₁)
  • Heat rejection occurs at minimum temperature (T₂)
  • All processes are either reversible isothermal or reversible adiabatic
  • Any other cycle between the same temperature limits has lower efficiency

Practical Limitations

  • Carnot engine is a theoretical ideal, not practically realizable
  • Would require infinitely slow operation (zero power output)
  • No real process can be perfectly reversible
  • Real heat engines use irreversible processes and have lower efficiency
  • Examples of real engines: Otto cycle (cars), Diesel cycle (diesel engines)

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Problems

HOTS 1: Multi-Process Analysis

Problem: One mole of an ideal diatomic gas initially at 300 K and 1 atm pressure undergoes the following processes sequentially:

  1. Isothermal expansion from 1 atm to 0.5 atm
  2. Adiabatic compression back to 1 atm

Questions:

  • Calculate the volume at each state
  • Find the final temperature after adiabatic compression
  • Calculate total heat absorbed and work done in the complete process
  • What is the entropy change? (For isothermal: ΔS = nR ln(V₂/V₁))

Solution Approach: For isothermal: PV = const, for adiabatic: TV^(γ-1) = const (γ = 7/5 for diatomic). Track changes through each process using First Law and entropy definitions.

HOTS 2: Carnot Cycle Analysis

Problem: A Carnot engine operates between a hot reservoir at 500 K and a cold reservoir at 250 K. The engine performs 1000 J of work in one complete cycle.

Questions:

  • Calculate the heat absorbed from the hot reservoir
  • Calculate the heat rejected to the cold reservoir
  • Verify that Q₁/Q₂ = T₁/T₂
  • If this were replaced by a non-Carnot engine doing the same 1000 J work, what would be the minimum heat that must be absorbed?

Solution Approach: Use η = W/Q₁ = 1 - T₂/T₁ for Carnot. Calculate Q₁ from work and efficiency, then find Q₂. Compare with other engines.

HOTS 3: Real Engine vs Ideal Engine

Problem: A power plant uses a heat engine with hot steam at 400 K and cooling water at 300 K. It absorbs 2000 MW of heat from the steam.

Questions:

  • What is the theoretical maximum power output (assuming Carnot efficiency)?
  • If the actual power output is only 300 MW, what is the actual efficiency?
  • Calculate the actual heat rejected to cooling water
  • Discuss why real engines cannot achieve Carnot efficiency

Solution Approach: Calculate Carnot efficiency first: η = 1 - 300/400 = 25%. Maximum work = 0.25 × 2000 MW. Compare with actual. Discuss irreversibilities, friction, finite temperature differences.

Case-Based Questions

Case Study 1: Air Conditioning System

Scenario: A room air conditioner removes 5 kW of heat from a room and operates between an indoor temperature of 25°C and an outdoor temperature of 45°C. The compressor consumes 2 kW of electrical power.

Questions:

  • Calculate the actual COP of the air conditioner
  • Calculate the theoretical maximum COP (Carnot refrigerator)
  • What is the heat rejected to the outdoor air?
  • Compare actual vs theoretical and explain the difference

Expected Answer: Actual COP = Q₂/W = 5/2 = 2.5. Carnot COP = T₂/(T₁-T₂) = 298/(318-298) = 14.9. The device is far less efficient than ideal due to irreversibilities in compressor, piping, etc.

Case Study 2: Diesel Engine Compression

Scenario: In a diesel engine, air is compressed adiabatically from an initial state of 1 atm, 300 K before ignition. The compression ratio (V₁/V₂) is 20:1. (For air, γ = 1.4, R = 287 J/(kg·K))

Questions:

  • Calculate the final temperature after compression
  • Calculate the work done on the air (for 1 kg of air)
  • Is the final temperature sufficient for ignition of diesel (≈ 500-700 K)? Why/why not?
  • Discuss the role of adiabatic compression in diesel engines

Expected Answer: Use T₂ = T₁(V₁/V₂)^(γ-1) = 300 × 20^0.4 ≈ 880 K. W = Cᵥ(T₂-T₁) = 0.717 × 580 ≈ 416 kJ/kg. Temperature is well above ignition point, showing adiabatic compression is the key to diesel engine operation without spark plugs.

Case Study 3: Power Generation Efficiency

Scenario: A thermal power plant uses the following cycle:
Stage 1 (Isobaric heating): Gas at constant pressure absorbs 2000 kJ of heat
Stage 2 (Adiabatic expansion): Gas expands adiabatically doing 1500 kJ of work
Stage 3 (Isobaric cooling): Gas rejects heat at constant pressure back to initial state

Questions:

  • What is the total work output of the cycle?
  • Calculate total heat input to the cycle
  • What is the thermal efficiency?
  • Compare with Carnot efficiency if T_hot = 600 K and T_cold = 400 K

Expected Answer: Total work ≈ 1500 kJ (adiabatic) + work in isobaric stages. Use First Law to find stage 3 heat. Efficiency = W_net/Q_in. Carnot: η = 1 - 400/600 = 33.3%. Real cycle efficiency will be less due to irreversibilities.

  • Zeroth Law: Establishes temperature concept through thermal equilibrium
  • First Law: Energy conservation; ΔU = Q - W
  • Second Law: Entropy increases in isolated systems; no 100% efficient heat engine
  • Carnot Limit: No engine can exceed Carnot efficiency for same temperature reservoirs
  • State vs Path Functions: U and S are state functions; Q and W are path functions

Sign Conventions Quick Reference

  • Q > 0: Heat absorbed by system
  • Q < 0: Heat released by system
  • W > 0: Work done by system (expansion)
  • W < 0: Work done on system (compression)
  • ΔU > 0: Internal energy increases (temperature increases)
  • ΔU < 0: Internal energy decreases (temperature decreases)

Common Values

  • Gas constant: R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)
  • Absolute zero: 0 K = -273.15°C
  • Standard pressure: 1 atm = 101,325 Pa
  • Boltzmann constant: k_B = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K
