Work and Energy

Class 8 · Chapter 10 · CBSE Science

🔋 Why does climbing stairs make you tired but pushing a wall doesn't? Discover the scientific secrets of work and energy!

⚙️ Understanding Work: Beyond the Everyday Definition

In everyday language, we say "work" means doing a job or task. But in physics, workDefined by force × displacement has a very specific scientific meaning that might surprise you!

The Scientific Definition of Work

Work is done when:

  • A force acts on an object, AND
  • The object moves in the direction of the force

If either condition is missing, no work is done—scientifically speaking!

Everyday vs Scientific Work: Examples

❌ Everyday work BUT No scientific work:

  • Pushing a brick wall with all your might—force acts but no displacement = Zero work
  • Carrying a heavy suitcase horizontally—gravity acts downward, but movement is horizontal = Zero work (by gravity)
  • Holding a heavy book still—you exert force but nothing moves = Zero work

✓ Scientific work (even if it doesn't feel hard):

  • A porter lifting luggage—force upward, displacement upward = Work done!
  • Pushing a cart on a flat surface—force applied, cart moves = Work done!

The Work Formula

Work (when force is in the direction of displacement):
$$W = F \times s$$

Where:

  • W = Work (in joules, J)
  • F = Force (in newtons, N)
  • s = Displacement (in meters, m)

Unit of Work: The Joule

1 joule (J) = 1 newton (N) × 1 meter (m)

This means if you apply a force of 1 newton to move an object 1 meter in the direction of that force, you've done 1 joule of work.

Types of Work

Positive Work (Force and displacement in the SAME direction)

The force helps the motion. Energy is being given to the object.

Real-world example: A porter lifts a 15 kg bag vertically upward by 1.5 m. The force acts upward, and displacement is upward, so work is positive.
$$W = mgs = 15 \times 10 \times 1.5 = 225 \text{ J}$$
Negative Work (Force and displacement in OPPOSITE directions)

The force opposes the motion. Energy is being taken away from the object.

Real-world example: Friction acts backward when a car brakes. The friction force opposes the forward motion, so work done by friction is negative (it slows the car down).
Zero Work (Force perpendicular to displacement, or no displacement)

When force acts at right angles to motion, or when there's no motion at all.

Real-world example: Carrying a book horizontally. Gravity (downward force) does zero work because displacement is horizontal (perpendicular to gravity).
💡 Analogy: Think of work like pushing a shopping cart. If you push forward and the cart moves forward, you're doing positive work (helping it move). If you push backward while it's moving forward, you're doing negative work (resisting motion). If you push sideways while it moves forward, you're doing zero work in the forward direction.

⚡ What is Energy? The Capacity to Do Work

EnergyCapacity to do work, measured in joules is one of the most fundamental concepts in physics. It's the ability or capacity of an object to do work.

Energy: A Simple Definition

💡 Think of it this way: If you have energy, you have the power to change things—to move objects, heat things up, light things, or do any kind of work. An object with energy can exert a force and do work on other objects.

Units of Energy

Energy is measured in the same units as work:

  • Joule (J) - standard unit
  • Kilojoule (kJ) - 1 kJ = 1,000 J
  • Kilowatt-hour (kWh) - used for electricity bills

The Sun: Our Biggest Natural Energy Source

The Sun provides nearly all energy on Earth! From solar energy to fossil fuels to wind and water, almost everything traces back to the Sun's energy.

Different Forms of Energy

Mechanical Energy

Energy due to motion and position. It's split into two types:

  • Kinetic Energy (KE) - energy of motion
  • Potential Energy (PE) - energy of position/configuration
Other Forms of Energy
  • Heat Energy - thermal energy from temperature
  • Chemical Energy - energy stored in chemical bonds (food, batteries)
  • Electrical Energy - energy from moving charges
  • Light Energy (Radiant Energy) - energy from electromagnetic waves
  • Sound Energy - energy from vibrating particles
  • Nuclear Energy - energy from atomic nuclei

Key Insight: Energy and Work

An object with energy can exert a force on other objects and do work. That's why we define energy as the capacity to do work. Energy is what makes changes happen in the universe!

🏃 Kinetic Energy: Energy of Motion

Kinetic EnergyEnergy possessed by a moving object is the energy an object has because of its motion. Anything that's moving has kinetic energy!

The Kinetic Energy Formula

Kinetic Energy:
$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

Where:

  • E_k = Kinetic energy (in joules, J)
  • m = Mass of object (in kg)
  • v = Velocity/speed of object (in m/s)

Why is the formula ½mv²?

The Derivation (for curious minds!)

Using Newton's equations of motion and the work-energy theorem:

From kinematics: $$s = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a}$$

Work done: $$W = Fs = ma \times \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a} = \frac{1}{2}m(v^2 - u^2)$$

If starting from rest (u = 0):

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

Important Properties of Kinetic Energy

  • Always positive - even if moving in negative direction, v² is positive
  • Depends on mass and speed² - doubling speed quadruples KE!
  • Zero at rest - stationary objects have no kinetic energy
  • Scalar quantity - only has magnitude, no direction

Practical Example

Problem: A 15 kg object moves at 4 m/s. Calculate its kinetic energy.
$$E_k = \frac{1}{2} \times 15 \times (4)^2$$ $$E_k = \frac{1}{2} \times 15 \times 16$$ $$E_k = 120 \text{ J}$$

So this moving object has 120 joules of kinetic energy!

The Work-Energy Theorem

The Work-Energy Theorem states:
$$W_{\text{net}} = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}m(v_f^2 - v_i^2)$$

The work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy. This is one of the most powerful ideas in physics!

💡 Real-world analogy: Why is it harder to stop a heavy truck than a light car at the same speed? Because the truck has much more kinetic energy (due to its larger mass). The faster something moves, the more KE it has (and the harder it is to stop).

⬆️ Potential Energy: Energy of Position

Potential EnergyEnergy stored due to position or configuration is the energy stored in an object due to its position, shape, or configuration. It's energy waiting to be released!

Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational Potential Energy:
$$E_p = mgh$$

Where:

  • E_p = Potential energy (in joules, J)
  • m = Mass (in kg)
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity (≈ 10 m/s² or 9.8 m/s²)
  • h = Height above reference level (in meters, m)

Key point: PE depends on height from a reference level. We choose our reference (often the ground), and measure height from there.

Practical Example

Problem: A 10 kg object is placed on a shelf 6 m above the ground. Calculate its potential energy. (Use g = 9.8 m/s²)
$$E_p = mgh = 10 \times 9.8 \times 6 = 588 \text{ J}$$

This object has 588 joules of potential energy stored due to its height!

Other Forms of Potential Energy

Elastic Potential Energy

Energy stored in objects that can stretch or compress:

  • Stretched rubber band
  • Wound spring in a watch or toy
  • Bent bow with arrow
  • Compressed spring

When released, this PE converts to kinetic energy and does work.

Chemical Potential Energy

Energy stored in chemical bonds:

  • Food (nutrients store chemical energy)
  • Batteries (chemical reactions produce electrical energy)
  • Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas)
💡 Analogy: PE is like money in the bank. A book on a high shelf has "stored" energy. When it falls, that PE converts to KE (the book moving fast). Just like withdrawing money from the bank converts stored wealth into cash you can spend.

♻️ Law of Conservation of Energy

This is one of the most fundamental laws in all of physics and nature!

The Law

Law of Conservation of Energy:

Energy can neither be created nor destroyed. It can only be transformed from one form to another. The total energy in a closed system remains constant.

Energy Transformation in a Free-Falling Object

This is the classic example of energy conservation. When an object falls:

Total Mechanical Energy (constant for free fall):
$$E_{\text{total}} = E_p + E_k = mgh + \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \text{constant}$$
Energy changes as an object falls (detailed breakdown)

At the top (before release):

  • Height = h, Velocity = 0
  • PE = mgh (maximum)
  • KE = 0 (at rest)
  • Total = mgh

At some point during fall:

  • Height = h₁, Velocity = v₁
  • PE = mgh₁ (decreased)
  • KE = ½mv₁² (increased)
  • Total = mgh₁ + ½mv₁² = mgh (still constant!)

At the bottom (just before hitting ground):

  • Height = 0, Velocity = v (maximum)
  • PE = 0 (all converted)
  • KE = ½mv² (maximum)
  • Total = ½mv² = mgh (still the same!)

Energy Conversions All Around Us

Examples of energy transformation:

  • Light bulb: Electrical energy → Light energy (+ heat)
  • Solar panel: Light energy → Electrical energy
  • Moving car: Chemical energy (fuel) → Kinetic energy
  • Hydroelectric dam: Gravitational PE of water → Electrical energy
  • Microwave: Electrical energy → Heat energy
  • Wind turbine: Kinetic energy of wind → Electrical energy

Important Note: Energy Loss?

In real situations, energy seems to "disappear" as heat due to friction. But it's not lost—it's been converted to thermal energy (heat) that spreads out. The total energy is still conserved; it's just been transferred to the environment.

⚡ Power: Rate of Doing Work

PowerWork done per unit time measures how fast work is done or how quickly energy is transferred. A powerful device does the same work in less time.

The Power Formula

Power:
$$P = \frac{W}{t}$$

Where:

  • P = Power (in watts, W)
  • W = Work done (in joules, J)
  • t = Time taken (in seconds, s)

Unit of Power: The Watt

1 watt (W) = 1 joule per second (J/s)

A 100-watt light bulb uses 100 joules of electrical energy every second.

Practical Example: Two Girls, Different Power

Scenario: Two girls lift the same weight (400 N) to the same height (8 m).

Girl A: Does it in 20 seconds

$$W = F \times s = 400 \times 8 = 3200 \text{ J}$$ $$P_A = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{3200}{20} = 160 \text{ W}$$

Girl B: Does it in 50 seconds

$$P_B = \frac{3200}{50} = 64 \text{ W}$$

Conclusion: Girl A is more powerful! She does the same work in less time. Power is about speed, not just strength.

Another Example: Climbing Stairs

Scenario: A 50 kg boy climbs 45 steps of 15 cm height each in 9 seconds.

Total height: 45 × 0.15 = 6.75 m

Work done against gravity:

$$W = mgh = 50 \times 10 \times 6.75 = 3375 \text{ J}$$

Power:

$$P = \frac{3375}{9} = 375 \text{ W}$$

Other Units of Power

  • Kilowatt (kW) = 1,000 W
  • Megawatt (MW) = 1,000,000 W (used for power plants)
  • Horsepower (hp) = 746 W (older unit, still used for engines)

Commercial Energy Unit: Kilowatt-Hour

When electricity bills come, they measure energy in kilowatt-hours (kWh):

Energy from Power:
$$E = P \times t$$

1 kWh = 1 kilowatt running for 1 hour

Conversion:

$$1 \text{ kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J} = 3,600,000 \text{ J}$$
💡 Analogy: Work is like the total amount of water you need to pour. Power is like how fast you pour it. Two people might pour the same amount of water, but one pours faster (higher power). A powerful motor does work quickly; a weak motor does the same work slowly.

Exam Preparation: HOTS & Case-Based Questions

Higher Order Thinking Skills and application-based questions to deepen understanding.

HOTS Questions

HOTS Q1: Energy Transformation in a Pendulum
A 2 kg pendulum bob is released from rest at a height of 0.5 m above its lowest point. Assuming no friction:

a) What is its maximum potential energy at the release point?
b) What will be its kinetic energy at the lowest point?
c) What will be its velocity at the lowest point?
d) Explain why PE at top = KE at bottom using energy conservation.

Solution:

a) Maximum PE at release (h = 0.5 m):

E_p = mgh = 2 × 10 × 0.5 = 10 J

b) Kinetic energy at lowest point:

By conservation of energy, all PE converts to KE at the lowest point:

E_k = E_p = 10 J

c) Velocity at lowest point:

E_k = ½mv²
10 = ½ × 2 × v²
10 = v²
v = √10 ≈ 3.16 m/s

d) Energy conservation explanation:

At the release point: Total energy = PE + KE = 10 + 0 = 10 J

At the lowest point: Total energy = PE + KE = 0 + 10 = 10 J

Total mechanical energy remains 10 J throughout. As the bob falls, PE decreases and KE increases, but their sum is always constant. At the lowest point, all energy is kinetic because height is zero.

HOTS Q2: Comparing Work and Power
Two workers carry the same 1000 kg load up a 10 m ramp:
- Worker A takes 50 seconds
- Worker B takes 20 seconds

Calculate: a) Work done by each, b) Power of each, c) Who is more powerful and by how much?

Solution:

a) Work done by each (same for both):

W = mgh = 1000 × 10 × 10 = 100,000 J = 100 kJ
(Both workers do the same work because they move the same mass to the same height)

b) Power of each:

Worker A:

P_A = W/t = 100,000 / 50 = 2,000 W = 2 kW

Worker B:

P_B = W/t = 100,000 / 20 = 5,000 W = 5 kW

c) Comparison:

Worker B is more powerful.

Difference = 5 kW - 2 kW = 3 kW more powerful

Ratio = 5 kW / 2 kW = 2.5 times more powerful

Key insight: Both did the same work, but B did it faster, showing higher power. Power is about efficiency and speed!

HOTS Q3: Work-Energy Theorem Application
A 1500 kg car accelerates from rest to 20 m/s. If friction and air resistance do -10,000 J of negative work:

a) What is the change in kinetic energy?
b) How much work must the engine do to overcome friction AND accelerate the car?
c) If the engine delivers this work over 10 seconds, what is its power?

Solution:

a) Change in kinetic energy:

ΔKE = ½m(v² - u²)
ΔKE = ½ × 1500 × (20² - 0²)
ΔKE = 750 × 400 = 300,000 J = 300 kJ

b) Total work by engine:

Using the work-energy theorem: W_engine + W_friction = ΔKE

W_engine + (-10,000) = 300,000
W_engine = 300,000 + 10,000 = 310,000 J = 310 kJ

(The engine must do 300 kJ to accelerate AND 10 kJ to overcome friction)

c) Power delivered by engine:

P = W / t = 310,000 / 10 = 31,000 W = 31 kW

Case-Based Questions

Case Q1: Electricity Bill Mystery
Mrs. Sharma's electricity meter reads 1,500 kWh this month. Her bill shows charges of ₹9 per kWh. She wonders:

a) How much energy did she consume in joules?
b) If she runs a 3 kW air conditioner for how many hours would it consume this much energy?
c) Calculate her total bill amount.
d) Suggest two ways to reduce energy consumption.

Solution:

a) Energy consumed in joules:

Energy = 1,500 kWh
1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Energy = 1,500 × 3.6 × 10⁶ = 5.4 × 10⁹ J
= 5.4 billion joules!

b) Hours of AC operation:

Energy = Power × Time
1,500 kWh = 3 kW × t
t = 1,500 / 3 = 500 hours
= Approximately 20.8 days of continuous operation

c) Total electricity bill:

Bill = 1,500 kWh × ₹9/kWh = ₹13,500

d) Ways to reduce energy consumption:

  • Use LED bulbs instead of incandescent (consume 75% less energy)
  • Turn off appliances when not in use (eliminate standby power waste)
  • Use ceiling fans instead of AC when possible
  • Insulate home properly to reduce heating/cooling needs
  • Unplug phone chargers when not in use
Case Q2: Roller Coaster Design
Engineers are designing a roller coaster. A 500 kg car is released from rest at the top of a 30 m high hill. At the bottom of the hill, before any braking:

a) Calculate the PE at the top (use g = 10 m/s²)
b) Calculate the maximum possible speed at the bottom (assuming no friction)
c) In reality, friction removes 200 kJ of energy. What is the actual speed at the bottom?
d) Explain why the actual speed is less than theoretical maximum.

Solution:

a) PE at the top:

E_p = mgh = 500 × 10 × 30 = 150,000 J = 150 kJ

b) Maximum speed at bottom (ideal, no friction):

All PE converts to KE:

E_p = E_k
150,000 = ½ × 500 × v²
150,000 = 250v²
v² = 600
v = √600 ≈ 24.5 m/s

c) Actual speed with friction:

Energy available for KE = Initial PE - Friction loss
Energy for KE = 150 - 200 = -50 kJ

Wait! This shows friction loss (200 kJ) exceeds available PE (150 kJ)!

Correction: Let's assume friction removes 50 kJ instead:
Energy for KE = 150 - 50 = 100 kJ

100,000 = ½ × 500 × v²
100,000 = 250v²
v² = 400
v = 20 m/s

d) Why actual speed is less:

Friction (and air resistance) convert mechanical energy into heat energy. Not all gravitational PE transforms to KE—some is "lost" to the environment as thermal energy. This demonstrates energy conservation: energy isn't destroyed, just converted to different forms.

Case Q3: Sports Performance Analysis
A 60 kg sprinter runs 100 m in 10 seconds, reaching a maximum speed of 12 m/s at the 60 m mark.

a) What is the kinetic energy at maximum speed?
b) If she accelerates uniformly from 0 to 12 m/s in the first 6 seconds, what average force did her muscles exert?
c) If her constant power output is the same throughout, calculate her power output.
d) Compare this with a 60 kg cyclist who can sustain 1 kW of power. Who works harder, and by how much?

Solution:

a) Kinetic energy at maximum speed:

E_k = ½mv² = ½ × 60 × (12)²
E_k = 30 × 144 = 4,320 J ≈ 4.3 kJ

b) Average force during acceleration:

Using work-energy theorem: W = ΔKE
F × s = ½m(v² - u²)

Distance in first 6s (uniform acceleration):
s = ut + ½at² or using average velocity
v_avg = (0 + 12)/2 = 6 m/s
s = 6 × 6 = 36 m

F × 36 = 4,320
F = 4,320 / 36 = 120 N

c) Power output:

P = W / t = 4,320 / 6 = 720 W

d) Comparison with cyclist:

Sprinter: 720 W

Cyclist: 1,000 W

Difference: 1,000 - 720 = 280 W more for cyclist

The cyclist works harder! However, note that sprinting involves explosive power for short bursts, while cycling sustains power over longer periods. Both are impressive athletic feats!

Cheat Sheet: Quick Reference Guide

Key Formulas

Work
W = F × s
Kinetic Energy
E_k = ½mv²
Potential Energy (Gravitational)
E_p = mgh
Power
P = W/t
Energy from Power
E = P × t
Work-Energy Theorem
W_net = ΔKE

Units to Remember

Joule (J) = Standard unit of work and energy. 1 J = 1 N·m
Watt (W) = Unit of power. 1 W = 1 J/s
Kilowatt-hour (kWh) = Commercial unit of energy. 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Newton (N) = Unit of force
g ≈ 10 m/s² = Acceleration due to gravity (sometimes 9.8 m/s²)

Important Definitions Glossary

Work = Force × Displacement in direction of force. No displacement = zero work.
Kinetic Energy = Energy of motion. Always positive. Depends on mass and velocity squared.
Potential Energy = Stored energy due to position (height) or configuration (spring, elastic).
Power = Rate of doing work. Higher power = faster work.
Law of Conservation of Energy = Energy can't be created or destroyed, only transformed.
Work-Energy Theorem = Net work done on object equals its change in kinetic energy.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing work with effort. Effort without displacement ≠ work scientifically.
  • Forgetting that KE depends on v² not v. Doubling speed quadruples KE, not doubles.
  • Ignoring direction. Work can be negative if force opposes motion.
  • Measuring PE from wrong reference level. Always specify height from what point.
  • Forgetting units. Always include J, W, kWh in your answers.
  • Mixing up power and energy. Power is rate (W/t), energy is capacity (J).

Memory Tips & Mnemonics

  • Work = Force × Displacement → Both must be in the same direction for positive work
  • KE = ½mv² → Speed is squared, so velocity has big impact on kinetic energy
  • PE = mgh → Higher position = higher potential to do work
  • Power = Work/Time → More power means same work in less time (faster)
  • Energy Conservation → Total energy before = Total energy after (just different forms)
  • From J to kWh → 1 kWh = 3.6 million joules (huge difference!)

Quick Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Read carefully - Note all given values and what's asked
  2. Identify the concept - Is it work? Energy? Power? Conservation?
  3. Draw it mentally - Visualize direction of forces and displacement
  4. Write the formula - Don't jump to calculation
  5. Check units - Convert to SI units (J, N, m, s, kg)
  6. Substitute and calculate - Plug in numbers carefully
  7. Check your answer - Does it make physical sense?

CBSE Board Exam Tips

  • Questions often compare work, energy, and power. Practice these together.
  • Free-fall problems always involve energy conservation. Draw energy diagrams.
  • Power questions often hide in electricity context (kilowatt-hours, bills).
  • Always mention "assuming no friction" or "including friction" in your answers.
  • Use g = 10 m/s² unless specifically told to use 9.8 m/s².
  • For full marks, show your work step-by-step, not just final answers.
