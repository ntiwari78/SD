Chapter 9: Gravitation

Class 9 · Physics · NCERT Curriculum

"What keeps the Moon circling Earth? Why does an apple fall down, never up? Newton realized the same force governs both — and his discovery changed our understanding of the entire universe."

1 Gravitation

Gravitation is a fundamental force of nature that acts between all objects in the universe. Every object with mass attracts every other object with mass, regardless of the distance between them. This attraction is what we call the gravitational force.

What is Gravitation?

Gravitation is the natural phenomenon by which all things with mass are brought toward one another. It is the weakest of the four fundamental forces in nature, yet it is the most significant on macroscopic scales. Without gravitation:

  • Planets would not orbit stars
  • Stars would not form from cosmic dust
  • Objects would not fall to the ground
  • The universe would be a fundamentally different place

Newton's Apple

The legend of Newton's apple is perhaps one of the most famous stories in the history of science. According to the story (though historical accuracy is debated), Isaac Newton was sitting in an apple orchard when an apple fell from a tree and struck him. This event sparked his curiosity about why the apple fell downward and not in any other direction.

Newton pondered whether the same force that pulls the apple downward also acts on the Moon, keeping it in orbit around Earth. This revolutionary insight led him to formulate the law of universal gravitation, one of the most important laws of physics.

This simple observation by Newton led to the discovery that gravity is a universal force acting on all objects in the universe. The same force that pulls an apple down keeps the Moon in its orbit and the planets revolving around the Sun.

2 Universal Law of Gravitation

Newton's law of universal gravitation states that every particle of matter in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation:
$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{d^2}$$

Understanding the Formula

  • F = Gravitational force between the two objects (in Newtons, N)
  • G = Universal gravitational constant = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N⋅m²/kg²
  • m₁ and m₂ = Masses of the two objects (in kilograms, kg)
  • d = Distance between the centers of the two objects (in meters, m)

The Gravitational Constant (G)

The gravitational constant G was first measured experimentally by Henry Cavendish in 1798 using a torsion balance. Its value is incredibly small (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹), which explains why we don't notice the gravitational attraction between everyday objects. However, for massive objects like planets and stars, gravity becomes the dominant force.

Properties of Gravitational Force

  • Always Attractive: Gravity always pulls objects toward each other; it never repels.
  • Acts Along Line of Centers: The force acts along the imaginary line connecting the centers of mass of the two objects.
  • Proportional to Product of Masses: If either mass increases, the force increases proportionally.
  • Inversely Proportional to Square of Distance: If the distance doubles, the force becomes one-fourth. This is known as the inverse square law.

Importance of Gravitational Force

  • Holds the Solar System Together: The Sun's gravity keeps planets in their orbits.
  • Causes Tides: The Moon's gravity causes oceanic tides on Earth.
  • Keeps Atmosphere: Earth's gravity prevents its atmosphere from escaping into space.
  • Galaxy Formation: Gravity binds billions of stars together to form galaxies.
  • Star and Planet Formation: Gravity causes cosmic dust and gas to collapse and form stars and planets.

Worked Example 1Gravitational Force Between Earth and an Object

Problem: Calculate the gravitational force between Earth (mass = 6 × 10²⁴ kg) and an object of mass 1 kg at Earth's surface. The radius of Earth is 6.4 × 10⁶ m. (Use G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N⋅m²/kg²)

Solution:

Given:

  • m₁ (Earth) = 6 × 10²⁴ kg
  • m₂ (object) = 1 kg
  • d = 6.4 × 10⁶ m
  • G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N⋅m²/kg²

Using the formula:

$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{d^2} = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times \frac{6 \times 10^{24} \times 1}{(6.4 \times 10^6)^2}$$
$$F = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times \frac{6 \times 10^{24}}{40.96 \times 10^{12}}$$
$$F = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 1.465 \times 10^{11} = 9.78 \text{ N}$$

Answer: The gravitational force is approximately 9.8 N. This is equal to the weight of the 1 kg object on Earth's surface!

3 Free Fall

Free fall occurs when an object moves under the influence of gravity alone, with no other forces acting on it (ignoring air resistance). All objects in free fall experience the same acceleration due to gravity, regardless of their mass.

Acceleration Due to Gravity

The acceleration experienced by an object falling freely under gravity is called the acceleration due to gravity, denoted by 'g'.

Acceleration Due to Gravity:
$$g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2 \approx 10 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Galileo's Insight

Galileo Galilei discovered through experimentation (the famous, though possibly apocryphal, Leaning Tower of Pisa experiment) that all objects, regardless of their mass, fall with the same acceleration in the absence of air resistance. This was a revolutionary finding because it contradicted Aristotle's belief that heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones.

Equations of Motion Under Gravity

When an object moves under gravity, we can use the standard equations of motion with acceleration 'a' replaced by 'g' (or '-g' when thrown upward):

First Equation of Motion (Velocity):
$$v = u + gt \text{ (for falling)} \quad \text{or} \quad v = u - gt \text{ (for thrown upward)}$$
Second Equation of Motion (Displacement):
$$h = ut + \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
Third Equation of Motion (Velocity-Displacement):
$$v^2 = u^2 + 2gh$$

Where:

  • u = Initial velocity (m/s)
  • v = Final velocity (m/s)
  • t = Time (s)
  • h = Displacement or height (m)
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s² or 10 m/s²)

Key Points About Free Fall

  • All objects fall with the same acceleration (g) regardless of their mass
  • Air resistance is neglected in free fall calculations
  • Velocity increases linearly with time
  • Displacement increases with the square of time

Worked Example 2Stone Dropped from a Building

Problem: A stone is dropped from a height of 80 m. Find: (a) the time taken to reach the ground, and (b) the velocity when it hits the ground. (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Solution (a): Time to reach ground

Given:

  • Initial velocity, u = 0 (dropped, not thrown)
  • Height, h = 80 m
  • g = 10 m/s²

Using: $h = ut + \frac{1}{2}gt^2$

$$80 = 0 \times t + \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times t^2$$ $$80 = 5t^2$$ $$t^2 = 16$$ $$t = 4 \text{ seconds}$$
Solution (b): Velocity at impact

Using: $v^2 = u^2 + 2gh$

$$v^2 = 0^2 + 2 \times 10 \times 80$$ $$v^2 = 1600$$ $$v = 40 \text{ m/s}$$

Answers: (a) Time = 4 seconds, (b) Velocity at impact = 40 m/s

4 Mass vs Weight

Although the terms "mass" and "weight" are often used interchangeably in everyday language, they have distinct meanings in physics. Understanding the difference is crucial for grasping gravitational concepts.

Property Mass Weight
Definition Amount of matter in an object Force exerted by gravity on an object
Type Scalar quantity Vector quantity
Symbol m W
SI Unit Kilogram (kg) Newton (N)
Varies? Constant everywhere Changes with location
Formula — W = mg

Mass

Mass is a fundamental property of matter that measures the amount of substance in an object. It is:

  • Constant: The mass of an object remains the same wherever it is in the universe.
  • Scalar: Mass has magnitude only, no direction.
  • Inertial: Mass measures an object's resistance to acceleration (inertia).

Weight

Weight is the force exerted by gravity on an object. It is:

  • Variable: Weight changes with the strength of the gravitational field.
  • Vector: Weight always acts vertically downward (toward the center of a celestial body).
  • Dependent on g: Weight depends on the local acceleration due to gravity.
Weight Formula:
$$W = mg$$

Where: W = Weight (N), m = Mass (kg), g = Acceleration due to gravity (m/s²)

Weight on Different Planets

The acceleration due to gravity varies from one celestial body to another. Here's how weight changes across different locations:

Example: Weight on the Moon

The Moon's gravity is approximately 1/6th of Earth's gravity (g_moon ≈ 1.63 m/s²). Therefore, an astronaut with a mass of 60 kg would weigh:

  • On Earth: W = 60 × 9.8 = 588 N
  • On the Moon: W = 60 × 1.63 = 97.8 N

The astronaut's mass remains 60 kg in both locations, but weight is only 1/6th on the Moon!

Weightlessness

Weightlessness occurs when an object is in free fall, even though gravity is still acting on it. In this state, the object and everything inside it fall together at the same rate, creating the sensation of having no weight. Examples include:

  • Astronauts in orbiting spacecraft
  • Objects inside a freely falling elevator
  • Planes during parabolic flight

Important: Weightlessness does NOT mean the absence of gravity. The gravitational force is still acting; it's just that there is no normal force pushing back on the object.

5 Thrust and Pressure

Thrust and pressure are related concepts that describe how forces are distributed over an area. Understanding the difference between them is important for explaining many everyday phenomena.

Thrust

Thrust is the total force acting perpendicular to a surface. It is:

  • A vector quantity (has direction)
  • Measured in Newtons (N)
  • Independent of the area over which it acts

Example: When you stand on the ground, your weight is the thrust you exert on the ground.

Pressure

Pressure is the force per unit area. It describes how concentrated a force is over a given area.

Pressure Formula:
$$P = \frac{F}{A}$$

Where: P = Pressure (Pa or N/m²), F = Force or Thrust (N), A = Area (m²)

SI Unit: Pascal (Pa) = Newton per square meter (N/m²)

Other common units for pressure include:

  • Atmosphere (atm) = 101,325 Pa
  • Bar (bar) = 100,000 Pa
  • Kilopascal (kPa) = 1,000 Pa

Why Sharp Objects Cut Better

A sharp knife cuts better than a blunt one because it applies the same force over a smaller area. For example:

Comparison of a Sharp vs Blunt Knife:
$$P_{sharp} = \frac{F}{A_{small}} \quad \text{vs} \quad P_{blunt} = \frac{F}{A_{large}}$$ $$P_{sharp} > P_{blunt}$$

The higher pressure of the sharp knife allows it to penetrate materials more easily.

Practical Applications of Pressure

  • Broad Tires on Heavy Vehicles: Reduces pressure on the road, preventing damage.
  • Snowshoes: Distribute the wearer's weight over a large area to prevent sinking in snow.
  • Camel's Broad Feet: Allow camels to walk on sandy desert without sinking.
  • Thin Nails: Concentrate force to penetrate walls easily.
  • Foundation Plates: Spread the weight of buildings over a large area to prevent settling.

Pressure in Fluids

Pressure in fluids (liquids and gases) acts in all directions at a given depth. Key principles include:

  • Pressure increases with depth in a fluid
  • Pressure at a given depth is the same in all directions
  • Pressure is caused by the weight of the fluid above
Pressure in Fluids:
$$P = P_0 + \rho gh$$

Where: P₀ = Atmospheric pressure, ρ = Density of fluid, h = Depth

6 Buoyancy

Buoyancy is the upward force exerted by a fluid on an object that is immersed in it. This force is responsible for making objects feel lighter in water and allows some objects to float.

The Buoyant Force

When an object is immersed in a fluid, the fluid exerts an upward force on it. This upward force is called the buoyant force. The buoyant force acts opposite to the direction of weight (gravity).

Why Objects Feel Lighter in Water

When you are in water, you experience a buoyant force that partially counteracts your weight. This is why swimming feels effortless compared to walking on land. The buoyant force reduces the effective weight you feel.

Floating and Sinking

Whether an object floats or sinks depends on comparing two forces:

  • If buoyant force ≥ weight: The object floats or remains suspended in the fluid.
  • If buoyant force < weight: The object sinks through the fluid.

Why Ships Float

Despite being made of heavy materials like steel, ships float because their shape allows them to displace a large volume of water, creating a buoyant force equal to or greater than their weight. The air inside the ship reduces its overall density below that of water.

7 Archimedes' Principle

Archimedes' principle is a fundamental law that explains buoyancy and provides a method for calculating the buoyant force on an object.

Archimedes' Principle

"An object immersed in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the object."

Buoyant Force:
$$F_b = W_{fluid\,displaced} = m_{fluid} \times g = \rho_{fluid} \times V_{displaced} \times g$$

The Golden Crown Story

Archimedes used his principle to solve a famous problem: determining whether King Hiero II's crown was made of pure gold or if the goldsmith had cheated by mixing in silver. According to legend, Archimedes discovered the principle while taking a bath, noticing how water was displaced when he entered the tub. He was so excited that he ran through the streets naked, shouting "Eureka!" ("I found it!").

The method was elegant: he weighed the crown and then submerged it in water, measuring the volume of water displaced. By comparing this to the volume of water displaced by pure gold of the same weight, he could determine if the crown was authentic.

Applications of Archimedes' Principle

  • Ship Design: Engineers use this principle to calculate how much cargo a ship can carry while remaining afloat.
  • Submarines: By controlling how much water they take in, submarines can adjust their buoyancy to sink or rise.
  • Hot Air Balloons: The buoyant force of displaced air allows balloons to lift off the ground.
  • Hydrometers: Instruments used to measure the density of liquids based on how deep they sink.
  • Life Jackets: Provide buoyancy to keep a person afloat in water.

Worked Example 3Floating Block of Wood

Problem: A block of wood floats in water with 2/3 of its volume submerged. Find the density of the wood. (Density of water = 1000 kg/m³)

Solution:

Let the total volume of the block be V.

Volume submerged = (2/3)V

For a floating object, buoyant force = Weight of object

$$\rho_{water} \times V_{displaced} \times g = \rho_{wood} \times V_{total} \times g$$

Substituting values:

$$1000 \times \frac{2}{3}V \times g = \rho_{wood} \times V \times g$$

Canceling V and g:

$$1000 \times \frac{2}{3} = \rho_{wood}$$ $$\rho_{wood} = 666.67 \text{ kg/m}^3$$

Answer: The density of wood is approximately 667 kg/m³ or 2/3 the density of water.

8 Relative Density

Relative density (also called specific gravity) is a dimensionless quantity that compares the density of a substance to the density of water. It provides a convenient way to express density without units.

Relative Density Formula:
$$\text{Relative Density} = \frac{\text{Density of substance}}{\text{Density of water}}$$

Since the density of water is 1000 kg/m³ = 1 g/cm³:

  • Relative density has NO units (it's a pure ratio)
  • The density of water has a relative density of exactly 1
  • Substances denser than water have RD > 1
  • Substances less dense than water have RD < 1

Relative Density and Floating

  • If RD < 1: The substance is less dense than water and will float.
  • If RD = 1: The substance has the same density as water and will be neutrally buoyant.
  • If RD > 1: The substance is denser than water and will sink.
Substance Density (kg/m³) Relative Density Floats or Sinks?
Cork 240 0.24 Floats
Wood (average) 700 0.70 Floats
Water 1000 1.00 Neutral
Ice 917 0.92 Floats
Aluminum 2700 2.70 Sinks
Iron 7800 7.80 Sinks
Gold 19300 19.30 Sinks

Instruments Using Relative Density

  • Hydrometer: Measures the relative density of liquids. It floats deeper in less dense liquids.
  • Lactometer: Specifically designed to measure the purity of milk by its density.
  • Hygrometer: Sometimes uses relative density principles to measure humidity.

R Remediation and Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Confusing Mass and Weight

Common Error: Students often use the terms "mass" and "weight" interchangeably, or fail to recognize that weight changes with location while mass doesn't.

Clarification: Mass is the amount of matter in an object (constant everywhere, measured in kg). Weight is the gravitational force on an object (varies with location, measured in N). Remember: W = mg, so weight depends on g, which varies on different planets.

Practice: Calculate the weight of a 60 kg person on Earth (g = 9.8 m/s²), on the Moon (g = 1.63 m/s²), and on Jupiter (g = 24.79 m/s²). Their mass stays 60 kg, but weight changes dramatically!

Mistake 2: Misunderstanding the Inverse Square Law

Common Error: Students struggle to apply the inverse square law correctly. For example, they might think that if distance doubles, force halves, when actually it becomes one-fourth.

Clarification: In the formula F = G(m₁m₂)/d², the distance appears as d². This means if d becomes 2d, then d² becomes 4d², so force becomes 1/4 of its original value. The relationship is inversely proportional to the SQUARE of distance, not just the distance.

Practice: If the distance between two objects triples, what happens to the gravitational force? (Answer: It becomes 1/9 of the original)

Mistake 3: Not Recognizing Free Fall in Different Scenarios

Common Error: Students don't realize that objects in orbit, falling elevators, and skydivers are all in free fall, despite appearing very different.

Clarification: Free fall means falling under gravity alone with no other forces acting (ignoring air resistance). An orbiting satellite is constantly falling toward Earth but also moving sideways fast enough that it keeps missing. This is different from resting on the ground, where the normal force prevents you from falling.

Practice: Identify which scenarios involve free fall: (a) A ball dropped from a building, (b) A spaceship coasting with engines off in space, (c) A plane flying at constant altitude, (d) An astronaut inside an orbiting spacecraft.

Mistake 4: Misapplying Pressure Concepts

Common Error: Students calculate pressure incorrectly or fail to understand that the same force over a smaller area creates greater pressure.

Clarification: Pressure = Force/Area. A smaller area means higher pressure for the same force. This is why needles puncture skin (small area, high pressure) and snowshoes distribute weight (large area, low pressure).

Practice: A 50 N force is applied to an object with contact area 0.5 m². What is the pressure? (Answer: 100 Pa). What happens if the area reduces to 0.1 m²? (Answer: 500 Pa, five times higher!)

Mistake 5: Misunderstanding Buoyancy

Common Error: Students think buoyancy is a mysterious force that acts opposite to gravity, without recognizing it's simply a result of pressure differences in fluids.

Clarification: Buoyancy arises because fluid pressure increases with depth. The bottom of a submerged object experiences greater pressure than the top, creating an upward net force. This upward force is the buoyant force, and by Archimedes' principle, it equals the weight of displaced fluid.

Practice: A wooden block has a relative density of 0.7. Will it float or sink? What fraction of it will be submerged when floating? (Answer: It floats, with 70% submerged)

Mistake 6: Confusing Relative Density

Common Error: Students forget that relative density is unitless and fail to remember the critical values (RD = 1 for water, RD < 1 for floating, RD > 1 for sinking).

Clarification: Relative Density = Density of substance / Density of water. Since we're dividing two densities, the units cancel and RD has no unit. It's simply a comparison: if RD = 2, the substance is twice as dense as water.

Practice: A substance has a density of 800 kg/m³. What is its relative density? Will it float in water? (Answer: RD = 0.8, yes it floats)
