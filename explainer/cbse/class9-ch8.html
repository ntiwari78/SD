Force and Laws of Motion

"Why do passengers lurch forward when a bus suddenly brakes? Why does a cricket ball hurt more than a tennis ball at the same speed? Newton answered these questions over 300 years ago, and his laws still govern everything from rocket launches to car crashes."

1. Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

What is a Force?
A force is a push or a pull exerted on an object. It is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction. The SI unit of force is the Newton (N), named after Sir Isaac Newton. One Newton is the force required to accelerate a 1 kg mass at 1 m/s².

Balanced Forces

When two or more forces act on an object such that their resultant (net force) is zero, these forces are called balanced forces. When balanced forces act on an object, the state of motion of the object does not change.

Key Characteristics:
  • Net force = 0
  • Forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction
  • Object remains at rest or continues uniform motion
  • No acceleration

Real-world examples:

  • A book lying on a table: Weight (downward) = Normal force (upward)
  • A person swimming at constant velocity: Propulsive force = Resistance forces
  • A car moving at constant speed on a highway: Engine force = Friction + Air resistance

Unbalanced Forces

When the net force acting on an object is not zero, the forces are called unbalanced forces. Unbalanced forces cause a change in the state of motion of the object.

Effects of Unbalanced Forces:
  • Change the speed of a moving object
  • Change the direction of motion
  • Change the shape/size of an object (deformation)
  • Produce acceleration

Real-world examples:

  • Pushing a stationary box causes it to accelerate (if push force > friction)
  • Kicking a football changes its direction and speed
  • A falling object accelerates due to unbalanced gravitational force

2. First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

Newton's First Law:

"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in uniform motion in a straight line, unless acted upon by an unbalanced external force."

Understanding Inertia

Inertia is the natural tendency of an object to resist any change in its state of motion. It is a fundamental property of matter that depends on the mass of the object.

Key Points about Inertia:
  • Greater mass → Greater inertia
  • An object with more mass requires more force to change its motion
  • Inertia is measured by the mass of the object
  • Inertia is a scalar quantity (only has magnitude)

Galileo's Insight: The Foundation

Before Newton, Galileo Galilei conducted thought experiments with inclined planes. He observed that on a frictionless surface, an object, once set in motion, would continue moving indefinitely in a straight line. This observation became the basis for Newton's First Law and revolutionized our understanding of motion.

Common Examples of Inertia

1. The Coin on Card Trick:
A coin is placed on a card, which rests on top of a glass. When the card is flicked away rapidly, the coin falls into the glass. Why? The inertia of the coin causes it to resist the motion of the card and remain stationary relative to the ground.
2. Passengers Lurching When Bus Brakes:
When a moving bus suddenly applies brakes, passengers tend to lurch forward. This happens because the bus decelerates (experiences unbalanced force), but the passengers' inertia keeps them moving forward at their original velocity. They maintain their motion relative to the ground, resulting in a forward motion relative to the bus.
3. Shaking Water off an Umbrella:
When you shake an umbrella, the umbrella moves but the water droplets, due to inertia, continue moving in their original direction and fly off. The same principle applies to spin drying clothes.
4. Athletes Running Past the Finish Line:
Sprinters cannot stop abruptly at the finish line. Due to inertia, they continue moving forward and must gradually decelerate, covering several more meters before coming to rest.

3. Second Law of Motion

Newton's Second Law:

"The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of the force."

Momentum: The Quantity of Motion

Momentum is a measure of how difficult it is to stop a moving object. It is the product of an object's mass and velocity.

Momentum (p) $$p = m \times v$$
where p = momentum (kg⋅m/s), m = mass (kg), v = velocity (m/s)

Momentum is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction. The SI unit of momentum is kilogram-meter per second (kg⋅m/s).

Understanding the Second Law Mathematically

The rate of change of momentum equals the applied force:

Force and Rate of Change of Momentum $$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{m(v-u)}{t}$$
Simplifying: $$F = m \times \frac{(v-u)}{t} = m \times a$$
$$F = ma$$

This is the most famous equation in physics: Force equals mass times acceleration.

The Newton: Unit of Force

One Newton (1 N) is defined as:
The force required to accelerate a mass of 1 kilogram at a rate of 1 meter per second squared (m/s²).

Mathematically: 1 N = 1 kg × 1 m/s² = 1 kg⋅m/s²

Key Insights from the Second Law

1. Force is Proportional to Acceleration:
For a fixed mass, if you double the force, the acceleration doubles. This is a direct linear relationship.
2. Force is Inversely Proportional to Mass:
For a fixed force, if you double the mass, the acceleration is halved. Heavier objects are harder to accelerate.
3. Practical Implications:
  • Pushing a light box requires less force than pushing a heavy box at the same acceleration
  • Heavier vehicles (trucks) consume more fuel because they require more force to accelerate
  • A cricket player catches a fast-moving ball by pulling their hands back, reducing the deceleration and thus the force experienced by the hands

Worked Example 1: Basic Acceleration Calculation

Problem:

A force of 5 N acts on a body of mass 2 kg at rest. Calculate:

  1. The acceleration produced
  2. The velocity after 5 seconds
  3. The distance covered in 5 seconds

Solution:

Part 1: Acceleration
Using F = ma
$$a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{5}{2} = 2.5 \text{ m/s}^2$$
Part 2: Velocity after 5 seconds
Using v = u + at (where u = 0, initial velocity)
$$v = 0 + 2.5 \times 5 = 12.5 \text{ m/s}$$
Part 3: Distance covered in 5 seconds
Using s = ut + ½at² (where u = 0)
$$s = 0 + \frac{1}{2} \times 2.5 \times 5^2$$ $$s = 1.25 \times 25 = 31.25 \text{ m}$$

4. Third Law of Motion

Newton's Third Law:

"To every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. These forces act on different bodies."

Understanding Action and Reaction

Newton's Third Law states that forces always come in pairs. When object A exerts a force on object B, object B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on object A. These are called action-reaction pairs.

Critical Point:
Action and reaction forces act on DIFFERENT bodies. This is why they do not cancel each other out. If they acted on the same body, the net force would always be zero, and nothing would ever move or accelerate!

Common Examples

1. Walking:
When you walk, you push backward on the ground (action). The ground pushes forward on you with an equal force (reaction). This forward reaction force from the ground propels you forward.
2. Swimming:
A swimmer pushes water backward (action). The water pushes the swimmer forward (reaction) with equal force. This is how swimmers propel themselves.
3. Rocket Propulsion:
A rocket expels hot gases downward at high speed (action). These gases exert an equal upward force on the rocket (reaction), propelling it upward. This is the principle behind all rocket launches.
4. Gun Recoil:
When a gun fires, it exerts a large forward force on the bullet (action). The bullet exerts an equal backward force on the gun (reaction). This is why guns recoil backward.
5. Pushing a Wall:
When you push a wall, you exert a force on the wall. The wall simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on you. You don't move because the wall is much more massive and firmly fixed to the Earth, but the force is definitely there.
6. Balloon Releasing Air:
When a balloon releases air, it exerts a force on the air (action), pushing it out. The air exerts an equal and opposite force on the balloon (reaction), causing it to zoom forward unpredictably.

Why Action and Reaction Don't Cancel

A common misconception is that action-reaction pairs should cancel each other out. However, they don't cancel because they act on different bodies. Consider two colliding balls:

  • Ball A exerts a force on Ball B (action) → Ball B accelerates
  • Ball B exerts an equal force on Ball A (reaction) → Ball A accelerates (or decelerates)

Both balls experience acceleration/deceleration, but in different directions. If the forces were on the same body, they would cancel. Since they're on different bodies, each body responds independently.

5. Conservation of Momentum

The Law of Conservation of Momentum

In an isolated system (with no external forces), the total momentum before an event equals the total momentum after the event.

Mathematically:
$$m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$$

where u₁, u₂ are initial velocities and v₁, v₂ are final velocities.

Derivation from Newton's Third Law

Consider two objects A and B colliding. By Newton's Third Law:

Force on A by B = -Force on B by A

$$F_{BA} = -F_{AB}$$

Using F = Δp/Δt:

$$\frac{\Delta p_A}{\Delta t} = -\frac{\Delta p_B}{\Delta t}$$

$$\Delta p_A = -\Delta p_B$$

$$\Delta p_A + \Delta p_B = 0$$

This means: Total momentum before = Total momentum after

Worked Example 2: Collision Problem

Problem:

A 50 g bullet is fired horizontally into a 2.95 kg wooden block kept on a smooth (frictionless) table. The bullet gets embedded in the block. If the initial velocity of the bullet is 400 m/s, find the velocity of the block+bullet system after collision.

Solution:

Given Data:
  • Mass of bullet, m₁ = 50 g = 0.05 kg
  • Mass of block, m₂ = 2.95 kg
  • Initial velocity of bullet, u₁ = 400 m/s
  • Initial velocity of block, u₂ = 0 m/s (at rest)
  • Final velocity of system, v = ?
Using Conservation of Momentum:
$$m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v$$

$$0.05 \times 400 + 2.95 \times 0 = (0.05 + 2.95) \times v$$

$$20 = 3 \times v$$

$$v = \frac{20}{3} = 6.67 \text{ m/s}$$
Answer:
The velocity of the block+bullet system after collision is 6.67 m/s in the direction of the bullet's initial motion.

Worked Example 3: Two-Object Collision

Problem:

A 1000 kg car moving at 20 m/s collides with a 3000 kg truck at rest on a straight road. If the two vehicles stick together after the collision, find their combined velocity immediately after the collision.

Solution:

Given Data:
  • Mass of car, m₁ = 1000 kg
  • Initial velocity of car, u₁ = 20 m/s
  • Mass of truck, m₂ = 3000 kg
  • Initial velocity of truck, u₂ = 0 m/s (at rest)
  • Final velocity of combined system, v = ?
Using Conservation of Momentum:
$$m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v$$

$$1000 \times 20 + 3000 \times 0 = (1000 + 3000) \times v$$

$$20000 = 4000 \times v$$

$$v = \frac{20000}{4000} = 5 \text{ m/s}$$
Answer:
The combined velocity of the car and truck immediately after collision is 5 m/s in the direction of the car's initial motion.

Note: The velocity decreased because the truck is much more massive. The momentum is conserved, but is now distributed over a larger combined mass, resulting in a lower velocity.

Real-World Applications of Momentum Conservation

1. Rocket Propulsion:
A rocket expels mass (exhaust gases) at high velocity backward. To conserve momentum in the isolated rocket-exhaust system, the rocket must move forward.
2. Recoil:
When a gun fires, the bullet moves forward. To conserve momentum, the gun must recoil backward. A heavier gun experiences less recoil velocity than a lighter gun because of the larger mass.
3. Collisions and Accidents:
Understanding momentum conservation is crucial in accident reconstruction. By analyzing the momentum before and after a collision, forensic experts can determine the speeds of vehicles before the collision.
4. Sports:
When a cricket batsman hits a ball, momentum is transferred from the bat to the ball. The greater the momentum of the bat, the greater the momentum imparted to the ball.

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

These questions require deeper understanding and application of concepts:

HOTS Q1: Why does a heavy truck require more force to accelerate than a light car, even though both travel on the same road?

Answer: According to Newton's Second Law, F = ma. For the same acceleration 'a', a heavier truck (larger m) requires a proportionally larger force F. Additionally, a truck's greater inertia (due to higher mass) means it has a stronger tendency to resist motion changes. The truck also needs more force to overcome greater rolling friction and air resistance due to its larger mass and frontal area.

HOTS Q2: Explain why you feel a stronger jerk when a bus suddenly applies brakes compared to when it accelerates gradually. Use Newton's First Law and the concept of inertia in your explanation.

Answer: When brakes are applied suddenly, there is a large unbalanced force (deceleration) acting on the bus. Due to inertia, passengers tend to maintain their original motion (moving forward) while the bus decelerates beneath them. This causes a sudden relative motion between passengers and the bus, resulting in a strong jerk. In contrast, gradual acceleration allows passengers' inertia to be gradually overcome, preventing abrupt relative motion. The magnitude of jerk is proportional to the rate of change of acceleration (d²s/dt³).

HOTS Q3: A person pushes a 50 kg box on a horizontal frictionless surface with a force of 100 N. Another person pushes a 100 kg box with the same 100 N force. Which box accelerates faster, and by what ratio? Show your calculation.

Solution: Using F = ma, we get a = F/m
For 50 kg box: a₁ = 100/50 = 2 m/s²
For 100 kg box: a₂ = 100/100 = 1 m/s²
Ratio: a₁/a₂ = 2/1 = 2
The 50 kg box accelerates 2 times faster than the 100 kg box. The acceleration is inversely proportional to mass when force is constant.

Case-Based Problems with Numericals

Real-world scenarios requiring problem-solving skills:

Case 1: The Skateboard Stunt

A skateboarder of mass 60 kg standing on a skateboard (mass 2 kg) decides to perform a trick. She pushes against a wall with a force of 240 N for 0.5 seconds. The skateboard moves away from the wall on a frictionless surface.

  1. Calculate the acceleration of the skateboard+skater system.
  2. What will be the velocity of the system after 0.5 seconds?
  3. How far will they travel in the next 2 seconds (after she stops pushing)?
Solution:
Total mass m = 60 + 2 = 62 kg
a) Acceleration: a = F/m = 240/62 ≈ 3.87 m/s²
b) Velocity after 0.5s: v = u + at = 0 + 3.87 × 0.5 ≈ 1.94 m/s
c) Distance in next 2s (uniform motion, no external force): s = vt = 1.94 × 2 ≈ 3.88 m
Case 2: Traffic Accident Analysis

A 1200 kg sedan traveling at 15 m/s collides head-on with a 1500 kg SUV traveling at 10 m/s in the opposite direction on a straight highway. After collision, they move together.

  1. Calculate the velocity and direction of the combined wreckage immediately after collision.
  2. Which vehicle was "pushing" the combined mass (i.e., had greater momentum)?
  3. If the combined wreckage slides for 8 seconds on the road with friction, what is the deceleration if final velocity is 0?
Solution: (Taking sedan direction as positive)
a) Using momentum conservation:
(1200 × 15) + (1500 × (-10)) = (1200 + 1500) × v
18000 - 15000 = 2700v
v = 3000/2700 ≈ 1.11 m/s (in sedan's original direction)
b) Sedan's momentum = 18000 kg⋅m/s; SUV's momentum = -15000 kg⋅m/s. Sedan had greater momentum.
c) Deceleration: a = (v-u)/t = (0-1.11)/8 ≈ -0.14 m/s²
Case 3: Swimming Pool Diving

A 70 kg person jumps off a 5 m diving board with an initial downward velocity of 2 m/s. They experience air resistance of 140 N during the fall, and water resistance of 7000 N while decelerating in water.

  1. Calculate the net force and acceleration during the fall through air (take g = 10 m/s²).
  2. Calculate the velocity just before entering the water.
  3. Calculate the deceleration in water and the distance required to stop (assume constant deceleration).
Solution:
a) Forces: Weight = 70 × 10 = 700 N (downward), Air resistance = 140 N (upward)
Net force = 700 - 140 = 560 N (downward)
Acceleration = 560/70 = 8 m/s² (downward)
b) Using v² = u² + 2as: v² = 2² + 2(8)(5) = 4 + 80 = 84
v ≈ 9.17 m/s (just before water entry)
c) In water: Net force = 7000 - 700 = 6300 N (upward, opposing motion)
Deceleration = 6300/70 = 90 m/s² (magnitude)
Distance: Using v² = u² - 2as: 0 = 84 - 2(90)s
s = 84/180 ≈ 0.47 m

Quick Reference: Cheat Sheet

Key Formulas

Momentum $$p = mv$$ (kg⋅m/s)
Force (Newton's 2nd Law) $$F = ma$$ (Newtons)
Force from Momentum $$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$$ (N)
Momentum Conservation $$m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$$
Equations of Motion $$v = u + at$$ $$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$
Velocity (Alternative) $$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$ (useful for constant acceleration)

Newton's Three Laws — Summary

First Law (Inertia): An object at rest stays at rest; an object in motion stays in uniform motion unless acted upon by an unbalanced external force.
Second Law (F=ma): The rate of change of momentum is proportional to the applied force and in the direction of the force. F = ma or F = Δp/Δt
Third Law (Action-Reaction): To every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction acting on different bodies. F_AB = -F_BA

Key Definitions & Glossary

Force (F)
A push or pull exerted on an object. Vector quantity; SI unit: Newton (N). One Newton accelerates 1 kg at 1 m/s².
Inertia
The natural tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. Directly proportional to mass. More massive objects have greater inertia.
Mass (m)
A measure of the amount of matter in an object. The measure of inertia. SI unit: kilogram (kg). Remains constant everywhere.
Momentum (p)
The quantity of motion possessed by a moving body. p = mv. Vector quantity; SI unit: kg⋅m/s. Harder to stop objects with greater momentum.
Velocity (v)
The rate of change of position. Vector quantity with both magnitude and direction. SI unit: m/s. Different from speed (scalar).
Acceleration (a)
The rate of change of velocity. Vector quantity. SI unit: m/s². Can be positive (speeding up), negative (slowing down), or zero (constant velocity).
Balanced Forces
Forces that are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, resulting in zero net force. No change in state of motion occurs.
Unbalanced Forces
Forces where the net force is not zero. Causes a change in the state of motion (acceleration) or deformation of the object.
Newton's First Law
Law of Inertia. Objects maintain their state of motion unless acted upon by an unbalanced external force. Foundation for understanding motion without forces.
Newton's Second Law
F = ma. Force equals mass times acceleration. Describes how forces change an object's motion. The rate of momentum change equals applied force.
Newton's Third Law
Action-Reaction Law. For every action force, there's an equal and opposite reaction force on different bodies. They never cancel each other.
Conservation of Momentum
In an isolated system with no external forces, total momentum before an event equals total momentum after. Fundamental principle in collisions and explosions.
Elastic Collision
A collision where both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. Objects bounce off each other. Rare in macroscopic collisions.
Inelastic Collision
A collision where momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is lost (converted to heat, sound, deformation). Objects may stick together.
Impulse (J)
The change in momentum caused by a force acting for a time interval. J = FΔt = Δp. Explains why pulling back while catching reduces force.

Quick Problem-Solving Guide

To Find Acceleration when Force & Mass are Given:
Use F = ma → a = F/m
To Find Velocity After Collision:
Use Conservation of Momentum: m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂)v
To Find Force from Momentum Change:
Use F = Δp/Δt or F = m(v-u)/t
To Reduce Force Impact (Catching, Stopping):
Increase the time of deceleration (pull hands back). Since F = Δp/Δt, larger Δt means smaller F.

Exam Preparation Guide

Common Misconceptions to Avoid:
  1. Action-Reaction cancellation: They act on different bodies, so they never cancel. Both bodies experience effects.
  2. Inertia vs. Mass: Inertia is the property; mass is the measure of inertia. Don't confuse them.
  3. Force and Motion: A force doesn't need to be applied continuously for motion to occur. Objects in motion continue unless a force acts.
  4. Heavier = More Force Always: F = ma. Greater force is needed for greater acceleration, but a light object can have high acceleration with small force.
  5. Momentum = Kinetic Energy: They're different quantities. Momentum is mv; kinetic energy is ½mv².
Tips for Solving Numerical Problems:
  1. Always identify given data and what you need to find.
  2. Choose the correct formula based on what's given and what's asked.
  3. In momentum problems, watch signs. Opposite directions = opposite signs.
  4. In collision problems, use conservation of momentum before solving for other unknowns.
  5. Always include units in your answers and show intermediate steps.
  6. Check if your answer makes physical sense (e.g., a heavier object slowing down more than a lighter one in collision).
Remediation Areas (If You're Struggling):

If you struggle with Newton's Laws: Revisit the concept of forces as pushes and pulls. Use real-world examples. Draw free body diagrams showing all forces.

If you struggle with momentum: Remember: momentum = mass × velocity. A bowling ball and a marble at the same speed have different momentum. Practice with actual numbers.

If you struggle with collisions: Always use momentum conservation first. Set up your equation carefully with correct signs. Practice at least 5 different collision problems.

Real-World Applications of Force and Motion

1. Automobile Safety Systems

Airbags inflate instantly during a crash. They increase the time of deceleration from ~0.1s (direct impact) to ~0.5s. Since F = Δp/Δt, a longer deceleration time means lower force on passengers, reducing injuries. Seatbelts work similarly by spreading impact over a larger area and time.

2. Sports and Athletics

Cricket players move their hands backward while catching a fast ball. This increases the time of momentum change, reducing the force experienced by hands (less pain). Similarly, high jumpers land on mats instead of hard ground to increase deceleration time and reduce injury.

3. Rocket and Space Propulsion

Rockets work on Newton's Third Law and momentum conservation. By expelling mass (fuel) at high velocity backward, the rocket experiences an equal and opposite forward force. This principle works in the vacuum of space where there's no air to "push against."

4. Vehicle Braking Systems

Anti-lock braking systems (ABS) modulate brake force to prevent skidding. By maintaining the maximum friction force (F = μN), ABS shortens stopping distance more effectively than conventional brakes. This is an application of F = ma and friction force optimization.

5. Gymnastics and Dance

Gymnasts use the concept of angular momentum and rotational inertia. By bringing their limbs closer to their body, they reduce rotational inertia and spin faster (like an ice skater). By extending limbs, they slow down rotation, demonstrating conservation of angular momentum.

6. Maritime and Aviation

Ships and planes cannot stop abruptly due to their large inertia. They require significant distance and time to decelerate. This is why aircraft carriers are so long and why ships need powerful reverse thrusters. The greater the mass, the greater the required force for same deceleration.

Conceptual Check: Test Your Understanding

Question: Which would be harder to push: a car at rest or a car moving at constant velocity?
Conceptual Answer: Neither! Both are equally easy. A car at rest requires force to overcome static friction and start accelerating. A car moving at constant velocity requires no net force (Newton's First Law) — it maintains motion. Any small push will speed it up or slow it down. The difference is that static friction opposes the initial push, not the concept of inertia itself.
Question: If momentum is conserved in all collisions, why do objects sometimes stop after colliding?
Conceptual Answer: Momentum is conserved, but it can be distributed differently. In a perfectly inelastic collision, when a moving object hits a stationary object of equal mass, the combined system moves at half the original velocity. If the moving object hits a much more massive object, the light object rebounds backward while the heavy object barely moves. Overall momentum is conserved, even if individual objects seem to "stop."
Question: Can two objects have the same momentum but different kinetic energies?
Conceptual Answer: Yes! Example: A 1 kg object at 10 m/s has momentum 10 kg⋅m/s and KE = 50 J. A 2 kg object at 5 m/s has the same momentum but KE = 25 J. Momentum (mv) and kinetic energy (½mv²) are fundamentally different quantities with different dependence on mass and velocity. This is why momentum conservation alone doesn't determine final velocities in all cases.

Extended Examples: Real Calculations

Extended Example 1: Multi-Stage Rocket

A 2-stage rocket has total mass 1000 kg at launch. Stage 1 (800 kg) burns and expels 600 kg of fuel at 3000 m/s relative to the rocket. Find:

  1. Velocity of rocket+Stage 2 after Stage 1 burnout
  2. Momentum of exhaust gases
Solution: Using momentum conservation (taking rocket direction as positive):
Initial momentum = 0 (at rest)
Final: (200 + 200) kg × v_rocket + 600 kg × (-3000 + v_rocket) = 0
400v + 600v - 1,800,000 = 0
v_rocket ≈ 1,800 m/s (approximately, simplified calculation)
Actual rocket calculations are more complex but follow this principle.

Extended Example 2: Coupled Motion

Two blocks connected by a string on a frictionless surface: M₁ = 3 kg and M₂ = 2 kg. A horizontal force F = 20 N is applied to M₁. Find the acceleration and tension in the string.

Solution:
Total mass = 3 + 2 = 5 kg
Total acceleration a = F/(M₁ + M₂) = 20/5 = 4 m/s²
For block M₂ (only tension acts): T = M₂ × a = 2 × 4 = 8 N
Verify with M₁: F - T = M₁ × a → 20 - T = 3 × 4 → T = 8 N ✓
