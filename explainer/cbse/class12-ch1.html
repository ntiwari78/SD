Electric Charges and Fields

Understanding the foundation of electrostatics and the behavior of charged particles

Did you ever wonder: Why does a plastic comb rubbed through dry hair attract tiny pieces of paper? Why does lightning strike certain objects? What invisible force governs how particles interact across space? This chapter reveals the deep physics behind these phenomena—introducing you to electric charge, the most fundamental property of matter after mass.

1. Electric Charge: Nature's Hidden Force

Definition: Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force in an electromagnetic field. It is denoted by q and measured in Coulombs (C).

1.1 Properties of Electric Charge

1.1.1 Quantization of Charge

Charge exists in discrete units—you cannot have fractional elementary charge. All charges are integer multiples of the elementary charge e.

Quantization Formula $$q = ne$$ where n = integer, e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (elementary charge)
Example: An electron has charge q = −1e = −1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. A proton has charge q = +1e = +1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. An object with 10 excess electrons has total charge q = −10e.

1.1.2 Conservation of Charge

Electric charge cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one object to another. In any isolated system, the total charge remains constant.

Conservation Example

Scenario:
Two identical conducting spheres, one with charge +Q and the other with charge −3Q, are brought into contact.
Solution:
Initial total charge = Q + (−3Q) = −2Q
When they touch, charge distributes equally (both spheres are identical):
Each sphere gets: −2Q / 2 = −Q
Final state: Both spheres have charge −Q (conservation verified)

1.1.3 Additivity of Charge

When multiple charges coexist in a region, the total charge is simply the algebraic sum of all individual charges.

Total Charge $$q_{total} = q_1 + q_2 + q_3 + \ldots + q_n$$

1.2 Types of Materials: Conductors and Insulators

Property Conductors Insulators
Free Electrons Many free electrons (loosely bound) Few/no free electrons (tightly bound)
Charge Movement Charges move freely throughout material Charges cannot move easily
Examples Copper, aluminum, silver, iron Glass, rubber, wood, plastic
Charge Distribution Charge distributes on the surface Charge remains where placed
E-field Inside Zero at equilibrium Non-zero generally

1.3 Methods of Charging

1.3.1 Charging by Friction

When two different materials are rubbed together, electrons transfer from one to the other due to their different affinities for electronsTendency to attract electrons. One becomes positively charged (loses electrons) and the other becomes negatively charged (gains electrons).
Real-world Example
Rubbing a glass rod with silk transfers electrons from glass to silk:
  • Glass rod becomes positively charged (+)
  • Silk cloth becomes negatively charged (−)
  • They attract each other

1.3.2 Charging by Conduction

When a charged conductor touches an uncharged conductor, charge flows until both reach the same potential. They end up with charge distributed based on their surface areas (for identical conductors, charge splits equally).
Real-world Example
A charged metal sphere (+10 C) touches an identical uncharged sphere:
  • After contact: both have +5 C
  • Charge flows through the connecting path
  • Process continues until potential is equal

1.3.3 Charging by Induction

A charged object is brought near (but not touching) an uncharged conductor. The electric field rearranges charges within the conductor—negative charges move toward positive inducer, positive charges move away—creating a net induced charge without any contact or charge transfer.
Real-world Example
A negatively charged rod is brought near a neutral metallic sphere:
  • Positive charges in sphere move toward the rod
  • Negative charges move away to the far side
  • Near side becomes positively charged (induced)
  • Sphere and rod attract each other
  • No charge has transferred
Learn: Why doesn't induction transfer charge?
Induction works because the charged object creates an electric field that polarizes the conductor. The positive charges are attracted toward the negative rod (and vice versa), but because the conductor is neutral overall and the charges cannot reach the external rod, no actual transfer occurs. It's a rearrangement, not a transfer. When the inducing object is removed, the charges return to their original distribution and the conductor becomes neutral again.

2. Coulomb's Law: The Force Between Charges

Coulomb's Law: The electric force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their magnitudes and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. The force is attractive if charges are opposite, repulsive if charges are the same.

2.1 Mathematical Form

Coulomb's Law (Scalar Form) $$F = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$ where:
  • F = magnitude of force (Newtons)
  • k = Coulomb constant = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
  • q₁, q₂ = magnitudes of charges (Coulombs)
  • r = distance between charges (meters)
Alternative form: $$F = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$ where ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²) is the permittivity of free space.

2.2 Vector Form of Coulomb's Law

For a charge q₁ at position r₁ and charge q₂ at position r₂, the force on q₂ is:

Vector Form $$\vec{F}_{12} = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r}$$ where r̂ is the unit vector from q₁ to q₂.

Worked Example 2.1: Coulomb Force Calculation

Problem:
Two point charges q₁ = +2 μC and q₂ = +3 μC are separated by r = 20 cm. Calculate the force between them.
Given:
q₁ = 2 × 10⁻⁶ C, q₂ = 3 × 10⁻⁶ C, r = 0.2 m, k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
Solution:
$$F = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{2 \times 10^{-6} \times 3 \times 10^{-6}}{(0.2)^2}$$ $$F = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{6 \times 10^{-12}}{0.04}$$ $$F = 9 \times 10^9 \times 1.5 \times 10^{-10} = 1.35 \text{ N}$$
Answer:
F = 1.35 N (repulsive, since both charges are positive)

2.3 Superposition Principle

Superposition Principle: When multiple charges are present, the net force on any one charge is the vector sum of forces due to all other charges individually. Each charge acts independently; the presence of other charges doesn't modify individual Coulomb interactions.
Net Force (Superposition) $$\vec{F}_{net} = \vec{F}_{1} + \vec{F}_{2} + \vec{F}_{3} + \ldots + \vec{F}_{n}$$

Worked Example 2.2: Superposition of Forces

Problem:
Three charges are arranged on a line: q₁ = +2 C at x = 0, q₂ = +2 C at x = 1 m, q₃ = −2 C at x = 2 m. Find the net force on q₂.
Force from q₁ on q₂ (repulsive, rightward):
$$F_{12} = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{12}^2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{2 \times 2}{1^2} = 36 \times 10^9 \text{ N (rightward)}$$
Force from q₃ on q₂ (attractive, rightward):
$$F_{32} = k\frac{|q_3||q_2|}{r_{32}^2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{2 \times 2}{1^2} = 36 \times 10^9 \text{ N (rightward)}$$
Net Force:
$$F_{net} = 36 \times 10^9 + 36 \times 10^9 = 72 \times 10^9 \text{ N (rightward)}$$
Learn: Why does Coulomb's Law follow the inverse-square rule?
Electric field strength decreases with the square of distance because the field lines spread out over an area proportional to 4πr². As you move twice as far from a charge, the same field lines are spread over 4 times the area (2² = 4), so the density (and strength) of the field is 1/4. This is a fundamental consequence of three-dimensional space geometry—it's why gravity also follows an inverse-square law!

2.4 Key Characteristics

  • Range: Coulomb's law acts over all distances (no maximum range)
  • Central Force: Force acts along the line joining the charges
  • Linear dependence on charge: Doubling a charge doubles the force
  • Inverse-square dependence: Doubling the distance reduces force to 1/4
  • Obeys Newton's 3rd law: Forces on charges are equal and opposite

3. Electric Field: The Mediator of Force

Definition: The electric field E is a region of space where an electric force acts on a charged particle. It is the force per unit charge at any point in space.

3.1 Electric Field Strength

Electric Field Definition $$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0}$$ where:
  • E = electric field strength (N/C or V/m)
  • F = force on test charge (N)
  • q₀ = test charge (C)
Test Charge: The test charge q₀ is assumed to be infinitesimally small and positive. Its purpose is to probe the field without significantly affecting it. By convention, we take q₀ = +1 C for simplicity, so E numerically equals F.

3.2 Electric Field Due to a Point Charge

Field of Point Charge $$\vec{E} = k\frac{q}{r^2}\hat{r}$$ or equivalently: $$\vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{q}{r^2}\hat{r}$$ where:
  • q = source charge (C)
  • r = distance from charge (m)
  • r̂ = unit vector from charge toward point
Direction: For a positive charge, field lines point radially outward. For a negative charge, field lines point radially inward. Magnitude depends on charge and distance as shown above.

Worked Example 3.1: Field Due to a Point Charge

Problem:
A point charge of +4 μC is placed at the origin. Find the electric field at a distance of 2 m from the charge.
Given:
q = 4 × 10⁻⁶ C, r = 2 m, k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
Solution:
$$E = k\frac{q}{r^2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{4 \times 10^{-6}}{(2)^2}$$ $$E = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{4 \times 10^{-6}}{4} = 9 \times 10^3 = 9000 \text{ N/C}$$
Direction:
Radially outward from the origin (positive charge)

3.3 Electric Field Lines

Field Lines: Imaginary lines that represent the direction and relative strength of the electric field. They originate from positive charges and terminate on negative charges.

3.3.1 Properties of Field Lines

  • Direction: The direction of the field at any point is tangent to the field line passing through that point
  • Density: The number of field lines per unit area is proportional to the field strength (closer lines = stronger field)
  • Origin and termination: Lines originate on positive charges and terminate on negative charges; they never cross each other
  • Continuity: Field lines are continuous curves between charges
  • Perpendicularity at conductors: Inside a conductor at equilibrium, field is zero; at the surface, field is perpendicular to the surface

3.3.2 Field Line Patterns

Common Field Patterns
  • Single positive charge: Radial lines pointing outward; strength decreases with r²
  • Single negative charge: Radial lines pointing inward; strength decreases with r²
  • Like charges (+–+): Repulsive pattern; field lines diverge between them
  • Opposite charges (+–): Attractive pattern; field lines converge from + to −
  • Parallel plate capacitor: Uniform field between plates (parallel, equally spaced lines); field zero outside

3.4 Superposition of Electric Fields

Superposition: The net electric field at any point due to multiple charges is the vector sum of fields due to individual charges.
Net Electric Field $$\vec{E}_{net} = \vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2 + \vec{E}_3 + \ldots + \vec{E}_n$$

Worked Example 3.2: Superposition of Fields

Problem:
Two point charges: q₁ = +2 μC at (0, 0) and q₂ = +2 μC at (3 m, 0). Find the electric field at point P located at (1.5 m, 0).
Distance from q₁ to P:
r₁ = 1.5 m
Distance from q₂ to P:
r₂ = 3 − 1.5 = 1.5 m
Field from q₁ at P:
$$E_1 = k\frac{q_1}{r_1^2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{2 \times 10^{-6}}{(1.5)^2} = 8000 \text{ N/C (rightward)}$$
Field from q₂ at P:
$$E_2 = k\frac{q_2}{r_2^2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{2 \times 10^{-6}}{(1.5)^2} = 8000 \text{ N/C (leftward)}$$
Net field:
E_net = 8000 − 8000 = 0 N/C (the fields cancel at the midpoint)

3.5 Electric Dipole Field

Electric Dipole: Two equal and opposite charges (−q and +q) separated by a small distance d. The dipole is characterized by its dipole momentp = qd, pointing from −q to +q p = qd.
Dipole Moment $$\vec{p} = q\vec{d}$$ where:
  • q = magnitude of charge (C)
  • d = separation vector (m), pointing from −q to +q
  • p = dipole moment (C·m)

3.5.1 Field on the Axial Line

For a point at distance r on the axis passing through the dipole:

Axial Field (for r >> d) $$E_{axial} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{2p}{r^3}$$

3.5.2 Field on the Equatorial Line

For a point at distance r perpendicular to the dipole at its center:

Equatorial Field (for r >> d) $$E_{equatorial} = -\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{p}{r^3}$$ (negative sign indicates field opposite to dipole direction)

Worked Example 3.3: Dipole Field

Problem:
An electric dipole has dipole moment p = 4 × 10⁻³⁰ C·m. Find the electric field on the axial line at a distance of 10⁻¹⁰ m from the center.
Solution:
$$E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{2p}{r^3} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{2 \times 4 \times 10^{-30}}{(10^{-10})^3}$$ $$E = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{8 \times 10^{-30}}{10^{-30}} = 9 \times 10^9 \times 8 = 7.2 \times 10^{10} \text{ N/C}$$

4. Electric Dipole in an External Field

4.1 Torque on a Dipole

When a dipole of moment p is placed in an external electric field E, it experiences a torque that tends to align it with the field.

Torque on Dipole $$\vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E}$$ Magnitude: $$\tau = pE\sin\theta$$ where θ is the angle between p and E.
Physical Interpretation: The dipole experiences maximum torque when it's perpendicular to the field (θ = 90°). When aligned with the field (θ = 0°), torque is zero.

Worked Example 4.1: Torque on Dipole

Problem:
A dipole of moment 5 × 10⁻³⁰ C·m is placed in a uniform electric field of 2 × 10⁵ N/C. If the dipole makes an angle of 30° with the field, find the torque.
Given:
p = 5 × 10⁻³⁰ C·m, E = 2 × 10⁵ N/C, θ = 30°
Solution:
$$\tau = pE\sin\theta = 5 \times 10^{-30} \times 2 \times 10^5 \times \sin(30°)$$ $$\tau = 5 \times 10^{-30} \times 2 \times 10^5 \times 0.5 = 5 \times 10^{-26} \text{ N·m}$$

4.2 Potential Energy of a Dipole

Potential Energy $$U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E} = -pE\cos\theta$$
Interpretation:
  • When θ = 0° (dipole aligned with field): U = −pE (minimum, stable equilibrium)
  • When θ = 180° (dipole opposite to field): U = +pE (maximum, unstable equilibrium)
  • When θ = 90° (perpendicular): U = 0

Worked Example 4.2: Dipole Potential Energy

Problem:
A dipole with p = 3 × 10⁻³⁰ C·m is rotated from alignment (θ = 0°) to perpendicular (θ = 90°) in a field E = 10⁵ N/C. Calculate the work done against the electric field.
Initial PE (θ = 0°):
U₁ = −pE = −3 × 10⁻³⁰ × 10⁵ = −3 × 10⁻²⁵ J
Final PE (θ = 90°):
U₂ = 0 J
Work done against field:
W = U₂ − U₁ = 0 − (−3 × 10⁻²⁵) = 3 × 10⁻²⁵ J
Learn: Why do polar molecules align with electric fields?
Polar molecules like water have permanent dipole moments. When placed in an electric field, they experience a torque that tends to align them with the field. This is energetically favorable because alignment reduces the potential energy of the system. In your microwave oven, the rapidly oscillating electromagnetic field causes polar water molecules to constantly rotate, and friction from this rotation heats the food!

5. Continuous Charge Distributions

Overview: In many situations, charges are distributed continuously throughout a region rather than being discrete point charges. We describe this using charge densities.

5.1 Charge Density Definitions

Type Symbol Definition Unit
Linear Charge Density λ $$\lambda = \frac{dq}{dl}$$ (charge per unit length) C/m
Surface Charge Density σ $$\sigma = \frac{dq}{dA}$$ (charge per unit area) C/m²
Volume Charge Density ρ $$\rho = \frac{dq}{dV}$$ (charge per unit volume) C/m³

5.2 Electric Field Due to Infinite Line Charge

Consider an infinitely long straight line with uniform linear charge density λ.

Field of Line Charge $$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r}$$ where r is the perpendicular distance from the line.
Direction: Radially outward if λ > 0, radially inward if λ < 0. Field lines are perpendicular to the line.

Worked Example 5.1: Field of Line Charge

Problem:
An infinitely long line charge has linear charge density λ = 2 × 10⁻⁸ C/m. Find the electric field at perpendicular distance r = 10 cm from the line.
Given:
λ = 2 × 10⁻⁸ C/m, r = 0.1 m, ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²)
Solution:
$$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r} = \frac{2 \times 10^{-8}}{2\pi \times 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \times 0.1}$$ $$E = \frac{2 \times 10^{-8}}{5.57 \times 10^{-12}} = 3.59 \times 10^3 \text{ N/C} \approx 3600 \text{ N/C}$$

5.3 Electric Field Due to Charged Ring

A ring of radius R carries total charge Q uniformly distributed.

Field on Axis of Ring $$E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{Qz}{(R^2 + z^2)^{3/2}}$$ where z is distance from ring center along axis.
Special cases:
  • At center (z = 0): E = 0
  • At z >> R: E ≈ kQ/z² (behaves like point charge)
  • Maximum field at z = R/√2

5.4 Electric Field Due to Charged Disc

A disc of radius R carries uniform surface charge density σ.

Field on Axis of Disc $$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}\left(1 - \frac{z}{\sqrt{R^2 + z^2}}\right)$$ where z is distance from disc center along axis.
Special cases:
  • At center (z = 0): E = σ/(2ε₀)
  • At z >> R: E ≈ Q/(4πε₀z²) = σπR²/(4πε₀z²)
  • Very close to surface: E ≈ σ/(2ε₀) (constant)

Worked Example 5.2: Field of Charged Disc

Problem:
A uniformly charged disc of radius 0.2 m carries surface charge density σ = 10⁻⁶ C/m². Find the electric field at z = 0.1 m from the center on the axis.
Given:
R = 0.2 m, z = 0.1 m, σ = 10⁻⁶ C/m², ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²)
Solution:
$$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}\left(1 - \frac{z}{\sqrt{R^2 + z^2}}\right)$$ $$\sqrt{R^2 + z^2} = \sqrt{(0.2)^2 + (0.1)^2} = \sqrt{0.05} = 0.224 \text{ m}$$ $$E = \frac{10^{-6}}{2 \times 8.85 \times 10^{-12}}\left(1 - \frac{0.1}{0.224}\right)$$ $$E = 5.65 \times 10^4 \times (1 - 0.446) = 5.65 \times 10^4 \times 0.554 = 3.13 \times 10^4 \text{ N/C}$$

6. Gauss's Law: The Power of Symmetry

Gauss's Law: The electric flux through a closed surface is proportional to the charge enclosed by the surface. This elegant law relates the electric field to the charge distribution—it's one of Maxwell's four fundamental equations.

6.1 Electric Flux

Electric Flux Definition $$\Phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$$ where:
  • E = electric field
  • dA = infinitesimal area vector (perpendicular to surface)
  • ∮ = integral over closed surface
Physical Meaning: Flux is a measure of the number of electric field lines passing through a surface. It's positive when field lines exit the surface and negative when they enter.

6.2 Gauss's Law (Integral Form)

Gauss's Law $$\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0}$$ or equivalently: $$\Phi_E = \frac{q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0}$$ where:
  • q_enc = total charge enclosed by the surface
  • ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²)
Learn: Why is Gauss's Law so powerful?
While Coulomb's Law requires you to integrate over all charge distributions (computationally intensive), Gauss's Law allows you to find the field in highly symmetric situations almost instantly. By choosing a clever "Gaussian surface" that matches the symmetry of the problem, you can often reduce a complex integral to simple algebra. This is why Gauss's Law is preferred for calculations involving planar symmetry, cylindrical symmetry, or spherical symmetry.

6.3 Application 1: Infinite Line Charge

Worked Example 6.1: Field of Infinite Line Charge Using Gauss's Law

Setup:
Consider an infinite line charge with uniform linear charge density λ. We want to find E at perpendicular distance r.
Symmetry Analysis:
The field must be:
  • Perpendicular to the line (by symmetry)
  • Same at all points equidistant from the line
  • Independent of position along the line
Gaussian Surface:
Choose a cylinder of radius r and length L, with axis coinciding with the line charge.
Calculate Flux:
Flux through end caps: 0 (field perpendicular to area vector)
Flux through curved surface: E × (2πrL)
Total flux: $$\Phi = E \times 2\pi r L$$
Enclosed Charge:
q_enc = λL (charge within length L)
Apply Gauss's Law:
$$E \times 2\pi r L = \frac{\lambda L}{\epsilon_0}$$ $$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r}$$
Result:
We've derived the field of an infinite line charge with minimal calculation!

6.4 Application 2: Infinite Charged Plane

Worked Example 6.2: Field of Infinite Charged Plane Using Gauss's Law

Setup:
Consider an infinite plane with uniform surface charge density σ. Find E at distance z from the plane.
Symmetry Analysis:
The field must be:
  • Perpendicular to the plane
  • Same magnitude on opposite sides of the plane
  • Independent of distance z (for infinite plane)
Gaussian Surface:
Choose a pillbox (cylinder) with end caps of area A parallel to the plane, symmetric about the plane.
Calculate Flux:
Flux through two end caps: E × A + E × A = 2EA
Flux through side: 0 (field parallel to side)
Total flux: $$\Phi = 2EA$$
Enclosed Charge:
q_enc = σA (charge within the area A)
Apply Gauss's Law:
$$2EA = \frac{\sigma A}{\epsilon_0}$$ $$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}$$
Result:
The field of an infinite charged plane is uniform and independent of distance!

6.5 Application 3: Uniformly Charged Sphere

Worked Example 6.3: Field of Uniformly Charged Sphere

Setup:
Solid sphere of radius R with uniform volume charge density ρ. Find E at distance r from center.
Case 1: Outside the sphere (r > R)
Using Gaussian surface (sphere of radius r):
  • Total enclosed charge: $$q_{enc} = \rho \times \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 = Q$$
  • Flux: $$\Phi = E \times 4\pi r^2$$
  • Apply Gauss: $$E \times 4\pi r^2 = \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0}$$
  • Result: $$E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} = \frac{kQ}{r^2}$$ (same as point charge!)
Case 2: Inside the sphere (r < R)
Using Gaussian surface (sphere of radius r):
  • Enclosed charge: $$q_{enc} = \rho \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$$
  • Since $$\rho = \frac{Q}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3}$$, we have $$q_{enc} = Q\frac{r^3}{R^3}$$
  • Flux: $$E \times 4\pi r^2 = \frac{Qr^3}{R^3\epsilon_0}$$
  • Result: $$E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{r}{R^3} = \frac{kQr}{R^3}$$ (field increases linearly inside)

6.6 Application 4: Conducting Sphere

Worked Example 6.4: Field of Conducting Sphere

Setup:
Conducting sphere of radius R carrying total charge Q. Find E everywhere.
Key Property:
In electrostatic equilibrium, charge resides entirely on the surface; field inside is zero.
Case 1: Inside (r < R)
$$E = 0$$ (always)
Case 2: On surface (r = R)
$$E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R^2}$$ (perpendicular to surface)
Case 3: Outside (r > R)
$$E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2}$$ (same as point charge at center)
Shell Theorem (Conducting Sphere): A uniformly charged conducting shell produces no electric field inside it. Outside, it produces a field as if all charge were concentrated at the center.

6.7 Summary Table: Gauss's Law Applications

Infinite charged plane (σ) $$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}$$ Perpendicular to plane

Exam Preparation: HOTS & Case Studies

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

HOTS Question 1: Force Balance

Problem: Three point charges q₁ = +1 C, q₂ = +4 C, and q₃ are arranged on a horizontal line with q₁ at origin and q₂ at distance 3 m. Where should q₃ be placed such that the net force on q₃ is zero? Assume q₃ is positive.

Analysis: For force balance on q₃:

  • If q₃ is between q₁ and q₂, forces point in opposite directions (possible balance)
  • If q₃ is outside, forces point in same direction (no balance)
  • Set $$\frac{kq_1}{x^2} = \frac{kq_2}{(3-x)^2}$$ where x is position of q₃
  • $$\frac{1}{x^2} = \frac{4}{(3-x)^2}$$, so $$\frac{1}{x} = \frac{2}{3-x}$$
  • $$3 - x = 2x$$, thus $$x = 1 \text{ m}$$

Answer: q₃ should be placed 1 m from q₁ (or 2 m from q₂)

HOTS Question 2: Field Superposition in 2D

Problem: Two point charges +Q are placed at corners of a square of side a. Find the electric field at the center of the square.

Analysis:

  • Distance from each corner to center: $$r = \frac{a}{\sqrt{2}}$$
  • Field from each charge: $$E = \frac{kQ}{(a/\sqrt{2})^2} = \frac{2kQ}{a^2}$$
  • By symmetry, components perpendicular to diagonal cancel
  • Component along diagonal from each charge: $$E\cos(45°) = \frac{2kQ}{a^2} \times \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$
  • Two charges contribute, so net field: $$E_{net} = 2 \times \frac{2kQ}{a^2} \times \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}kQ}{a^2}$$

Answer: $$E = \frac{2\sqrt{2}kQ}{a^2}$$ directed along the diagonal toward the opposite corner

HOTS Question 3: Gauss's Law with Non-uniform Charge

Problem: A sphere of radius R has charge density ρ(r) = ρ₀r/R (increases linearly from center). Find the electric field at r = R/2.

Analysis:

  • Use Gauss's Law: $$\oint E \cdot dA = \frac{q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0}$$
  • Find enclosed charge: $$q_{enc} = \int_0^{R/2} \rho_0 \frac{r'}{R} 4\pi r'^2 dr' = \frac{4\pi\rho_0}{R} \int_0^{R/2} r'^3 dr'$$
  • $$q_{enc} = \frac{4\pi\rho_0}{R} \times \frac{(R/2)^4}{4} = \frac{4\pi\rho_0 R^3}{R \times 64} = \frac{\pi\rho_0 R^3}{16}$$
  • Apply Gauss: $$E \times 4\pi (R/2)^2 = \frac{\pi\rho_0 R^3}{16\epsilon_0}$$
  • $$E = \frac{\rho_0 R}{16\epsilon_0}$$

Answer: $$E = \frac{\rho_0 R}{16\epsilon_0}$$

Case-Based Questions

Case Study 1: Lightning Protection

Context: A lightning rod is a metal conductor placed on buildings to safely conduct lightning to ground. The rod has a sharp point at the top.

Q1a:
Explain why the point at the top is important using concepts of electric field and charge density.
Answer 1a:
The pointed top has a very small radius of curvature. According to Gauss's Law applied to a conductor surface, the electric field near a sharp point is inversely proportional to the radius of curvature. Thus E is very large at the point, causing ionization of surrounding air and discharge of the charge before it builds up dangerously.
Q1b:
Why should the lightning rod be a good conductor?
Answer 1b:
A good conductor allows free movement of charges. When lightning strikes, the massive charge must be conducted to ground with minimal resistance. A poor conductor would generate heat (I²R losses) and potentially melt or catch fire.

Case Study 2: Electrostatic Dust Collection

Context: Industrial air purifiers use electric fields to remove dust particles from air. Dust particles in air are given charge (typically negative) by an ionizer, then passed through a region with a strong electric field.

Q2a:
A dust particle of mass 10⁻⁶ kg acquires charge q = −10⁻⁸ C and is placed in a uniform field E = 10⁵ N/C directed horizontally. Calculate the acceleration of the dust particle.
Answer 2a:
Force on particle: F = qE = 10⁻⁸ × 10⁵ = 10⁻³ N
Acceleration: a = F/m = 10⁻³ / 10⁻⁶ = 10³ m/s² = 1000 m/s²
Q2b:
The dust particles move toward and stick to the positive electrode. Why is the field needed if they're already charged?
Answer 2b:
Without the field, the charged dust particles would undergo random motion (Brownian motion) and take forever to settle. The electric field provides a strong directional force that rapidly transports them to the collection electrode, making the device practical.

Case Study 3: Van de Graaff Generator

Context: A Van de Graaff generator is a device that produces very high electrostatic voltages (up to millions of volts) using a rotating belt and metallic dome.

Q3a:
The belt transfers charge to the dome continuously. Why does the dome reach a maximum charge instead of continuing to accumulate charge indefinitely?
Answer 3a:
As charge accumulates, the electric field inside the dome increases. Eventually, the field becomes strong enough to ionize the surrounding air (air breakdown), allowing charge to leak away through the ionized air. At equilibrium, charge transfer rate equals leakage rate.
Q3b:
Why is the dome typically made spherical?
Answer 3b:
A sphere has the smallest surface area for a given volume, minimizing surface charge density σ. Lower σ means lower surface electric field E = σ/ε₀, thus delaying air breakdown and allowing the dome to accumulate more charge and reach higher potential.

Glossary: Key Definitions

1. Electric Charge
A fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience electromagnetic forces. It comes in positive and negative varieties, measured in Coulombs (C).
2. Elementary Charge (e)
The magnitude of charge carried by a single electron or proton, equal to 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. It is the smallest unit of charge.
3. Charge Quantization
The principle that electric charge always exists in integer multiples of the elementary charge e. You cannot have a fraction of an elementary charge.
4. Conservation of Charge
A fundamental law stating that in an isolated system, the total electric charge remains constant. Charge can be transferred but not created or destroyed.
5. Conductor
A material with many free electrons that can move easily throughout the material, allowing charge to flow freely. Examples: copper, aluminum, silver.
6. Insulator
A material with few or no free electrons, preventing easy charge movement. Charges become localized where placed. Examples: rubber, glass, wood.
7. Coulomb's Law
The law stating that the electrostatic force between two point charges is proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
8. Coulomb Constant (k)
The proportionality constant in Coulomb's Law, equal to 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C², or 1/(4πε₀).
9. Electric Field (E)
A region of space where an electric force acts on charged particles. Defined as the electric force per unit charge: E = F/q₀. Measured in N/C or V/m.
10. Test Charge
A hypothetical infinitesimally small positive charge used to probe the electric field without significantly affecting it. By convention, taken as q₀ = +1 C for calculations.
11. Field Line
An imaginary line whose direction at each point indicates the direction of the electric field. The spacing of field lines indicates field strength (closer lines = stronger field).
12. Electric Dipole
A pair of equal and opposite charges separated by a small distance. Characterized by its dipole moment p = qd, pointing from negative to positive charge.
13. Dipole Moment (p)
A vector quantity representing the strength and direction of an electric dipole, defined as p = qd where q is the charge magnitude and d is the separation vector.
14. Gauss's Law
A fundamental law of electrostatics stating that the electric flux through any closed surface is proportional to the charge enclosed: ∮E·dA = q_enc/ε₀.
15. Electric Flux (Φ)
A measure of the number of electric field lines passing through a surface, defined as Φ = ∮E·dA. A measure of the "flow" of the electric field through a surface.

Struggling? Remediation Guide

Common Misconception 1: "Electric field only exists near the charge."
Reality: Electric field extends to infinity around every charge, decreasing as 1/r². It's always present in space surrounding a charge, just weaker far away.
Common Misconception 2: "Electric field lines are real physical objects."
Reality: Field lines are mathematical constructs we draw to visualize the field. They represent the direction and relative strength but aren't physical.
Common Misconception 3: "A conductor in an electric field has the same field inside as outside."
Reality: In electrostatic equilibrium, the field inside a conductor is always zero. Charges redistribute on the surface to create an internal field that exactly cancels the external field.
Common Misconception 4: "Gauss's Law only works for spherically symmetric distributions."
Reality: Gauss's Law works for any charge distribution. However, it's most useful (easy to calculate) for problems with high symmetry: spherical, cylindrical, or planar.
Strategy 1: Approaching Force Calculations
Always start by identifying: (a) What charges are present? (b) What are the distances? (c) Are charges in 1D, 2D, or 3D? Then use Coulomb's Law for each pair and superposition to add vector forces. Draw a diagram showing charge positions and force directions.
Strategy 2: Approaching Field Calculations
Ask: (a) Is this a point charge, dipole, or continuous distribution? (b) Do I use direct integration or Gauss's Law? For high-symmetry problems, always try Gauss's Law first. Choose a Gaussian surface that matches the symmetry.
Strategy 3: Handling Vector Addition
When adding electric fields or forces: (1) Resolve each into components (x, y, z), (2) Add like components, (3) Find magnitude using Pythagorean theorem. Never try to add magnitudes directly if vectors aren't parallel!
Practice Tip: Work through the worked examples in this chapter multiple times until you can solve them from scratch without looking. Then attempt similar problems from your textbook. Start with single-charge problems, progress to multiple charges, then continuous distributions.
