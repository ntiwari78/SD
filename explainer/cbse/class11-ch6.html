Systems of Particles & Rotational Motion

NCERT Class 11 Physics | Chapter 6 | Complete Study Guide

When an ice skater pulls in her arms, she spins faster. A bicycle wheel balances upright as it moves. A dancer performs a perfect pirouette. These aren't magic—they're physics! This chapter reveals the hidden mathematics of motion in a spinning world, from the dynamics of rotating bodies to the mysterious conservation of angular momentum. By the end, you'll understand why objects spin, how to calculate their rotational energy, and why the cosmos follows these elegant rules.

1. Centre of Mass (CM)

Definition and Concept

The centre of mass is the point that moves as if all the mass of a system were concentrated there. For a symmetric, homogeneous body, the CM coincides with the geometric center. For irregular shapes, it's the weighted average position.

Centre of Mass for Discrete Particles

For a system of n particles with masses \( m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_n \) at positions \( \vec{r}_1, \vec{r}_2, \ldots, \vec{r}_n \):

\[ \vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{m_1\vec{r}_1 + m_2\vec{r}_2 + \cdots + m_n\vec{r}_n}{m_1 + m_2 + \cdots + m_n} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i\vec{r}_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i\vec{r}_i}{M} \]

where M is the total mass.

Centre of Mass for Continuous Bodies

For a continuous body with density \( \rho \):

\[ \vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{\int \vec{r} \, dm}{\int dm} = \frac{\int \rho \vec{r} \, dV}{M} \]

Velocity of Centre of Mass

The velocity of the CM is the mass-weighted average of all particle velocities:

\[ \vec{V}_{cm} = \frac{d\vec{R}_{cm}}{dt} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i\vec{v}_i}{M} \]

Acceleration and Force on CM

Differentiating velocity:

\[ \vec{A}_{cm} = \frac{d\vec{V}_{cm}}{dt} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i\vec{a}_i}{M} \]

\[ M\vec{A}_{cm} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i\vec{a}_i = \sum \vec{F}_{ext} = \vec{F}_{net} \]

Key Insight: The centre of mass of an isolated system (no external forces) moves with constant velocity. Even if internal forces cause rotation, the CM is unaffected.
Analogy: Think of a fireworks shell exploding in mid-air. The individual fragments scatter in all directions, but the CM follows the same parabolic path as before the explosion, as if the explosion never happened.

Practical Application

For a system of two particles:

\[ x_{cm} = \frac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2}{m_1 + m_2} \]

If \( m_1 = m_2 \), then \( x_{cm} = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2} \) (midpoint)

2. Vector Product (Cross Product)

Definition

The cross product of two vectors \( \vec{A} \) and \( \vec{B} \) is a vector perpendicular to both, with magnitude:

\[ |\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = AB\sin\theta \]

Direction: Given by the right-hand rule. Point fingers in direction of \( \vec{A} \), curl them toward \( \vec{B} \), thumb points in direction of \( \vec{A} \times \vec{B} \).

Properties of Cross Product

  • Anti-commutative: \( \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = -\vec{B} \times \vec{A} \)
  • Not associative: \( (\vec{A} \times \vec{B}) \times \vec{C} \neq \vec{A} \times (\vec{B} \times \vec{C}) \)
  • Distributive: \( \vec{A} \times (\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = \vec{A} \times \vec{B} + \vec{A} \times \vec{C} \)
  • Orthogonal vectors: If \( \vec{A} \perp \vec{B} \), then \( |\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = AB \)
  • Parallel vectors: If \( \vec{A} \parallel \vec{B} \), then \( \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = 0 \)

Component Form

If \( \vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j} + A_z\hat{k} \) and \( \vec{B} = B_x\hat{i} + B_y\hat{j} + B_z\hat{k} \):

\[ \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ A_x & A_y & A_z \\ B_x & B_y & B_z \end{vmatrix} \]

Torque: Application of Cross Product

When a force \( \vec{F} \) acts at position \( \vec{r} \) from a pivot point, it creates a torque (turning effect):

\[ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} \]

\[ \tau = rF\sin\theta \]

where \( \theta \) is the angle between \( \vec{r} \) and \( \vec{F} \).

Example: A door opens easiest when you push perpendicular to it (θ = 90°). Pushing along the door (θ = 0°) creates no torque.

Angular Momentum: Another Cross Product Application

Angular momentum about a point is:

\[ \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} = \vec{r} \times m\vec{v} \]

For a rigid body rotating with angular velocity \( \omega \):

\[ \vec{L} = I\vec{\omega} \]

where I is the moment of inertia.

3. Angular Velocity and Angular Acceleration

Angular Displacement

When an object rotates about a fixed axis through angle \( \theta \) (in radians):

  • 1 revolution = \( 2\pi \) radians = 360°
  • 1 radian = 57.3°

Angular Velocity

The rate of change of angular displacement:

\[ \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \] (in rad/s)

\[ \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T} \]

where f is frequency and T is period.

Angular Acceleration

The rate of change of angular velocity:

\[ \alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt} = \frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} \] (in rad/s²)

Relationship Between Linear and Angular Quantities

For a point at distance r from the rotation axis:

\[ v = \omega r \]

\[ a_t = \alpha r \] (tangential acceleration)

\[ a_c = \omega^2 r \] (centripetal acceleration)

Linear Motion Rotational Motion Relationship
Displacement: x Angular displacement: θ x = rθ
Velocity: v Angular velocity: ω v = rω
Acceleration: a Angular acceleration: α a = rα
Mass: m Moment of inertia: I I = mr² (point mass)
Analogy: Just as linear motion has velocity v and acceleration a, rotational motion has angular velocity ω and angular acceleration α. The formulas are parallel!

4. Torque and Angular Momentum

Torque: The Rotational Force

Torque is the rotational analog of force. It measures the tendency of a force to rotate an object about a pivot point.

\[ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} \]

\[ \tau = rF\sin\theta \]

Maximum when \( \theta = 90° \) (force perpendicular to lever arm)

Zero when \( \theta = 0° \) or \( 180° \) (force parallel to lever arm)

Moment Arm (Perpendicular Distance)

Alternative formula: \( \tau = r_\perp F \), where \( r_\perp = r\sin\theta \) is the perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of force.

Example: Opening a door with a push 1 m from the hinge creates more torque than pushing 0.5 m away. A wrench 0.5 m long needs less force to loosen a bolt than a 0.2 m wrench.

Angular Momentum

Angular momentum is the rotational analog of linear momentum:

\[ \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} = \vec{r} \times m\vec{v} \]

For rotation about a fixed axis:

\[ L = I\omega \]

where I is moment of inertia and ω is angular velocity.

Rotational Analog of Newton's 2nd Law

\[ \vec{\tau}_{net} = \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt} \]

For constant moment of inertia:

\[ \vec{\tau} = I\vec{\alpha} \]

Linear Rotational
\( \vec{F} = m\vec{a} \) \( \vec{\tau} = I\vec{\alpha} \)
\( \vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} \) \( \vec{\tau} = \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt} \)

Conservation of Angular Momentum

If the net external torque on a system is zero, angular momentum is conserved:

\[ \tau_{ext} = 0 \Rightarrow L = constant \]

\[ I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2 \]

Classic Example: An ice skater spins faster when pulling in her arms. As I decreases, ω must increase to keep L constant. This is why diving athletes rotate faster with arms tucked!
Analogy: Just as momentum is conserved when no external forces act, angular momentum is conserved when no external torques act. Nature preserves "rotational motion" like it preserves linear motion.

5. Equilibrium of Rigid Body

Conditions for Equilibrium

A rigid body is in equilibrium when it has no linear acceleration and no angular acceleration. Two conditions must be satisfied simultaneously:

Translational Equilibrium: \( \sum \vec{F} = 0 \)

Rotational Equilibrium: \( \sum \vec{\tau} = 0 \)

Both must hold at the same time!

Component Form

\( \sum F_x = 0, \quad \sum F_y = 0, \quad \sum F_z = 0 \)

\( \sum \tau_x = 0, \quad \sum \tau_y = 0, \quad \sum \tau_z = 0 \)

Types of Equilibrium

  • Stable Equilibrium: Object returns to original position after small displacement (ball at bottom of bowl)
  • Unstable Equilibrium: Object moves away after small displacement (ball at top of hill)
  • Neutral Equilibrium: Object remains in equilibrium at new position (ball on flat surface)

Couple

A couple is a pair of equal and opposite forces acting on different lines, producing a net torque but no net force.

\[ \tau_{couple} = F \cdot d \]

where F is the magnitude of each force and d is the perpendicular distance between them.

Example: A couple rotates a steering wheel. The forces at top and bottom are equal and opposite, canceling out (no net force on the wheel), but they create torque that spins the wheel.

Principle of Moments

For equilibrium about any point, the sum of clockwise torques equals the sum of counterclockwise torques:

\[ \sum \tau_{clockwise} = \sum \tau_{counterclockwise} \]

Center of Gravity vs. Center of Mass

In uniform gravitational field, center of gravity coincides with center of mass. For extended objects in non-uniform fields, they differ. An object balances at its center of gravity.

Practical Application: A see-saw balances when torques about the fulcrum are equal: \( m_1 g d_1 = m_2 g d_2 \), or \( m_1 d_1 = m_2 d_2 \).

6. Moment of Inertia

Definition

Moment of inertia (I) is the rotational analog of mass. It measures resistance to changes in rotational motion. The greater the moment of inertia, the harder it is to change the angular velocity.

For Discrete Particles

\[ I = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i r_i^2 \]

where \( r_i \) is the perpendicular distance of particle i from the rotation axis.

For Continuous Bodies

\[ I = \int r^2 \, dm = \int r^2 \rho \, dV \]

where \( \rho \) is the density and the integral is over the entire body.

Key Insight

Moment of inertia depends on:

  • The mass of the object (greater mass → greater I)
  • The distribution of mass (mass far from axis → greater I)
  • The location of the rotation axis (different axes → different I)
Analogy: Just as a heavier object resists acceleration more (larger m in F = ma), an object with mass concentrated far from the axis resists angular acceleration more (larger I in τ = Iα).

Parallel Axis Theorem

If I_cm is the moment of inertia about an axis through the center of mass, and we want I about a parallel axis at distance d away:

\[ I = I_{cm} + Md^2 \]

where M is the total mass.

Example: A uniform rod of length L rotating about its center has \( I_{cm} = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 \). Rotating about one end: \( I = I_{cm} + M(\frac{L}{2})^2 = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 + \frac{1}{4}ML^2 = \frac{1}{3}ML^2 \).

Perpendicular Axis Theorem

For planar bodies (objects in a plane), if x and y are in the plane and z is perpendicular:

\[ I_z = I_x + I_y \]

This simplifies calculations for flat objects like discs and rectangular plates.

Table of Moments of Inertia

For common shapes rotating about standard axes:

Shape Axis of Rotation Moment of Inertia
Thin Rod (mass M, length L) Through center, perpendicular to length \( I = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 \)
Thin Rod (mass M, length L) About one end \( I = \frac{1}{3}ML^2 \)

7. Kinematics of Rotational Motion

Equations of Rotational Motion

Rotational kinematics with constant angular acceleration α mirrors linear kinematics with constant acceleration a:

\[ \theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 \]

\[ \omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t \]

\[ \omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta \]

where \( \omega_0 \) is initial angular velocity, ω is final angular velocity, α is angular acceleration, and θ is angular displacement.

Linear Motion Rotational Motion
\( x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \) \( \theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 \)
\( v = v_0 + at \) \( \omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t \)
\( v^2 = v_0^2 + 2ax \) \( \omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta \)
\( v_{avg} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \) \( \omega_{avg} = \frac{\omega_0 + \omega}{2} \)

Common Cases

Case 1: Starting from Rest (\( \omega_0 = 0 \))

\[ \theta = \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 \]

\[ \omega = \alpha t \]

\[ \omega^2 = 2\alpha\theta \]

Case 2: Uniform Angular Velocity (\( \alpha = 0 \))

\[ \theta = \omega t \]

\[ \omega = constant \]

Example Problem: A wheel starts from rest and rotates with constant angular acceleration of 2 rad/s². How many revolutions does it make in 5 seconds?

\( \theta = \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2)(5)^2 = 25 \) rad
Revolutions = \( \frac{25}{2\pi} \approx 3.98 \) revolutions

8. Dynamics of Rotational Motion

Newton's Second Law for Rotation

The rotational analog of F = ma is:

\[ \sum \vec{\tau} = I\vec{\alpha} \]

or in scalar form for motion in a plane:

\[ \tau = I\alpha \]

This states that net torque equals moment of inertia times angular acceleration.

Rotational Kinetic Energy

A rotating object has kinetic energy:

\[ KE_{rot} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 \]

Compare with translational kinetic energy: \( KE_{trans} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \)

Analogy: Just as KE = ½mv² for linear motion, KE = ½Iω² for rotational motion. I plays the role of m, and ω plays the role of v.

Work-Energy Theorem for Rotation

Work done by torque equals change in rotational kinetic energy:

\[ W = \int \tau \, d\theta = \Delta KE_{rot} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}I\omega_i^2 \]

Power in Rotational Motion

Power is the rate of work:

\[ P = \frac{dW}{dt} = \tau \omega \]

Comparison Table

Quantity Linear Rotational
Newton's 2nd Law \( F = ma \) \( \tau = I\alpha \)
Kinetic Energy \( KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \) \( KE = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 \)
Momentum \( p = mv \) \( L = I\omega \)
Work \( W = Fx \) \( W = \tau\theta \)
Power \( P = Fv \) \( P = \tau\omega \)
Important: Every rotational quantity has a linear analog. This symmetry makes dynamics problems systematic.

9. Rolling Motion

Rolling Without Slipping

Rolling is a combination of translation and rotation. For an object rolling without slipping on a surface:

\[ v_{cm} = \omega R \]

where \( v_{cm} \) is the velocity of the center of mass, ω is angular velocity, and R is the radius.

This is the no-slip condition: the contact point has zero velocity relative to the ground.

Total Kinetic Energy in Rolling

Rolling motion combines translational and rotational KE:

\[ KE_{total} = KE_{trans} + KE_{rot} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 \]

Using \( I = kMR^2 \) and \( v_{cm} = \omega R \):

\[ KE_{total} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2 + \frac{1}{2}(kMR^2)\left(\frac{v_{cm}}{R}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2(1 + k) \]

where k is a shape factor:

\( k = \frac{1}{2} \) for solid sphere

\( k = 1 \) for thin hollow sphere

\( k = \frac{1}{2} \) for solid cylinder

\( k = 1 \) for thin hollow cylinder

Alternatively: \[ KE_{total} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2\left(1 + \frac{k^2}{R^2}\right) \text{ is WRONG} \]

Correct form: \[ KE_{total} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2\left(1 + \frac{I}{mR^2}\right) = \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2(1 + k) \]

Rolling on an Inclined Plane

For an object rolling without slipping down an incline of angle θ:

\[ a = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1 + \frac{I}{mR^2}} = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1 + k} \]

Note: This is less than \( g\sin\theta \) (free sliding without friction) because some gravitational potential energy goes into rotational KE.

Comparison of Rolling Objects

Shape k factor Acceleration Down Incline Time to Bottom (same height)
Solid Sphere 2/5 = 0.4 \( a = \frac{5g\sin\theta}{7} \) Slowest
Solid Cylinder 1/2 = 0.5 \( a = \frac{2g\sin\theta}{3} \) Medium
Hollow Sphere 2/3 ≈ 0.67 \( a = \frac{3g\sin\theta}{5} \) Medium-slow
Thin Hoop/Ring 1 \( a = \frac{g\sin\theta}{2} \) Fastest
Interesting Fact: A solid sphere reaches the bottom of an incline faster than a ring, even though both are rolling without slipping! The sphere's kinetic energy is distributed more efficiently between translation and rotation.

Rolling Motion: Energy Conservation Approach

If an object rolls from height h without slipping:

\[ mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 \]

\[ mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2(1 + k) \]

\[ v_{cm} = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1 + k}} \]

Example: A solid sphere rolls from rest down a 1 m high incline.
\( k = \frac{2}{5} \) for solid sphere
\( v_{cm} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 10 \times 1}{1 + 0.4}} = \sqrt{\frac{20}{1.4}} \approx 3.78 \) m/s
Compare to frictionless sliding: \( v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{20} \approx 4.47 \) m/s (faster!)

Friction in Rolling

For rolling without slipping:

  • Static friction acts (not kinetic), preventing slipping
  • This friction doesn't dissipate energy because the contact point is instantaneously at rest
  • The friction provides the torque needed for angular acceleration
Analogy: Think of rolling as many instantaneous "kicks" from friction at the contact point, each pushing the object forward and causing it to spin. Clever nature ensures these don't waste energy!

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

HOTS 1: Angular Momentum Conservation

Question: A student sits on a rotating stool holding two dumbbells at arm's length. Initially, the system rotates at 2 rad/s. When the student pulls the dumbbells toward their body, the rotation speed increases to 8 rad/s. Explain why this happens using angular momentum conservation. Calculate the ratio of final to initial moment of inertia.

Answer: The external torque is zero (no friction is mentioned), so angular momentum is conserved: \( L_i = L_f \Rightarrow I_i \omega_i = I_f \omega_f \). Thus \( I_f / I_i = \omega_i / \omega_f = 2/8 = 1/4 \). The moment of inertia decreased because the mass (dumbbells) moved closer to the rotation axis, decreasing \( \sum m_i r_i^2 \).

HOTS 2: Rolling vs. Sliding Energy

Question: Two identical spheres start at the top of an incline. Sphere A rolls without slipping; Sphere B slides without friction. Which reaches the bottom first? Why? Calculate the ratio of their final velocities.

Answer: Sphere B slides faster. For rolling sphere: \( mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_{A}^2(1 + k) \Rightarrow v_A = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1.4}} \). For sliding sphere: \( mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_{B}^2 \Rightarrow v_B = \sqrt{2gh} \). Ratio: \( v_A / v_B = \sqrt{1/1.4} \approx 0.845 \). The rolling sphere shares energy between translation and rotation, leaving less for translation. Also, friction limits acceleration of rolling sphere.

HOTS 3: Equilibrium and Stability

Question: A uniform rod of length 1 m is suspended from a pivot point. When hanging freely, is it in equilibrium? Is this equilibrium stable? What happens if you displace it slightly? Use torque analysis.

Answer: Yes, it's in equilibrium because the weight acts through the center of mass, directly below the pivot. At this position, torque τ = r × F = 0 (force is parallel to position vector from pivot). This is stable equilibrium: displacing it creates a restoring torque. The weight vector now has a perpendicular component relative to the rod, creating a torque that brings it back down. This is why pendulums oscillate.

Case-Based Questions

Case 1: Merry-Go-Round Physics

Scenario: A merry-go-round (mass 500 kg, radius 4 m) rotates initially at 0.5 rad/s. A child (mass 30 kg) runs tangentially at 2 m/s and jumps on at the edge.

Questions:

  1. Calculate the angular momentum of the child before jumping on.
  2. Find the angular velocity after the child lands (assuming no external torque).
  3. How much kinetic energy is lost in the collision?

Solution Hints:

1) \( L_{child} = mvr = 30 \times 2 \times 4 = 240 \) kg·m²/s

2) Initial: \( L_i = I_{disk} \omega_i + L_{child} = \frac{1}{2}(500)(4^2)(0.5) + 240 = 2000 + 240 = 2240 \) kg·m²/s

Final: \( I_{total} = 2000 + 30(4^2) = 2480 \) kg·m²/s

\( \omega_f = L_i / I_{total} = 2240 / 2480 \approx 0.903 \) rad/s

3) Energy lost = \( \frac{1}{2}(30)(2^2) + \frac{1}{2}(2000)(0.5^2) - \frac{1}{2}(2480)(0.903^2) \)

Case 2: Ladder Equilibrium Problem

Scenario: A 50 kg uniform ladder (length 5 m) leans against a frictionless wall at 60° to the horizontal. The ground has friction coefficient μ = 0.4.

Questions:

  1. Find the normal forces at the wall and ground.
  2. Find the friction force at the ground.
  3. Is the ladder stable? (Check if friction is sufficient.)

Solution Hints:

1) For equilibrium: \( \sum F_x = 0 \): \( N_{wall} = f \) and \( \sum F_y = 0 \): \( N_{ground} = 500 \) N

Taking torques about the base: \( N_{wall} \times h = mg \times x \)

where h = 5 cos(60°) = 2.5 m and x = 2.5 sin(60°) = 2.17 m

\( N_{wall} = \frac{500 \times 2.17}{2.5} = 434 \) N

2) f = 434 N

3) Maximum static friction = μN_ground = 0.4 × 500 = 200 N < 434 N

Ladder is NOT stable and will slip!

Case 3: Spinning Disk Energy

Scenario: A solid disk (mass 10 kg, radius 0.5 m) is spun to 1200 rpm. An identical disk, initially at rest, is gently placed on top, and friction brings them to a common angular velocity.

Questions:

  1. Find the initial angular velocity in rad/s.
  2. Calculate the final common angular velocity.
  3. How much kinetic energy is dissipated?

Solution Hints:

1) \( \omega_i = 1200 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 40\pi \) rad/s ≈ 125.7 rad/s

2) \( I = \frac{1}{2}(10)(0.5^2) = 1.25 \) kg·m² for each disk

Conservation: \( (1.25)(40\pi) + (1.25)(0) = (2.5)\omega_f \)

\( \omega_f = 20\pi \) rad/s ≈ 62.8 rad/s

3) \( \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}(1.25)(40\pi)^2 - \frac{1}{2}(2.5)(20\pi)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1.25)(1600\pi^2) - \frac{1}{2}(2.5)(400\pi^2) \)

\( = 1000\pi^2 - 500\pi^2 = 500\pi^2 \approx 4934 \) J dissipated

Key Memory Points

  • Rotational = Linear analogs: τ ↔ F, I ↔ m, ω ↔ v, α ↔ a, L ↔ p
  • Cross product right-hand rule: Point fingers along first vector, curl toward second, thumb is result
  • Angular momentum conserved when: τ_ext = 0 (same as momentum conserved when F_ext = 0)
  • Parallel axis theorem: Use when axis doesn't pass through CM
  • Rolling energy: Always split between translation and rotation (unless sliding)
  • Equilibrium needs both: ΣF = 0 AND Στ = 0 (not just one!)
  • Solid sphere vs ring: Sphere faster downhill because I_sphere < I_ring (same mass, radius)

Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Identify what's being asked: Translational, rotational, or rolling motion?
  2. Draw a diagram: Show all forces and torques clearly
  3. Choose pivot point wisely: For torque, choose a point that simplifies calculations
  4. Apply conservation laws: Energy, momentum, angular momentum when applicable
  5. Use analogies: Rotational problems follow same structure as linear ones
  6. Check units: Torque in N·m, I in kg·m², ω in rad/s
  7. Verify answer makes sense: Does it follow physical intuition?
