Chapter 11: Sound

Exploring the Physics of Vibrations and Waves
Class 9 Science · NCERT Curriculum
When you clap your hands, you create invisible waves that travel through the air at 340 meters per second and reach someone's ear in a fraction of a second. But what IS sound exactly, and why can't we hear it in space? Discover how vibrations transform into the symphony of your world.

1. Production of Sound

Sound is produced when objects vibrate. These vibrations create disturbances in the medium surrounding the object, which eventually reach our ears. Let's explore how different objects produce sound.

Vibrating Objects

Any object that oscillates back and forth around its equilibrium position can produce sound. The key requirement is mechanical vibration.

Key Principle

Sound is produced by vibrating objects. The vibration creates compressions and rarefactions in the medium, forming sound waves that propagate outward.

Common Examples of Sound Production

  • Tuning Fork: When struck, the prongs vibrate at a specific frequency, producing a pure tone
  • Vocal Cords: In humans, air passing through stretched vocal cords causes them to vibrate, producing speech and song
  • Drums: When struck, the membrane vibrates, creating sound waves
  • Guitar Strings: Plucked strings vibrate to produce musical notes
  • Loudspeakers: Electromagnets cause a cone to vibrate, producing sound from electrical signals
Demonstration: Touch a vibrating tuning fork lightly against water. You'll see ripples forming, demonstrating how vibrations disturb the medium. This same principle applies to air—vibrations disturb air molecules to create sound waves.

2. Propagation of Sound

Sound requires a medium to travel. It cannot exist in a vacuum because there are no particles to vibrate and carry the disturbance.

Sound Needs a Medium

Fundamental Rule

Sound cannot travel in a vacuum because there are no particles to transmit vibrations. A medium (solid, liquid, or gas) is absolutely essential for sound propagation.

Speed of Sound in Different Media

Sound travels at different speeds depending on the density and elasticity of the medium. Generally: Solids > Liquids > Gases

Medium Speed of Sound (m/s) Conditions
Air ~344 At 20°C
Water ~1498 At 25°C
Steel ~5960 At room temperature
Concrete ~3850 At room temperature
Hydrogen gas ~1284 At 0°C

Why Speed Varies

Sound travels faster in solids because molecules are more closely packed, allowing vibrations to transfer more quickly. In gases, molecules are far apart, so sound travels slower.

Observation: Have you noticed that if you place your ear on a railway track, you can hear the train approaching much earlier than through the air? This is because sound travels faster through the steel track (~5960 m/s) than through air (~344 m/s).

3. Sound Waves as Longitudinal Waves

Sound waves are longitudinal waves where particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave propagation. This is different from transverse waves (like light) where particles vibrate perpendicular to the direction of propagation.

Longitudinal Wave Characteristics

  • Particles move back and forth along the same direction as wave propagation
  • Composed of alternating regions of compression and rarefaction
  • No crests and troughs (those are for transverse waves)

Compressions and Rarefactions

Compression

A region where particles are pushed together, resulting in high pressure and high density. Sound energy is concentrated in compressions.

Rarefaction

A region where particles are spread apart, resulting in low pressure and low density. These are regions of reduced sound energy.

In a longitudinal wave, one complete compression followed by one complete rarefaction constitutes one complete wavelength.

Analogy: Imagine a spring. If you push one end rapidly back and forth, compressions and rarefactions travel along the spring—similar to how sound waves form in a medium.

4. Characteristics of Sound Waves

Sound waves are defined by several measurable properties that determine their perceptual quality.

Wavelength (λ)

Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive compressions or two consecutive rarefactions.

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

Where v = speed of sound, f = frequency

Frequency (ν or f)

Frequency is the number of vibrations per second. SI unit: Hertz (Hz)

$$\nu = \frac{1}{T}$$

Where T = time period (in seconds)

Amplitude (A)

Definition

Amplitude is the maximum displacement of a particle from its mean (equilibrium) position. Greater amplitude means the wave carries more energy.

Time Period (T)

Time Period is the time taken for one complete oscillation.

$$T = \frac{1}{f}$$

Wave Equation (Relationship)

$$v = \lambda \nu$$

Where v = speed, λ = wavelength, ν = frequency

Pitch, Loudness, and Quality

Property Depends On Meaning
Pitch Frequency How high or low a sound appears. Higher frequency = higher pitch
Loudness Amplitude How loud or soft a sound is. Greater amplitude = louder sound
Quality/Timbre Overtones & harmonics Distinguishes sounds of same pitch and loudness (e.g., piano vs. violin)
Example: A violin and a piano playing the same note have the same pitch (frequency) and can have the same loudness (amplitude), but they sound different due to different quality/timbre.

5. Speed of Sound

The speed of sound depends on properties of the medium through which it travels.

Factors Affecting Speed of Sound

1. Temperature

Sound travels faster in warmer media. In air, for every 1°C increase in temperature, speed increases by approximately 0.61 m/s.

$$v = 331 + 0.6T \text{ m/s (in air)}$$

Where T is temperature in Celsius

2. Nature of Medium

The density and elasticity of the medium determine sound speed. Denser, more elastic materials transmit sound faster.

3. Humidity (in air)

Sound travels fastest in moderately humid air. Extremely dry or saturated air reduces sound speed slightly.

Calculation: At 20°C, speed of sound in air = 331 + 0.6(20) = 331 + 12 = 343 m/s ≈ 344 m/s

6. Reflection of Sound

Sound waves reflect off hard surfaces following the laws of reflection (angle of incidence = angle of reflection). This creates phenomena like echo and reverberation.

Echo

Definition

An echo is a reflected sound heard distinctly after the original sound has ceased. The human ear can distinguish an echo only if there's a time gap of at least 0.1 seconds between the original sound and the reflected sound.

Minimum Distance for Echo

For an echo to be heard, the sound must travel to the reflecting surface and back.

$$d = \frac{v \times t}{2}$$

Where d = distance to surface, v = speed of sound, t = time delay (minimum 0.1 s)

Calculation: At 20°C (v ≈ 344 m/s), minimum distance for echo:
$$d = \frac{344 \times 0.1}{2} = 17.2 \text{ m}$$

So you need to be at least 17.2 meters from a reflecting wall to hear a distinct echo.

Reverberation

Definition

Reverberation is the persistence of sound in an enclosed space due to repeated reflections. Multiple reflections occur so rapidly that they blend together, creating a lingering sound.

Soundproofing

To reduce unwanted reflections and reverberation, soft, porous materials like foam, carpets, and curtains are used. These materials absorb sound energy rather than reflecting it.

Real-world Application: Concert halls are designed with curved ceilings and special absorptive materials to balance reverberation—enough to enrich the sound, but not so much that it becomes muddled.

7. Range of Hearing

Different organisms can hear sounds in different frequency ranges. Understanding these ranges helps us identify applications and limitations.

Human Hearing Range

Normal Human Hearing

Humans can typically hear frequencies from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz). This range varies with age—as we age, the upper limit decreases.

Infrasound

Definition

Infrasound refers to sound waves with frequencies below 20 Hz. Humans cannot hear infrasound, but many animals can.

Sources of Infrasound:

  • Elephant communication (frequencies as low as 5 Hz)
  • Earthquakes and volcanic eruptions
  • Ocean waves and storms
  • Whale calls

Ultrasound

Definition

Ultrasound refers to sound waves with frequencies above 20,000 Hz (20 kHz). Humans cannot hear ultrasound, but it has many applications.

Animals that use ultrasound:

  • Bats: Use echolocation with ultrasound (20 kHz to 200 kHz) for navigation and hunting
  • Dolphins: Use ultrasound (40 kHz to 130 kHz) for communication and echolocation
  • Dogs: Can hear up to 40-60 kHz
Frequency Range Category Human Audible? Examples
Below 20 Hz Infrasound No Elephants, earthquakes
20 Hz - 20 kHz Audible Sound Yes Music, speech, environmental sounds
Above 20 kHz Ultrasound No Bats, dolphins, medical imaging

8. Applications of Ultrasound

Ultrasound has become invaluable in numerous fields due to its unique properties and ability to penetrate matter.

SONAR (Sound Navigation and Ranging)

Principle

SONAR uses ultrasound waves to detect and locate objects underwater. A transmitter sends ultrasound waves, which reflect off objects, and the time delay helps calculate distance.

Distance calculation in SONAR:

$$d = \frac{v \times t}{2}$$

Where d = distance, v = speed of sound in water, t = time for sound to travel to object and back

Applications of SONAR:

  • Naval vessels for detection of submarines and underwater objects
  • Fishing: locating fish schools
  • Oceanographic surveys and mapping
  • Underwater archaeology

Medical Applications

Echocardiography

Ultrasound imaging of the heart to detect abnormalities in structure and function.

Ultrasonography (Sonography)

General ultrasound imaging used to visualize internal organs, detect tumors, monitor fetal development, and assess soft tissue injuries.

Advantages:

  • Non-invasive procedure
  • No radiation exposure (unlike X-rays)
  • Real-time imaging capability
  • Cost-effective

Industrial Applications

Crack Detection

Ultrasound is used to detect internal cracks and defects in metal components without damaging them. The presence of cracks causes changes in ultrasound reflection patterns.

Cleaning

Ultrasonic cleaners vibrate objects at very high frequencies (typically 40 kHz), creating cavitation bubbles that dislodge dirt and contaminants. Used for:

  • Jewelry and watch cleaning
  • Industrial parts cleaning
  • Dental and surgical instrument sterilization
Clinical Example: During pregnancy, ultrasound safely allows doctors to visualize the fetus, measure its development, detect multiple pregnancies, and identify certain abnormalities—all without the risks associated with radiation.

9. Structure of Human Ear

The human ear is a sophisticated sensory organ comprising three main parts that work together to convert sound waves into electrical signals interpreted by the brain.

Outer Ear

Pinna (Auricle)

The visible, funnel-shaped part of the ear that collects sound waves and directs them into the ear canal.

Ear Canal (External Auditory Meatus)

A tube approximately 2.5 cm long that channels sound waves toward the eardrum. It also has hair and cerumen (earwax) that protect against dust and insects.

Middle Ear

Eardrum (Tympanum)

A thin membrane that vibrates when sound waves strike it. These vibrations are then transmitted to the three tiny bones in the middle ear.

Three Bones of the Middle Ear (Ossicles)

  • Hammer (Malleus): Attached to the eardrum; transmits vibrations to the anvil
  • Anvil (Incus): Middle bone that transfers vibrations from hammer to stirrup
  • Stirrup (Stapes): Transmits vibrations to the inner ear fluid

These three bones work as mechanical levers, amplifying sound vibrations by about 30 times.

Eustachian Tube

Connects the middle ear to the throat, allowing air pressure to equalize on both sides of the eardrum. When you yawn or swallow, this tube opens.

Inner Ear

Cochlea

A spiral-shaped, fluid-filled structure containing thousands of hair cells. When vibrations reach the cochlea, these hair cells convert mechanical vibrations into electrical signals through a process called transduction.

Hair Cells

Specialized sensory cells within the cochlea that bend when stimulated by vibrations, creating electrical impulses that encode the frequency and intensity of sound.

Auditory Nerve

Carries electrical signals from hair cells to the brain's auditory cortex, where sound is interpreted and processed, allowing us to perceive sound.

The Sound Journey Through the Ear

  1. Sound waves collected by pinna
  2. Waves travel through ear canal
  3. Waves strike and vibrate the eardrum
  4. Vibrations pass through ossicles (amplified ~30x)
  5. Vibrations reach the cochlea
  6. Hair cells convert vibrations to electrical signals
  7. Auditory nerve transmits signals to brain
  8. Brain interprets signals as sound
Clinical Note: Hearing loss can result from problems at any stage—from cerumen impaction blocking the ear canal, to damaged ossicles, to hair cell loss in the cochlea, to auditory nerve damage.

Higher-Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

Challenge yourself with these deeper, analytical questions.

HOTS 1: Sound Speed Variation

Question: If sound travels faster in denser media (like steel vs. air), why doesn't it travel at infinite speed in the densest materials? What other factor besides density might be important?

Key Insight: While density matters, elasticity (how well the medium springs back) is equally important. A denser but rigid material may not transmit vibrations efficiently. The speed depends on both density and elasticity: v = √(Elasticity/Density). An extremely dense but brittle material might actually conduct sound slower than expected.

HOTS 2: Echo vs. Reverberation

Question: In a large cathedral, you might hear distinct echoes, but in a small bedroom, you don't. Yet both rooms have sound reflections. Explain the difference and why it occurs.

Key Insight: The time gap between the original sound and its reflection determines perception. In large spaces, the time delay is >0.1 s, allowing the ear to distinguish the echo. In small spaces, reflections return in <0.1 s, blending with the original sound as reverberation. The ear's temporal resolution (~0.1 s) creates this distinction.

HOTS 3: SONAR and Doppler Effect

Question: A submarine moving toward a SONAR source receives ultrasound. How might the frequency received differ from the transmitted frequency, and why? How could this help detect submarine motion?

Key Insight: This is the Doppler effect. As the submarine approaches, sound wave compression increases, raising the observed frequency. If moving away, frequency decreases. By comparing transmitted and received frequencies, SONAR can determine not just distance but also velocity and direction of motion—crucial for naval operations.

Case-Based Questions

Apply your knowledge to real-world scenarios.

Case 1: Stadium Acoustics

Scenario: A large stadium has a problem: speeches are difficult to understand because of excessive reverberation. The sound bounces around, creating echoes that overlap with the original speech.

Q1a. Why does a large stadium experience more reverberation than a small room?

Answer: Larger spaces have more surfaces and greater distances for sound to travel, creating multiple reflections that overlap in time.

Q1b. Design a solution using materials and arrangement principles to reduce reverberation.

Answer: Install soft, porous materials (foam panels, carpets, curtains) on walls and ceiling. These absorb sound energy rather than reflecting it, reducing reverberation. Also, limit hard, parallel surfaces that create standing waves.

Q1c. How would adding these materials affect the loudness of desired sounds?

Answer: The desired sounds would be slightly quieter due to absorption, but clarity improves dramatically. Modern stadiums use sophisticated acoustic engineering to balance these trade-offs.

Case 2: Prenatal Ultrasound Monitoring

Scenario: A pregnant woman undergoes an ultrasound scan to monitor fetal development. The doctor uses a frequency of 2 MHz (2,000,000 Hz) ultrasound.

Q2a. Why is ultrasound preferred over X-rays for fetal imaging?

Answer: Ultrasound is non-ionizing (no radiation), making it safe for the developing fetus. X-rays are ionizing and can damage cells and DNA.

Q2b. Calculate the wavelength of the ultrasound in soft tissue (speed ≈ 1540 m/s).

Answer: Using v = λf, λ = v/f = 1540 / 2,000,000 = 0.00077 m = 0.77 mm. This small wavelength allows detailed imaging of small structures.

Q2c. Explain how the echo principle is used to create an image.

Answer: The transducer sends ultrasound into tissue. Different tissues reflect ultrasound differently based on density differences. The reflected echoes are captured, and the time delay determines depth. Processing these signals creates a 2D image of internal structures.

Case 3: Whale Communication in the Ocean

Scenario: Blue whales communicate across vast ocean distances using low-frequency sounds (around 12 Hz to 25 Hz). These are infrasound frequencies.

Q3a. Why can whales communicate across such large distances underwater?

Answer: Sound travels faster and farther in water than in air due to water's greater density. Low-frequency sounds have long wavelengths that can travel hundreds or thousands of kilometers with less attenuation.

Q3b. Calculate the wavelength of a 20 Hz whale call in seawater (speed ≈ 1500 m/s).

Answer: λ = v/f = 1500 / 20 = 75 m. This enormous wavelength explains why the sound can propagate so far—long waves diffract around obstacles effectively.

Q3c. Explain why humans cannot hear whale communication but other whales can.

Answer: Human hearing range is 20 Hz to 20 kHz. Whale infrasound below 20 Hz falls outside our hearing range. Whale ears are adapted to detect these frequencies, and they likely perceive the vibrations differently than we do with sound.

Remediation & Common Misconceptions

Address frequently misunderstood concepts to strengthen your foundation.

Misconception 1: Sound travels faster in air than in water

Reality: Sound travels about 4.4 times faster in water (1498 m/s) than in air (344 m/s). This is because water molecules are more densely packed, allowing vibrations to transmit more efficiently.

Misconception 2: Sound can travel in space

Reality: Sound requires a medium. Space is nearly a perfect vacuum with very few particles, so sound cannot propagate. This is why astronauts use radios for communication, not speaking directly to each other.

Misconception 3: Frequency and wavelength are independent

Reality: Frequency and wavelength are inversely related through the wave equation v = λf. In a given medium, higher frequency means shorter wavelength. This relationship is fixed by the speed of sound in that medium.

Misconception 4: All sounds above 20 kHz are harmful

Reality: Ultrasound itself is not inherently harmful. Medical ultrasound at diagnostic frequencies is safe. Harmful effects depend on intensity and exposure time. High-intensity ultrasound can cause heating and cavitation damage, but diagnostic ultrasound is low-intensity.

Misconception 5: Pitch and loudness are the same thing

Reality: These are distinct properties. Pitch depends on frequency, while loudness depends on amplitude. You can have a high-pitched soft sound or a low-pitched loud sound—they're independent characteristics.

Misconception 6: The eardrum directly processes sound signals

Reality: The eardrum is a mechanical structure that vibrates and transmits mechanical energy to the ossicles. The actual signal processing happens in the cochlea, where hair cells convert vibrations to electrical signals interpreted by the brain.

Misconception 7: Temperature doesn't significantly affect sound speed

Reality: Temperature is a major factor. Speed increases by about 0.6 m/s per degree Celsius increase. A 20°C difference can change sound speed by ~12 m/s, which is significant for calculations.
