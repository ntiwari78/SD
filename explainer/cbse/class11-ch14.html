Chapter 14: Waves

The Final Frontier of Class 11 Physics

"What is a wave, really? It's not matter traveling through space—it's disturbance traveling through space. Energy without substance."

1. Types of Waves

Waves are disturbances that propagate through a medium (or space), transferring energy without transferring matter.

Classification by Particle Motion

  • Transverse Waves: Particles oscillate perpendicular to wave direction
    • Examples: Light on a string, electromagnetic waves, water surface waves
    • Features: Crests (maximum displacement upward), Troughs (maximum displacement downward)
    • Polarization: Can be polarized because of perpendicular oscillation
  • Longitudinal Waves: Particles oscillate parallel to wave direction
    • Examples: Sound waves in air, seismic P-waves, spring compressions
    • Features: Compressions (particles close together), Rarefactions (particles far apart)
    • Cannot be polarized

Classification by Medium Requirement

  • Mechanical Waves: Require a medium (string, air, water)
    • Speed depends on medium properties
    • Examples: Sound, seismic waves, waves on strings
  • Electromagnetic Waves: Don't require a medium, propagate through vacuum
    • Speed in vacuum = c = 3×10⁸ m/s (constant for all frequencies)
    • Are transverse waves
    • Examples: Light, radio, microwaves, X-rays
  • Matter Waves: Associated with moving particles (de Broglie waves)
    • All particles have wave-like properties
    • Wavelength λ = h/p (de Broglie relation)

2. Displacement Relation and Wave Parameters

Wave Equation

The displacement of a particle in a sinusoidal wave as a function of position and time:

Displacement Relation (Sinusoidal Wave)
$$y(x,t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi)$$

where: A = amplitude, k = wave number, ω = angular frequency, φ = initial phase

Key Wave Parameters

Parameter Definition Formula Unit
Wavelength (λ) Distance between successive crests (or troughs) λ m
Period (T) Time for one complete oscillation T s
Frequency (ν) Number of oscillations per unit time ν = 1/T Hz
Wave Number (k) Number of waves per unit length (2π version) k = 2π/λ rad/m
Angular Frequency (ω) Radians per unit time ω = 2π/T = 2πν rad/s
Wave Speed (v) Speed at which wave pattern propagates v = λν = ω/k = λ/T m/s

Relationships

Fundamental Wave Relations
$$v = \lambda \nu = \frac{\lambda}{T} = \frac{\omega}{k}$$ $$\omega = 2\pi \nu = \frac{2\pi}{T}$$ $$k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$$
Example: A wave has frequency 50 Hz and wavelength 6 m. Find the wave speed.

Solution: v = λν = 6 × 50 = 300 m/s

3. Speed of Waves

Speed of Transverse Wave on a String

The speed depends on the tension in the string and its linear mass density:

Speed on String
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}$$

where: T = tension (N), μ = linear mass density = M/L (kg/m)

Important Points About String Waves

  • Speed increases with tension (tighter string → faster wave)
  • Speed decreases with mass density (heavier string → slower wave)
  • Speed is independent of frequency and independent of amplitude
Example: A string of mass 2 kg and length 10 m has tension 500 N. Find wave speed.

μ = 2/10 = 0.2 kg/m

v = √(500/0.2) = √2500 = 50 m/s

Speed of Sound in Different Media

Newton's Formula (Isothermal Process)

Newton's Speed of Sound
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{B}{\rho}}$$

where: B = bulk modulus (Pa), ρ = density (kg/m³)

Laplace's Correction (Adiabatic Process)

Newton's formula underestimated sound speed. Laplace corrected it:

Laplace's Speed of Sound
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}}$$

where: γ = heat capacity ratio (Cp/Cv), P = pressure, ρ = density

Speed of Sound in Various Media at 0°C

Medium Speed (m/s) Nature
Air (0°C) 331 m/s Mechanical, Longitudinal
Water (25°C) 1500 m/s Mechanical, Longitudinal
Steel 5000 m/s Mechanical, Transverse & Longitudinal
Aluminum 6000 m/s Mechanical, Transverse & Longitudinal

Effect of Temperature on Sound Speed

Temperature Dependence
$$v_T = v_0 \sqrt{1 + \frac{T}{273}}$$

Approximately: v ∝ √T (where T is in Kelvin)
For every 1°C increase: v increases by ~0.61 m/s

Critical Point: Pressure Independence

At constant temperature, sound speed is independent of pressure. This is because both density and bulk modulus change proportionally with pressure, canceling out in the formula.

Example: Find speed of sound at 27°C (using v₀ = 331 m/s at 0°C).

T = 27°C = 300 K, T₀ = 0°C = 273 K

v = 331 × √(300/273) = 331 × 1.048 ≈ 347 m/s

4. Principle of Superposition and Interference

Superposition Principle

When two or more waves travel through the same region, the net displacement is the vector sum of individual displacements:

Superposition of Waves
$$y = y_1 + y_2 + y_3 + ...$$

Interference

When waves overlap, they can reinforce each other (constructive) or cancel each other (destructive).

Constructive Interference

  • Waves add up to create larger amplitude
  • Occurs when phase difference: Δφ = 2nπ (where n = 0, 1, 2, ...)
  • Path difference: Δx = nλ
  • Result: Amplitude = A₁ + A₂ (for equal amplitudes: A_max = 2A)

Destructive Interference

  • Waves cancel each other partially or completely
  • Occurs when phase difference: Δφ = (2n+1)π
  • Path difference: Δx = (2n+1)λ/2
  • Result: Amplitude = |A₁ - A₂| (for equal amplitudes: A_min = 0)

General Interference Formula

Resultant Amplitude for Two Waves
$$A = \sqrt{A_1^2 + A_2^2 + 2A_1A_2\cos(\Delta\phi)}$$

where: Δφ = phase difference between the waves

Coherent and Incoherent Sources

  • Coherent sources: Constant phase difference, produce stable interference patterns (e.g., two slits with same light source)
  • Incoherent sources: Random phase difference, no stable pattern (e.g., two independent light bulbs)

5. Reflection of Waves and Standing Waves

Reflection at Boundaries

Fixed End (Rigid Support)

  • Wave undergoes phase reversal (change by π radians)
  • Reflected wave inverted compared to incident wave
  • Node always forms at fixed end

Free End

  • Wave reflects with no phase change
  • Reflected wave in phase with incident wave
  • Antinode always forms at free end

Standing Waves

When incident and reflected waves interfere, standing waves form—waves that don't travel, with fixed nodes and antinodes.

Standing Wave Equation
$$y(x,t) = 2A\sin(kx)\cos(\omega t)$$

This comes from superposition of incident and reflected waves with phase reversal

Nodes and Antinodes

  • Nodes: Points of zero displacement always (y = 0 for all t)
    • Occur where sin(kx) = 0
    • Position: x = nλ/2 (n = 0, 1, 2, ...)
    • Energy = 0 (no kinetic or potential energy)
  • Antinodes: Points of maximum displacement (maximum amplitude)
    • Occur where sin(kx) = ±1
    • Position: x = (2n+1)λ/4 (n = 0, 1, 2, ...)
    • Energy maximum
  • Separation between consecutive nodes (or antinodes): λ/2

Standing Waves on String Fixed at Both Ends

The string is fixed at both ends, forcing nodes at both boundaries.

Wavelengths for nth Harmonic
$$\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n} \quad \text{(n = 1, 2, 3, ...)}$$ $$\nu_n = \frac{nv}{2L} = n\nu_1 \quad \text{(harmonics)}$$

where: L = length of string, ν₁ = fundamental frequency, v = wave speed

Resonance and Harmonics

  • Fundamental (1st harmonic): n = 1, single antinode in middle, λ₁ = 2L, ν₁ = v/2L
  • 2nd harmonic: n = 2, two antinodes, λ₂ = L, ν₂ = v/L = 2ν₁
  • 3rd harmonic: n = 3, three antinodes, λ₃ = 2L/3, ν₃ = 3v/2L = 3ν₁
  • General: The frequency ratio of harmonics = 1:2:3:4:...

Pipes (Open and Closed)

Open Pipe (Both Ends Open)

Antinodes at both ends (pressure antinodes)

Open Pipe (Both Ends Open)
$$\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n} \quad \text{(n = 1, 2, 3, ...)}$$ $$\nu_n = \frac{nv}{2L} \quad \text{(all harmonics present)}$$

Closed Pipe (One End Closed)

Node at closed end, antinode at open end

Closed Pipe (One End Closed)
$$\lambda_n = \frac{4L}{n} \quad \text{(n = 1, 3, 5, ... only odd n)}$$ $$\nu_n = \frac{nv}{4L} \quad \text{(only odd harmonics present)}$$

Comparison Table: Harmonics in Different Systems

System Boundary Conditions Wavelength Formula Frequency Formula Harmonics Present
String (Both Fixed) Node-Node λₙ = 2L/n νₙ = nv/2L 1, 2, 3, 4, ... (all)
Open Pipe Antinode-Antinode λₙ = 2L/n νₙ = nv/2L 1, 2, 3, 4, ... (all)
Closed Pipe Node-Antinode λₙ = 4L/n νₙ = nv/4L 1, 3, 5, 7, ... (odd only)
Example: A string of length 1 m with wave speed 400 m/s. Find first three harmonic frequencies.

ν₁ = v/2L = 400/(2×1) = 200 Hz
ν₂ = 2ν₁ = 400 Hz
ν₃ = 3ν₁ = 600 Hz

6. Beats

What are Beats?

When two waves of slightly different frequencies interfere, the intensity varies periodically. This variation is called beats—a "wah-wah-wah" sound in acoustics.

Beat Frequency

Beat Frequency
$$\nu_{beat} = |\nu_1 - \nu_2|$$

The number of intensity maxima (or minima) heard per second

Beat Period

Beat Period
$$T_{beat} = \frac{1}{\nu_{beat}} = \frac{1}{|\nu_1 - \nu_2|}$$

Mathematical Explanation

For two waves: y₁ = A sin(2πν₁t) and y₂ = A sin(2πν₂t)

The superposition gives beats with envelope modulation:

Beat Wave Form
$$y = 2A\cos(2\pi\frac{\nu_1-\nu_2}{2}t)\sin(2\pi\frac{\nu_1+\nu_2}{2}t)$$

The amplitude varies as: 2A cos(2π(ν₁-ν₂)t/2)

Practical Applications

  • Tuning instruments: Compare tone with reference frequency, adjust until beats disappear (equal frequency)
  • Frequency measurement: Use beat frequency with known reference
  • Heterodyne detection: In radio and electronics
Example: Two tuning forks have frequencies 256 Hz and 260 Hz. Find beat frequency and beat period.

νbeat = |260 - 256| = 4 Hz
Tbeat = 1/4 = 0.25 s (4 beats heard per second)

7. Doppler Effect

What is the Doppler Effect?

The apparent change in frequency (or wavelength) of a wave when the source or observer moves relative to the medium.

General Doppler Formula

Doppler Effect (General Formula)
$$\nu' = \nu \frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s}$$

where: ν = source frequency, v = wave speed in medium
v₀ = observer velocity, vₛ = source velocity
Upper signs when moving toward each other
Lower signs when moving away from each other

Special Cases

1. Observer Moving, Source Stationary

Observer Moving Toward Stationary Source
$$\nu' = \nu\left(1 + \frac{v_o}{v}\right)$$
Observer Moving Away From Stationary Source
$$\nu' = \nu\left(1 - \frac{v_o}{v}\right)$$

2. Source Moving, Observer Stationary

Source Moving Toward Stationary Observer
$$\nu' = \nu\frac{v}{v - v_s}$$
Source Moving Away From Stationary Observer
$$\nu' = \nu\frac{v}{v + v_s}$$

3. Both Moving

Both Source and Observer Moving
$$\nu' = \nu\frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s}$$

Use positive velocities toward each other, negative away

Important Points

  • Doppler effect only depends on relative motion along the line joining source and observer
  • Motion perpendicular to this line produces no Doppler shift
  • Wavelength also changes: λ' = v/ν'
  • Speed of wave in medium is unchanged (Doppler shifts frequency, not wave speed)
  • For v >> vₛ: ν' ≈ ν(1 ± vₛ/v) [approximate formula]

Worked Examples

Example 1: A car horn (320 Hz) approaches you at 20 m/s. Speed of sound = 340 m/s. Find apparent frequency.

ν' = ν × v/(v - vₛ) = 320 × 340/(340 - 20)
ν' = 320 × 340/320 = 340 Hz

Example 2: You approach a stationary siren (500 Hz) at 10 m/s. Speed of sound = 340 m/s.

ν' = ν(1 + v₀/v) = 500(1 + 10/340)
ν' = 500(1 + 0.0294) = 500 × 1.0294 ≈ 515 Hz

Example 3: A train whistle (600 Hz) moves away from you at 30 m/s. Speed of sound = 340 m/s.

ν' = ν × v/(v + vₛ) = 600 × 340/(340 + 30)
ν' = 600 × 340/370 ≈ 551 Hz

Applications of Doppler Effect

  • Police radar guns: Measure vehicle speed using microwave Doppler shift
  • Medical ultrasound: Doppler imaging to measure blood flow velocity
  • Astronomy: Redshift (z) indicates recession of galaxies, suggests expanding universe
  • Astrophysics: Determine orbital velocities of binary stars
  • Seismology: Analyze earthquake wave patterns
  • Vibration analysis: Non-destructive testing

Redshift and Blueshift

  • Redshift: Source moving away → frequency decreases → wavelength increases (toward red end)
  • Blueshift: Source moving toward → frequency increases → wavelength decreases (toward blue end)

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS Question 1: Wave Impedance and Energy

Scenario: Two strings with different densities are joined together. A wave travels from the lighter string to the heavier string.

Questions:

  1. Explain why the wave speed changes at the junction.
  2. What happens to the wavelength? Does it increase or decrease?
  3. Using energy conservation, explain why some energy is reflected and some is transmitted.
  4. If the strings have equal tension, which string transmits the wave faster? Why?

Concept tested: Relationship between tension, density, and wave speed; energy conservation; boundary conditions.

HOTS Question 2: Resonance in Pipes

Scenario: You have a closed pipe and an open pipe, both of length 1 m. A tuning fork of frequency 170 Hz excites both pipes.

Questions:

  1. Which harmonics are resonantly excited in the closed pipe? (Assume v = 340 m/s)
  2. What is the resonance frequency of the first harmonic in the open pipe?
  3. Why does a closed pipe produce only odd harmonics while an open pipe produces all harmonics?
  4. Design an experiment to distinguish between the acoustic output of a closed and open pipe.

Concept tested: Boundary conditions; harmonic series; resonance conditions; experimental design.

HOTS Question 3: Doppler Effect and Astrophysics

Scenario: Astronomers observe a distant galaxy emitting light at wavelength 500 nm (in lab frame). Due to cosmic expansion, the observed wavelength is 750 nm.

Questions:

  1. Calculate the redshift parameter z = (λ_observed - λ_lab)/λ_lab
  2. For non-relativistic speeds, the recession velocity is v ≈ cz. Estimate the recession velocity.
  3. Explain how Doppler effect supports the Big Bang theory.
  4. Why is it a "redshift" and not "blueshift"? What does this tell us about the universe?

Concept tested: Doppler formula; redshift; cosmology; astrophysics applications.

Background: A violin string has length 32 cm, mass 0.6 g, and is tensioned to 100 N.

  2. Find the fundamental frequency (first harmonic).
  3. What are the frequencies of the first three harmonics?

Case Study 2: Traffic Speed Detection

Background: Police use radar guns operating at 10 GHz. The radar sends out electromagnetic waves that reflect off a car and return. Doppler shift of the reflected wave indicates car speed.

Questions:

  1. If a car approaches at 30 m/s, what is the frequency shift of the reflected wave? (c = 3×10⁸ m/s)
  2. Explain why the radar gun receives a "double Doppler shift" (once on the way out, once on reflection).
  3. How would the reading change if the car is moving away?
  4. Why is EM radiation preferred over sound waves for this application?

Case Study 3: Seismic Wave Analysis

Background: Seismologists study earthquake waves: P-waves (longitudinal, v ≈ 6 km/s) and S-waves (transverse, v ≈ 3.5 km/s).

Questions:

  1. Which wave arrives first at a seismic station? Why?
  2. If P and S waves are detected 20 seconds apart, estimate the epicenter distance.
  3. Explain how P and S waves provide information about Earth's internal structure.
  4. Why can P waves travel through Earth's liquid outer core but S waves cannot?
