Improvement in Food Resources

Class 8 · Chapter 12 · CBSE Science
Why does India need to produce more food? How can we grow crops more efficiently without harming the environment? Let's explore the science of sustainable agriculture and animal farming!
1
Need for Improvement in Food Resources

Why Do We Need Food Security?

India's population is constantly growing, and with it, the demand for food is increasing at an unprecedented rate. Food security means ensuring that every person has consistent access to enough nutritious food to lead an active, healthy life.

Real-Life Analogy: Imagine your family grows every year, but your harvest stays the same. Soon, you won't have enough to feed everyone. That's India's challenge! We need to produce MORE food efficiently.

India achieved major food production milestones through two major revolutions:

  • Green Revolution (1960s onward): Focus on increasing food grain production through improved seeds, fertilizers, and irrigation
  • White Revolution (1970s onward): Focus on increasing milk production by improving dairy farming

However, we must balance production with environmental sustainability to protect soil, water, and air quality for future generations.

Major Food Sources

Our food comes from different plant sources, each providing important nutrients:

Food Source Main Nutrients Examples
Cereals Carbohydrates (energy) Rice, wheat, maize, jowar
Pulses Proteins (building blocks) Gram, peas, lentils, beans
Oil Seeds Fats & oils Mustard, groundnut, sunflower
Vegetables & Fruits Vitamins & minerals Spinach, carrots, oranges, apples

Crop Types: Kharif and Rabi

India's agriculture is seasonal. Two main crop seasons are:

Kharif Crops (Monsoon Season: June-October) ▼

These crops are planted during the monsoon when rainfall is abundant:

  • Paddy (rice)
  • Soyabean
  • Pigeon pea
  • Maize (corn)
  • Cotton
  • Green gram & Black gram

Think of it as "rain-fed farming" - the monsoon provides the water!

Rabi Crops (Winter Season: November-April) ▼

These crops require cool temperatures and less water:

  • Wheat
  • Gram (chickpea)
  • Peas
  • Mustard
  • Linseed

These depend on irrigation systems or stored soil moisture.

Fodder Crops: Special crops grown to feed livestock. Examples: Berseem, oats, and Sudan grass. These are crucial for dairy and livestock farming!
2
Crop Variety Improvement

What is Hybridisation?

HybridisationCrossing two genetically different plants to combine their best traits is the process of crossing two genetically different plants to produce offspring (hybrids) that combine the desirable traits of both parents.

Easy Analogy: Think of it like mixing two recipes to make a better dish. If one recipe has great taste but the other is healthier, a hybrid combines both strengths!

There are three main types of hybridisation:

  • Intervarietal Hybridisation: Crossing different varieties of the SAME species (e.g., two types of wheat)
  • Interspecific Hybridisation: Crossing different SPECIES within the same genus (e.g., wheat × barley)
  • Intergeneric Hybridisation: Crossing DIFFERENT GENERA - this is rare but possible (e.g., wheat × rye)

Genetically Modified (GM) Crops

Instead of traditional hybridisation, scientists can directly introduce specific genes from one organism to another using genetic engineering. This creates genetically modified (GM) crops.

Example: Bt-Cotton has a gene from the bacterium Bacillus thuringiensis that produces a toxin harmful to certain insect pests, so farmers need fewer pesticides!

Advantage: GM crops can solve specific problems quickly, but we must test them thoroughly for safety before use.

Goals of Crop Variety Improvement

When developing new crop varieties, scientists aim for:

Higher Yield

More grain per plant

Better Quality

Taste, nutrition, color

Disease Resistance

Resist biotic factors

Pest Resistance

Survive insect attacks

Stress Tolerance

Handle drought/heat

Wider Adaptability

Grow in various regions

Photoperiod: Sunlight Duration

PhotoperiodThe duration of daylight in a 24-hour period refers to the duration of sunlight in a day. Some crops flower only after receiving a specific photoperiod!

  • Short-day crops: Flower when day length is SHORT (e.g., rice, maize)
  • Long-day crops: Flower when day length is LONG (e.g., wheat)
  • Day-neutral crops: Flower regardless of day length (e.g., tomato, cotton)

This is why different crops are grown in different seasons!

3
Crop Production Management

a) Nutrient Management

Plants need various nutrients to grow. These come from three main sources:

Source Elements Provided How Plants Get It
Air Carbon (C), Oxygen (O) Through leaves via photosynthesis
Water Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O) Absorbed by roots
Soil Macro & micronutrients Roots absorb minerals

Macronutrients (needed in large amounts):

  • Nitrogen (N) - builds proteins, makes leaves green
  • Phosphorus (P) - helps roots develop & flowers bloom
  • Potassium (K) - strengthens plant structure & fruit quality
  • Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), Sulfur (S)

Micronutrients (needed in tiny amounts but essential):

  • Iron (Fe), Manganese (Mn), Boron (B), Zinc (Zn), Copper (Cu), Molybdenum (Mo), Chlorine (Cl)
Remember: An NPK fertilizer ratio of 10:26:26 means 10% Nitrogen, 26% Phosphorus, 26% Potassium. Farmers choose different ratios based on what their soil lacks!

Manure vs. Fertilizer

Aspect Manure Fertilizer
Source Decomposed plant/animal waste Commercially manufactured
Nutrients Small amounts of all nutrients High concentration of specific nutrients
Cost Low (organic waste) High (chemical processing)
Soil Impact Improves soil structure & holds moisture Can damage soil if overused
Environment Sustainable & eco-friendly Runoff pollutes water bodies
Types of Manure ▼
  • Compost: Decomposed plant waste (leaves, crop stalks) - takes 3-6 months
  • Vermi-compost: Decomposed waste broken down by earthworms - faster & nutrient-rich
  • Green Manure: Crops grown specifically to be ploughed into the soil to increase nitrogen (e.g., legumes like clover)
Organic Farming: Uses manure instead of chemical fertilizers. Also uses natural pest control (neem leaves), bio-agents, and crop rotation. It's sustainable but produces lower yields initially!

b) Irrigation Management

Most Indian agriculture depends on monsoon rains, but we also need irrigation systems to water crops during dry seasons.

Irrigation Systems:

  • Wells: Dug wells (shallow) and tube wells (deep) - draw groundwater
  • Canals: Distribute water from rivers or dams over large areas
  • River Lift Systems: Pumps lift water from rivers to fields
  • Tanks/Ponds: Store water during monsoon for use in dry season

Modern Techniques:

  • Rainwater Harvesting: Capture and store rainwater for later use
  • Watershed Management: Conserve water and prevent runoff through check dams and terracing
Water Management Analogy: Think of your home - you collect water when it rains, store it, and use it when needed. Farmers do the same on a larger scale!

c) Cropping Patterns

Farmers use different strategies to maximize productivity and maintain soil health:

Mixed Cropping ▼

Growing two or more crops simultaneously in the same field without a definite row pattern.

Example: Wheat + Gram grown together

Benefit: If one crop fails, the other survives. Better soil utilization.

Inter-cropping ▼

Growing two crops in a specific row pattern with definite spacing.

Example: Soyabean + Maize (tall corn provides shade, nitrogen-fixing soyabean enriches soil)

Benefit: Better resource utilization, pest control, higher total yield.

Crop Rotation ▼

Planting different crops in a pre-planned sequence on the same field in different seasons/years.

Example: Year 1: Wheat, Year 2: Gram (legume), Year 3: Cotton

Benefits:

  • Breaks pest and disease cycles (they can't build up)
  • Legumes restore nitrogen to soil
  • Reduces need for fertilizers
  • Maintains soil fertility naturally
4
Crop Protection Management

What Threatens Crops?

Threat Type Examples What They Do
Weeds Xanthium, Parthenium, Cyperus rotundus Compete with crops for water, nutrients, and light
Insect Pests Locusts, stem-borers, aphids Cut roots/stems, suck sap, bore into fruits
Diseases Bacteria, fungi, viruses Cause yellowing, wilting, rot, reduced yield

Control Methods

1. Chemical Control (Pesticides) ▼
  • Herbicides: Kill weeds (e.g., 2,4-D)
  • Insecticides: Kill insects (e.g., DDT, malathion)
  • Fungicides: Kill fungi (e.g., Bordeaux mixture)

Pros: Fast and effective

Cons: Expensive, harm environment, pesticide residues in food, kill beneficial insects

2. Mechanical Control ▼
  • Hand-pulling weeds
  • Using nets to catch insects
  • Removing infected plant parts

Pros: Safe, environment-friendly, free/low-cost

Cons: Labor-intensive, time-consuming

3. Biological Control (Bio-agents) ▼
  • Use natural predators (e.g., ladybugs eat aphids)
  • Use parasites (e.g., wasps parasitize caterpillars)
  • Use neem leaves (repel many insects naturally)

Pros: Safe, sustainable, no chemical residues

Cons: Slower, less predictable results

Preventive Measures

The best strategy is prevention! Here are smart practices:

  • Proper seed bed preparation: Remove old crop residues where pests hide
  • Timely sowing: Plant at the right season to avoid peak pest activity
  • Inter-cropping: Mixed crops confuse pests
  • Crop rotation: Breaks pest cycles
  • Use resistant varieties: Grow crops bred to resist specific diseases
  • Summer ploughing: Expose soil to sun, killing dormant pests and diseases
Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Combines all three methods - chemical (when necessary), mechanical, and biological - for the best results with minimal environmental impact.
5
Storage of Grains

Why Storage Matters

After harvesting, grains must be stored properly. If not, significant losses occur:

  • Quality loss: Taste, color, nutrition decrease
  • Weight loss: Moisture evaporation
  • Germinability loss: Seeds lose ability to sprout
  • Pest/disease damage: Complete crop destruction

Factors Causing Storage Loss

Biotic Factors (living organisms):

  • Insects (e.g., grain borers)
  • Rodents (rats, mice)
  • Fungi (cause mold & rot)
  • Mites (tiny arthropods)
  • Bacteria (cause spoilage)

Abiotic Factors (environmental conditions):

  • Moisture (excess humidity promotes fungal growth)
  • Temperature (high heat accelerates decay)
Storage Analogy: Storing grain is like keeping food in your refrigerator - you need the right temperature and moisture level to prevent spoilage!

Prevention Methods

Cleaning

Remove dust, debris, damaged grains before storage

Drying

Reduce moisture content to 10-12% (sun-drying)

Fumigation

Use toxic gases to kill insects & pests

Proper Warehousing

Cool, dry, ventilated storage with sealed containers

6
Animal Husbandry: Sustainable Farming

a) Cattle Farming

Cattle provide both milk (dairy) and farm labor. There are two main types in India:

Type Scientific Name Characteristics Use
Cows Bos indicus Heat-tolerant, hump-backed Dairy (milk) & draught (farm work)
Buffaloes Bos bubalis Prefer water, heavier milk production Dairy & draught

Lactation Period: The time a cow produces milk after giving birth. A good dairy cow can produce milk for 8-10 months.

Cross-breeding for Better Milk Production ▼

Farmers cross local Indian breeds with exotic (foreign) high-milk-producing breeds:

  • Exotic Breeds: Jersey, Brown Swiss (from Europe)
  • Local Breeds: Red Sindhi, Sahiwal (adapted to Indian climate)
  • Result: Hybrid calves with high milk production AND heat tolerance!

Cattle Feed:

  • Roughage: Fiber-rich food like hay, grass, maize stalks (provides bulk, aids digestion)
  • Concentrates: Protein-rich grains like soyabean, groundnut cake (builds muscle)

Common Cattle Diseases:

  • External parasites: Skin infections from ticks, mites, lice
  • Internal parasites: Worms, flukes in digestive system
  • Infectious diseases: Foot-and-mouth disease, brucellosis (spread animal-to-animal)
Prevention: Regular vaccination, clean housing, proper feeding, and quarantine of sick animals.

b) Poultry Farming

Farming birds (chickens, ducks, turkeys) for eggs and meat. Two main types:

Type Purpose Characteristics
Layers Egg production Small, lightweight, lay consistently
Broilers Meat production Large, fast-growing, heavy weight
Cross-breeding in Poultry ▼

Cross Indian breeds (Aseel) with exotic breeds (Leghorn, Rhode Island Red):

  • Indian Aseel: Hardy, aggressive, low egg production
  • Exotic Leghorn: High egg production, efficient feed conversion
  • Hybrid Result: Better productivity + disease resistance!

Desirable Traits in Poultry:

  • Chick quality (high hatchability)
  • Summer adaptation (heat tolerance)
  • Low maintenance cost
  • Disease resistance

Poultry Management:

  • Proper housing (protection from predators & weather)
  • Temperature control (neither too hot nor too cold)
  • Good hygiene (clean water, clean bedding)
  • Regular vaccination (to prevent diseases)

c) Fish Production

Fish farming is growing rapidly in India, providing protein-rich food.

Two Main Types:

Marine Fisheries (Ocean Fish) ▼

Important fish species: Pomfret, mackerel, tuna, sardines

Mariculture: Fish farming in coastal waters/sea farms

Advantages: High-value fish, supports coastal economies

Inland Fisheries (Freshwater Fish) ▼

Resources: Canals, ponds, reservoirs, rivers

Aquaculture: Controlled fish farming in ponds

Advantages: Low cost, high productivity, supports rural livelihoods

Composite Fish Culture: The clever way to farm multiple fish species together!

  • 5-6 fish species in one pond exploit different zones and food sources
  • Catla: Surface feeder (eats algae)
  • Rohu: Middle-layer feeder (eats plants)
  • Mrigal/Common Carp: Bottom feeder (eats organic matter)
  • Grass Carp: Feeds on aquatic weeds
Composite Culture Analogy: Like assigning each person in a team a different task so they don't compete. Each fish species has a "job" in the pond, maximizing productivity!

d) Bee-keeping

Apiculture (bee-keeping) produces honey and beeswax while also helping crop pollination!

Bee Species in India:

  • Apis cerana indica: Indian honey bee - small, docile, good for small farms
  • Apis dorsata: Rock bee - large, produces lots of honey but hard to manage
  • Apis florae: Little bee - tiny but defensive
  • Apis mellifera: Italian bee - imported, excellent honey producer

Products: Honey (food) and beeswax (cosmetics, candles)

Pasturage: The availability of flowers for nectar and pollen collection. Bee-keepers need to ensure good flower availability throughout the year by planting nectar-producing plants!

Bonus Benefit: Bees are essential pollinators. They transfer pollen between flowers, helping crops produce fruits and seeds. This makes bee-keeping good for both the bee-keeper and farmers!

Exam Preparation: Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

These questions require deep understanding and analysis. Click to reveal answers.

HOTS Question 1

A farmer notices that their wheat crop shows yellowing of leaves despite regular irrigation. Explain what nutrient deficiency this might indicate and how the farmer can resolve this without using chemical fertilizers.

Answer: The yellowing of leaves suggests nitrogen deficiency. Nitrogen is essential for chlorophyll production (the green pigment in leaves).

Solutions without chemical fertilizers:
  • Green manuring: Grow nitrogen-fixing crops (legumes like clover) and plough them into the soil
  • Organic compost: Add well-decomposed manure rich in nitrogen
  • Crop rotation: Follow wheat with a legume crop to restore soil nitrogen
  • Bio-fertilizers: Use nitrogen-fixing bacteria like Azospirillum
Key Learning: This demonstrates understanding of nutrient cycles, sustainable farming, and the relationship between symptoms and causes.
HOTS Question 2

Compare and contrast the effectiveness of chemical pesticides, mechanical control, and biological control in managing crop pests. Which method would you recommend for a small farmer? Justify your answer.

Answer:

Comparison:
  • Chemical Pesticides: Fast, effective for large infestations, but expensive, harmful to environment, kill beneficial insects
  • Mechanical Control: Safe, eco-friendly, low-cost, but labor-intensive and slow
  • Biological Control: Sustainable, no chemical residues, self-sustaining, but slower and less predictable

Recommendation for small farmers: A combination of all three (Integrated Pest Management) is best, but emphasis on mechanical + biological control because:
  • They can't afford expensive pesticides
  • They can use organic practices (neem, manual removal)
  • Small farms benefit from biodiversity and beneficial insects
  • Produces pesticide-free crops for premium market prices
Key Learning: Critical thinking about costs, benefits, and sustainability trade-offs.
HOTS Question 3

Explain how hybrid cattle are developed by crossing exotic breeds with Indian breeds. What are the advantages of this approach for Indian dairy farming? What challenges might arise?

Answer:

Development Process:
  • Identify desirable traits in exotic breeds (e.g., Jersey's high milk production)
  • Identify strengths in local breeds (e.g., Sahiwal's heat tolerance)
  • Cross-breed them to combine both traits in offspring

Advantages:
  • Higher milk production (exotic trait)
  • Better adaptation to Indian climate (local trait)
  • Increased farmer income
  • Improved food security

Challenges:
  • Hybrids may have lower disease resistance initially
  • Requires selective breeding over multiple generations
  • Good care and feeding essential to maintain health
  • Initial investment is high
Key Learning: Understanding of genetics, breeding programs, and balancing productivity with sustainability.

Case-Based Questions

Real-world scenarios to test applied understanding:

Case Study 1: Farmer Rajesh's Productivity Problem

Scenario: Farmer Rajesh cultivates 5 hectares in a village with limited rainfall. His wheat yields have been declining for 3 years despite using chemical fertilizers. He's concerned about cost and environmental impact.

Questions: What are the likely causes of declining yield? Suggest sustainable solutions using crop variety improvement, crop rotation, and nutrient management principles.

Analysis of the problem:
  • Likely causes: Soil exhaustion from repeated wheat monoculture, chemical fertilizer overuse damaging soil structure, declining soil fertility and microbial life

Sustainable solutions:
  • Crop Rotation: Alternate wheat with gram (legume) or other rabi crops. Legumes fix nitrogen naturally, restoring soil fertility
  • Variety Improvement: Use drought-resistant wheat varieties suited to limited rainfall
  • Manure Instead of Chemicals: Use compost/vermi-compost to improve soil structure and biology
  • Green Manuring: Grow nitrogen-fixing crops in off-season and plough them in
  • Rainwater Harvesting: Install tanks to store monsoon water for irrigation in dry months
Expected outcome: Higher yields, lower costs, improved soil health, sustainability.
Case Study 2: Pest Invasion in Organic Farm

Scenario: Priya runs an organic cotton farm. Suddenly, a large infestation of stem-borers attacks her crop, threatening 30% yield loss. She cannot use chemical pesticides as per her organic certification.

Questions: What immediate and long-term control measures should Priya implement? How do her crop protection choices align with sustainable farming?

Immediate measures (to prevent catastrophic loss):
  • Mechanical control: Manual removal of affected plant parts, hand-picking of larvae
  • Neem spray: Natural pesticide from neem leaves (approved for organic farming)
  • Pheromone traps: Attract and trap male moths to reduce breeding

Long-term prevention (next season):
  • Biological control: Release natural parasites of stem-borers (wasps)
  • Crop rotation: Don't plant cotton in same field repeatedly
  • Inter-cropping: Mix with other crops to confuse pests
  • Summer ploughing: Expose soil to sun to kill dormant pupae
  • Resistant varieties: Use cotton varieties bred for pest resistance

Alignment with sustainability: These practices maintain soil health, biodiversity, and long-term productivity while avoiding chemical residues in food.
Case Study 3: Fish Farm Expansion Decision

Scenario: A farmer cooperative in Karnataka plans to develop a fish farming project on 10 hectares of tanks. They must decide between monoculture (single species) vs. composite fish culture. Budget constraints exist.

Questions: Evaluate both approaches. Why would you recommend composite fish culture? What species would you choose for Karnataka and why?

Comparison:
  • Monoculture: Single species (e.g., only carp) - simpler management, but underutilizes pond space and resources, higher disease risk
  • Composite culture: 5-6 species together - maximizes productivity, uses all ecological niches, lower disease risk, sustainable

Recommendation: Composite Fish Culture
Reasons:
  • Each species feeds at different water layers (no competition)
  • Total yield is 3-4 times higher than monoculture
  • Better return on investment
  • More resilient to diseases
  • Environmentally sustainable

Recommended species for Karnataka tanks:
  • Catla: Surface feeder (eats algae, prevents water blooms)
  • Rohu: Mid-water feeder (eats aquatic vegetation)
  • Mrigal: Bottom feeder (eats detritus, cleans pond)
  • Common Carp: Omnivore, hardy in different temperatures
  • Grass Carp: Feeds on weeds (reduces maintenance)

Expected outcome: 20-25 tons/hectare/year production, sustainable livelihood, improved tank ecosystem health.

Cheat Sheet & Memory Tips

Key Terminology

Hybridisation
Crossing two genetically different plants to combine desirable traits from both. Example: Cross a high-yield variety with a disease-resistant variety to get both benefits.
Food Security
Availability and access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food for all people to live active and healthy lives.
Manure
Organic material from decomposed plant and animal waste. Examples: Compost, vermi-compost, green manure. Improves soil structure!
Fertilizer
Commercially manufactured nutrient supplement, usually containing NPK in high concentrations. Fast-acting but can damage soil if overused.
Kharif Crops
Crops planted during monsoon (June-October). Paddy, soyabean, maize. "Kharif" = summer season.
Rabi Crops
Crops planted during winter (November-April). Wheat, gram, peas. "Rabi" = winter season.
Inter-cropping
Growing two crops in a specific row pattern on the same field. Example: Soyabean (fixes N) + Maize (tall, provides shade).
Crop Rotation
Growing different crops in sequence on the same field over different seasons. Breaks pest cycles and restores soil nutrients naturally.
Biopesticides
Natural pest control agents: beneficial insects, neem leaves, or microorganisms that reduce pest populations.
Mariculture
Fish farming in marine (ocean) environments. Produces high-value fish like pomfret, mackerel.
Aquaculture
Fish farming in freshwater (inland) environments like ponds, tanks, canals. More accessible to small farmers.
Apiculture
Bee-keeping for honey and beeswax production. Bees also pollinate crops, providing double benefit!

Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

NPK Nutrients - Remember: "N is for itrogenic (green), P is for Phosphate (root), K is for Potassium (streNgth)"
  • N (Nitrogen): Makes leaves GREEN and lush
  • P (Phosphorus): Powers ROOT development and flowering
  • K (Potassium): Keeps plant STRONG and fruits quality
KHARIF vs RABI - "KH = summer (HoT), RA = cooler (rAin stops)"
  • Kharif (June-Oct): Monsoon, wet, hot - for rain-loving crops
  • Rabi (Nov-Apr): Winter, cool, dry - needs irrigation
Composite Fish Culture - "CRMG = Catla Rohu Mrigal Grass Carp"
  • C - Catla: Surface (top of pond)
  • R - Rohu: Middle layer
  • M - Mrigal: Bottom feeder
  • G - Grass Carp: Weed eater
Pest Control Methods - "CyBMe = Chemical, Bio, Mechanical"
  • C (Chemical): Fast but expensive & harmful
  • B (Biological): Natural, sustainable, slower
  • M (Mechanical): Manual, labor-intensive, safest
  • Best = combination (IPM)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Thinking fertilizers are always better than manure

Reality: Manure is often better for soil health! Fertilizers work fast but damage soil structure over time. Use both wisely.

Mistake 2: Confusing inter-cropping with crop rotation

Reality: Inter-cropping = same season, two crops together. Crop rotation = different seasons, different crops in sequence. They serve different purposes!

Mistake 3: Thinking all pesticides are harmful to the environment

Reality: Biopesticides (neem, beneficial insects) are pesticides too, but environment-friendly. Use the right tool for the right situation!

Mistake 4: Thinking hybridisation is the same as genetic modification

Reality: Hybridisation = traditional cross-breeding (humans have done for centuries). GM = direct gene insertion (modern technology). Both are different!

Mistake 5: Thinking storage is just about keeping grain dry

Reality: Storage needs to control BOTH moisture (abiotic) AND pests/microbes (biotic). Fumigation, proper warehouse, temperature control all matter!

Chapter Summary in One Minute

Why? India's growing population needs more food, sustainably.

How (Crop Side)?

  • Better crop varieties through hybridisation and GM
  • Smart farming: right nutrients (manure + fertilizers), irrigation, crop patterns (rotation/inter-cropping)
  • Protection: manage pests biologically, not just chemicals
  • Storage: control pests AND environment

How (Animal Side)?

  • Cross-breed for better milk/meat: exotic breeds (high production) × local breeds (heat-tolerance)
  • Farm multiple animals: cattle, poultry, fish, bees - each provides protein/income
  • Good management: housing, feeding, health, hygiene

Key principle: Balance productivity with sustainability. Short-term gains shouldn't harm long-term soil/environment!
