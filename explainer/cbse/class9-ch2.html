Is Matter Around Us Pure?

Class 9 · Chapter 2 · Chemistry · CBSE 2025-26

"When you stir sugar into water, it disappears — but is the water still 'pure'? What does 'pure' even mean in science? Let's find out how chemists think about purity."

Section 1: What Is a Pure Substance?

Beyond Everyday Language

Think of a glass of milk at a local dairy. When someone says "pure milk," they mean it hasn't been mixed with water or chemicals. But in chemistry, "purity" has a very different meaning.

In chemistry, a pure substance is a material that contains only one type of particle — it has a fixed composition and unchanging physical properties. Every sample of a pure substance behaves identically under the same conditions.

The Scientific Definition

A pure substance can be identified by its characteristic properties:

  • Definite melting point: Pure ice always melts at exactly 0°C (at 1 atmosphere pressure)
  • Definite boiling point: Pure water always boils at exactly 100°C (at 1 atmosphere pressure)
  • Constant density: The density doesn't change regardless of the sample size
  • Fixed composition: The ratio of particles remains the same

For example, if you have a beaker of pure water and another contains pure water that's been sitting for a week, both will boil at 100°C and freeze at 0°C. They're identical.

Did You Know? Pure Water Is Incredibly Rare!

Absolutely pure water (H₂O with nothing else) almost never exists in nature. Even distilled water in a laboratory will absorb carbon dioxide from the air within minutes, forming tiny amounts of carbonic acid. The water we call "pure" in labs is just pure enough for our experiments!

Types of Pure Substances

Pure substances are divided into two main categories:

1. Elements

Substances made of only one type of atom. Examples: pure iron (Fe), pure gold (Au), oxygen gas (O₂)

2. Compounds

Substances made of two or more elements chemically bonded in a fixed ratio. Examples: water (H₂O), table salt (NaCl), sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁)

Section 2: Mixtures — The Real World

Why Most Things Are Mixtures

Look around you. The air you breathe, the milk in your breakfast, the soil in a garden — almost everything in nature is a mixture. A mixture is formed when two or more pure substances are mixed together physically (not chemically combined).

The key word is physically. The individual substances in a mixture retain their own properties and don't form new substances.

Defining Characteristics of Mixtures

  • Variable composition: You can have salt water with 5g salt per 100g water, or 10g salt per 100g water. Both are salt water, but they're different mixtures.
  • Properties of components are retained: In a mixture of sand and iron filings, sand remains sand and iron remains iron. You could separate them with a magnet.
  • Separation by physical methods: You can separate a mixture using filtration, evaporation, magnetic separation, or other physical techniques.
  • No energy change: Unlike chemical reactions, mixing usually doesn't release or absorb significant heat.

Homogeneous vs Heterogeneous Mixtures

Homogeneous Mixtures (Solutions)

A homogeneous mixture has uniform composition throughout. You can't see different parts — it appears as a single substance.

  • Salt water: If you look at any tiny portion, you'll find the same ratio of salt to water
  • Air: A mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, and other gases, uniform throughout our atmosphere
  • Alloys: Brass (copper + zinc), steel (iron + carbon), bronze (copper + tin)
  • Ink: Looks solid blue, but it's a mixture of dyes dissolved in liquid
Did You Know? Alloys Are Mixtures, Not Compounds!

Brass is a mixture of copper and zinc, not a compound. This is why you can make brass with different percentages of copper and zinc. Yellow brass is 70% copper and 30% zinc, but red brass might be 85% copper and 15% zinc. Both are called brass! If brass were a compound (like water), it would always have the same ratio.

Heterogeneous Mixtures

A heterogeneous mixture has non-uniform composition. You can see different parts with distinct properties.

  • Muddy water: You can see water and mud are separate
  • Oil and water: They refuse to mix and stay in distinct layers
  • Sand and iron filings: Clearly two different substances mixed together
  • Fruit salad: You can identify individual pieces of fruit

Visual analogy: Think of homogeneous mixtures like well-mixed paint (same color everywhere) and heterogeneous mixtures like a bowl of mixed nuts (you can identify each type).

Section 3: Solutions — Deep Dive

What Makes a Solution?

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances where one substance (the solute) dissolves in another (the solvent).

Common solutions in everyday life:

  • Sugar in water: Tea, soft drinks, syrup
  • Oxygen in water: The medium where aquatic life survives
  • Alcohol in water: Hand sanitizers, medicines
  • Gases dissolved in liquids: Carbonated drinks (CO₂ in water)

Solute and Solvent

  • Solvent: The substance that dissolves another — usually present in larger amount. Water is the most common solvent.
  • Solute: The substance being dissolved — usually present in smaller amount. Salt, sugar, oxygen, alcohol are common solutes.

In a solution of salt water: water is the solvent, salt is the solute.

Key Properties of Solutions

  • Homogeneous: Uniform throughout, appears as a single substance
  • Transparent: Light passes through clearly (unless colored)
  • Particle size: Less than 1 nanometer (at molecular/ionic level)
  • Stable: Components don't separate on standing (won't settle)
  • Inseparable by filtration: Filter paper can't trap dissolved particles

Concentration of Solutions

Understanding Concentration

Imagine you make lemonade. If you add 1 spoon of lemon juice to 1 glass of water, it tastes tangy. If you add 5 spoons to the same glass, it tastes very sour. The concentration describes how much solute is dissolved.

Types of Solutions by Concentration

  • Dilute solution: Contains relatively little solute (weak tea, light salt water)
  • Concentrated solution: Contains relatively large amount of solute (strong tea, very salty water)
  • Saturated solution: Contains the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at that temperature. If you try to add more, it won't dissolve.
  • Unsaturated solution: Contains less than the maximum amount of solute. You could dissolve more if you added more solute.

Solubility

SolubilityThe maximum amount of solute that dissolves in 100 grams of solvent at a specific temperature is the maximum amount of a substance that will dissolve in 100g of solvent at a given temperature.

For example, at 20°C, the solubility of sugar in water is about 200g per 100g water. This means you can dissolve up to 200 grams of sugar in 100 grams of water at 20°C.

Did You Know? Temperature Changes Everything!

Most solid solutes become MORE soluble as temperature increases. This is why hot water can dissolve more sugar than cold water. However, gases behave oppositely — gases become LESS soluble in hot water. This is why carbonated drinks go flat faster in warm weather!

Mass Percentage Calculation

A common way to express concentration is mass percentage (or weight percentage):

$$\text{Mass Percentage} = \frac{\text{Mass of Solute}}{\text{Mass of Solution}} \times 100$$

Example: If you dissolve 40g of sugar in 200g of solution, what's the mass percentage?

$$\text{Mass Percentage} = \frac{40}{200} \times 100 = 20\%$$

This means 20% of the solution's mass is sugar, and 80% is solvent.

Section 4: Suspensions

The Visible Mixture

If you add chalk powder to water, it clouds up. You can see white particles floating around. This is a suspension — a heterogeneous mixture where particles are large enough to be visible and eventually settle.

Characteristics of Suspensions

  • Heterogeneous: Non-uniform composition, clearly shows different phases
  • Particle size: Greater than 100 nanometers, visible to naked eye
  • Opaque: Light doesn't pass through clearly
  • Settling: Particles settle to the bottom over time due to gravity
  • Separable by filtration: Filter paper can trap suspended particles

Common Examples

  • Muddy water: Soil particles suspended in water
  • Chalk water: Chalk powder in water
  • Flour in water: Flour particles floating
  • Dust in air: Dust particles in the atmosphere
  • Paint: Pigment particles suspended in liquid
Did You Know? Suspensions Need Shaking!

When you leave a can of paint for several weeks, the pigment particles settle at the bottom. This is why paint cans have "Shake well before use" written on them. When you shake it, the settling particles get mixed back up into suspension.

Section 5: Colloidal Solutions (Colloids)

The In-Between State

Colloids are the middle ground between solutions and suspensions. They look homogeneous like a solution, but particles are much larger than dissolved molecules. This hidden complexity makes colloids fascinating.

Characteristics of Colloids

  • Appearance: Heterogeneous but appears homogeneous
  • Particle size: 1 to 100 nanometers (between solutions and suspensions)
  • Transparency: Usually opaque or translucent
  • Settling: Particles don't settle on standing
  • Filtration: Can't be separated by ordinary filtration
  • Tyndall Effect: Scatters light visibly

The Tyndall Effect

Imagine driving a car on a foggy night with headlights on. The light beam becomes visible in the fog. This is the Tyndall effectThe scattering of light by colloidal particles, making the light path visible.

When light passes through a colloid, colloidal particles scatter it in all directions, making the path of light visible. This doesn't happen with true solutions (particles are too small) or suspensions (particles are so large they mostly block light).

Practical example: When you shine a torch beam into:

  • Salt solution: Light beam invisible (true solution)
  • Milk: Light beam clearly visible (colloid)
  • Muddy water: Light blocked mostly (suspension)
Did You Know? Your Blood Is a Colloid!

Blood is a complex colloid. Red blood cells are large enough to scatter light (Tyndall effect), which is why blood appears dark red. If you shine light through a thin layer of blood, you can see the light beam path. This same property allows scientists to use special microscopes to study blood cells.

Common Examples of Colloids

Colloids are everywhere in nature and industry:

  • Milk: Fat globules and protein particles in water
  • Fog and clouds: Tiny water droplets in air
  • Smoke: Tiny solid particles in air
  • Butter: Water droplets in fat
  • Ink: Pigment particles dispersed in liquid
  • Shaving cream: Gas bubbles in liquid
  • Jelly: Liquid trapped in gel network

Types of Colloids

Colloids are classified by the type of dispersed phase and dispersion medium:

Type Dispersed Phase Dispersion Medium Example
Sol Solid Liquid Ink, blood
Aerosol Liquid Gas Fog, mist, spray
Emulsion Liquid Liquid Milk, mayonnaise
Gel Liquid Solid Jelly, gelatin, agar
Foam Gas Liquid Shaving cream, whipped cream

Section 6: Separation of Mixtures

Why Separate Mixtures?

Industries need pure substances to manufacture medicines, metals, chemicals, and fuels. We use different separation techniques based on the properties of the components. Let's explore each method:

1. Evaporation

Principle: When a liquid evaporates, it leaves the dissolved solid behind.

Used to separate: Dissolved solids from liquids

Examples:

  • Obtaining salt from sea water (evaporate water, salt crystals remain)
  • Extracting sugar from sugarcane juice
  • Getting salt deposits from salt lakes

Limitation: If the solid decomposes on heating, evaporation damages the product.

2. Centrifugation

Principle: Using spinning motion to separate particles of different densities.

Used to separate: Denser particles from lighter ones

Examples:

  • Separating cream from milk (cream is lighter, collects on top)
  • Blood components separation in medical labs (red cells sink, plasma floats)
  • Uranium enrichment in nuclear facilities

Advantage: Much faster than settling, used in medical diagnostics.

3. Separating Funnel (Separatory Funnel)

Principle: Immiscible liquids (liquids that don't mix) can be separated by layers because they have different densities.

Used to separate: Two immiscible liquids

Examples:

  • Oil and water (oil floats on water)
  • Kerosene and water
  • Chloroform and water

How it works: The denser liquid (water) sinks to the bottom. A tap at the bottom lets you drain the lower layer into a new container.

Did You Know? Oil Spills Use This Principle!

When oil spills in the ocean, it floats on water. Recovery efforts use the separating funnel principle — skimming the floating oil layer off the water surface. Some oil booms create physical barriers to prevent mixing and keep oil accessible for separation.

4. Sublimation

Principle: Some solids convert directly to gas without becoming liquid. When cooled, they recrystallize, leaving non-sublimable substances behind.

Used to separate: Sublimable substances from non-sublimable solids

Examples:

  • Ammonium chloride from salt mixture (ammonium chloride sublimes at 340°C)
  • Camphor from sand (camphor sublimes readily)
  • Iodine from sand

5. Chromatography

Principle: Different substances dissolve at different rates in a solvent, so they move different distances up filter paper.

Used to separate: Soluble substances that travel at different speeds

Paper Chromatography Explained:

  1. Draw a line with pencil near the bottom of filter paper
  2. Put the mixture (e.g., ink) on this line
  3. Dip the paper in solvent (e.g., water or acetone)
  4. Solvent travels up, carrying different components different distances
  5. Components separate as different colored spots

Examples:

  • Separating ink colors (ballpoint pen ink contains multiple dyes)
  • Food coloring analysis (identifying artificial food colors)
  • Separating leaf pigments (chlorophyll, carotene, xanthophyll)
  • Forensic analysis (checking if inks match)
Did You Know? Chromatography Solved a Crime!

Forged documents are detected using paper chromatography. If someone writes a check with the wrong ink, chromatography can separate the inks and show they don't match genuine documents. This is because each ink manufacturer uses a unique combination of dyes, and changing even one component changes the chromatography pattern.

6. Distillation

Principle: Liquids with different boiling points separate when heated. Lower boiling point liquid vaporizes first, is collected, and condensed back to liquid.

Used to separate: Miscible liquids with different boiling points

Simple Distillation Setup:

  1. Heat the liquid mixture in a flask
  2. Lower boiling point liquid vaporizes and enters a condenser tube
  3. Cold water runs through the condenser, cooling the vapor back to liquid
  4. Pure liquid collects in a receiving flask

Examples:

  • Separating acetone from water (acetone boils at 56°C, water at 100°C)
  • Purifying alcohol from fermented liquids
  • Desalination (obtaining fresh water from sea water)

7. Fractional Distillation

Principle: When liquids have very close boiling points, a fractionating columnA tower packed with glass beads or similar material that provides more surface area for liquid-vapor contact, enabling better separation of liquids with similar boiling points is used to separate them more effectively.

How Fractional Distillation Works:

A fractionating column contains a packing material (glass beads, porcelain). As vapor rises and liquid condenses repeatedly throughout the column, liquids separate better than in simple distillation.

Industrial Applications:

  • Petroleum refining: Crude oil separated into petrol, diesel, kerosene, fuel oil, bitumen based on boiling points
  • Air separation: Oxygen and nitrogen separated from liquid air (nitrogen boils at -196°C, oxygen at -183°C)
  • Alcohol purification: Separating different alcohols or water from alcohol

8. Crystallisation

Principle: When a hot saturated solution cools, dissolved solute crystallizes out as pure solid.

Used to separate: Pure solids from solution, especially when the solid decomposes at high temperatures

Crystallisation Process:

  1. Prepare a hot saturated solution
  2. Filter to remove insoluble impurities
  3. Cool slowly to allow crystals to form
  4. Filter to separate crystals
  5. Dry the pure crystals

Examples:

  • Purifying copper sulfate (better than evaporation as it doesn't decompose)
  • Extracting alum from alum solution
  • Salt crystallization from sea water

Why better than evaporation: Evaporation can decompose heat-sensitive compounds, but crystallisation uses lower temperatures and produces pure crystals.

Did You Know? Crystals Are So Pure They're Used in Medicine!

Many medicines are crystalline compounds because crystallisation produces extremely pure solids. Aspirin, ibuprofen, and other pharmaceuticals are purified using crystallisation. The purity is so high that impurities measured in parts per million can affect effectiveness or side effects.

Section 7: Physical and Chemical Changes

Understanding the Difference

When matter changes, it can do so in two fundamentally different ways. Understanding the difference helps explain why we can separate mixtures but not compounds.

Physical Changes

A physical change alters the form or state of matter, but no new substance is created. The chemical composition remains the same.

Characteristics:

  • Reversible (usually)
  • No new substance formed
  • Original substance can be recovered
  • No significant heat absorbed or released (usually)

Examples:

  • Melting ice: H₂O changes from solid to liquid, but it's still H₂O
  • Dissolving sugar: Sugar particles spread through water, but sugar and water don't change their nature
  • Tearing paper: Paper becomes smaller pieces but remains paper
  • Boiling water: Water becomes steam (water vapor), still H₂O
  • Cutting wood: You get smaller pieces of wood, not a new substance

Chemical Changes

A chemical change (or chemical reaction) transforms one substance into completely different substance(s) with new properties.

Characteristics:

  • Usually irreversible (not easily reversed)
  • New substance(s) with different properties formed
  • Original substance cannot be easily recovered
  • Energy is usually absorbed or released

Examples:

  • Burning paper: Paper becomes ash, carbon dioxide, and water vapor — completely new substances
  • Rusting iron: Iron (Fe) combines with oxygen (O₂) to form iron oxide (rust)
  • Cooking an egg: Protein structure changes; you can't "uncook" an egg
  • Photosynthesis: Plants combine carbon dioxide and water to make glucose
  • Neutralization: Acid + base produce salt + water

The Connection to Mixtures and Compounds

This distinction is crucial:

  • Mixtures are separated by PHYSICAL methods because no new substance forms — they're just mixed, not chemically bonded
  • Compounds need CHEMICAL methods to separate because elements are chemically bonded, requiring a chemical reaction to break apart

For example, you can separate salt from water by evaporation (physical method) because they're just mixed. But to separate the hydrogen and oxygen that make up water, you need to pass electricity through it (electrolysis) — a chemical method.

Section 8: Elements

What Are Elements?

An element is the simplest pure substance — it cannot be broken down into simpler substances by any physical or chemical method. Every element is made of only one type of atom.

Historical Context

The concept of elements evolved over time:

  • Robert Boyle (1661): Defined elements as substances that cannot be broken down further
  • Antoine Lavoisier (1789): First systematic list of 33 substances he considered elements
  • John Dalton (1808): Atoms are the basis — elements are made of identical atoms

Classification of Elements

Metals

Properties:

  • Shiny metallic luster
  • Malleable (can be hammered into thin sheets)
  • Ductile (can be drawn into wires)
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity
  • Usually solid at room temperature

Examples: Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Gold (Au), Silver (Ag), Aluminum (Al)

Non-metals

Properties:

  • No metallic luster (usually dull)
  • Brittle (break easily)
  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity (except graphite)
  • Can be solid, liquid, or gas

Examples: Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N), Carbon (C), Sulfur (S), Chlorine (Cl)

Metalloids (Semi-metals)

Properties: Show properties of both metals and non-metals

Examples: Boron (B), Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), Arsenic (As)

The Modern Periodic Table

Today, we know of 118 elements

Did You Know? Gold and Silver Were Known Since Ancient Times!

Gold was one of the first elements known to humans because it occurs naturally in pure form (no chemical separation needed). Egyptians, Greeks, and Romans all worked with gold thousands of years ago. Silver also occurs naturally pure. Most other elements were discovered much later — nitrogen in 1772, oxygen in 1774, chlorine in 1774 — because they naturally exist in compounds that needed chemical separation to isolate.

Section 9: Compounds

Definition

A compound is a pure substance formed when two or more elements chemically combine in a fixed ratio. The properties of a compound are completely different from its constituent elements.

Key Characteristics of Compounds

  • Fixed composition: Always the same ratio of elements. Water is always H₂O (2 hydrogen atoms for every 1 oxygen atom)
  • Different properties: Properties are new, not the average of component elements
  • Chemical bonding: Elements are held together by chemical bonds, not just mixed
  • Definite melting and boiling points: Like all pure substances
  • Requires chemical methods to separate: Can't be separated by physical methods like filtration or evaporation

Dramatic Examples of Property Change

Water (H₂O)

  • Hydrogen: Colorless gas that burns explosively in oxygen
  • Oxygen: Colorless gas that supports combustion
  • Water: Liquid that extinguishes fire!

The properties are completely opposite to what you'd expect from the elements.

Table Salt (NaCl)

  • Sodium: Soft metal so reactive it spontaneously combusts in air. Must be stored in oil.
  • Chlorine: Poisonous yellow-green gas used as a disinfectant
  • Table salt: Essential nutrient for life, white crystalline solid used to preserve and flavor food
Did You Know? Salt Was Once Worth Its Weight in Gold!

In ancient Rome, salt was so valuable that soldiers were sometimes paid in salt (the origin of the word "salary" from the Latin "salarium"). This precious compound was essential for preserving meat before refrigeration. The transformation from reactive sodium and poisonous chlorine into this valuable substance demonstrates the magic of chemical compounds.

Mixture vs Compound — Key Differences

Property Mixture Compound
Composition Variable — can have different ratios Fixed — always same ratio
Properties Same as components Different from components
Separation method Physical methods (filtration, evaporation, etc.) Chemical methods (electrolysis, reaction, etc.)
Energy change Usually none Energy absorbed or released
Example Salt water (can vary salt content) Water (always H₂O)

Exam Preparation: HOTS & Case-Based Questions

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

HOTS 1: Why is brass considered an alloy (mixture) and not a compound, even though it appears uniform?

Answer:

Brass appears uniform because it's a homogeneous mixture (solid solution). However, it's classified as a mixture (not a compound) for these reasons:

  1. Variable composition: Brass can have different percentages of copper and zinc. Yellow brass might be 70% Cu + 30% Zn, while red brass is 85% Cu + 15% Zn. A true compound always has a fixed ratio.
  2. Physical mixture: Copper and zinc atoms are mixed without chemical bonding — they occupy atomic positions in a crystalline structure. They don't form a new substance.
  3. Properties: Brass properties are intermediate between copper and zinc (harder than pure copper, different color, different melting point). In a compound, properties would be completely different from constituent elements.
  4. Separation method: While separation is difficult, brass can theoretically be separated by physical methods (though chemically intensive), not by simple chemical decomposition like a true compound.

HOTS 2: Sea water is a mixture. Describe step by step how you would obtain pure water and separate the salts from sea water.

Answer:

To obtain pure water and separate salts from sea water, use distillation:

  1. Heat: Add heat to the sea water in a distillation apparatus, gradually raising the temperature
  2. Evaporation: Water with a lower boiling point (100°C) evaporates and turns into steam, while dissolved salts (which have much higher boiling points) remain as liquid
  3. Condensation: Direct the steam into a condenser tube where cold running water cools it back to liquid
  4. Collection: Collect the condensed pure water in a receiving flask
  5. Salt residue: The salt and minerals remain behind in the original flask

Why this works: Water and salts have vastly different boiling points. Water boils at 100°C, while sodium chloride boils at 1413°C. By the time water vaporizes, salts remain behind.

Alternative: Reverse osmosis is used industrially because it's more efficient and doesn't require high heat.

HOTS 3: Why is crystallisation considered better than simple evaporation for obtaining pure solids?

Answer:

Crystallisation is superior to evaporation for several reasons:

  1. Heat-sensitive compounds: Many substances decompose at high temperatures. Evaporation heats the entire solution, potentially damaging heat-sensitive solutes. Crystallisation uses gentle cooling, keeping temperatures low.
  2. Purity: When a solution cools slowly, only the desired solute crystallizes out. Impurities remain dissolved. Evaporation might trap impurities in the final product.
  3. Crystal structure: Crystallisation produces well-formed crystals with impurities excluded from the crystal lattice. Evaporation produces a more amorphous (disorganized) solid that might include impurities.
  4. Selectivity: If multiple solutes have different solubilities at different temperatures, slow cooling allows them to crystallize at different temperatures, providing separation.
  5. Example: Copper sulfate crystals obtained by crystallisation are pure blue crystals. Evaporation might leave behind some impurities that were in the original solution.

Summary: Crystallisation is the preferred method in pharmaceutical and chemical industries precisely because it produces the purest solids at the lowest temperatures.

Case-Based Questions (CBSE 2025-26 Pattern)

Case Study 1: Solubility Experiment

A student adds different amounts of salt to water at the same temperature and records when no more dissolves. At 20°C, she finds that 36g of salt dissolves in 100g of water, but 37g won't dissolve. When she heats the solution to 50°C, all 37g dissolves, and she can even add more salt until 47g dissolves.

Questions:

  1. What is the solubility of salt at 20°C? (Assume it's a saturated solution with 36g salt in 100g water)
  2. Explain why more salt dissolved when temperature increased from 20°C to 50°C.
  3. What term describes the solution at 50°C that contains 37g salt in 100g water? Would more salt dissolve if you kept adding it?

Answers:

1. Solubility of salt at 20°C = 36g per 100g water (36g salt dissolves completely, 37g doesn't)

2. For most solid solutes, solubility increases with temperature. Heat energy increases the motion of water molecules, allowing them to surround more salt ions and dissolve them. This is why hot water can dissolve more salt than cold water.

3. At 50°C with 37g salt in 100g water, the solution is UNSATURATED (because we found that up to 47g can dissolve). Yes, more salt would dissolve if you kept adding it until you reach the saturation point at 47g per 100g water.

Case Study 2: Chromatography in Forensics

In a forensic lab, investigators analyze ink from a questioned document using paper chromatography. The suspect's pen and the questioned document are both tested. In the chromatogram, the questioned document shows three colored bands (red, blue, yellow), while the suspect's pen shows only two bands (red and blue). A reference ballpoint pen shows three bands matching the questioned document.

Questions:

  1. What principle of chromatography explains why different colors separate?
  2. Can the suspect's pen be matched to the questioned document? Explain your reasoning.
  3. What would be your conclusion if the suspect's pen showed all three colors in the same position as the questioned document?

Answers:

1. Different colored dyes (solutes) in ink dissolve at different rates in the solvent traveling up the filter paper. This difference in solubility causes them to travel different distances, separating the ink into its component dyes.

2. No, the suspect's pen cannot be matched to the questioned document. The suspect's pen produces only 2 bands (red, blue), while the questioned document has 3 bands (red, blue, yellow). The missing yellow component is significant evidence that the suspect's pen didn't write the document. Different ink manufacturers use different dye combinations, and even changing one dye component makes them distinguishable by chromatography.

3. If the suspect's pen showed all three colors in the exact same positions as the questioned document, it would be very strong evidence (though not absolute proof) that the suspect's pen could have written the document. The identical chromatography pattern would suggest identical ink composition.

Case Study 3: Petroleum Refining

At a petroleum refinery, crude oil undergoes fractional distillation. Crude oil is heated to about 350°C and enters a fractionating column (tower) that's hotter at the bottom and cooler at the top. Different fractions are collected at different heights: petrol (gasoline) near the top, kerosene in the middle, and diesel near the bottom. Bitumen (tar) remains at the very bottom.

Questions:

  1. Why are different fractions collected at different heights rather than all at one point?
  2. Explain why petrol (small molecules) comes out near the top while diesel (larger molecules) comes out near the bottom.
  3. Bitumen is collected at the very bottom. Predict its boiling point compared to other fractions.

Answers:

1. The fractionating column has a temperature gradient (hot at bottom, cool at top). Different components of crude oil have different boiling points. Each fraction condenses (returns to liquid) at the height where the temperature matches its boiling point. This separates them without mixing.

2. Petrol has small hydrocarbon molecules with low boiling points (~100-200°C). It vaporizes easily and rises to the cooler top of the column before condensing. Diesel has larger hydrocarbon molecules with higher boiling points (~200-350°C), so it doesn't vaporize as easily and condenses at a lower (hotter) position in the column.

3. Bitumen has an extremely high boiling point (>350°C). It remains liquid even at the bottom of the column and is collected as a residue. Its boiling point is much higher than all other fractions collected, which is why it never vaporizes during the process.

Key Terms Glossary

  • Pure substance: Material with fixed composition and unchanging properties
  • Element: Pure substance made of only one type of atom, cannot be broken down
  • Compound: Pure substance made of two or more elements chemically bonded in fixed ratio
  • Mixture: Combination of two or more substances mixed physically, not chemically
  • Solution: Homogeneous mixture where one substance dissolves in another
  • Solute: Substance being dissolved (usually smaller amount)
  • Solvent: Substance that dissolves another (usually larger amount)
  • Solubility: Amount of solute that dissolves in 100g solvent at specific temperature
  • Saturated solution: Maximum solute dissolved at given temperature
  • Unsaturated solution: Less than maximum solute, can dissolve more
  • Colloid: Mixture with particles 1-100 nm, appears homogeneous but scatters light
  • Tyndall effect: Scattering of light by colloidal particles
  • Suspension: Mixture with particles >100 nm, settles on standing
  • Physical change: Change in form without creating new substance
  • Chemical change: Transformation creating new substance(s)

Weak Areas & Remediation

If You Struggled With Solutions and Concentration...

Remember the key concept: Solubility is temperature-dependent! For most solids, higher temperature = more solute dissolves. Practice calculating mass percentages using: (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) × 100. Test yourself by calculating concentration in reverse: if a solution is 20% salt, how much salt is in 250g solution? (Answer: 50g)

If You Struggled With Separation Techniques...

Match the technique to the property: Evaporation (boiling point difference), Chromatography (solubility difference), Centrifugation (density difference), Separating funnel (immiscible liquids), Crystallisation (solubility + temperature). Create a flowchart: "What's mixed?" → "Are they liquids?" → "Do they mix?" If no → Separating funnel. If yes → Check boiling points → Distillation.

If You Struggled With Mixture vs Compound...

Remember the acid test: Can you vary the composition? Brass can be 70% Cu or 85% Cu (mixture). Water is always exactly H₂O (compound). Do properties change dramatically? Water's properties (liquid, extinguishes fire) are nothing like hydrogen (burns) or oxygen (supports fire). That's a compound! Salt water is just water with salt added — water still acts like water, salt still acts like salt (mixture).

If You Struggled With Colloids and Tyndall Effect...

Think "fog and headlights." The Tyndall effect is simply light being scattered by particles. True solutions have particles too tiny to scatter light (invisible beams). Suspensions have particles so large they block light (dark beams). Colloids are just right — you see the beam in fog, in milk, in blood. Quick test: Shine a torch through a glass. Can you see the beam path? If yes, likely a colloid!
