Chapter 7: Motion

Class 9 · Chapter 7 · Physics · CBSE 2025-26

HOOK: A ball thrown in the air, a car speeding on a highway, Earth spinning on its axis — motion is everywhere. But how do we describe motion precisely? And can we predict where a moving object will be in the future?

1. Describing Motion

What is Motion?

Motion is the change in position of an object with respect to time and its surroundings. Every object is either in motion or at rest, but this depends on the reference frame we choose.

Reference Point / Origin: A fixed point from which we measure the position of a moving object. The position of an object is described relative to this reference point. Motion is relative — an object may be in motion relative to one reference frame but at rest relative to another.

Example: Motion is Relative

Imagine you are sitting in a moving train. Relative to the train, you are stationary (at rest). However, relative to a person standing on the ground, you are moving at the same speed as the train. This demonstrates that motion depends on the choice of reference frame.

  • In the train's reference frame: You are at rest
  • In the ground's reference frame: You are moving
  • In the air's reference frame: You are moving
Key Insight: There is no absolute motion or absolute rest. Motion is always described with respect to a chosen reference point. The most common reference frame is the Earth's surface.

2. Motion Along a Straight Line

2.1 Distance vs. Displacement

Distance: The total length of the path traveled by an object, regardless of direction. Distance is a scalar quantity — it has only magnitude, no direction. Distance is always positive and is measured in meters (m) in SI units.

Distance Formula (Scalar): $$d = \text{Total path length traveled}$$

Key properties: Always positive, depends on the path taken, not affected by final position.

Displacement: The shortest distance from the initial position to the final position of an object, along with the direction. Displacement is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction. Displacement can be zero, positive, or negative.

Displacement Formula (Vector): $$\vec{s} = \text{Final position} - \text{Initial position}$$

Key properties: Can be zero even when distance is non-zero, independent of path taken, only depends on initial and final positions.

Important Relationship: Displacement ≤ Distance (always). The equality holds only when the object moves along a straight line in one direction without changing direction.

Worked Example 1: Distance vs. Displacement

Problem: A person walks 4 m north, then turns and walks 3 m east. Calculate the distance traveled and displacement.

Step 1: Calculate Distance
Distance = Total path length = 4 m (north) + 3 m (east) = 7 m
Step 2: Calculate Displacement
Displacement is the shortest straight-line distance from start to end point. Using the Pythagorean theorem: $$s = \sqrt{(4)^2 + (3)^2} = \sqrt{16 + 9} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{ m}$$ Direction: At an angle $$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right) \approx 36.87°$$ east of north
Answer:
Distance = 7 m (scalar)
Displacement = 5 m at 36.87° east of north (vector)

2.2 Uniform and Non-Uniform Motion

Uniform Motion: An object moves with uniform motion when it covers equal distances in equal intervals of time. The velocity remains constant throughout the motion.

Non-Uniform Motion: An object undergoes non-uniform motion when it covers unequal distances in equal intervals of time. The velocity changes with time, indicating the presence of acceleration.

Characteristic: In uniform motion, the velocity is constant and acceleration is zero. In non-uniform motion, the velocity changes, and acceleration is non-zero.

3. Speed and Velocity

Speed: The distance traveled per unit time. Speed is a scalar quantity with only magnitude and no direction. Speed is always positive. The SI unit is meter per second (m/s). Other common units are km/h and cm/s.

SPEED
$$v = \frac{d}{t}$$ where $d$ = distance (m), $t$ = time (s), $v$ = speed (m/s)

Average Speed: When an object travels a distance that is not covered at constant speed, we calculate the average speed. It is the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken.

AVERAGE SPEED
$$v_{avg} = \frac{\text{Total distance}}{\text{Total time}}$$

Velocity: The displacement covered per unit time, along with direction. Velocity is a vector quantity with both magnitude and direction. The SI unit is meter per second (m/s). Velocity can be zero, positive, or negative depending on the direction.

VELOCITY
$$\vec{v} = \frac{\vec{s}}{t}$$ where $\vec{s}$ = displacement (m), $t$ = time (s), $\vec{v}$ = velocity (m/s, with direction)

Average Velocity: For motion with uniform acceleration, the average velocity is the arithmetic mean of the initial and final velocities.

AVERAGE VELOCITY (Uniform Acceleration)
$$v_{avg} = \frac{u + v}{2}$$ where $u$ = initial velocity, $v$ = final velocity

3.1 Unit Conversion: m/s to km/h

Conversion Factor: $$1 \text{ km/h} = \frac{5}{18} \text{ m/s}$$ $$1 \text{ m/s} = \frac{18}{5} \text{ km/h} = 3.6 \text{ km/h}$$

Why this conversion?

1 km = 1000 m, 1 hour = 3600 seconds

$$1 \text{ km/h} = \frac{1000 \text{ m}}{3600 \text{ s}} = \frac{1}{3.6} \text{ m/s} = \frac{5}{18} \text{ m/s}$$

3.2 Important Distinction

Speed can be non-zero when Velocity is zero: Consider a particle moving in a circle and returning to its starting point. The particle has traveled a non-zero distance (the circumference), so its speed is non-zero. However, since the displacement is zero (it returned to the starting point), its velocity is zero. This is a fundamental difference between scalar and vector quantities.

4. Rate of Change of Velocity: Acceleration

Acceleration: The rate of change of velocity with time. Acceleration is a vector quantity with both magnitude and direction. The SI unit is meter per second squared (m/s²). Acceleration can be positive, negative, or zero.

ACCELERATION
$$a = \frac{v - u}{t}$$ where $u$ = initial velocity (m/s), $v$ = final velocity (m/s), $t$ = time (s), $a$ = acceleration (m/s²)

4.1 Types of Acceleration

Positive Acceleration: When the final velocity is greater than the initial velocity, or the velocity is increasing in the positive direction. The object is speeding up.

Example: A car starting from rest and accelerating forward. $a > 0$

Negative Acceleration (Deceleration / Retardation): When the final velocity is less than the initial velocity, or the velocity is decreasing. The object is slowing down.

Example: A car applying brakes. $a < 0$

Zero Acceleration: When the velocity does not change with time. The object is moving with constant velocity (which includes being at rest).

Example: A car driving at a constant speed on a straight road. $a = 0$

4.2 Uniform Acceleration

Uniform Acceleration: When acceleration remains constant, an object undergoes uniform acceleration. The velocity changes by equal amounts in equal time intervals. Examples: free fall under gravity (g = 9.8 m/s²), motion with constant acceleration.
Real-World Insight: In real life, most accelerations are non-uniform (they change with time). However, for many practical problems and short time intervals, we approximate motion as uniformly accelerated, which makes calculations manageable.

5. Graphical Representation of Motion

5.1 Distance-Time Graphs

What it shows: A distance-time graph plots the distance traveled by an object on the y-axis against time on the x-axis. It provides a visual representation of how an object's position changes with time.

Stationary Object

Appearance: A horizontal straight line parallel to the x-axis (time axis).

Meaning: The distance does not change with time. The object is at rest. The slope is zero, indicating zero velocity.

Example: A book lying on a table.

Uniform Speed (Constant Velocity)

Appearance: A straight line inclined to the x-axis with a constant positive slope.

Meaning: The distance increases uniformly with time. The slope of the line equals the speed: $\text{slope} = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t} = \text{speed}$

The steeper the slope, the greater the speed. Example: A car driving at constant speed on a highway.

Non-Uniform Speed (Variable Velocity)

Appearance: A curved line (not straight).

Meaning: The distance increases at a changing rate. The speed is not constant. The instantaneous speed at any point is given by the slope of the tangent to the curve at that point. A curve that gets steeper indicates increasing speed (acceleration). A curve that gets flatter indicates decreasing speed (deceleration).

Example: A car that accelerates or brakes.

General Rule for Distance-Time Graphs: The slope of the distance-time graph gives the speed of the object. A steeper slope means higher speed, a gentler slope means lower speed, and a horizontal line means the object is stationary.

5.2 Velocity-Time Graphs

What it shows: A velocity-time graph plots the velocity of an object on the y-axis against time on the x-axis. It provides information about how the velocity changes with time.

Uniform Velocity (Zero Acceleration)

Appearance: A horizontal straight line parallel to the x-axis.

Meaning: The velocity remains constant throughout the motion. The slope is zero, indicating zero acceleration.

Example: A car moving at a constant speed.

Uniform Acceleration

Appearance: A straight line inclined to the x-axis with a constant slope.

Meaning: The velocity changes uniformly with time. The slope of the line equals the acceleration: $\text{slope} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \text{acceleration}$

A positive slope indicates increasing velocity (speeding up), while a negative slope indicates decreasing velocity (slowing down).

Area Under Velocity-Time Graph: The area enclosed between the velocity-time curve and the time axis represents the distance (or displacement) traveled by the object. For a straight-line motion, Area = $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$ (for triangular areas) or Area = length × width (for rectangular areas).

Non-Uniform Acceleration

Appearance: A curved line (not straight).

Meaning: The velocity changes at a non-uniform rate. The acceleration is not constant. The instantaneous acceleration at any point is given by the slope of the tangent to the curve at that point. The area under the curve still represents the distance traveled.

6. Equations of Motion (Uniformly Accelerated Motion)

For objects moving in a straight line with uniform (constant) acceleration, we have three important equations that relate velocity, acceleration, time, and displacement. These equations are derived from the definitions of velocity and acceleration.

6.1 First Equation of Motion: v = u + at

Derivation of v = u + at

Starting Point: Definition of acceleration $$a = \frac{v - u}{t}$$
Step 1: Multiply both sides by $t$ $$at = v - u$$
Step 2: Rearrange to solve for $v$ $$v = u + at$$
Physical Meaning: The final velocity is equal to the initial velocity plus the change in velocity due to acceleration over time $t$.
FIRST EQUATION OF MOTION
$$v = u + at$$ where $u$ = initial velocity (m/s), $v$ = final velocity (m/s), $a$ = acceleration (m/s²), $t$ = time (s)
Application: This equation is useful when we know the initial velocity, acceleration, and time, and we need to find the final velocity.

6.2 Second Equation of Motion: s = ut + (1/2)at²

Derivation of s = ut + (1/2)at²

Method: Using Velocity-Time Graph
From the v-t graph, the area under the curve represents displacement.
Step 1: The graph forms a trapezoid with parallel sides $u$ and $v$, and height $t$. $$\text{Area} = \text{displacement} = \frac{1}{2}(u + v) \times t$$
Step 2: Substitute $v = u + at$ from the first equation $$s = \frac{1}{2}(u + u + at) \times t = \frac{1}{2}(2u + at) \times t$$
Step 3: Expand $$s = \frac{1}{2} \times 2u \times t + \frac{1}{2} \times a \times t \times t$$ $$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$
SECOND EQUATION OF MOTION
$$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$ where $s$ = displacement (m), $u$ = initial velocity (m/s), $a$ = acceleration (m/s²), $t$ = time (s)
Application: This equation is useful when we know the initial velocity, acceleration, and time, and we need to find the displacement.

6.3 Third Equation of Motion: v² = u² + 2as

Derivation of v² = u² + 2as

Method: Eliminating time from the first two equations
From the first equation: $v = u + at$, we get $t = \frac{v - u}{a}$
Step 1: Substitute this expression for $t$ into the second equation $$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$ $$s = u \left(\frac{v - u}{a}\right) + \frac{1}{2}a\left(\frac{v - u}{a}\right)^2$$
Step 2: Simplify the first term $$s = \frac{u(v - u)}{a} + \frac{1}{2}a \times \frac{(v - u)^2}{a^2}$$ $$s = \frac{u(v - u)}{a} + \frac{(v - u)^2}{2a}$$
Step 3: Find a common denominator $$s = \frac{2u(v - u)}{2a} + \frac{(v - u)^2}{2a}$$ $$s = \frac{2u(v - u) + (v - u)^2}{2a}$$
Step 4: Expand and simplify the numerator $$s = \frac{2uv - 2u^2 + v^2 - 2uv + u^2}{2a}$$ $$s = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a}$$
Step 5: Rearrange $$2as = v^2 - u^2$$ $$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$
THIRD EQUATION OF MOTION
$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$ where $v$ = final velocity (m/s), $u$ = initial velocity (m/s), $a$ = acceleration (m/s²), $s$ = displacement (m)
Application: This equation is useful when we know the initial velocity, acceleration, and displacement, and we need to find the final velocity — without needing to know the time.

6.4 Summary Table of Equations

Equation Formula Use When Unknown Variable
First $v = u + at$ $u$, $a$, $t$ known $v$ (final velocity)
Second $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ $u$, $a$, $t$ known $s$ (displacement)
Third $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ $u$, $a$, $s$ known $v$ (final velocity)

7. Worked Examples with Detailed Solutions

Worked Example 2: Finding Average Speed

Problem: A car travels 100 km in 2 hours, then 60 km in 1 hour. Calculate the average speed for the entire journey.

Given:
First leg: Distance $d_1 = 100$ km, Time $t_1 = 2$ h
Second leg: Distance $d_2 = 60$ km, Time $t_2 = 1$ h
Step 1: Calculate total distance
$$d_{total} = d_1 + d_2 = 100 + 60 = 160 \text{ km}$$
Step 2: Calculate total time
$$t_{total} = t_1 + t_2 = 2 + 1 = 3 \text{ h}$$
Step 3: Calculate average speed
$$v_{avg} = \frac{d_{total}}{t_{total}} = \frac{160}{3} = 53.33 \text{ km/h}$$
Step 4: Convert to m/s (optional)
$$v_{avg} = 53.33 \times \frac{5}{18} = 14.81 \text{ m/s}$$
Answer: The average speed is 53.33 km/h or 14.81 m/s.

Worked Example 3: Uniformly Accelerated Motion

Problem: A train starting from rest accelerates uniformly at 2 m/s² for 10 seconds. Find (a) the final velocity, and (b) the distance traveled.

Given:
Initial velocity $u = 0$ m/s (starts from rest)
Acceleration $a = 2$ m/s²
Time $t = 10$ s
Part (a): Finding final velocity
Using the first equation of motion: $v = u + at$
$$v = 0 + (2)(10) = 20 \text{ m/s}$$
Part (b): Finding distance traveled
Using the second equation of motion: $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$
$$s = (0)(10) + \frac{1}{2}(2)(10)^2$$ $$s = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(2)(100) = 100 \text{ m}$$
Verification: Using the third equation
$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$ $$20^2 = 0^2 + 2(2)(100)$$ $$400 = 400$$ ✓
Answer:
(a) Final velocity = 20 m/s
(b) Distance traveled = 100 m

Worked Example 4: Braking and Deceleration

Problem: A car moving at 20 m/s applies brakes and stops in 5 seconds. Find (a) the acceleration (deceleration), and (b) the distance covered before stopping.

Given:
Initial velocity $u = 20$ m/s
Final velocity $v = 0$ m/s (stops)
Time $t = 5$ s
Part (a): Finding acceleration
Using the first equation: $v = u + at$
$$0 = 20 + a(5)$$ $$-5a = 20$$ $$a = -4 \text{ m/s}^2$$ The negative sign indicates deceleration (negative acceleration).
Part (b): Finding distance
Using the second equation: $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$
$$s = (20)(5) + \frac{1}{2}(-4)(5)^2$$ $$s = 100 - 50 = 50 \text{ m}$$
Alternative method: Using third equation
$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$ $$0 = (20)^2 + 2(-4)s$$ $$0 = 400 - 8s$$ $$s = 50 \text{ m}$$
Answer:
(a) Acceleration = -4 m/s² (or deceleration of 4 m/s²)
(b) Distance covered = 50 m

8. Uniform Circular Motion

Uniform Circular Motion: When an object moves in a circular path at constant speed, it is said to be in uniform circular motion. Although the speed is constant, the velocity is not constant because the direction of motion continuously changes.

Critical Insight: Even though the speed is constant (uniform), the velocity continuously changes direction. Therefore, uniform circular motion is ACCELERATED motion. There is a continuous change in velocity, hence acceleration exists.

8.1 Characteristics of Uniform Circular Motion

  • The object moves along a circular path at constant speed
  • The velocity vector is always tangent to the circular path
  • The velocity direction changes continuously at every instant
  • There is continuous acceleration directed toward the center (centripetal acceleration)
  • The magnitude of velocity remains constant, but its direction changes

8.2 Speed in Circular Motion

Time Period (T): The time taken by the object to complete one full circular revolution. SI unit: second (s).
SPEED IN CIRCULAR MOTION
$$v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$$ where $v$ = speed (m/s), $r$ = radius of circular path (m), $T$ = time period (s)

Explanation: In one time period $T$, the object travels a distance equal to the circumference of the circle, which is $2\pi r$. Therefore, speed = distance/time = $\frac{2\pi r}{T}$

8.3 Examples of Uniform Circular Motion

  • Earth orbiting the Sun: Despite constant speed, Earth's velocity changes direction continuously due to its curved path.
  • Artificial satellites: Orbiting satellites move in circular paths with changing velocity direction.
  • Tip of a clock hand: The tip of the hour hand or minute hand traces a circular path at constant speed but with changing velocity.
  • Electrons in atoms: According to some models, electrons orbit nuclei in circular paths.
  • Planets in orbits: Planets orbit their stars in approximately circular (or elliptical) paths.
Velocity Direction in Circular Motion: At any point on the circular path, the velocity is directed along the tangent to the circle at that point. The tangent direction is perpendicular to the radius at that point.

10.2 Case-Based Questions with Numerical Problems

Case Study 1: Car Journey Analysis

A car's journey consists of three segments:

  • Segment A: Starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 3 m/s² for 8 seconds
  • Segment B: Moves at constant velocity for 12 seconds
  • Segment C: Decelerates uniformly at 2 m/s² until it stops

Part (a): Find the velocity at the end of Segment A.

Solution: Using $v = u + at$ with $u = 0$, $a = 3$ m/s², $t = 8$ s:
$v = 0 + (3)(8) = 24$ m/s

Part (b): Calculate the distance covered in Segment A.

Solution: Using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:
$s = (0)(8) + \frac{1}{2}(3)(8)^2 = 0 + 96 = 96$ m

Part (c): Find the distance covered in Segment B (constant velocity motion).

Solution: Distance = velocity × time = 24 × 12 = 288 m

Part (d): Find the time taken in Segment C and distance covered.

Solution: Using $v = u + at$ with final velocity = 0, initial velocity = 24 m/s, acceleration = -2 m/s²:
$0 = 24 + (-2)t$
$t = 12$ s
Distance = $\frac{1}{2}(u + v)t = \frac{1}{2}(24 + 0)(12) = 144$ m

Part (e): Find the total distance and average speed for the entire journey.

Solution: Total distance = 96 + 288 + 144 = 528 m
Total time = 8 + 12 + 12 = 32 s
Average speed = 528/32 = 16.5 m/s

Case Study 2: Free Fall Motion (Approximation with g = 10 m/s²)

An object is dropped from a height and falls under gravity. Assuming $g = 10$ m/s² and neglecting air resistance:

Part (a): What is the velocity of the object after falling for 3 seconds?

Solution: Using $v = u + at$ with $u = 0$, $a = g = 10$ m/s², $t = 3$ s:
$v = 0 + (10)(3) = 30$ m/s (downward)

Part (b): Calculate the distance fallen in 3 seconds.

Solution: Using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}gt^2$:
$s = (0)(3) + \frac{1}{2}(10)(3)^2 = 0 + 5 \times 9 = 45$ m

Part (c): From what height must an object be dropped to reach a velocity of 20 m/s?

Solution: Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2gs$ with $v = 20$ m/s, $u = 0$, $g = 10$ m/s²:
$(20)^2 = 0 + 2(10)s$
$400 = 20s$
$s = 20$ m

Case Study 3: Train Acceleration Problem

A high-speed train accelerates from rest along a straight track.

Given Information:

  • Initial velocity: 0 m/s
  • Uniform acceleration: 2.5 m/s²
  • Distance to reach required speed: 500 m

Part (a): What velocity is reached after traveling 500 m?

Solution: Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ with $u = 0$, $a = 2.5$ m/s², $s = 500$ m:
$v^2 = 0 + 2(2.5)(500) = 2500$
$v = 50$ m/s

Part (b): How much time does it take to reach this velocity?

Solution: Using $v = u + at$:
$50 = 0 + (2.5)t$
$t = 20$ s

Part (c): If the train continues at this constant velocity of 50 m/s, how long would it take to cover 1 km?

Solution: Distance = 1 km = 1000 m, Velocity = 50 m/s
Time = Distance/Velocity = 1000/50 = 20 s

13. Remediation Guide - Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1: Distance and Displacement are the same

Wrong: Distance and displacement are interchangeable terms.

Correct: Distance is scalar (total path), displacement is vector (shortest path). A closed loop has non-zero distance but zero displacement.

Misconception 2: Speed and Velocity are the same

Wrong: Speed and velocity mean the same thing.

Correct: Speed is scalar (always positive), velocity is vector (with direction). An object can have non-zero speed with zero velocity (circular motion returning to start).

Misconception 3: Acceleration only means speeding up

Wrong: Acceleration always means the object is going faster.

Correct: Acceleration is any change in velocity (speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction). Zero acceleration means constant velocity. Negative acceleration (deceleration) means slowing down.

Misconception 4: Circular motion at constant speed has no acceleration

Wrong: If the speed is constant, there is no acceleration.

Correct: Circular motion always has acceleration because velocity (a vector) changes direction continuously. Speed being constant doesn't mean velocity is constant.

Misconception 5: The three equations of motion can be used for all types of motion

Wrong: The equations $v = u + at$, $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$, and $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ work for any motion.

Correct: These equations ONLY apply to uniformly accelerated motion (constant acceleration). For non-uniform acceleration, these equations cannot be used.

Misconception 6: Instantaneous speed equals average speed

Wrong: Instantaneous speed and average speed are the same.

Correct: Instantaneous speed is the speed at a particular moment (slope of tangent to distance-time curve). Average speed is total distance divided by total time. They are equal only for uniform motion.
