Motion in a Straight Line

Class 11 · Chapter 2 · CBSE Physics · NCERT

🎯 What Does It Truly Mean to Move?

Every moment, you're moving—the Earth spins on its axis, orbits the Sun, and travels through the galaxy. Yet, when you sit in a train, you might feel stationary while the landscape rushes past. This paradox lies at the heart of motion: it depends on your perspective. In this chapter, we'll explore motion from first principles—understanding how position changes with time, how velocity differs from speed, and why acceleration tells the deeper story of dynamics. By the end, you'll see that motion isn't just physics; it's the language of the universe.

📍 1. Introduction: What is Motion?

Change in Position with Time

Motion is fundamentally defined as a change in position of an object with respect to time. Without time, there is no motion—only location. Think of a snapshot: it captures position but tells nothing about motion. A movie, however, shows frames changing over time—that's motion.

Frame of Reference

Motion is relative. We cannot describe motion without specifying a frame of reference—a coordinate system from which we observe. When you sit in a stationary car, you're at rest relative to Earth. But relative to a satellite orbiting overhead, you're moving at thousands of kilometers per hour!

💡 Did You Know?
Galileo's principle of relativity (1632) established that the laws of motion are the same in all inertial (non-accelerating) frames of reference. This concept later inspired Einstein's theory of special relativity!

Point Object Approximation

In kinematics, we often treat extended objects as point objects—idealized bodies with mass concentrated at a single point. This simplification works when the object's size is negligible compared to distances traveled. A car moving between cities can be treated as a point; a car moving through a parking garage cannot.

One-Dimensional Motion

This chapter focuses on motion along a straight line (one dimension). Later chapters extend to 2D and 3D motion. A car on a straight highway, an athlete running on a track, or a ball falling vertically—these are all examples of 1D motion.

📏 2. Position, Path Length, and Displacement

Position as a Coordinate

In one dimension, position is simply a coordinate on a number line. If we choose our origin (0) and direction (positive to the right, negative to the left), every point has a unique position value. A particle at position \(x = 5 \text{ m}\) is 5 meters to the right of the origin.

Path Length: The Road Traveled

Path length is the total distance an object travels—the actual length of the route, always positive. If you drive from home to the market (5 km) and back (5 km), your path length is 10 km, regardless of direction.

Path Length (Scalar)

\[\text{Path Length} = \text{Total distance traveled} \geq 0\]

Always positive; doesn't consider direction; scalar quantity.

Displacement: Straight Line to the Destination

Displacement is the change in position—the straight-line distance from start to finish, including direction. Using the same example: from home to market and back, displacement is 0 because you've returned to your starting point!

Displacement (Vector)

\[\Delta x = x_f - x_i\]

where \(x_f\) = final position, \(x_i\) = initial position

Can be positive, negative, or zero; considers direction; vector quantity.

Quantity Type Always Positive? Considers Direction? Example
Path Length Scalar Yes No 10 km (home → market → home)
Displacement Vector No Yes 0 m (starts and ends at home)

The Relationship: Path Length ≥ |Displacement|

A fundamental principle: the magnitude of displacement can never exceed path length. The straight-line distance is always the shortest path.

Example 1: Position vs. Displacement

Scenario: A particle moves from \(x = 2 \text{ m}\) to \(x = 8 \text{ m}\) to \(x = 5 \text{ m}\).

Path Length: \(|8-2| + |5-8| = 6 + 3 = 9 \text{ m}\)

Displacement: \(\Delta x = 5 - 2 = 3 \text{ m}\)

Observation: Path length (9 m) > |displacement| (3 m) ✓

⚡ 3. Average and Instantaneous Velocity

Average Velocity: Direction Matters

Average velocity is the rate of change of displacement with respect to time. It's a vector—it has both magnitude and direction.

Average Velocity

\[\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_f - x_i}{t_f - t_i}\]

Units: m/s. Can be positive (right), negative (left), or zero.

Average Speed: Distance Over Time

Average speed is the rate of change of path length. It's a scalar—always positive.

Average Speed

\[\bar{s} = \frac{\text{Total Path Length}}{\Delta t}\]

Units: m/s. Always positive; ignores direction.

Key Insight: Average Speed ≥ |Average Velocity|

Because path length ≥ |displacement|, the average speed magnitude is always greater than or equal to the magnitude of average velocity. They're equal only when motion is unidirectional (no reversals).

Quantity Definition Vector/Scalar Formula
Avg Velocity Change in displacement per unit time Vector \(\bar{v} = \Delta x / \Delta t\)
Avg Speed Change in path length per unit time Scalar \(\bar{s} = \text{Path Length} / \Delta t\)

Example 2: Velocity vs. Speed

Scenario: You run 100 m east in 10 s, then 50 m west in 5 s.

Displacement: \(\Delta x = 100 - 50 = 50 \text{ m (east)}\)

Total time: \(\Delta t = 10 + 5 = 15 \text{ s}\)

Average velocity: \(\bar{v} = 50 / 15 = 3.33 \text{ m/s (east)}\)

Average speed: \(\bar{s} = (100 + 50) / 15 = 10 \text{ m/s}\)

Observation: Average speed (10 m/s) > |average velocity| (3.33 m/s) ✓

Instantaneous Velocity: The Limit

As time intervals shrink, average velocity approaches instantaneous velocity—the velocity at a specific instant.

Instantaneous Velocity (Derivative Form)

\[v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}\]

The slope of the position-time (x-t) graph at that instant.

Instantaneous Speed

Instantaneous speed is the magnitude of instantaneous velocity:

\[\text{Instantaneous speed} = |v| = \left| \frac{dx}{dt} \right|\]

The x-t Graph Interpretation

Position-Time Graphs

What it shows: An x-t graph plots position on the vertical axis and time on the horizontal axis.

Slope = Velocity: The slope at any point gives instantaneous velocity. A steep slope = high velocity. A horizontal line = zero velocity (object at rest).

Curved line: Indicates changing velocity (acceleration). Parabolic curves are characteristic of constant acceleration.

Example: If an x-t curve is a parabola opening upward, the object is accelerating in the positive direction.

⏩ 4. Acceleration: Rate of Change of Velocity

Average Acceleration

Average acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time.

Average Acceleration

\[\bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t_f - t_i}\]

Units: m/s². Can be positive (speeding up in positive direction or slowing down in negative direction) or negative.

Instantaneous Acceleration

Instantaneous acceleration is the acceleration at a specific instant—the derivative of velocity with respect to time:

Instantaneous Acceleration

\[a = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}\]

The second derivative of position; the slope of the v-t graph.

The v-t Graph Interpretation

Velocity-Time Graphs

What it shows: A v-t graph plots velocity on the vertical axis and time on the horizontal axis.

Slope = Acceleration: The slope at any point gives instantaneous acceleration. A horizontal line = zero acceleration (constant velocity).

Positive slope: Object is accelerating (velocity increasing). Negative slope (downward): Object is decelerating (velocity decreasing).

Area under curve = Displacement: The area between the v-t curve and the time axis gives total displacement during that time interval.

Example: For a v-t graph that's a straight line starting at 0 and reaching 10 m/s over 5 s: acceleration = (10-0)/(5-0) = 2 m/s², and displacement = (1/2) × 5 × 10 = 25 m.

Positive vs. Negative Acceleration

Positive acceleration: Velocity increasing in the positive direction (or decreasing in the negative direction).

Negative acceleration (Retardation): Velocity decreasing in the positive direction (or increasing in the negative direction).

💡 Did You Know?
"Deceleration" and "retardation" both describe slowing down, but physicists prefer "acceleration in the negative direction" or specify whether the object is speeding up or slowing down relative to its direction of motion. The sign of acceleration depends on the chosen positive direction!
Condition Sign of a Effect on Motion Example
v and a same sign Both + or both − Speeding up Car accelerating east (v>0, a>0)
v and a opposite signs + and − or − and + Slowing down Car braking while moving east (v>0, a<0)
a = 0 Zero Constant velocity Cruise control on highway

🧮 5. Kinematic Equations for Uniformly Accelerated Motion

When acceleration is constant, we can derive simple equations relating position, velocity, acceleration, and time. These are the kinematic equations—the workhorses of classical mechanics.

The Four Kinematic Equations

Equation 1: Velocity-Time Relation

\[v = v_0 + at\]

Relates velocity to acceleration and time. (No displacement.)

Equation 2: Position-Time Relation

\[x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\]

Relates displacement to initial velocity, acceleration, and time. (No final velocity.)

Equation 3: Velocity-Displacement Relation (No Time)

\[v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\]

Relates final and initial velocities to acceleration and displacement. Very useful when time is unknown.

Equation 4: Average Velocity Form

\[x = x_0 + \frac{1}{2}(v + v_0)t\]

Displacement equals average velocity times time.

Graphical Derivation of Equation 1: \(v = v_0 + at\)

On a v-t graph with constant acceleration, velocity changes linearly. Starting at \(v_0\) at time 0, the slope (acceleration) is constant:

\[\text{Slope} = a = \frac{v - v_0}{t - 0} \implies v = v_0 + at\]

Graphical Derivation of Equation 2: \(x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

The area under the v-t curve gives displacement. For constant acceleration:

Area = Rectangle + Triangle = \(v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}(at) \cdot t = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

\[\therefore x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\]

Derivation of Equation 3: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)

From Equation 1: \(v = v_0 + at \implies t = \frac{v - v_0}{a}\)

Substitute into Equation 2 and simplify:

\[\Delta x = v_0 \left(\frac{v - v_0}{a}\right) + \frac{1}{2}a\left(\frac{v - v_0}{a}\right)^2\]

\[a \Delta x = v_0(v - v_0) + \frac{1}{2}(v - v_0)^2 = (v - v_0)\left(v_0 + \frac{v - v_0}{2}\right) = (v - v_0) \cdot \frac{v + v_0}{2}\]

\[2a\Delta x = v^2 - v_0^2 \implies v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x\]

Example 3: Car Stopping Distance

Scenario: A car travels at 20 m/s (72 km/h) and applies brakes with deceleration of 5 m/s².

Find: (a) Time to stop, (b) Stopping distance

Solution:

(a) Using \(v = v_0 + at\):

\[0 = 20 + (-5)t \implies t = 4 \text{ s}\]

(b) Using \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x\):

\[0 = (20)^2 + 2(-5)\Delta x \implies \Delta x = \frac{400}{10} = 40 \text{ m}\]

Answer: The car takes 4 seconds to stop and travels 40 meters while braking.

Example 4: Free Fall from Rest

Scenario: A stone is dropped from a cliff. How far does it fall in 3 seconds? (Use \(g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2\))

Solution:

Using \(x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\) with \(v_0 = 0\), \(a = g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2\), \(t = 3 \text{ s}\):

\[x - x_0 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(10)(3)^2 = 5 \times 9 = 45 \text{ m}\]

Answer: The stone falls 45 meters in 3 seconds.

🔄 6. Relative Velocity: Motion in Different Frames

The Concept of Relative Velocity

The velocity of an object depends on the frame of reference. Relative velocity describes how fast an object is moving as seen from another moving observer.

Relative Velocity

\[v_{AB} = v_A - v_B\]

Velocity of A as observed by B (or relative to B).

Understanding the Notation

\(v_{AB}\) is read as "velocity of A with respect to B." It's the velocity you'd measure if you were at B's position moving at B's velocity.

Example 5: Trains Passing

Scenario: Train A travels east at 80 km/h. Train B travels east at 60 km/h on a parallel track.

Find: (a) Velocity of A relative to B (b) Velocity of B relative to ground

Solution:

(a) Taking east as positive:

\[v_{AB} = v_A - v_B = 80 - 60 = 20 \text{ km/h (east)}\]

Train A appears to move at 20 km/h eastward to someone on Train B.

(b) \(v_B = 60 \text{ km/h}\) (unchanged)

Example 6: Rain and Umbrella

Scenario: Rain falls vertically at 5 m/s (relative to ground). A person runs horizontally at 3 m/s.

Find: At what angle should the person tilt the umbrella to avoid getting wet?

Solution:

Relative velocity of rain with respect to the person:

\[v_{rain, person} = \sqrt{(5)^2 + (3)^2} = \sqrt{25 + 9} = \sqrt{34} \approx 5.83 \text{ m/s}\]

Direction: \[\tan \theta = \frac{3}{5} \implies \theta \approx 31° \text{ from vertical}\]

Answer: The umbrella should be tilted 31° in the direction of motion.

💡 Did You Know?
The concept of relative velocity is crucial in collision problems, rocket science, and even astronomy. When astronomers measure the velocity of distant galaxies, they're measuring velocities relative to Earth. The observation that most galaxies are moving away (with velocity relative to us) provided evidence for the expanding universe!

📉 7. Free Fall: Motion Under Gravity

Definition and Characteristics

Free fall is motion under the influence of gravity alone, with no other forces (like air resistance) acting on the object. On Earth, all freely falling objects experience the same acceleration: gAcceleration due to gravity at Earth's surface = 9.8 m/s² (or often approximated as 10 m/s² for calculations).

💡 Galileo's Insight
In the late 1500s, Galileo Galilei challenged Aristotle's claim that heavier objects fall faster. Using the leaning tower of Pisa (or inclined planes), Galileo showed that all objects, regardless of mass, fall with the same acceleration in the absence of air resistance. This was revolutionary!

Free Fall Equations

Free fall is just constant acceleration motion with \(a = g\). The kinematic equations become:

Free Fall Equations (Taking downward as positive)

\[v = v_0 + gt\]

\[y = y_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2\]

\[v^2 = v_0^2 + 2g(y - y_0)\]

where \(g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2 \approx 10 \text{ m/s}^2\) (downward positive)

Sign Conventions in Free Fall

Choose upward as positive or downward as positive—be consistent!

If Upward is Positive: Then:
g = -9.8 m/s² Acceleration acts downward (negative direction)
Upward velocity is +v Downward velocity is -v
Height increase is +Δy Height decrease is -Δy

Example 7: Vertical Motion (Upward then Downward)

Scenario: A ball is thrown upward from ground level with initial velocity 20 m/s. Find: (a) Maximum height, (b) Time to return to ground

Solution: Taking upward as positive, \(g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\)

(a) At maximum height, \(v = 0\):

\[v^2 = v_0^2 + 2g(y - y_0)\]

\[0 = (20)^2 + 2(-9.8)(h - 0)\]

\[h = \frac{400}{19.6} \approx 20.4 \text{ m}\]

(b) When it returns to ground, \(y = y_0 = 0\):

\[y = y_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2\]

\[0 = 0 + 20t + \frac{1}{2}(-9.8)t^2\]

\[20t - 4.9t^2 = 0 \implies t(20 - 4.9t) = 0\]

\[t = 0 \text{ or } t = \frac{20}{4.9} \approx 4.08 \text{ s}\]

Answer: Maximum height ≈ 20.4 m; Time to return ≈ 4.1 seconds.

Example 8: Dropped Object

Scenario: A brick is dropped from a building of height 45 m. How long does it take to hit the ground? What's its final velocity? (Use \(g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2\))

Solution: Taking downward as positive, \(v_0 = 0\), \(h = 45 \text{ m}\), \(g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2\)

(a) Time to fall:

\[h = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies 45 = 0 + 5t^2 \implies t = 3 \text{ s}\]

(b) Final velocity:

\[v = v_0 + gt = 0 + 10(3) = 30 \text{ m/s}\]

Answer: Time = 3 seconds; Final velocity = 30 m/s (downward).

🎓 Exam Preparation: Higher Order Thinking & Case Studies

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS Q1: Graphical Analysis of Complex Motion

Question: An object moves with the following motion pattern: 0–2 s: accelerates from 0 to 8 m/s; 2–5 s: moves at constant 8 m/s; 5–7 s: decelerates uniformly from 8 m/s to 0. Sketch the v-t graph and calculate the total displacement.

Analysis: Break into three phases. Phase 1 is triangular (0 to 8 m/s over 2 s), Phase 2 is rectangular (8 × 3), Phase 3 is triangular (8 to 0 over 2 s). Use v-t graph interpretation: area under curve = displacement.

Solution Hint: Displacement = Area₁ + Area₂ + Area₃ = ½(2×8) + (8×3) + ½(2×8) = 8 + 24 + 8 = 40 m

HOTS Q2: Two-Object Chase Problem

Question: Object A starts at position x = 0 with velocity 10 m/s (constant). Object B starts at x = 100 m with initial velocity 0 but acceleration 2 m/s². (a) When does B catch up to A? (b) What is the separation when both have the same velocity?

Analysis: Write position equations: \(x_A = 10t\) and \(x_B = 100 + 0 \cdot t + ½(2)t^2 = 100 + t^2\). For catch-up, set \(x_A = x_B\). For same velocity, use \(v_A = v_B\).

Solution Hint: (a) \(10t = 100 + t^2 \implies t^2 - 10t + 100 = 0\)... (use quadratic formula; check if real solutions exist). (b) \(10 = 0 + 2t \implies t = 5 \text{ s}\); separation = |10(5) - (100 + 25)| = 25 m

HOTS Q3: Integration of Concepts

Question: A ball is thrown vertically upward from a building of height 50 m with initial velocity 30 m/s. (a) What is the maximum height above the ground? (b) At what time(s) is the ball at height 80 m? (c) What is the velocity just before hitting the ground? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Analysis: Set up equation: \(y = 50 + 30t - 5t^2\). For max height, use calculus or \(v = 0\). For part (b), solve \(80 = 50 + 30t - 5t^2\). For part (c), use \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2g\Delta y\) or solve when y = 0.

Solution Hint: (a) Max height at \(t = 3\) s: \(y = 50 + 90 - 45 = 95 \text{ m}\). (b) Two times: rising and falling. (c) \(v = \sqrt{900 + 2(10)(50)} = 40 \text{ m/s (downward)}\)

Case-Based Questions

Case Q1: Highway Safety Analysis

Scenario: A highway has a speed limit of 80 km/h. During heavy braking, a car can decelerate at 8 m/s². A student driver claims they need 20 m to stop safely.

Question: (a) Convert 80 km/h to m/s. (b) Is the student's claim realistic? (c) What is the actual stopping distance? (d) If the speed were 100 km/h, how much additional distance is needed?

Hint: Use \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x\). For part (c), 80 km/h = 22.22 m/s; \(\Delta x = \frac{(22.22)^2}{2 \times 8} \approx 31 \text{ m}\).

Case Q2: Free Fall from Different Heights

Scenario: Skydivers practice at different altitudes. At 1000 m altitude (negligible air resistance for initial phase), a skydiver jumps with zero initial velocity.

Question: (a) How long until reaching 500 m altitude? (b) What is the velocity at 500 m? (c) How long total for ground impact? (d) Compare kinetic energies at 500 m and ground level (mass = 70 kg).

Hint: Use \(y = y_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) and \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2g\Delta y\). KE = ½mv². Note: At 500 m fall, \(\Delta y = 500\) m; \(v = \sqrt{2 \times 10 \times 500} = 100 \text{ m/s}\).

Case Q3: Relative Motion in Sports

Scenario: In cricket, a bowler throws a ball at 30 m/s relative to Earth. The batter (running toward the bowler) moves at 5 m/s. From the batter's perspective, how fast is the ball approaching?

Questions: (a) Calculate relative velocity of ball with respect to batter. (b) If the reaction time is 0.5 s, how far must the batter's bat reach to intercept the ball? (c) Explain why faster movement toward incoming objects increases apparent speed.

Hint: \(v_{ball, batter} = v_{ball} - v_{batter} = 30 - (-5) = 35 \text{ m/s}\) (ball approaching at 35 m/s). In 0.5 s, it covers 17.5 m, but this varies with position.

Glossary of Key Terms

Acceleration Rate of change of velocity; can be positive (speeding up in positive direction) or negative (slowing down or moving backward). Units: m/s².
Average Speed Total path length divided by total time taken. Always positive scalar. Can exceed average velocity magnitude.
Average Velocity Displacement divided by time taken. Vector quantity with direction. Can be zero even if object moved.
Deceleration Commonly used term for negative acceleration or slowing down. Physicists prefer specifying the direction of acceleration.
Displacement Change in position from initial to final location. Vector quantity; can be negative, positive, or zero.
Frame of Reference Coordinate system from which motion is observed. All motion is relative to a chosen frame; no absolute frame exists.
Free Fall Motion under gravity alone, with no air resistance. All objects near Earth's surface experience g ≈ 9.8 m/s² downward.
Instantaneous Velocity Velocity at a specific instant; mathematically, the derivative of position with respect to time (slope of x-t graph at that point).
Kinematics Branch of mechanics studying motion without considering causes (forces). Focuses on position, velocity, and acceleration.
Path Length Total distance traveled along the actual path taken. Always positive scalar; always ≥ |displacement|.
Position Location of an object on a coordinate system. In 1D, expressed as a single number (coordinate on a number line).
Relative Velocity Velocity of one object as observed from another moving frame. Calculated as difference of individual velocities.
Uniformly Accelerated Motion Motion with constant acceleration. Velocities change linearly with time; position changes quadratically with time.
v-t Graph Graph with velocity on vertical axis and time on horizontal. Slope = acceleration; area under curve = displacement.
x-t Graph Graph with position on vertical axis and time on horizontal. Slope = velocity; curve shape reveals acceleration.

Common Mistakes & Remediation

❌ Mistake 1: Confusing Path Length and Displacement

Students often add all distances as "displacement." Remember: Displacement only cares about start and end points.

✓ Fix: Always draw a diagram showing initial and final positions. Displacement = final − initial, regardless of intermediate path.

❌ Mistake 2: Sign Errors in Kinematic Equations

Forgetting that acceleration can be negative. A car slowing down has negative acceleration if moving in the positive direction.

✓ Fix: Always establish a sign convention first. Define which direction is positive, then apply consistently. Check if your answer makes physical sense.

❌ Mistake 3: Misinterpreting Graphs

Slope of x-t is velocity (not distance). Area under v-t is displacement (not time). Curving lines indicate changing velocity.

✓ Fix: Memorize: x-t slope = v; v-t slope = a; v-t area = Δx. Practice drawing graphs for different scenarios.

❌ Mistake 4: Relative Velocity Calculation

Computing \(v_{AB} = v_A + v_B\) instead of \(v_{AB} = v_A - v_B\). Opposite-direction motions need careful sign handling.

✓ Fix: Write it out: "Velocity of A with respect to B." Remember the formula is always \(v_{AB} = v_A - v_B\). Check directions.

❌ Mistake 5: Forgetting Initial Conditions

Dropping \(v_0\) or \(x_0\) terms. Not all motion starts from rest or origin!

✓ Fix: Always identify: Is the object starting from rest (\(v_0 = 0\))? From the origin (\(x_0 = 0\))? Write the full equation with all terms.
