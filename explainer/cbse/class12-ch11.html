1. Photoelectric Effect

Observations of the Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect is the phenomenon in which electrons are emitted from a material when light falls on it. This occurs across a wide range of materials and frequencies, though with different efficiencies depending on the material and light wavelength.

  • Electron Emission: When light of sufficient energy strikes a metal surface, electrons are ejected almost instantaneously.
  • Kinetic Energy: The kinetic energy of ejected electrons is independent of the intensity of incident light but depends on its frequency.
  • Saturation Current: Beyond a certain potential difference, the photoelectric current reaches a maximum value called saturation current, which is proportional to light intensity.
  • Stopping Potential: A negative potential (retarding potential) is needed to stop even the fastest electrons from reaching the anode.
  • Threshold Frequency: For each metal, there exists a minimum frequency ν₀ (threshold frequency) below which no photoelectric emission occurs, regardless of intensity.

Threshold Frequency (ν₀)

The threshold frequency is the minimum frequency of incident light required to cause photoelectric emission. Below this frequency, no electrons are emitted, no matter how intense the light is.

ν₀ = φ/h where: φ = work function (in joules) h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)

The threshold wavelength (λ₀) is related to threshold frequency by:

λ₀ = c/ν₀ = hc/φ where: c = speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s)

Stopping Potential (V₀)

Stopping potential is the negative potential (retarding potential) that just prevents the most energetic photoelectrons from reaching the anode. At this potential, the kinetic energy of the fastest electrons equals the work done against the electric field.

K_max = eV₀ where: e = charge of electron (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C) V₀ = stopping potential (in volts)
The stopping potential is independent of light intensity but varies with the frequency of incident light. A graph of stopping potential vs. frequency is linear with slope h/e.

2. Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

The Equation and Work Function

Einstein explained the photoelectric effect by proposing that light consists of discrete packets of energy called photons. Each photon carries energy equal to hν. When a photon strikes a metal surface, it transfers all its energy to a single electron.

EINSTEIN'S PHOTOELECTRIC EQUATION: hν = φ + K_max OR K_max = hν - φ = hν - hν₀ where: h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s) ν = frequency of incident light φ = work function of the metal hν₀ = φ (energy required to remove one electron) K_max = maximum kinetic energy of photoelectron

Work Function (φ)

The work function is the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the surface of a metal. It is a characteristic property of the material and depends on the nature of the metal and its surface condition.

Work function φ = hν₀, where ν₀ is the threshold frequency. For photoelectric emission to occur, the incident photon energy must be at least equal to the work function: hν ≥ φ.

Alternative Form Using Stopping Potential

Since K_max = eV₀, Einstein's equation can be written as:

eV₀ = hν - φ V₀ = (h/e)ν - φ/e where: φ/e = work function in electron volts (eV) h/e = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV·s

Work Functions of Common Metals

Metal Work Function (eV) Work Function (J) Threshold Wavelength (nm)
Cesium (Cs) 1.9 3.04 × 10⁻¹⁹ 653
Potassium (K) 2.3 3.68 × 10⁻¹⁹ 540
Sodium (Na) 2.75 4.40 × 10⁻¹⁹ 450
Aluminum (Al) 4.28 6.85 × 10⁻¹⁹ 290
Copper (Cu) 4.47 7.16 × 10⁻¹⁹ 277
Zinc (Zn) 4.6 7.37 × 10⁻¹⁹ 269

Physical Interpretation

  • Energy Conservation: The energy of the incident photon (hν) is used to overcome the work function (φ) and to provide kinetic energy to the ejected electron (K_max).
  • No Threshold with Classical Theory: Classical electromagnetic theory predicted that increasing light intensity would increase electron energy, which contradicts observations. Einstein's photon theory resolves this.
  • Independence from Intensity: K_max depends only on frequency (ν), not on intensity. Intensity affects the number of photons and hence the number of photoelectrons (saturation current).
  • Instantaneous Emission: Photoelectric emission is instantaneous (< 10⁻⁹ s), which is explained by photon transfer of energy to individual electrons.

3. Photon Picture of Light

Energy of a Photon

A photon is a discrete packet of electromagnetic radiation carrying energy and momentum. The energy of a photon is directly proportional to its frequency and inversely proportional to its wavelength.

E = hν = hc/λ where: h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s) ν = frequency of light (Hz) c = speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s) λ = wavelength of light (m)
Example: Calculate the energy of a photon with wavelength 500 nm (green light).
E = hc/λ = (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ × 3 × 10⁸)/(500 × 10⁻⁹)
E = 3.98 × 10⁻¹⁹ J ≈ 2.48 eV

Momentum of a Photon

Although photons have no rest mass, they carry momentum. This momentum is related to their energy and wavelength by de Broglie's relation.

p = h/λ = E/c = hν/c where: p = momentum of photon (kg·m/s) h = Planck's constant λ = wavelength E = energy of photon c = speed of light

Effective Mass of a Photon

Although photons have zero rest mass, they have an effective mass when moving. This concept helps understand photon-matter interactions and radiation pressure.

m = E/c² = hν/c² This is the RELATIVISTIC MASS concept: From E = mc², we get m = E/c² Since photons always travel at c, we use: E = hν and p = h/λ = hν/c

Radiation Pressure

Since photons carry momentum, when they strike a surface, they exert pressure. This radiation pressure can be calculated from the momentum transfer.

Radiation Pressure (P) = Energy per unit area per unit time / c P = I/c where: I = intensity of light (W/m²) c = speed of light

Characteristics of Photons

  • Always travel at speed of light (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)
  • Have zero rest mass but finite momentum
  • Energy depends only on frequency: E = hν
  • Cannot be created or destroyed, only converted
  • Carry both energy and momentum
  • Exhibit both wave and particle properties (wave-particle duality)

4. de Broglie Hypothesis: Wave Nature of Matter

de Broglie's Hypothesis

In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that just as light exhibits both wave and particle properties, electrons and other material particles also possess wave-like properties. Every moving particle has an associated wavelength called the de Broglie wavelength.

DE BROGLIE WAVELENGTH: λ = h/p = h/mv where: h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s) p = momentum of particle (kg·m/s) m = mass of particle (kg) v = velocity of particle (m/s)
The de Broglie wavelength is inversely proportional to momentum. Particles with larger momentum have shorter wavelengths and vice versa.

de Broglie Wavelength for Electrons

For electrons accelerated through a potential difference V, the wavelength can be expressed in a more convenient form.

For electrons accelerated through potential V: K.E. = eV = (1/2)mv² p = √(2meV) λ = h/√(2meV) λ = 1.227/√V nm where: V = accelerating potential (volts) λ = wavelength (nanometers) The constant 1.227 comes from: h/√(2me) = 1.227 × 10⁻⁹ m·√V⁻¹

Key Results and Examples

  • At V = 100 V: λ = 1.227/√100 = 0.1227 nm = 1.227 Å
  • At V = 1000 V: λ = 1.227/√1000 = 0.0388 nm = 0.388 Å
  • At V = 10000 V: λ = 1.227/√10000 = 0.01227 nm = 0.1227 Å
Example Calculation: Find the de Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through 150 V.
λ = 1.227/√150 = 1.227/12.247 = 0.1002 nm ≈ 1.00 Å
This wavelength is comparable to atomic dimensions (~1-3 Å), explaining why electron waves exhibit diffraction and interference.

Wave-Particle Duality

de Broglie's hypothesis establishes that all particles exhibit wave properties. The dual nature of matter is now universally accepted:

Property Particle Nature Wave Nature
Localization Well-defined position Delocalized over space
Energy E = K.E. + P.E. E = hν
Momentum p = mv p = h/λ
Behavior Follows classical mechanics Shows diffraction, interference
Detection Discrete hits on detector Continuous pattern distribution

Significance and Applications

  • Electron Microscopy: Due to their short wavelength, electrons can resolve details smaller than visible light (microscope resolution limited by wavelength).
  • Crystal Structure: Electron diffraction patterns reveal crystal structures, similar to X-ray diffraction.
  • Quantum Mechanics: Forms the foundation of quantum mechanics and the Schrödinger wave equation.
  • Tunneling: Explains quantum tunneling, where particles pass through potential barriers.
  • Atomic Structure: Explains why electrons exist in discrete energy levels (standing wave pattern around nucleus).

5. Davisson-Germer Experiment: Confirmation of Electron Waves

Experimental Setup

In 1927, Clinton Davisson and Lester Germer performed an experiment that provided experimental confirmation of de Broglie's hypothesis. They demonstrated that electrons, like X-rays, exhibit diffraction when scattered by atoms in a crystal.

  • Electron Gun: Produced a beam of electrons with controlled kinetic energy (measured in eV)
  • Crystal Target: Used a nickel crystal with known atomic spacing (d = 0.215 nm)
  • Detector: Measured the number of scattered electrons at different angles
  • Variable Accelerating Voltage: Changed electron energy (50-150 eV) to vary the de Broglie wavelength

Experimental Procedure

  1. Electrons from a hot filament are accelerated through a potential difference V
  2. The electron beam hits a nickel crystal at a fixed angle of incidence
  3. Electrons are scattered in various directions
  4. A detector measures the number of electrons scattered at different angles
  5. The process is repeated for different accelerating voltages

Key Observations

  • Diffraction Peaks: Sharp maximum in scattered electron intensity at specific angles
  • Bragg's Law Compliance: The diffraction patterns followed Bragg's law of crystal diffraction (2d sin θ = nλ)
  • Wavelength Matching: The measured wavelengths matched perfectly with de Broglie's theoretical predictions
  • Variable with Energy: As accelerating voltage increased, wavelength decreased, and diffraction maxima shifted

Bragg's Law and Diffraction Analysis

BRAGG'S LAW: 2d sin θ = nλ where: d = spacing between atomic planes (0.215 nm for Ni crystal) θ = angle of incidence (angle between ray and crystal plane) n = order of diffraction (1, 2, 3, ...) λ = wavelength of electrons

Quantitative Results

For the first-order diffraction (n = 1) at θ = 65°:

2 × 0.215 × sin(65°) = λ 2 × 0.215 × 0.906 = λ λ = 0.390 nm Using de Broglie's formula: For V = 54 V: λ = 1.227/√54 = 0.167 nm (1st order, n=1 not satisfied) For V = 50 V: λ = 1.227/√50 = 0.174 nm For higher accelerating voltages: different diffraction orders observed

Experimental Verification

Accelerating Voltage (V) de Broglie λ (nm) Observed Diffraction Bragg's Condition
50 0.174 Strong diffraction peaks 2d sin θ matched observed
60 0.158 Peaks at different angles Shifts as λ decreases
100 0.123 Additional higher-order peaks n = 2, 3 diffraction orders
150 0.100 More diffraction orders visible Smaller λ → multiple orders

Theoretical Predictions vs. Experimental Results

The remarkable agreement between theoretical de Broglie wavelengths and experimental diffraction patterns provided conclusive proof that electrons behave like waves. This single experiment confirmed the wave nature of matter and established the wave-particle duality principle that forms the foundation of quantum mechanics.

Significance of the Experiment

  • De Broglie Confirmation: Experimentally proved de Broglie's hypothesis that moving electrons possess wave properties
  • Wave-Particle Duality: Established that matter exhibits both particle and wave characteristics
  • Quantum Mechanics Foundation: Provided crucial evidence supporting quantum mechanics
  • Crystal Diffraction: Showed that electron diffraction can be used to study crystal structures, leading to electron microscopy
  • Universal Wave Nature: Demonstrated that all matter, not just light, exhibits wave behavior
  • Nobel Prize Recognition: Both Davisson and Germer received the Nobel Prize for this discovery

Modern Applications

  • Electron Microscopes: Use electron diffraction and short de Broglie wavelengths to achieve atomic-scale resolution
  • X-ray Diffraction: Electron diffraction techniques similar to X-ray crystallography for structure determination
  • Quantum Computing: Understanding electron waves is crucial for designing quantum devices
  • Surface Science: Low-energy electron diffraction (LEED) studies surface structures

Question 1: Which metal will definitely NOT show photoelectric emission?
