Ever wondered why you hear an echo in a canyon, or how dogs can hear sounds you can't? Dive into the fascinating world of sound waves, vibrations, and the invisible waves traveling through the air around you right now!

🎵 Production of Sound

Sound is one of the most familiar phenomena we experience daily. But what exactly creates sound? The answer is elegantly simple: vibrationRapid back-and-forth movement of objects.

What is Vibration?

Vibration is the rapid to-and-fro (back and forth) motion of an object. Every sound you hear originates from something vibrating. This could be:

  • Tuning fork: When struck, the prongs oscillate back and forth
  • Vocal cords: Vibrate as air passes through them, creating your voice
  • Stretched rubber band: Oscillates when plucked
  • Drum surface: Vibrates when hit with a stick or hand
  • Guitar string: Oscillates when plucked or strummed

How Do We Produce Sound?

Objects vibrate through various methods:

  • Plucking: Like a guitar string
  • Scratching: Like a nail on a surface
  • Rubbing: Like a violin bow on strings
  • Blowing: Wind instruments like flutes
  • Shaking: Like a rattle or bell
  • Hitting/Striking: Drums, cymbals, or percussion instruments

Example: Human Voice Production

Your voice is produced when air from your lungs passes through your vocal cords (two folds of tissue in your larynx). These vocal cords vibrate rapidly, creating sound waves. The frequency of vibration determines whether your voice is high or low-pitched. Men typically have lower-frequency vocal cord vibrations (lower pitch), while women and children have higher frequencies (higher pitch).

Key Takeaway:
  • Sound is produced by vibrating objects
  • Vibration is rapid back-and-forth motion
  • Different methods of vibration create different sounds

🌊 Propagation of Sound

Now that we understand how sound is produced, the next question is: How does sound travel from one place to another? This is where the concept of propagation comes in.

Sound Needs a Medium

This is perhaps the most important principle about sound: Sound cannot travel through a vacuumA space with no matter or air. Sound always requires a mediumAny substance (solid, liquid, or gas) through which sound can travel to travel through:

  • Solids: Metal, wood, concrete, walls
  • Liquids: Water, oil
  • Gases: Air (most common), helium

Why No Sound in Space?

In outer space, there is no atmosphere or medium. This is why astronauts cannot hear each other unless they use radios. However, light can travel through the vacuum of space because light is an electromagnetic wave, not a mechanical wave like sound.

How Does Sound Travel?

When an object vibrates, it pushes against the particles of the medium around it. These particles then push against neighboring particles, creating a disturbance that travels through the medium. Important: The particles themselves don't travel to your ear—only the disturbance travels.

Compressions and Rarefactions

Sound waves are characterized by alternating regions of high and low density:

  • Compressions (C): Regions where particles are crowded together, resulting in high pressure and high densityMore particles in the same space
  • Rarefactions (R): Regions where particles are spread apart, resulting in low pressure and low densityFewer particles in the same space
A sound wave in its simplest form: C—R—C—R—C—R...

Where C = Compression, R = Rarefaction

Longitudinal Waves

Sound waves are longitudinal wavesWaves where particle motion is parallel to wave direction. This means particles move back and forth parallel to (in the same direction as) the direction the wave is traveling. This is different from transverse wavesWaves where particle motion is perpendicular to wave direction like water waves or light waves, where particles move perpendicular to (at right angles to) the wave direction.

Longitudinal vs Transverse: A Comparison

Sound (Longitudinal): Particles vibrate back and forth along the direction of sound propagation. Imagine pushing and pulling a spring along its length.

Water Waves (Transverse): Particles bob up and down perpendicular to the direction waves travel. The wave moves forward while particles move up and down.

Key Takeaway:
  • Sound needs a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel
  • Sound cannot travel through a vacuum
  • Particles don't move far; the disturbance travels
  • Sound waves consist of compressions and rarefactions
  • Sound waves are longitudinal waves

📊 Characteristics of Sound Waves

Every sound wave has specific characteristics that determine how we perceive it. Understanding these properties helps us describe and compare different sounds scientifically.

Frequency (ν)

FrequencyThe number of complete oscillations or vibrations per unit time is the number of vibrations or oscillations per second. It's measured in Hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 oscillation per second.

$$\nu = \frac{\text{Number of oscillations}}{\text{Time}}$$

Unit: Hertz (Hz) = oscillations/second

Frequency Example

If a tuning fork vibrates 256 times in one second, its frequency is 256 Hz. A higher frequency means more vibrations per second.

Time Period (T)

Time periodThe time taken for one complete oscillation is the time taken for one complete oscillation. It's measured in seconds.

$$T = \frac{1}{\nu}$$

Frequency and time period are inversely related

Wavelength (λ)

WavelengthThe distance between two consecutive compressions or rarefactions is the distance between two consecutive compressions or two consecutive rarefactions. It's measured in meters (m).

Visualizing Wavelength

If you imagine a sound wave: C—R—C—R—C, the wavelength (λ) is the distance from one C to the next C (or one R to the next R).

Amplitude (A)

AmplitudeThe maximum displacement of a particle from its mean (rest) position is the maximum displacement of particles from their mean position. Measured in meters.

Amplitude determines loudness: Larger amplitude = louder sound. Smaller amplitude = softer sound.

Speed of Sound

Sound travels at different speeds depending on the medium and temperature. The relationship between speed (v), wavelength (λ), and frequency (ν) is:

$$v = \lambda \nu$$

Speed = Wavelength × Frequency

Calculating Speed

A sound wave has a frequency of 2 kHz (2000 Hz) and wavelength of 0.35 m (35 cm).

Speed = λ × ν = 0.35 × 2000 = 700 m/s

This is much faster than sound in air (around 346 m/s), suggesting this sound is traveling through a denser medium.

Pitch and Loudness

Pitch: How high or low a sound seems. Determined by frequency.

  • High frequency → High pitch (treble, high notes)
  • Low frequency → Low pitch (bass, deep notes)

Loudness: How soft or loud a sound seems. Determined by amplitude.

  • Large amplitude → Louder sound
  • Small amplitude → Softer sound

Quality/Timbre

Quality (or timbre)The characteristic that distinguishes sounds of the same pitch and loudness is what makes a piano sound different from a violin even if they play the same note at the same volume. It's determined by the mixture of different frequencies present in the sound.

Tone, Note, and Noise

  • Tone: A sound of single frequency (pure, like a tuning fork)
  • Note: A mixture of multiple frequencies with a pleasant quality (like musical notes)
  • Noise: An unpleasant sound with irregular frequency distribution
Key Takeaway:
  • Frequency (ν): Oscillations per second in Hertz
  • Time period (T): Time for one oscillation, T = 1/ν
  • Wavelength (λ): Distance between compressions or rarefactions
  • Amplitude (A): Maximum displacement, determines loudness
  • Speed: v = λν
  • Pitch depends on frequency; loudness depends on amplitude

⚡ Speed of Sound in Different Media

Sound travels at different speeds depending on two main factors: the nature of the medium and its temperature.

Key Principle

Speed in solids > Speed in liquids > Speed in gases (at the same temperature)

Why? Because in solids, particles are tightly packed and closely bonded, allowing disturbances to transfer quickly. In gases, particles are far apart, so the disturbance travels more slowly.

Speed of Sound at Different Temperatures

In Air:

  • At 0°C: 331 m/s
  • At 22°C: 344 m/s

Notice that as temperature increases, the speed of sound increases. This is true for all media.

Speed in Various Materials

Medium Speed (m/s) Type
Aluminium 6,420 Solid
Steel 5,960 Solid
Water 1,531 Liquid
Air (at 22°C) 346 Gas

Application: Measuring Distance Using Sound

If you hear thunder 5 seconds after seeing lightning (light travels almost instantaneously), and the speed of sound in air is 346 m/s:

Distance = Speed × Time = 346 × 5 = 1,730 meters (1.73 km)

The lightning strike occurred about 1.73 km away.

Effect of Temperature

In any medium, speed of sound increases with temperature. This is because higher temperature means particles have more kinetic energy and can transfer the disturbance more quickly.

Mathematical Relationship Between Speed, Wavelength, and Frequency

We learned that v = λν. This relationship tells us something important: if the speed of sound in a medium is constant and the frequency of a source doesn't change, then the wavelength must remain constant. However, if sound travels from one medium to another (like from air to water), the speed changes, so the wavelength changes too, even though the frequency remains the same.

Example: A 1 kHz sound has a wavelength of 0.34 m in air (at 22°C, speed = 340 m/s). In water (speed = 1531 m/s), the same frequency has a wavelength of 1.531 m. The sound got "stretched" because it travels faster in water!

Key Takeaway:
  • Speed in solids > liquids > gases at same temperature
  • Speed increases with temperature in any medium
  • Speed of sound in air at 22°C ≈ 346 m/s
  • Speed of sound in water ≈ 1,531 m/s
  • Use distance = speed × time to calculate distances

🔊 Reflection of Sound

Just like light, sound can be reflected. When sound waves hit a hard surface, they bounce back according to the same laws that govern light reflection.

Laws of Reflection

Sound obeys the same laws of reflection as light:

  • Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection (both measured from the normal to the surface)
  • The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal all lie in the same plane

Why Hard Surfaces Reflect Sound Better

Smooth, hard surfaces like concrete, marble, and glass reflect sound very well because they don't absorb much sound energy. Soft, porous surfaces like foam, carpets, and curtains absorb sound and reflect very little.

Echo: Reflected Sound

An echoA reflected sound that we hear as a separate sound is the reflected sound that reaches your ear after bouncing off a surface. For an echo to be heard as a distinct sound (separate from the original), the time gap between the original and reflected sound must be at least 0.1 seconds.

$$d = \frac{v \times t}{2}$$

Where d = distance to reflecting surface, v = speed of sound, t = time gap

The factor of 2 accounts for the sound traveling to the surface and back

Minimum Distance for Distinct Echo

For a distinct echo with a time gap of 0.1 seconds at 344 m/s (speed in air at 22°C):

Calculating Minimum Echo Distance

d = (v × t) / 2 = (344 × 0.1) / 2 = 17.2 meters

This means you need to be at least 17.2 meters away from a reflecting surface to hear a distinct echo. A canyon wall or large building at this distance would produce an obvious echo.

Reverberation

ReverberationThe persistence of sound in a space due to repeated reflections is the persistence of sound due to multiple reflections from different surfaces. In concert halls, auditoriums, and churches, you hear the original sound plus many reflected copies arriving at slightly different times, creating a "lingering" or "echoing" effect.

Problem: Excessive reverberation makes speech difficult to understand in large halls.

Solution: Use sound-absorbing materialsMaterials that reduce sound reflection like:

  • Fibreboard
  • Rough plaster
  • Draperies and curtains
  • Carpets and rugs
  • Foam panels

Reverberation in Your Bathroom

Ever notice how your voice sounds like you're singing in a cathedral when you sing in the bathroom? This is due to reverberation. Hard tiles reflect sound, and the small space causes multiple reflections to reach your ear in quick succession. A bathroom has very few sound-absorbing materials.

Key Takeaway:
  • Sound reflects following the same laws as light
  • Echo is distinct when time gap ≥ 0.1 seconds
  • Minimum echo distance at 344 m/s ≈ 17.2 meters
  • Reverberation is caused by multiple reflections
  • Use sound-absorbing materials to reduce reverberation

🎤 Uses of Multiple Reflection of Sound

The reflection of sound has many practical applications. By strategically using multiple reflections, we can direct sound, amplify it, or transmit it effectively.

Megaphones and Horns

A megaphone uses a conical or flared tube shape to reflect sound waves. The sound from your mouth enters the tube and reflects off the inner curved surface, directing all the sound in one direction (forward). This concentrates the sound energy and makes it much louder in that direction, while very little sound goes sideways or backwards.

How a Megaphone Works

Without a megaphone: Your voice spreads out in all directions equally. With a megaphone: The conical walls reflect sound forward, concentrating it like a spotlight of sound. A person in front of the megaphone hears a much louder version of your voice, while someone to the side hears less.

Stethoscope

A stethoscope used by doctors works on a different principle. The earpieces are designed so sound (like heartbeats) enters from the small diameter tube at the chest piece. Multiple reflections inside the two branches of the Y-tube concentrate and deliver the sound effectively to both of the doctor's ears, allowing them to hear faint internal sounds clearly.

Concert Halls and Auditoriums

The ceiling and walls of concert halls are carefully curved (often parabolic or curved surfaces) to reflect sound waves uniformly throughout the hall. This ensures that:

  • Sound reaches all audience members with similar intensity
  • No "dead zones" exist where sound is too soft
  • Multiple reflections create pleasant, diffused sound rather than harsh echoes

Soundboards Behind Stages

You often see a large, curved, hard surface (soundboard or reflector) behind the stage in auditoriums. This curved board reflects sound from the performers toward the audience, ensuring:

  • Improved sound projection
  • Better clarity for listeners far from the stage
  • Uniform sound distribution
Why Curved Surfaces Instead of Flat?

A flat surface reflects sound according to the law of reflection—if sound comes in at an angle, it reflects at an equal angle. A curved surface can focus sound (like a concave mirror focuses light). Parabolic curves, in particular, reflect all parallel rays to a single focal point, making them excellent for concentrating or distributing sound effectively.

Key Takeaway:
  • Megaphones direct sound in one direction using conical tubes
  • Stethoscopes use multiple reflections to amplify heartbeat sounds
  • Concert hall ceilings use curves to spread sound evenly
  • Soundboards reflect stage sound to the audience
  • Strategic reflection can focus or distribute sound effectively

👂 Range of Hearing

Not all sounds are audible to all creatures. Different animals have different hearing ranges. Understanding the full spectrum of sound helps us appreciate nature and develop technology.

Human Audible Range

Humans can hear frequencies between approximately 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz).

  • Lower limit: 20 Hz (very deep bass sounds)
  • Upper limit: 20 kHz (very high treble sounds, like a whistle)

This range varies slightly with age. Children and dogs can hear frequencies up to about 25 kHz, but as humans age, the upper limit decreases.

Infrasound

InfrasoundSound with frequency below 20 Hz is sound with frequency below 20 Hz. Humans cannot hear infrasound, but many animals can:

  • Elephants communicate using infrasound over long distances (up to 10 km)
  • Whales and dolphins use infrasound for long-range ocean communication
  • Rhinoceroses produce infrasound

Natural Sources: Earthquakes produce infrasound, which animals can sense before humans even know an earthquake is happening. This may explain how some animals "predict" earthquakes.

Elephant Communication

Elephants in the African savanna communicate across vast distances using infrasound calls. A call at 14 Hz can travel over 10 kilometers through the ground and air. Other elephants sense these vibrations, allowing them to coordinate herd movements across huge territories.

Ultrasound

UltrasoundSound with frequency above 20 kHz is sound with frequency above 20 kHz. Many animals produce and use ultrasound:

  • Dolphins use ultrasound echolocation (up to 130 kHz)
  • Bats produce ultrasound for navigation and hunting
  • Porpoises use ultrasound for communication
  • Shrews and some other small animals also produce ultrasound

Bat Echolocation

Bats emit ultrasonic calls (20-200 kHz) and listen to the echoes bouncing off insects and obstacles. The time delay and frequency shift (Doppler effect) of the echo tells the bat the size, distance, and even the wingbeat frequency of prey. This biological sonar is incredibly precise—a bat can detect a human hair!

SONAR: Sound Navigation and Ranging

SONARAcronym for Sound Navigation And Ranging is a technology that uses ultrasound to detect and locate objects underwater. SONAR works by emitting ultrasonic waves and analyzing the reflected echoes.

$$d = \frac{v \times t}{2}$$

Where d = distance to object, v = speed of sound in water, t = time for echo to return

SONAR Application: Ocean Depth Measurement

A ship sends an ultrasonic pulse downward. The sound travels through water (speed ≈ 1531 m/s), hits the ocean floor, and reflects back. If the echo returns after 2 seconds:

Depth = (1531 × 2) / 2 = 1,531 meters

SONAR is used to map ocean floors, detect submarines, and locate fish schools.

Frequency Spectrum of Sound

The complete frequency spectrum of sound can be visualized as:

Infrasound (< 20 Hz) | Human Hearing (20 Hz - 20 kHz) | Ultrasound (> 20 kHz)

The human hearing range of 20 Hz to 20 kHz represents only a small fraction of the sound spectrum. Animals have evolved to fill ecological niches by detecting frequencies outside our range!

Key Takeaway:
  • Human hearing range: 20 Hz to 20 kHz
  • Infrasound (< 20 Hz): Used by elephants, whales, rhinoceroses
  • Ultrasound (> 20 kHz): Used by bats, dolphins, porpoises
  • SONAR uses ultrasound for underwater detection and navigation
  • SONAR formula: d = vt/2 (same as echo distance)

🏥 Applications of Ultrasound

Ultrasound technology has revolutionized many fields, from medicine to manufacturing. The ability to use sound waves beyond human hearing has opened up remarkable possibilities.

Medical Applications

Ultrasonography (Medical Imaging)

Ultrasonography uses ultrasound waves to create images of internal organs and tissues. A transducer (device that converts electrical signals to sound) sends ultrasonic pulses into the body. The ultrasound reflects off different tissues at different rates, and these echoes are converted into images on a screen.

  • Organ imaging: Liver, kidney, pancreas, spleen
  • Obstetrics: Fetal monitoring during pregnancy
  • Cardiology: Echocardiography (heart imaging)
  • Thyroid: Detecting nodules or abnormalities

Fetal Ultrasound

Pregnant women receive ultrasound scans to monitor fetal development. The ultrasound is safe (non-ionizing radiation) and provides clear images of the developing baby. Doctors can check for proper growth, detect anomalies, and estimate the due date. Parents often see and hear their baby's heartbeat during these scans—an amazing first encounter!

Echocardiography

This is ultrasound imaging of the heart. It allows doctors to assess:

  • Heart valve function
  • Blood flow patterns
  • Heart chamber size
  • Detect congenital heart defects

Breaking Kidney Stones

Lithotripsy is a procedure using focused ultrasound (or shock waves) to break up kidney stones. The ultrasonic waves fracture the stones into smaller pieces that can pass through the urinary system naturally, avoiding painful surgery.

Industrial Applications

Cleaning

Ultrasonic cleaning uses high-frequency sound waves to create cavitation bubbles in a liquid. These bubbles implode near surfaces, creating pressure waves that dislodge dirt, grease, and contaminants from hard-to-reach places. Uses include:

  • Cleaning jewelry and delicate items
  • Removing grease from engine parts
  • Sterilizing medical instruments
  • Cleaning precision components

Detecting Flaws and Cracks

Ultrasonic testing is used in manufacturing and quality control to:

  • Detect internal cracks or voids in metal blocks, welds, and structures
  • Measure material thickness
  • Identify structural defects before they become safety hazards

Engineers send ultrasound pulses into materials. If there's a crack or void, the ultrasound reflects back differently, alerting inspectors to the defect.

Ultrasonic Quality Control in Manufacturing

Aircraft manufacturers use ultrasonic testing on critical components like wings and fuselage joints to ensure there are no hidden cracks or delaminations that could cause structural failure. A single missed defect could be catastrophic, so this non-destructive testing is essential.

Other Applications

  • Pest control: Ultrasonic devices that emit high-frequency sounds to repel rodents and insects
  • Fish finders: Fishing sonar that uses ultrasound to locate fish underwater
  • Non-destructive testing: Material inspection in construction and aerospace
  • Dental applications: Ultrasonic scalers for cleaning teeth
Key Takeaway:
  • Ultrasonography: Medical imaging of organs and tissues
  • Echocardiography: Heart imaging using ultrasound
  • Ultrasonic cleaning: Removes contaminants using cavitation
  • Flaw detection: Non-destructive testing of materials
  • Lithotripsy: Breaking kidney stones with ultrasound
  • Ultrasound is safe, effective, and non-invasive

Exam Preparation

Higher-Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

Q1.
A person standing in a canyon shouts and hears the echo after 2 seconds. If the speed of sound in air is 340 m/s, calculate the distance to the canyon wall and explain why echoes are heard more clearly in canyons than in flat open areas.
Solution:

Part 1: Calculate Distance

Using the formula: d = (v × t) / 2

d = (340 × 2) / 2 = 340 m

The canyon wall is 340 meters away.

Part 2: Why Canyons?

Canyons create clear echoes because:

  • Steep rock walls are smooth and hard, reflecting sound effectively
  • The walls are usually at suitable distances (> 17.2 m) for distinct echoes
  • Sound bounces between opposite walls, creating strong reflections
  • No absorbing materials (carpet, furniture) to dampen reflections
  • The open space allows sound to travel far before hitting a wall
Q2.
A sound wave travels through three different media: air, water, and steel in sequence. If the frequency of the sound remains constant at 1000 Hz, how do the wavelengths change in each medium? Use speeds: Air = 340 m/s, Water = 1500 m/s, Steel = 5000 m/s. Explain the physics behind these differences.
Solution:

Using λ = v / ν, where ν = 1000 Hz (constant):

In Air: λ = 340 / 1000 = 0.34 m = 34 cm

In Water: λ = 1500 / 1000 = 1.5 m

In Steel: λ = 5000 / 1000 = 5 m

Observation: As the medium becomes denser, the wavelength increases while frequency stays constant!

Physics Explanation: The speed of sound depends on the density and elasticity of the medium. In denser, more elastic materials, particles are more tightly bonded and can transfer vibrations more rapidly. Since v = λν and ν is constant, a larger v means a larger λ. Sound "stretches" in faster media.

Q3.
Dolphins produce ultrasound with frequencies up to 130 kHz for echolocation. A dolphin emits a 100 kHz ultrasonic pulse toward a fish. The echo returns after 0.1 seconds. Calculate the distance to the fish. Also explain why dolphins use ultrasound instead of audible sound for hunting.
Solution:

Part 1: Calculate Distance

Using d = (v × t) / 2, where v = 1500 m/s (speed in water):

d = (1500 × 0.1) / 2 = 75 meters

The fish is 75 meters away.

Part 2: Why Ultrasound for Hunting?

  • Shorter wavelength: At 100 kHz in water, λ = 1500/100,000 = 0.015 m = 1.5 cm. This short wavelength can resolve small objects like fish and insects.
  • Better directionality: Shorter wavelengths can be focused into a narrower beam for precise targeting.
  • Stealth: Most fish cannot hear ultrasound above ~5 kHz, so dolphins hunt without alerting prey.
  • Faster information: Higher frequency = faster oscillations = more detailed information in the returning echoes.
  • Less interference: Natural underwater sounds (waves, animal calls) are mostly in the infrasound to lower frequency range, so ultrasound echoes are cleaner.

Case-Based Questions

Case 1: Ultrasound in Medical Imaging
A pregnant woman undergoes an ultrasound scan to monitor her baby's development. The ultrasound machine operates at a frequency of 2.5 MHz (2.5 million Hz) and uses sound waves at a speed of 1540 m/s through human tissue (which is mostly water).

(a) Calculate the wavelength of this ultrasound in human tissue.
(b) Why is this much shorter wavelength compared to audible sound crucial for medical imaging?
(c) Could a normal speaker emitting audible sound (e.g., 1000 Hz) produce the same quality images? Explain.
Solution:

(a) Calculate Wavelength

Using λ = v / ν:

λ = 1540 / 2,500,000 = 0.000616 m = 0.616 mm = 616 micrometers

(b) Why Short Wavelength Matters for Imaging

The wavelength determines the resolution (clarity) of the image. Shorter wavelengths can:

  • Resolve finer details and smaller structures
  • Distinguish between different organs and tissues
  • Detect small abnormalities or defects
  • Create detailed images of the fetus (size, development, position)

(c) Why Not Audible Sound?

An audible sound at 1000 Hz would have:

λ = 1540 / 1000 = 1.54 m = 1540 mm

This is about 2500 times larger than the ultrasound wavelength! Such long wavelengths cannot resolve small structures and would produce only blurry, useless images. The image would look like dark shadows rather than clear anatomical details.

Case 2: Sound Management in Concert Halls
A concert hall has a serious problem: performers on stage can barely hear each other during performances because of excessive reverberation from reflections off the hard marble walls and polished wooden floors. The hall is 50 meters long, 30 meters wide, and 15 meters high.

(a) Estimate the time delay between the direct sound from a performer and the first reflection from the back wall (assuming speed of sound = 346 m/s).
(b) Does this time delay create an echo that interferes with hearing? (Remember: distinct echo requires ≥ 0.1 s delay)
(c) Suggest specific materials and placements to reduce reverberation without damaging the concert hall's acoustics.
Solution:

(a) Time Delay Calculation

Distance to back wall = 50 meters

Time for sound to reach back wall: t = d / v = 50 / 346 = 0.145 seconds

(b) Echo Analysis

0.145 seconds > 0.1 seconds, so yes, this creates a distinct echo. The reflected sound returns 0.145 seconds after the original, creating a separate audible "ghost" sound that interferes with direct speech and music.

(c) Solutions to Reduce Reverberation

  • Acoustic panels: Install absorbing panels on side walls (the main reflection sources) using fibreboard or foam
  • Carpeting: Replace polished wooden floors with thick carpets
  • Draperies: Hang heavy curtains from the ceiling
  • Strategic placement: Focus absorption on hard, reflective surfaces while preserving the hall's shape and appearance
  • Curved reflectors: Add strategically placed curved surfaces behind the stage to direct sound to the audience rather than to the back wall
  • Avoid uniform absorption: Too much absorption kills acoustics. Concert halls need some reverberation for richness; the goal is controlled, pleasant reverberation, not complete dampening
Case 3: Deep-Sea Exploration Using SONAR
A research vessel is mapping the ocean floor using active SONAR. The SONAR system emits an ultrasonic pulse at 40 kHz frequency and receives an echo after 4 seconds. The speed of sound in seawater is approximately 1500 m/s.

(a) Calculate the depth of the ocean at this location.
(b) Calculate the wavelength of the 40 kHz ultrasound in seawater. What advantage does this have over lower frequencies?
(c) Why would using audible sound (e.g., 10 kHz) instead of ultrasound reduce the effectiveness of deep-sea SONAR?
Solution:

(a) Ocean Depth Calculation

Using d = (v × t) / 2:

d = (1500 × 4) / 2 = 3000 meters

The ocean floor is 3 kilometers (3000 m) deep at this location.

(b) Wavelength of 40 kHz Ultrasound

λ = v / ν = 1500 / 40,000 = 0.0375 m = 3.75 cm

Advantage of short wavelength:

  • Can resolve small features and rocks on the ocean floor
  • Can detect underwater objects (submarines, shipwrecks) with precision
  • Creates high-resolution seafloor maps

(c) Why Not Use Audible Sound?

At 10 kHz in seawater:

λ = 1500 / 10,000 = 0.15 m = 15 cm

Problems with audible sound:

  • Longer wavelength (15 cm vs 3.75 cm) reduces resolution 4-fold
  • Cannot resolve fine details of seafloor features
  • Audible sound (< 20 kHz) would include frequencies that marine animals can hear, disrupting whale and dolphin communication
  • Over long distances (3 km), lower frequencies scatter and attenuate more, reducing echo clarity
  • Ships' machinery and underwater volcanoes emit noise in the audible range, creating interference

Glossary of Key Terms

Amplitude
Maximum displacement from mean position; determines loudness
Compression
Region of high pressure/density in a sound wave
Rarefaction
Region of low pressure/density in a sound wave
Frequency
Number of oscillations per second; measured in Hertz (Hz)
Wavelength
Distance between two consecutive compressions or rarefactions
Echo
Reflected sound heard as a distinct separate sound (≥ 0.1 s delay)
Reverberation
Persistence of sound due to multiple reflections
Pitch
How high or low a sound seems; depends on frequency
Loudness
How loud or soft a sound seems; depends on amplitude
Timbre/Quality
Characteristic distinguishing sounds of same pitch and loudness
Longitudinal Wave
Wave where particle motion is parallel to wave direction (like sound)
Transverse Wave
Wave where particle motion is perpendicular to wave direction (like light)
SONAR
Sound Navigation and Ranging; uses ultrasound for detection

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Confusing Pitch and Loudness

Wrong: "A loud sound has a high pitch"
Correct: Pitch depends on frequency, loudness depends on amplitude. A deep bass drum can be very loud but low-pitched.

Mistake 2: Thinking Sound Travels the Same Speed Everywhere

Wrong: "Sound always travels at 340 m/s"
Correct: Speed depends on the medium and temperature. Sound travels at 1531 m/s in water, 5960 m/s in steel, and varies with temperature even in air.

Mistake 3: Forgetting the Factor of 2 in Echo Calculations

Wrong: "d = v × t" (for echo)
Correct: "d = (v × t) / 2" because sound travels to the surface AND back.

Mistake 4: Assuming Sound Can Travel Through a Vacuum

Wrong: "Sound can travel through space"
Correct: Sound is a mechanical wave and REQUIRES a medium. In space (vacuum), there are no particles to vibrate, so sound cannot travel.

Mistake 5: Confusing Frequency and Wavelength Relationship

Wrong: "Higher frequency means longer wavelength"
Correct: Higher frequency means shorter wavelength. v = λν remains constant, so if ν increases, λ must decrease.

Mistake 6: Thinking Particles Move Far in Sound Waves

Wrong: "Sound particles travel from the source to your ear"
Correct: Particles vibrate back and forth in place. Only the disturbance (energy) travels to your ear. Otherwise, we'd blow away from speakers!

Memory Tips and Mnemonics

  • "Solids > Liquids > Gases": Sound travels fastest in solids because particles are tightly packed and bonded. Easy to remember because density generally follows this order.
  • "0.1 seconds for distinct echo": Remember 0.1 s (one-tenth of a second) is the minimum time gap. About the time it takes to say "one-one-thousand"!
  • "v = λν": Velocity equals wavelength times frequency. Think: to find speed, multiply the two wave properties.
  • "Pitch = Frequency, Loudness = Amplitude": Think "High pitch, high frequency" and "Loud = big amplitude".
  • "20 to 20k Hz": Human hearing range from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Remember "twenty to twenty-k".
  • "Infra- below, Ultra- above": Infrasound is below 20 Hz (infra = below), ultrasound is above 20 kHz (ultra = beyond).
  • "C-R-C-R pattern": Sound waves alternate Compression-Rarefaction. The underline shows they alternate continuously.
