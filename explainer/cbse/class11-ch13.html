Chapter 13: Oscillations

Master the fundamentals of oscillations, from pendulums to spring-mass systems

Why Study Oscillations?

Oscillations are everywhere in nature and technology. A heartbeat oscillates at roughly 60-100 times per minute. The alternating current in your home oscillates 50 or 60 times per second. Atoms in solids oscillate about equilibrium positions, affecting material properties. Radio and television transmission rely on electromagnetic oscillations. Buildings and bridges must be designed to resist dangerous oscillations caused by wind or earthquakes. By understanding oscillations, we understand the rhythm of the universe itself—from tiny atomic vibrations to massive cosmic phenomena.

1. Periodic and Oscillatory Motion

Definitions

Periodic Motion: Motion that repeats itself in equal intervals of time. Examples: Earth's revolution around the Sun, circular motion of a merry-go-round.

Oscillatory Motion: Periodic motion about a mean position. The object returns to the same position repeatedly. Examples: pendulum swing, vibrating tuning fork, mass on a spring.

Key Parameters

Period (T): Time taken to complete one full oscillation, measured in seconds (s).

Frequency (ν or f): Number of oscillations per unit time, measured in Hertz (Hz) or s⁻¹.

Frequency and Period Relationship: $$\nu = \frac{1}{T}$$ where ν is frequency (Hz) and T is period (s)

Angular Frequency (ω): Relates frequency to angle (radians) per second.

Angular Frequency: $$\omega = 2\pi \nu = \frac{2\pi}{T}$$ ω is measured in rad/s

Key Concepts

  • An object can exhibit periodic motion without oscillatory motion (e.g., uniform circular motion)
  • All oscillatory motions are periodic, but not all periodic motions are oscillatory
  • Phase: Position in the oscillation cycle at a given time
  • Higher frequency means shorter period; lower frequency means longer period

2. Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Definition and Equations

Simple Harmonic Motion is a special type of periodic motion where acceleration is always directed toward a fixed point (mean position) and is proportional to the displacement from that point.

Displacement in SHM (two forms): $$x(t) = A \sin(\omega t + \phi)$$ or $$x(t) = A \cos(\omega t + \phi)$$ where:
A = amplitude (maximum displacement from mean)
ω = angular frequency (rad/s)
t = time
φ = initial phase constant (radians)

Velocity in SHM

Velocity is the rate of change of displacement. At any position x, velocity can be expressed as:

Velocity Equations: $$v = \frac{dx}{dt} = A\omega \cos(\omega t + \phi)$$ or $$v = \pm\omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2}$$
Maximum velocity: $v_{max} = A\omega$ (at mean position, x = 0)

Acceleration in SHM

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. The defining characteristic of SHM is that acceleration is proportional to displacement and always directed toward the mean position:

Acceleration Equation: $$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = -\omega^2 x = -\omega^2 A \sin(\omega t + \phi)$$
Maximum acceleration: $a_{max} = \omega^2 A$ (at extremes, x = ±A)

Position-Velocity-Acceleration Relationships

Position Velocity Acceleration Description
x = 0 (mean) v = max (±Aω) a = 0 Maximum speed, no acceleration
x = ±A (extreme) v = 0 a = max (∓ω²A) Momentary rest, maximum acceleration
x = ±A/2 v = ±(√3/2)Aω a = ∓ω²A/2 Intermediate values

SHM Characteristics

  • Restoring Force: F = -kx (proportional to displacement, opposite direction)
  • Amplitude: Maximum displacement; remains constant in ideal SHM
  • Phase: φ represents the initial condition of the oscillation
  • Angular Frequency: ω depends on system properties (mass, spring constant, length)
  • Time Period: Same as for periodic motion; T = 2π/ω

Phase in SHM

The phase of an oscillation at time t is the argument of the sine or cosine function: (ωt + φ). It tells us the state of the oscillation at that instant.

  • Initial Phase (φ): Value at t = 0; depends on starting conditions
  • Phase Difference: Between two oscillations indicates their relative state
  • In-phase: Phase difference = 0 or 2π
  • Out-of-phase: Phase difference = π
  • Phase relationship example: v(t) = Aω cos(ωt + φ) is 90° ahead of x(t) = A sin(ωt + φ)

3. Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy depends on velocity. In SHM, velocity varies from zero at extremes to maximum at the mean position.

Kinetic Energy in SHM: $$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(A^2 - x^2)$$ where m is mass, v is velocity, x is displacement, A is amplitude
Maximum KE: $KE_{max} = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2$ (at x = 0)
Minimum KE: $KE_{min} = 0$ (at x = ±A)

Potential Energy

Potential energy in SHM depends on position. A restoring force F = -kx gives rise to potential energy.

Potential Energy in SHM: $$PE = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2$$ (Note: ω² = k/m for a spring-mass system)
Maximum PE: $PE_{max} = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2$ (at x = ±A)
Minimum PE: $PE_{min} = 0$ (at x = 0)

Total Energy and Energy Conservation

In the absence of damping, total mechanical energy remains constant.

Total Energy in SHM: $$E_{total} = KE + PE = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2 = \text{constant}$$ This is one of the fundamental laws of energy conservation.

Energy Oscillation Frequency

While displacement, velocity, and acceleration oscillate with angular frequency ω, kinetic and potential energy oscillate with angular frequency 2ω (twice the fundamental frequency).

Energy Oscillation: At time t:
$KE(t) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2 [1 - \cos(2\omega t + 2\phi)]$
$PE(t) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2 [1 + \cos(2\omega t + 2\phi)]$
Both oscillate with frequency 2ν (where ν is the oscillation frequency)
Energy Graphs in SHM: In an ideal SHM graph, the total energy line is horizontal (constant), while KE and PE are mirror images about the horizontal center line. At mean position, KE is maximum and PE is minimum. At extremes, KE is zero and PE is maximum. The two curves cross at E/2 when x = ±A/√2.

4. Oscillatory Systems: Spring-Mass and Pendulum

Spring-Mass System

A mass attached to a spring is one of the most fundamental oscillatory systems. When displaced and released, it oscillates in SHM.

Spring-Mass Angular Frequency: $$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$ where k is the spring constant (N/m) and m is mass (kg)
Spring-Mass Period: $$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$ Period depends only on mass and spring constant, independent of amplitude or gravity.

Combination of Springs

Springs can be connected in series or parallel, affecting the effective spring constant.

Springs in Series: $$\frac{1}{k_{eff}} = \frac{1}{k_1} + \frac{1}{k_2} + \frac{1}{k_3} + ...$$ The effective spring constant is less than any individual spring constant.
Springs in Parallel: $$k_{eff} = k_1 + k_2 + k_3 + ...$$ The effective spring constant is the sum of individual spring constants.

Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum consists of a point mass suspended by a weightless, inextensible string. Under small angle approximation (θ < 15°), it executes SHM.

Simple Pendulum Period (small angles): $$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$ where L is the length of the pendulum (m) and g is acceleration due to gravity (m/s²)
Simple Pendulum Frequency: $$\nu = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}$$ $$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}$$

Factors Affecting Pendulum Period

Factor Effect on Period T Reason

Gravity (g) T ∝ 1/√g Stronger gravity accelerates motion

Energy in Pendulum

In a simple pendulum, mechanical energy oscillates between kinetic energy (maximum at lowest point) and gravitational potential energy (maximum at highest point).

Pendulum Energy: At lowest point: KE = max, PE = 0 At highest point: KE = 0, PE = max
Total energy: E = mgh = mgL(1 - cosθ₀)
where h is height difference and θ₀ is initial amplitude

5. Damped Oscillations

Introduction to Damping

In real oscillatory systems, energy is dissipated due to friction, air resistance, or internal friction. This causes the amplitude to decrease with time. Such oscillations are called damped oscillations.

Damped Oscillation Equation

For a damping force proportional to velocity (F = -bv), the displacement varies as:

Damped Oscillation: $$x(t) = Ae^{-\frac{b}{2m}t} \cos(\omega' t + \phi)$$ where:
A = initial amplitude
b = damping constant
m = mass
ω' = damped angular frequency

Damped Angular Frequency

Damped Frequency Relation: $$\omega' = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \left(\frac{b}{2m}\right)^2}$$ where ω₀ = √(k/m) is the natural (undamped) angular frequency

Types of Damping

Type Condition Characteristic Example
Under-damped b < 2√(km) Oscillates while decaying; ω' is real Pendulum in air, car shock absorber
Critically damped b = 2√(km) Returns to equilibrium fastest without overshooting; ω' = 0 Door closer, precision instruments
Over-damped b > 2√(km) Returns to equilibrium slowly without oscillating Viscous oil damper in heavy machinery
Damped Oscillation Graphs: An under-damped oscillation appears as a sine or cosine wave that gradually shrinks within an exponential envelope (the dashed boundary formed by ±Ae^(-bt/2m)). A critically damped system quickly settles without crossing equilibrium multiple times. An over-damped system creeps toward equilibrium even more slowly than critically damped.

Energy Decay in Damped Oscillations

In damped oscillations, total mechanical energy decreases exponentially as energy is dissipated by the damping force.

Energy Decay: $$E(t) = E_0 e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}$$ where E₀ is the initial energy at t = 0
The energy half-life is: $t_{1/2} = \frac{m \ln(2)}{b}$

Quality Factor (Q-factor)

The Q-factor measures the damping level of an oscillatory system. Higher Q means less damping and more oscillations before decay.

Quality Factor: $$Q = \frac{\omega_0 m}{b} = 2\pi \times \frac{\text{Energy stored}}{\text{Energy lost per cycle}}$$ High Q: Lightly damped, many oscillations
Low Q: Heavily damped, few oscillations

6. Forced Oscillations and Resonance

Forced Oscillations

When an external periodic force is applied to an oscillatory system, the system is forced to oscillate at the frequency of the applied force. This is called forced oscillation.

Driven Damped Oscillator Equation: $$m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + b\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = F_0 \cos(\omega_d t)$$ where F₀ is the amplitude of driving force and ωd is the driving frequency

Steady-State Solution

After transient behavior dies out, the system oscillates at the driving frequency with a steady-state amplitude that depends on the driving frequency and damping.

Steady-State Amplitude: $$A(\omega_d) = \frac{F_0/m}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2 - \omega_d^2)^2 + (\frac{b}{m}\omega_d)^2}}$$ where ω₀ = √(k/m) is the natural frequency

Resonance

Resonance occurs when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency of the system (ωd = ω₀). At resonance, the amplitude reaches maximum.

Resonance Frequency (Amplitude Resonance): $$\omega_d = \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$ At resonance: Maximum amplitude = $A_{max} = \frac{F_0}{b\omega_0}$

Resonance Phenomena

  • Amplitude Resonance: Maximum amplitude occurs at ωd ≈ ω₀ (exact match for undamped or lightly damped systems)
  • Energy Resonance: Maximum energy transfer occurs exactly at ωd = ω₀
  • Power Resonance: Maximum power absorption occurs at the natural frequency
  • Phase Resonance: Phase difference between driving force and response changes from 0° to 180° as driving frequency increases past resonance
  • Sharpness of Resonance: Inversely proportional to damping. Lightly damped systems have sharp, tall resonance peaks; heavily damped systems have broad, flat peaks.

Resonance Curve Characteristics

Damping Level Peak Amplitude Peak Width Practical Implication
Light damping (small b) Very large; A_max ≈ F₀/(bω₀) Narrow; sharp peak High Q factor; sensitive frequency selection
Critical damping Moderate; no sharp peak Broad; smooth curve Balanced response; stable
Heavy damping (large b) Small; A_max ≈ F₀/(bωd) Very broad; flat curve Low Q factor; responds to wide frequency range
Resonance Curves: Resonance curves show amplitude vs driving frequency. For an undamped system, amplitude approaches infinity at ωd = ω₀. For lightly damped systems, a sharp peak appears near ω₀. For heavily damped systems, the curve is broad and smooth without a sharp peak. The curve shape is determined by the Q-factor: Q = ω₀/(Δω), where Δω is the width at half-maximum amplitude.

Applications of Resonance

  • Radio and TV Tuning: LC circuits resonate at specific frequencies to select desired stations
  • Tuning Forks: Designed to vibrate with maximum amplitude at their natural frequency for accurate sound production
  • Ultrasound Imaging: Uses resonance of piezoelectric crystals at ultrasonic frequencies
  • Microwave Ovens: Magnetron produces microwaves at the resonance frequency of water molecules
  • Building Design: Engineers avoid building natural frequencies matching earthquake or wind frequencies

Dangers of Resonance

  • Structural Damage: The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed due to wind-induced oscillations near its natural frequency
  • Mechanical Failure: Machine parts can fail if driven at resonance frequencies
  • Electrical Overload: Circuit resonance can cause excessive currents and damage equipment
  • Damping Solutions: Adding dampers (shock absorbers) shifts the resonance frequency and reduces its sharpness

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS 1: Energy Transfer in Coupled Oscillators

Q: Two identical pendulums are connected by a weak spring. When one pendulum is released from its highest point while the other hangs at rest, how does energy transfer between them? Explain the mechanism.

Answer:

Initially, the first pendulum has maximum gravitational PE. As it swings, this converts to KE. The spring connecting them acts as a force couple. When the first pendulum reaches the mean position, the spring is compressed/extended maximally, storing elastic PE. As the first pendulum continues, the spring force decelerates it while accelerating the second pendulum. Over several oscillations:

  • Energy oscillates between the two pendulums
  • The period of energy transfer is called the "beat period"
  • At the beat frequency, one pendulum reaches maximum amplitude while the other momentarily stops
  • The weak coupling ensures gradual, not instantaneous, energy transfer

This demonstrates energy conservation and the principle of coupled oscillations used in quantum mechanics and signal processing.

HOTS 2: Amplitude Dependence in Real Pendulums

Q: The formula T = 2π√(L/g) assumes small oscillations. For a real pendulum with large amplitude (say, 60°), the period increases. Derive why and estimate the percentage increase.

Answer:

The small angle approximation assumes sin(θ) ≈ θ. For large angles, sin(θ) < θ, making the actual restoring force less than approximated:

  • Restoring force: F = -mg sin(θ), not -mg(θ) as assumed
  • This weaker restoring force causes slower acceleration
  • The pendulum takes longer to complete its oscillation

Mathematical correction: The exact period involves an elliptic integral:

T = T₀ [1 + (1/4)sin²(θ₀/2) + (9/64)sin⁴(θ₀/2) + ...]

For θ₀ = 60° = π/3 rad: sin(30°) = 0.5, so the correction factor ≈ 1.073

Percentage increase: About 7.3% longer period than predicted by the simple formula.

HOTS 3: Resonance and Structural Failure

Q: The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed in 1940 due to wind-induced oscillations at resonance. Explain how wind can produce periodic forcing at a specific frequency, and why increasing damping could have prevented the collapse.

Answer:

Wind-Induced Forcing: Wind passing over the bridge creates vortex shedding—alternating low-pressure zones behind the cables. This produces periodic lift forces on the bridge deck:

  • Vortex shedding frequency: f = (St × V) / D, where St is Strouhal number, V is wind speed, D is bluff body diameter
  • The 1940 wind conditions created a vortex shedding frequency matching the bridge's natural frequency (~0.2 Hz)
  • This external forcing at ωd = ω₀ triggered resonance

Why Damping Helps: From the amplitude formula:

A = (F₀/m) / √[(ω₀² - ωd²)² + (bωd/m)²]

  • Increasing damping (b) increases the denominator
  • At resonance (ωd = ω₀), only the damping term (bωd/m) remains in the denominator
  • Higher damping significantly reduces the peak amplitude: A_max = F₀/(bω₀)
  • Also broadens the resonance curve, making it harder to excite

Practical Solutions: Modern bridges use tuned mass dampers, viscoelastic dampers, or aerodynamic modifications to avoid dangerous resonances.

Case-Based Questions

Case 1: Design of a Seismic Isolation System

A building of mass M = 5000 tons is designed to rest on isolating springs. Engineers must choose spring constant such that the building's natural frequency is well below typical earthquake frequencies (1-10 Hz).

(a) If the building's natural frequency is set to 0.5 Hz, what should be the spring constant k?

Solution: f = (1/2π)√(k/M), so k = 4π²f²M = 4π²(0.5)²(5×10⁶ kg) ≈ 4.93 × 10⁶ N/m

(b) Why is it beneficial to have the building's frequency below typical earthquake frequencies?

Answer: When ωd >> ω₀, the amplitude of forced oscillations becomes very small (A → F₀/mωd² → 0). The building acts almost statically, not resonating with the earthquake motion.

(c) If damping is added with b = 2√(kM), what is the damping type?

Answer: This is critical damping (b = 2√(km) is the critical condition). The building returns to equilibrium fastest without overshooting, protecting against multiple oscillations.

Case 2: Tuning a Radio Receiver

A radio receiver contains an LC circuit with inductance L and capacitance C. The resonant frequency is f = 1/(2π√(LC)). A listener wants to tune to FM station at 100 MHz.

(a) If L = 2 μH, what should be the capacitance C to resonate at 100 MHz?

Solution: f = 1/(2π√(LC)), so C = 1/(4π²f²L) = 1/(4π²(10⁸)²(2×10⁻⁶)) ≈ 1.27 pF

(b) Why does the radio respond most strongly to the 100 MHz station?

Answer: At f = 100 MHz, the driving frequency equals the LC circuit's natural resonance frequency. The impedance is minimum (only resistance), current is maximum, and the received signal is amplified most.

(c) The circuit has resistance R. How does increasing R affect tuning?

Answer: Resistance introduces damping. Higher R increases the bandwidth (Q = ωL/R decreases), making the receiver less selective. It picks up nearby frequencies better but with less tuning precision.

Case 3: Water Oscillations in a Lake

A lake experiences seiches—standing oscillations of water surfaces. A long, narrow lake of length L = 20 km acts like a spring-mass system, with the entire water body oscillating back and forth.

(a) The seiche period is approximately T = 2L/√(gH), where H is average depth. For H = 200 m, what is the seiche period?

Solution: T = 2(20×10³)/√(10×200) = 4×10⁴/√2000 ≈ 4×10⁴/44.7 ≈ 895 seconds ≈ 15 minutes

(b) If wind pushes the lake surface with periodic gusts at 0.0011 Hz (≈ period 15 minutes), what happens?

Answer: The wind frequency matches the seiche frequency! Resonance occurs, and the oscillation amplitude grows with successive wind gusts. The water level rises and falls with increasing amplitude, potentially causing flooding at lake shores.

(c) How does the lake's depth affect seiche frequency?

Answer: Since f ∝ √(gH), deeper lakes have higher seiche frequencies. Shallower lakes oscillate more slowly. This is analogous to springs: stiffer springs (higher g/H) oscillate faster.
