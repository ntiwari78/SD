1. Rutherford's Model of the Atom

Introduction to Atomic Models

Before the discovery of the nucleus, scientists believed atoms were homogeneous spheres with uniformly distributed positive charge. However, Ernest Rutherford's experiments fundamentally changed our understanding of atomic structure.

Alpha Particle Scattering Experiment

In 1909, Rutherford, along with Geiger and Marsden, conducted the famous alpha particle scattering experiment. A beam of alpha particles (helium nuclei) was directed at a thin gold foil. The results were unexpected and revolutionary.

Key Observations from Alpha Scattering:

  • Most alpha particles passed through the foil without deflection
  • Some particles were deflected by large angles
  • A few particles (approximately 1 in 20,000) bounced back almost at 180°
  • The scattering followed an inverse square law

Rutherford's Nuclear Model

Based on the experimental observations, Rutherford proposed that:

Key Features of Rutherford's Model:

  • Nucleus: All positive charge and nearly all mass is concentrated in a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center
  • Electrons: Electrons orbit around the nucleus in a manner similar to planets around the sun
  • Mostly Empty Space: The atom is mostly empty space
  • Coulomb Force: The nucleus and electrons are held together by electrostatic force

Limitations of Rutherford's Model

Despite its success in explaining the alpha particle scattering experiment, Rutherford's model had serious theoretical problems:

Major Limitations:

  • Stability Problem: According to classical electromagnetism, an accelerating electron should radiate electromagnetic energy continuously. This would cause the electron to lose energy and spiral into the nucleus in a fraction of a second, making atoms unstable.
  • Radiation Problem: The atom should emit a continuous spectrum as electrons spiral down, but we observe discrete line spectra.
  • No Explanation for Atomic Sizes: The model could not explain why atoms have definite sizes.
  • No Quantization: The model did not include any quantization conditions, yet atoms are inherently quantized systems.

These limitations made it clear that a new theory was needed to explain atomic structure, leading Niels Bohr to develop his revolutionary model incorporating quantum theory.

2. Bohr's Model of the Hydrogen Atom

Background and Motivation

In 1913, Niels Bohr proposed a revolutionary model that combined classical mechanics with the emerging ideas of quantum theory. He focused on the hydrogen atom because it's the simplest and has been most thoroughly studied spectroscopically.

Bohr's Postulates

The Fundamental Postulates of Bohr's Model:

  1. Quantized Orbits: Electrons can occupy only certain discrete orbits (energy levels) around the nucleus. Not all orbits allowed by classical mechanics are permitted.
  2. No Radiation in Stable Orbits: Electrons in these allowed orbits do not radiate electromagnetic energy, contrary to classical electromagnetism. An electron in a stable orbit maintains its energy and does not spiral into the nucleus.
  3. Angular Momentum Quantization: The angular momentum of an electron in an allowed orbit is quantized and must be an integral multiple of ℏ (h/2π), where h is Planck's constant.
  4. Energy Transitions: An electron can jump from one allowed orbit to another by absorbing or emitting a photon. The energy of the photon equals the difference between the initial and final energy levels.

Quantization Condition

The key mathematical condition for allowed orbits is based on angular momentum quantization:

mvr = nℏ = n(h/2π)
where n = 1, 2, 3, ... (principal quantum number)

Bohr Radii

By combining the quantization condition with Coulomb's law for the centripetal force, Bohr derived the radius of the nth orbit:

rn = n² a₀

where a₀ = 0.529 Å = 0.529 × 10⁻¹⁰ m = 0.0529 nm
This is called the Bohr radius

For the first orbit (n=1):
r₁ = a₀ = 0.529 Å

Bohr Radius Properties:

  • The radius increases as n²
  • For hydrogen, r₁ = 0.529 Å is often used as the size of an atom
  • The distance between consecutive orbits increases with n

Energy Levels in Hydrogen Atom

The energy of an electron in the nth orbit of a hydrogen atom is:

En = -13.6/n² eV

For the first few levels:
E₁ = -13.6 eV (ground state)
E₂ = -3.4 eV
E₃ = -1.51 eV
E₄ = -0.85 eV
E∞ = 0 eV (ionization limit)

Energy Level Characteristics:

  • All energy levels are negative, indicating bound states
  • The constant 13.6 eV is called the ionization energy or Rydberg energy
  • Energy levels become more closely spaced as n increases
  • The energy becomes zero at infinity, where the electron is free
  • To ionize an atom in its ground state requires 13.6 eV

Velocities in Bohr Orbits

The velocity of an electron in the nth Bohr orbit is related to the velocity in the first orbit:

vn = v₁/n

where v₁ = 2.2 × 10⁶ m/s (approximately c/137)

So electrons move slower in higher orbits

Derivation Summary

The Bohr model successfully explains the quantization by balancing:

  • Centripetal Force: mv²/r = ke²/r²
  • Angular Momentum: mvr = nℏ

Solving these equations simultaneously yields the formulas for r_n, E_n, and v_n.

3. Hydrogen Spectrum and Spectral Series

Introduction to Spectroscopy

Spectroscopy is the study of light emitted or absorbed by matter. When hydrogen atoms are excited (by heating, electrical discharge, or other means), electrons jump to higher energy levels. When they return to lower levels, they emit light in the form of discrete spectral lines.

The Rydberg Formula

The wavelength of light emitted or absorbed during transitions in hydrogen atoms is given by:

1/λ = R(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²)

where:
λ = wavelength of emitted/absorbed light
R = Rydberg constant = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹
n₁ = lower energy level
n₂ = higher energy level (n₂ > n₁)

Rydberg Constant:

  • R = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹ = 1.097 × 10⁵ cm⁻¹
  • Derived from Bohr theory: R = me⁴/(2ε₀²h³c) = 13.6 eV/hc
  • Same constant applies to all hydrogen-like ions with appropriate modifications

Spectral Series of Hydrogen

Different series of spectral lines are observed depending on which lower level the electrons transition to:

Series Lower Level (n₁) Upper Levels (n₂) Region Discovered
Lyman 1 2, 3, 4, 5, ... Ultraviolet (UV) 1906
Balmer 2 3, 4, 5, 6, ... Visible Light 1885
Paschen 3 4, 5, 6, 7, ... Infrared (IR) 1908
Brackett 4 5, 6, 7, 8, ... Infrared (IR) 1922
Pfund 5 6, 7, 8, 9, ... Far Infrared (IR) 1924

Details of Important Series

Lyman Series (UV Region)

1/λ = R(1/1² - 1/n₂²) where n₂ = 2, 3, 4, ...

Lyman alpha (n₁=1 to n₂=2): λ = 121.6 nm
Lyman beta (n₁=1 to n₂=3): λ = 102.6 nm
Series limit (n₂→∞): λ = 91.2 nm

Balmer Series (Visible Region)

1/λ = R(1/2² - 1/n₂²) where n₂ = 3, 4, 5, ...

H-alpha (n₁=2 to n₂=3): λ = 656.3 nm (red)
H-beta (n₁=2 to n₂=4): λ = 486.1 nm (cyan-blue)
H-gamma (n₁=2 to n₂=5): λ = 434.0 nm (blue-violet)
H-delta (n₁=2 to n₂=6): λ = 410.2 nm (violet)
Series limit (n₂→∞): λ = 364.6 nm

Paschen Series (IR Region)

1/λ = R(1/3² - 1/n₂²) where n₂ = 4, 5, 6, ...

Series limit (n₂→∞): λ = 820.0 nm

Energy Transitions

The energy of a photon emitted during a transition from level n₂ to n₁ is:

E_photon = hf = hc/λ = E_n₂ - E_n₁
= 13.6(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²) eV

Line Spectra Explanation

Why Do We See Line Spectra?

  • Each transition corresponds to a specific energy difference and thus a specific wavelength
  • Only discrete transitions are possible due to quantized energy levels
  • This explains why hydrogen shows a discrete line spectrum, not a continuous spectrum
  • The intensities of lines depend on the probability of transitions and the initial population of levels

4. de Broglie's Explanation of Quantization

The Wave-Particle Duality of Matter

In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that matter, like electromagnetic radiation, exhibits wave-particle duality. Just as light can behave as both waves and particles (photons), electrons and other particles can also exhibit wave properties.

de Broglie's Hypothesis

de Broglie suggested that any particle with momentum p has an associated wavelength, called the de Broglie wavelength:

λ = h/p = h/(mv)

where:
h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
p = momentum of the particle
m = mass of the particle
v = velocity of the particle

Key Implications:

  • Every particle has an associated wave nature
  • Heavier particles have shorter wavelengths (for same velocity)
  • Faster-moving particles have shorter wavelengths
  • This explains why macroscopic objects don't show obvious wave behavior (their wavelengths are extremely small)

Standing Waves in Bohr Orbits

The quantization condition in the Bohr model can be beautifully explained using de Broglie's concept of matter waves. When an electron orbits the nucleus, its de Broglie wave forms a standing wave pattern in the orbit.

The Quantization Condition

For a stable orbit of radius r, the electron must complete an integer number of wavelengths around the circumference. This gives us the standing wave condition:

2πr = nλ

where n = 1, 2, 3, ... (an integer)

This is the condition for constructive interference and a standing wave

Derivation of Bohr's Condition

Substituting λ = h/(mv) into the standing wave condition:

2πr = nh/(mv)

Rearranging:
mvr = nh/2π = nℏ

This is exactly Bohr's angular momentum quantization condition!

Significance of de Broglie's Explanation:

  • Provides a physical basis for Bohr's quantization postulate
  • Shows that quantization arises naturally from wave properties
  • Explains why only certain orbits are allowed (those where standing waves can form)
  • De Broglie waves represent the probability amplitude of finding the electron
  • Leads naturally to modern quantum mechanics and the concept of orbitals

Example Calculation: de Broglie Wavelength of Electron in Bohr Orbit

For an electron in the first Bohr orbit (n=1):

v₁ = 2.2 × 10⁶ m/s
λ = h/(m_e × v₁)
= (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴)/(9.109 × 10⁻³¹ × 2.2 × 10⁶)
= 3.33 × 10⁻¹⁰ m

Circumference of first orbit:
2πr₁ = 2π × 0.529 × 10⁻¹⁰
= 3.33 × 10⁻¹⁰ m

Perfect agreement! The first orbit accommodates exactly one de Broglie wavelength.

Limitations and Extensions

While de Broglie's explanation elegantly explains Bohr quantization, it's important to note:

  • The modern interpretation is not that electrons literally orbit as classical particles with waves
  • Rather, electrons occupy orbitals described by wavefunctions
  • The wavefunction squared gives the probability density of finding the electron
  • De Broglie's work was a crucial bridge between Bohr's model and modern quantum mechanics

Practice Quizzes

Quiz 1: Rutherford's Alpha Scattering

In Rutherford's alpha scattering experiment, what fraction of alpha particles were deflected by angles greater than 90°?

Answer: A) About 1 in 20,000. This small deflection proved the existence of the nucleus.

Quiz 2: Bohr Radius Calculation

What is the radius of the second Bohr orbit (n=2) of hydrogen?

Answer: C) 2.116 Å. Since r_n = n²a₀, r₂ = 4 × 0.529 = 2.116 Å

Quiz 3: Ionization Energy

What is the minimum energy required to ionize a hydrogen atom in its ground state?

Answer: B) 13.6 eV. This is E₁ - E∞ = -13.6 - 0 = 13.6 eV

Quiz 4: Hydrogen Spectrum Series

Which series of the hydrogen spectrum corresponds to transitions ending in n=2?

Answer: B) Balmer series. Balmer transitions end at n=2 and appear in the visible region.

Quiz 5: Rydberg Formula

Using the Rydberg formula, find the wavelength of the Lyman alpha line (n=2 to n=1):

Answer: A) 121.6 nm. 1/λ = R(1/1² - 1/2²) = 1.097×10⁷(3/4) gives λ = 121.6 nm

Quiz 6: de Broglie Waves

What is the significance of the condition 2πr = nλ in Bohr's model?

Answer: B) It represents standing waves. This condition ensures that an integer number of wavelengths fits around the orbit, explaining quantization through de Broglie waves.

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

HOTS Question 1: Comparison of Atomic Models

Compare and contrast Rutherford's and Bohr's models of the atom. Why was Rutherford's model unstable, and how did Bohr solve this problem?

Answer Guide:
Rutherford's Model:

  • Electrons orbit nucleus like planets around sun
  • Explains alpha scattering but fails to explain stability
  • Classical mechanics predicts electrons should radiate and collapse into nucleus
  • Cannot explain discrete line spectra (predicts continuous spectrum)

Bohr's Model:
  • Introduces quantization of angular momentum
  • Only certain orbits allowed (those satisfying mvr = nℏ)
  • Electrons don't radiate energy in stable orbits
  • Explains line spectra through discrete energy transitions

Key improvement: Incorporation of quantum ideas (quantization) into classical orbital model

HOTS Question 2: Energy Transitions and Spectral Lines

Why does the Balmer series appear in the visible region while the Lyman series appears in the ultraviolet? Explain using energy calculations.

Answer Guide:
Energy of transition from level n to level 1:
ΔE = 13.6(1/1² - 1/n²) eV

For Lyman alpha (n=2→1): ΔE = 13.6(1 - 1/4) = 10.2 eV
f = E/h = (10.2 × 1.6×10⁻¹⁹)/(6.626×10⁻³⁴) = 2.47×10¹⁵ Hz (UV)

For Balmer alpha (n=3→2): ΔE = 13.6(1/4 - 1/9) = 1.89 eV
f = 4.57×10¹⁴ Hz (visible red)

Larger energy transitions (ending at n=1) give higher frequencies (shorter wavelengths) in UV region. Smaller transitions (ending at higher levels) give lower frequencies (longer wavelengths) in visible/IR regions.

HOTS Question 3: de Broglie Waves and Quantum Mechanics

How does de Broglie's concept of matter waves provide theoretical justification for Bohr's quantization condition? What are the limitations of this explanation?

Answer Guide:
Justification:

  • Electrons have wave properties with λ = h/p
  • Only orbits where 2πr = nλ have standing waves (no destructive interference)
  • This naturally gives mvr = nℏ, explaining why only certain orbits exist
  • Provides physical mechanism for quantization

Limitations:
  • Still treats electron as particle in orbit (wrong picture)
  • Doesn't explain why electron has wave nature
  • Leads to incomplete picture of electron behavior
  • Modern quantum mechanics uses probability waves (wavefunctions) instead
  • Schrödinger equation provides complete description without particle orbits

Case-Based Questions

Case Study 1: Hydrogen Atom Spectroscopy in Astronomy

Context: Astronomers use the hydrogen spectrum to study distant stars. The Balmer series, particularly the H-alpha line (656.3 nm, red), is prominently used because it's visible and easily detected. By observing how these lines are shifted due to the Doppler effect, astronomers can determine whether stars are moving toward or away from Earth.

Question 1: If a hydrogen atom on a distant star transitions from n=3 to n=2, what is the wavelength of the emitted photon? How does this relate to the Balmer series?

Solution:
1/λ = R(1/2² - 1/3²) = 1.097×10⁷(1/4 - 1/9)
= 1.097×10⁷(9-4)/(36) = 1.097×10⁷(5/36)
= 1.523×10⁶ m⁻¹
λ = 656.3 nm

This is the H-alpha line of the Balmer series (n₁=2).

Question 2: If the star is moving away from Earth at 3000 km/s, by how much would the wavelength shift?

Solution:
Doppler shift: Δλ/λ = v/c (non-relativistic)
Δλ = λ(v/c) = 656.3 × (3×10⁶/3×10⁸)
= 656.3 × 0.01 = 6.563 nm
New wavelength = 656.3 + 6.563 = 662.9 nm (redshift)

This redshift indicates the star is receding from Earth.

Case Study 2: Bohr Model Applied to Hydrogen-Like Ions

Context: The Bohr model can be extended to hydrogen-like ions (ions with only one electron). For an ion with nuclear charge Z, the formulas modify as follows:
r_n = n²a₀/Z
E_n = -13.6Z²/n² eV
v_n = v₁Z/n

Question 1: Consider He⁺ (singly ionized helium, Z=2). What is the radius of the first Bohr orbit and the ground state energy?

Solution:
r₁ = a₀/Z = 0.529 Å / 2 = 0.2645 Å
E₁ = -13.6 × 4 / 1 = -54.4 eV

The orbit is much smaller and the energy is much more negative (more tightly bound) compared to hydrogen.

Question 2: What is the ionization energy of He⁺?

Solution:
Ionization energy = E₁ - E∞ = -54.4 - 0 = 54.4 eV

This is 4 times that of hydrogen (13.6 eV), as expected since Z²=4.

Case Study 3: Quantum Transitions in Medical Applications

Context: Modern medical imaging and spectroscopy techniques rely on understanding atomic transitions. For example, fluorescence spectroscopy uses the fact that atoms absorb photons and emit them at characteristic wavelengths.

Question: In a medical fluorescence device, an electron in a hydrogen atom absorbs a 10.2 eV photon. If it was initially in the ground state (n=1), what energy level does it reach? When it relaxes, what wavelengths are emitted?

Solution:
Initial energy: E₁ = -13.6 eV
After absorbing 10.2 eV: E_final = -13.6 + 10.2 = -3.4 eV
This corresponds to n=2 level: E₂ = -13.6/4 = -3.4 eV ✓

Possible relaxation paths:
1. Direct n=2→n=1: ΔE = -3.4 - (-13.6) = 10.2 eV
λ = hc/ΔE = 1240 eV·nm / 10.2 eV = 121.6 nm (Lyman alpha, UV)

The electron emits a UV photon (Lyman alpha line) returning to ground state.

Chapter 12 Cheat Sheet: Quick Reference

Rutherford's Nuclear Model

  • Most atom is empty space
  • Nucleus at center contains most mass and all positive charge
  • Electrons orbit nucleus
  • Limited by classical instability problem

Bohr's Postulates

  1. Electrons in quantized orbits don't radiate
  2. Angular momentum: mvr = nℏ (n = 1, 2, 3...)
  3. Energy transitions: ΔE = hf = hc/λ

Key Formulas

  • Bohr Radius: r_n = n² × 0.529 Å
  • Energy Levels: E_n = -13.6/n² eV
  • Velocity: v_n = 2.2×10⁶/n m/s
  • Ionization Energy: 13.6 eV (for n=1 to ∞)

Rydberg Formula

  • 1/λ = R(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²)
  • R = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹
  • Valid for hydrogen and hydrogen-like ions

Spectral Series Memory Aid

  • Lyman: n₁=1 (UV)
  • Balmer: n₁=2 (Visible)
  • Paschen: n₁=3 (IR)
  • Brackett: n₁=4 (IR)
  • Pfund: n₁=5 (Far IR)

de Broglie Matter Waves

  • Wavelength: λ = h/p = h/(mv)
  • Standing wave: 2πr = nλ
  • Explains quantization naturally
  • Wavelength h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s

Common Photon Wavelengths

  • Lyman alpha (2→1): 121.6 nm
  • Balmer alpha/H-α (3→2): 656.3 nm (red)
  • Balmer beta/H-β (4→2): 486.1 nm (blue)
  • Balmer gamma/H-γ (5→2): 434.0 nm (violet)

Problem-Solving Tips

  • Always use E_n = -13.6/n² eV for hydrogen energy
  • For wavelength: use 1/λ = R(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²) directly
  • For photon energy: E_photon = hc/λ = 1240 eV·nm/λ(nm)
  • Remember: higher n means less negative (higher) energy
  • Emission occurs when n decreases; absorption when n increases

Constants and Conversions

  • h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s = 4.136 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV·s
  • c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
  • hc = 1240 eV·nm (useful for λ-energy conversions)
  • a₀ = 0.529 Å = 0.0529 nm = 5.29 × 10⁻¹¹ m
  • 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
