The Amazing World of Solutes, Solvents, and Solutions

Class 8 · Chapter 9 · CBSE Science

When you stir sugar into tea and it vanishes, has it truly disappeared — or is something far more fascinating happening at the particle level?

🧪 Solutions and Their Types

📚 What is a Solution?
Definition: A solution is a homogeneous mixtureUniform composition throughout of two or more substances where one substance (solute) is dissolved in another substance (solvent).

A solution has:

  • Solute: The substance being dissolved (usually present in smaller amount)
  • Solvent: The substance that dissolves the solute (usually present in larger amount)
  • Solution: The resulting homogeneous mixture
Analogy: Imagine salt dissolving in water. The salt is like a puzzle piece and the water is like the puzzle board. Once dissolved, they form a perfect, uniform fit where you can't separate them with just your eyes.
💧 Water: The Universal Solvent

Water is called the "universal solvent" because it can dissolve more substances than any other liquid. This is due to its polar molecular structure, which allows it to interact with many types of solutes.

Examples of solutions with water as solvent:
  • Salt water (ionic compounds)
  • Sugar solution (polar covalent compounds)
  • Vinegar solution (miscible liquids)
  • Oxygen in water (gases)
⚗️ Types of Solutions by Physical States
Type Example Solute Solvent
Solid in Liquid Sugar water, Salt solution Solid (Sugar) Liquid (Water)
Liquid in Liquid Vinegar in water, Alcohol in water Liquid (Vinegar) Liquid (Water)
Gas in Liquid Aerated drinks (Colas), Oxygen in water Gas (CO₂, O₂) Liquid (Water)
Gas in Gas Air (O₂, N₂ in other gases) Gas (O₂, etc.) Gas (N₂ mainly)
Solid in Solid Brass (Cu+Zn), Bronze (Cu+Sn) Solid (Zn, Sn) Solid (Cu)
Real-world Example: Brass is a mixture of copper and zinc. It's a solid solution where zinc atoms are dissolved in the solid copper lattice. This creates a material harder than pure copper!

🔬 Properties of Solutions

All true solutions share common characteristic properties that distinguish them from suspensions and colloidal solutions:

  • Homogeneous: Uniform composition throughout the mixture
  • Transparent: Light can pass through easily; they are clear
  • Particle Size: Particles are extremely small, less than 1 nanometer (1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m)
  • Don't Scatter Light: Too small to scatter light (no Tyndall effect)
  • Stable: Particles don't settle to the bottom on standing
  • Filterable: Particles are so small they can pass through filter paper
🧬 Why These Properties Matter

The particle size in solutions (<1 nm) is so incredibly small that:

  • Solute particles are surrounded by solvent molecules individually
  • They behave almost like individual atoms or molecules
  • They can pass through the pores of filter paper (which are much larger)
  • The solution appears perfectly uniform to the naked eye
  • Light passes through without hitting any particles

This is fundamentally different from suspensions where particles are visible and from colloids where particles are large enough to scatter light.

📊 Concentration of Solutions

⚖️ Types of Solutions by Concentration
  • Dilute Solution: Contains less solute and more solvent (weak solution)
  • Concentrated Solution: Contains more solute and less solvent (strong solution)
  • Saturated Solution: Contains the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at a given temperature
  • Unsaturated Solution: Contains less solute than the maximum; can still dissolve more solute
  • Supersaturated Solution: Contains more solute than normally possible at that temperature (unstable)
Example: At 20°C, water can dissolve up to 36 g of salt per 100 g of water. A solution with 30 g salt is unsaturated. A solution with 36 g salt is saturated. You can't normally dissolve 40 g salt, but under special conditions, you might create a supersaturated solution with 40 g salt!
🧮 Calculating Concentration
Mass Percentage Formula
$$\text{Mass Percentage} = \frac{\text{Mass of Solute}}{\text{Mass of Solution}} \times 100$$
Calculation Example:

If 10 g of salt is dissolved in 90 g of water:

  • Mass of solute = 10 g (salt)
  • Mass of solvent = 90 g (water)
  • Mass of solution = 10 + 90 = 100 g
$$\text{Mass Percentage} = \frac{10}{100} \times 100 = 10\%$$

So this is a 10% salt solution.

Volume Percentage Formula
$$\text{Volume Percentage} = \frac{\text{Volume of Solute}}{\text{Volume of Solution}} \times 100$$
Example: If 20 mL of alcohol is mixed with 80 mL of water:
$$\text{Volume Percentage} = \frac{20}{100} \times 100 = 20\% \text{ alcohol}$$

💎 Solubility and Its Factors

Definition: SolubilityMax amount of solute in solvent is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in 100 g of solvent at a given temperature, forming a saturated solution.
Example: The solubility of sugar in water at 25°C is approximately 200 g/100 g H₂O. This means you can dissolve up to 200 g of sugar in 100 g of water at this temperature.
🌡️ Effect of Temperature on Solubility
  • For Most Solid Solutes: Solubility increases with increase in temperature
    Example: KNO₃ (potassium nitrate) solubility increases significantly from 13 g/100g H₂O at 0°C to 246 g/100g H₂O at 100°C
  • Some Exceptions: A few solids like Ce₂(SO₄)₃ (cerium sulfate) show decreasing solubility with temperature increase
  • For Gases: Solubility decreases with increase in temperature
    Example: Oxygen is more soluble in cold water than hot water, which is why fish thrive in cold water
  • For Gases and Pressure: Solubility increases with increase in pressure (Henry's Law)
    Example: Carbonated drinks have high CO₂ solubility due to high pressure in the bottle
Temperature Analogy: Think of heat like a bustling marketplace. At higher temperatures, the solvent molecules move faster and can "accommodate" more solute particles (like a busy marketplace with more space). For gases, higher temperature means particles escape more easily, so fewer dissolve.
🧲 The "Like Dissolves Like" Principle

"Like dissolves like" is a fundamental principle in chemistry:

  • Polar solvents dissolve polar solutes well (e.g., water dissolves salt)
  • Non-polar solvents dissolve non-polar solutes well (e.g., benzene dissolves oil)
  • Polar solvents do NOT dissolve non-polar solutes well (water doesn't mix well with oil)
Examples:
  • Water (polar) dissolves sugar (polar) easily ✓
  • Water (polar) does not dissolve oil (non-polar) well ✗
  • Kerosene (non-polar) dissolves grease (non-polar) easily ✓
  • Kerosene (non-polar) does not dissolve salt (polar) ✗
📈 Solubility Curves

A solubility curve is a graph showing how the solubility of a substance varies with temperature. These curves help predict whether a solution is saturated or unsaturated at a particular temperature.

  • On the curve: Solution is SATURATED (at maximum solubility)
  • Below the curve: Solution is UNSATURATED (can dissolve more)
  • Above the curve: Solution is SUPERSATURATED (unstable, may crystallize)

Example Reading: If a solubility curve shows KNO₃ has solubility of 30 g/100g H₂O at 20°C:

  • A solution with 25 g KNO₃ in 100g H₂O at 20°C is UNSATURATED
  • A solution with 30 g KNO₃ in 100g H₂O at 20°C is SATURATED
  • A solution with 35 g KNO₃ in 100g H₂O at 20°C is SUPERSATURATED

☁️ Suspensions: A Different Kind of Mixture

Definition: A suspension is a heterogeneous mixtureNon-uniform composition where insoluble solid particles are dispersed in a liquid medium but do not dissolve.
🔍 Characteristics of Suspensions
Property Solution Suspension
Particle Size < 1 nm > 100 nm (visible)
Appearance Transparent, clear Turbid, opaque, cloudy
Homogeneity Homogeneous Heterogeneous
Settling Don't settle Settle on standing
Light Scattering No Tyndall effect Tyndall effect visible

Stability Stable Unstable
💡 The Tyndall Effect
Tyndall Effect: The scattering of light by colloidal or suspended particles, making the path of light visible in the medium.
Real-Life Examples:
  • A beam of sunlight through dusty air becomes visible
  • Light from a cinema projector becomes visible in a smoky room
  • A flashlight beam visible in fog or mist
  • Muddy water appears opaque when light shines through it
📋 Common Examples of Suspensions
  • Muddy water: Soil particles suspended in water
  • Chalk in water: Chalk particles floating in water
  • Sand in water: Sand particles suspended in water
  • Flour in water: Flour particles in water
  • Pollen in air: Pollen grains suspended in air
  • Smoke: Carbon particles suspended in air

🌫️ Colloidal Solutions: The In-Between

Definition: A colloidal solution is a heterogeneous mixture where particles of size between 1-100 nm are dispersed in a dispersing medium, appearing homogeneous but remaining heterogeneous at the molecular level.

A colloidal system has two components:

  • Dispersed Phase: The solute-like substance (the particles being dispersed)
  • Dispersion Medium: The solvent-like substance (the medium in which particles are dispersed)
🎪 Particle Size Comparison

Particle Size Scale

Solutions: < 1 nm (Very small)

Colloids: 1-100 nm (Medium)

Suspensions: > 100 nm (Visible)

⚙️ Properties of Colloids
  • Appearance: Appear homogeneous to the naked eye, but are heterogeneous
  • Tyndall Effect: Show Tyndall effect; light path is visible
  • Settling: Don't settle under normal conditions (stable)
  • Particle Size: Cannot be separated by normal filtration, but can be separated by special methods (ultrafiltration)
  • Dispersed Phase: Can be solid, liquid, or gas
  • Dispersion Medium: Can be solid, liquid, or gas
📊 Types of Colloidal Solutions
Type Dispersed Phase Medium Name Example
1 Liquid Liquid Emulsion Milk, cream, mayonnaise
2 Solid Liquid Sol Ink, paint, starch solution
3 Gas Liquid Foam Soap bubbles, shaving cream
4 Solid Gas Aerosol Smoke, dust in air
5 Liquid Gas Aerosol Fog, mist, clouds
6 Liquid Solid Gel Jelly, cheese, butter
7 Solid Solid Solid Sol Ruby glass, alloys
🥛 Real-World Examples
Milk (Emulsion - Liquid in Liquid):
  • Fat droplets are dispersed in water
  • Appears uniform white color
  • Shows Tyndall effect
  • Can be separated by centrifugation into cream (fat) and milk solids
Fog (Aerosol - Liquid in Gas):
  • Water droplets suspended in air
  • Reduces visibility dramatically
  • Shows Tyndall effect (reduced visibility)
  • Eventually settles as moisture
Smoke (Aerosol - Solid in Gas):
  • Carbon particles suspended in air
  • Visible as dark clouds
  • Shows Tyndall effect (visible beam in projector)
  • From burning of materials
Jelly (Gel - Liquid in Solid):
  • Water trapped in a solid gelatin network
  • Jello dessert is the most common example
  • Semi-solid consistency
  • Can be separated by warming or special filters

⚗️ Separation of Mixtures

Different mixtures require different separation techniques. Choose based on the properties of components:

💧 1. Evaporation
Method: Heating a solution to convert the solvent (usually water) into vapor, leaving the solute behind.
  • Used for: Obtaining solids from aqueous solutions
  • Best for: Solutes with high boiling points or thermal stability
  • Example: Extracting salt from seawater by boiling
Process: Seawater → Heat → Water evaporates → Salt crystals remain behind
🔗 2. Filtration
Method: Separating insoluble solids from liquids using filter paper with small pores.
  • Used for: Separating solid particles from liquids
  • Works for: Suspensions and mixtures with large particles (>1 μm)
  • Does NOT work for: Solutions and colloids (particles too small)
  • Example: Separating chalk powder from water, separating sand from water
Analogy: Filter paper is like a net. Big fish (particles) get caught, but water flows through. Very small fish (solution particles) slip right through.
🌡️ 3. Distillation
Method: Separating liquids with different boiling points by heating and condensing the vapor.
  • Used for: Separating liquids from liquid-liquid mixtures or liquid from solids
  • Based on: Different boiling points of liquids
  • Process: Heat → Liquid with lower boiling point evaporates first → Cool → Condense back to liquid
  • Example: Obtaining pure water from salt water, separating alcohol from water
  1. Heating: Mixture is heated in a distillation flask
  2. Evaporation: Liquid with lower boiling point evaporates first (travels as vapor)
  3. Condensation: Vapor enters a condenser cooled by cold water
  4. Liquefaction: Vapor cools and converts back to liquid
  5. Collection: Pure liquid is collected in a receiving flask
🔬 4. Fractional Distillation
Method: Separating liquids with similar boiling points using a fractionating column.
  • Used for: Complex mixtures of liquids with close boiling points
  • Primary use: Petroleum refining (crude oil separation)
  • Advantage over simple distillation: Better separation of components
  • Example: Separating petrol, diesel, kerosene from crude oil
Petroleum Products by Boiling Point:
  • Petrol (gasoline): 40-200°C
  • Kerosene: 150-300°C
  • Diesel: 250-350°C
  • Fuel oil: > 350°C
🎨 5. Chromatography
Method: Separating dissolved substances based on their different rates of movement through a medium.
  • Used for: Separating different dissolved substances (especially useful for colored substances)
  • Principle: Different compounds move at different speeds through the medium
  • Types: Paper chromatography, thin-layer chromatography, column chromatography
  • Examples: Separating colors in ink, separating food dyes, analyzing food additives
Paper Chromatography Example (Separating colors in black ink):
  • Place black ink spot on filter paper
  • Dip bottom edge in water solution (mobile phase)
  • Water travels up the paper (capillary action)
  • Different color dyes move at different rates
  • Red might move 5 cm, yellow 7 cm, blue 3 cm from starting point
  • Results in separated colored bands
💠 6. Crystallization
Method: Obtaining pure solid crystals of a substance from its solution by cooling or evaporating the solvent.
  • Used for: Obtaining pure solid solutes from solutions
  • Process: Prepare hot saturated solution → Cool slowly → Crystals form → Filter and dry
  • Why it works: As temperature decreases, solubility decreases; excess solute crystallizes
  • Examples: Rock candy from sugar solution, salt crystals, sugar crystals
Analogy: Imagine a crowded room (hot solution with dissolved solute). As the room cools, people start leaving and standing outside (crystallizing). By early morning, most have left and organized into neat groups (crystals).
🌀 7. Centrifugation
Method: Using rapid spinning to separate components based on density differences.
  • Used for: Separating substances of different densities
  • Principle: Denser particles move outward; less dense remain near center
  • Examples: Separating cream from milk, separating blood components, separating cells from body fluids
  • Speed: Works faster than gravity settling for fine separations
Centrifugation Example (Milk separation):
  • Whole milk in centrifuge
  • Spin at high speed (1000+ rpm)
  • Heavy milk solids move outward
  • Light fat globules (cream) stay toward center
  • Result: Separated cream and skim milk
🔄 8. Separating Funnel (for Immiscible Liquids)
Method: Separating two immiscible liquids (liquids that don't mix) using a separating funnel.
  • Used for: Separating liquids that don't dissolve in each other
  • Principle: Liquids form distinct layers; denser liquid sinks to bottom
  • Process: Pour mixture into funnel → Layers separate → Open stopcock → Collect heavier liquid first
  • Example: Separating oil and water, separating benzene and water
Example (Oil and Water Separation):
  • Oil and water mixture in separating funnel
  • Let stand: Oil floats on top, water sinks to bottom
  • Open stopcock: Water drains out first
  • Close when oil reaches stopcock level
  • Result: Separated oil and water
📋 Choosing the Right Separation Method
Mixture Type Best Method Reason
Solid + Liquid (insoluble) Filtration Particles large enough to be trapped
Solid from solution Evaporation or Crystallization Solvent removed by heat or cooling
Liquid + Liquid (different BP) Distillation Different boiling points enable separation
Liquids with similar BP Fractional Distillation Fractionating column provides better separation
Colored dissolved substances Chromatography Different movement rates in medium
Immiscible liquids Separating Funnel Liquids don't mix; form distinct layers
Substances of different density Centrifugation Density differences exploit spinning force

🌡️ Effect of Temperature on Solubility

Temperature is one of the most important factors affecting solubility. However, the effect depends on whether the solute is a solid, liquid, or gas.

📈 Solids: Solubility Usually Increases with Temperature
Most Common Pattern:
  • KNO₃ (Potassium Nitrate): 13 g/100g H₂O at 0°C → 246 g/100g H₂O at 100°C
  • Sugar (Sucrose): 180 g/100g H₂O at 0°C → 487 g/100g H₂O at 100°C
  • NaCl (Salt): ~36 g/100g H₂O at all temperatures (nearly constant)
Why This Happens: Higher temperature means more kinetic energy. Solvent molecules move faster and break apart solute particles more effectively. The dissolved particles can move freely in the hotter, more energetic solvent.

While rare, some solids show decreasing solubility with increasing temperature:

  • Ce₂(SO₄)₃ (Cerium Sulfate): Solubility decreases as temperature increases
  • This unusual behavior is due to special interactions between the solute and solvent
  • For most purposes, you can assume solubility increases with temperature for solids
📉 Gases: Solubility Decreases with Temperature
Pattern for Gases:
  • Oxygen solubility in water: Higher at 0°C, lower at 25°C
  • CO₂ solubility in water: Decreases significantly as temperature rises
  • This is why hot water goes flat faster than cold water
Why This Happens: Gas molecules are weakly bound in liquid. Higher temperature gives gas molecules more energy to escape from the solution back into the gas phase. It's easier for them to "run away" when it's hot!
Real-World Implication: Fish thrive better in cold water because cold water can hold more dissolved oxygen. In warm water, there's less oxygen available for fish to breathe through their gills.
🔧 Gases and Pressure: Henry's Law
Henry's Law: The solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the pressure of the gas above the liquid.
Henry's Law
$$P = kH \times C$$

Where P = pressure, C = concentration, kH = Henry's Law constant

Practical Example (Carbonated Drinks):
  • Bottles are filled under high pressure (typically 2-4 atm of CO₂)
  • High pressure forces more CO₂ to dissolve in the liquid
  • When you open the bottle, pressure drops suddenly
  • CO₂ escapes as bubbles (reduces solubility)
  • After some time, the drink goes flat
Pressure Analogy: Think of gas molecules trying to stay dissolved. High pressure pushes them down into the liquid. Low pressure lets them escape easily, like releasing pressure on a balloon.
📊 Summary: Temperature and Pressure Effects
Solute Type Temperature Effect Pressure Effect Reason
Solid Usually increases No significant effect Higher temp = faster solvent molecules
Gas Decreases Increases (Henry's Law) Higher temp = gases escape; Higher P = gases dissolve more

📚 Exam Preparation: HOTS & Case-Based Questions

Prepare for higher-order thinking questions that may appear in your exams with these challenging questions.

Higher-Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

HOTS
A scientist wants to obtain pure salt crystals from seawater. She has equipment for evaporation, crystallization, and filtration. In what order should she use these methods, and why?
Answer Approach:
  • Step 1 - Evaporation: Heat seawater to evaporate most water. This concentrates the dissolved salts.
  • Step 2 - Crystallization: Cool the concentrated solution slowly. Pure salt crystals will form and precipitate.
  • Step 3 - Filtration: Filter to separate solid salt crystals from the remaining liquid (brine).
  • Why this order: Evaporation removes most solvent, crystallization purifies the salt, and filtration isolates the solid product.
HOTS
Explain why a saturated solution at 20°C might become supersaturated when cooled to 10°C, even though the solubility of the solute decreases with decreasing temperature. What might happen to the solution?
Answer Approach:
  • Initial situation: Saturated solution at 20°C contains max solute for that temperature.
  • When cooled to 10°C: Solubility decreases (lower temperature = less can dissolve).
  • Result: The solution now contains MORE solute than can normally dissolve at 10°C = SUPERSATURATED.
  • What happens: The excess solute will precipitate out as crystals, restoring saturation at the new temperature. This process might be rapid or slow depending on conditions.
HOTS
You observe that a carbonated drink goes flat faster when left at room temperature (25°C) than when kept in a refrigerator (4°C). Using the principles of gas solubility, explain this observation.
Answer Approach:
  • Principle: Solubility of gases decreases with increasing temperature.
  • At 25°C (room temperature): Gas solubility is low, so CO₂ escapes easily from the solution as bubbles.
  • At 4°C (refrigerator): Gas solubility is high, so more CO₂ remains dissolved. Fewer bubbles escape.
  • Conclusion: The drink goes flat faster at higher temperature because gases escape more readily when solubility is lower.

Case-Based Questions

Case-Based
Case: A student mixed three substances: sugar solution, muddy water, and milk. He needs to classify each as a solution, suspension, or colloid based on their properties.
Sub-questions:
  • a) Which mixture would show the Tyndall effect? Why?
  • b) Which mixture would completely pass through filter paper? Why?
  • c) Which mixture can be separated by normal filtration? Why?
  • d) Arrange the three in order of particle size (smallest to largest).
Answers:
  • a) Muddy water and milk show Tyndall effect (larger particles scatter light). Particle size 1-100 nm range.
  • b) Sugar solution passes through (particles < 1 nm - too small to be trapped by filter).
  • c) Muddy water can be filtered (suspension with particles > 100 nm).
  • d) Sugar solution < Milk < Muddy water (solution < colloid < suspension)
Case-Based
Case: A laboratory has samples of: (A) Tap water with salt dissolved, (B) Oil mixed with water, (C) A mixture of alcohol and water (both miscible).
Sub-questions:
  • a) Which sample would require a separating funnel for separation?
  • b) For sample A, suggest a separation method and explain why it works.
  • c) For sample C, which separation method would be most suitable?
  • d) Explain why you cannot use evaporation to separate sample B.
Answers:
  • a) Sample B (oil and water) - they are immiscible liquids that form distinct layers.
  • b) Evaporation or crystallization - heating removes water, leaving salt crystals behind.
  • c) Distillation - alcohol and water have different boiling points (78°C and 100°C respectively).
  • d) Oil and water don't form a solution; evaporation would remove water but wouldn't separate oil properly. Separating funnel is better.
Case-Based
Case: A chemist prepares three solutions of KNO₃: Solution 1 at 20°C, Solution 2 at 50°C, and Solution 3 at 80°C, all containing 50 g of KNO₃ in 100 g of water. (Solubility data: 20°C = 31 g/100g H₂O, 50°C = 85 g/100g H₂O, 80°C = 169 g/100g H₂O)
Sub-questions:
  • a) Which solution(s) are unsaturated?
  • b) Which solution is supersaturated? Is it stable?
  • c) If Solution 1 is cooled to 0°C (solubility = 13 g/100g H₂O), what would happen?
  • d) Arrange the three solutions in order of concentration (dilute to concentrated).
Answers:
  • a) Solutions 2 and 3 are unsaturated (50 g < 85 g and 50 g < 169 g respectively). Can dissolve more KNO₃.
  • b) Solution 1 is supersaturated (50 g > 31 g). NOT stable - crystals will precipitate out.
  • c) At 0°C, the solution can only hold 13 g KNO₃. The excess (50-13=37 g) will crystallize out as KNO₃ crystals.
  • d) All have same mass %, so concentration is same (20%). But Solution 1 is saturated, while 2 and 3 are unsaturated at their temperatures.

⚡ Cheat Sheet & Quick Reference

Key Formulas

Mass Percentage = (Mass of Solute / Mass of Solution) × 100
Volume Percentage = (Volume of Solute / Volume of Solution) × 100
Henry's Law: P = kH × C (Pressure ∝ Concentration of gas)

Particle Size Classification

Solutions: < 1 nm → Transparent, stable, pass through filter

Colloids: 1-100 nm → Tyndall effect, appear homogeneous, need special filters

Suspensions: > 100 nm → Opaque, settle, can be filtered, Tyndall effect

Types of Solutions by Physical State

  • Solid in Liquid: Sugar water, salt solution
  • Liquid in Liquid: Vinegar, alcohol in water
  • Gas in Liquid: Aerated drinks, carbonated water
  • Gas in Gas: Air
  • Solid in Solid: Brass, bronze

Separation Methods at a Glance

  • Evaporation: Liquid + Solid → Remove solvent by heating
  • Filtration: Suspension → Separate solid from liquid
  • Distillation: Liquids with different BP → Separate by boiling point
  • Fractional Distillation: Petroleum products → Separate close boiling point liquids
  • Chromatography: Dissolved colorants → Separate by movement rate
  • Crystallization: Solute from solution → Cool to form crystals
  • Centrifugation: Different density → Spin to separate
  • Separating Funnel: Immiscible liquids → Separate oil & water

Glossary of Important Terms

Solution: A homogeneous mixture where solute dissolves completely in solvent
Solute: The substance being dissolved (usually less amount)
Solvent: The substance doing the dissolving (usually more amount)
Saturated Solution: Maximum solute dissolved at given temperature; cannot dissolve more
Unsaturated Solution: Less than maximum solute; can dissolve more
Supersaturated Solution: More solute than normally possible; unstable
Solubility: Maximum amount of solute per 100g solvent at given temperature
Tyndall Effect: Light scattering by colloidal or suspended particles
Homogeneous Mixture: Uniform composition; appears as single phase
Heterogeneous Mixture: Non-uniform composition; multiple phases visible
Colloid: Mixture with 1-100 nm particles; appears homogeneous but is heterogeneous
Suspension: Mixture with particles > 100 nm; settles and can be filtered

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Mistake: Confusing solutions with suspensions based on appearance alone. ✓ Fix: Use Tyndall effect test or particle size to distinguish.
❌ Mistake: Forgetting to add solute mass to solvent mass when calculating mass percentage. ✓ Fix: Always use total solution mass = solute + solvent.
❌ Mistake: Assuming solubility increases with temperature for ALL substances. ✓ Fix: Remember gases decrease with temperature; most solids increase.
❌ Mistake: Using filtration to separate immiscible liquids. ✓ Fix: Use separating funnel for oil-water mixtures.
❌ Mistake: Not distinguishing between colloids and suspensions based on particle size. ✓ Fix: 1-100 nm = colloid (Tyndall effect), > 100 nm = suspension (settles).
