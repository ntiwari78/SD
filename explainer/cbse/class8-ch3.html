Health: The Ultimate Treasure

Class 8 Science | Chapter 3 | Biology - Health and Diseases

"You have a treasure more valuable than all the gold in the world — your health. But what exactly keeps this treasure safe, and what threatens it? In this chapter, we'll unlock the secrets of staying healthy and defeating the diseases that challenge us."

What is Health?

Official Definition (WHO)

"Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity."

— World Health Organization (WHO), 1946

Health is not just the absence of illness. It's a holistic concept that encompasses three critical dimensions working together:

Three Pillars of Health

1. Physical Health

  • Body functioning normally without disease
  • Strong immune system to fight infections
  • Proper growth and development
  • Energy and stamina for daily activities

2. Mental Health

  • Emotional well-being and stability
  • Ability to manage stress and emotions
  • Sound mind and good memory
  • Positive outlook towards life

3. Social Health

  • Good relationships with family and friends
  • Feeling accepted in the community
  • Ability to participate in social activities
  • Contributing meaningfully to society
Analogy: The Three-Legged Stool

Think of health as a three-legged stool:

  • Leg 1: Physical health
  • Leg 2: Mental health
  • Leg 3: Social health

Remove any one leg, and the entire stool topples! All three must be balanced for true health.

What Keeps Us Healthy?

Individual/Personal Health Factors:

  • Balanced Diet: Proteins, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, minerals, water, and roughage
  • Personal Hygiene: Regular bathing, clean clothes, handwashing, dental care, trimmed nails
  • Exercise & Sports: Regular physical activity strengthens muscles and bones
  • Proper Sleep & Rest: 7-9 hours daily for body recovery and mental rejuvenation
  • Stress Management: Meditation, hobbies, social interaction

Community/Public Health Factors:

  • Clean Water Supply: For drinking and hygiene
  • Sanitation & Sewage: Proper disposal prevents disease spread
  • Waste Management: Regular garbage collection and disposal
  • Public Parks & Recreation: Spaces for exercise and mental health
  • Healthcare System: Accessible clinics and hospitals
  • Health Education: Awareness programs about diseases and prevention

What is Disease?

The word "disease" literally means dis + ease = not at ease. It's a condition where the body doesn't function normally.

Disease Definition

Disease: Any abnormal condition of the body that impairs normal functioning and is characterized by specific symptoms.

Symptoms vs Signs

Symptoms (Subjective)

What the patient feels and reports:

  • Fever/chills
  • Pain or ache
  • Nausea
  • Weakness/fatigue
  • Dizziness

Signs (Objective)

What the doctor observes or measures:

  • High body temperature (>98.6°F or 37°C)
  • Rash or swelling
  • Abnormal pulse rate
  • Blood pressure changes
  • Visible discharge or bleeding

Two Main Types of Diseases

Communicable Diseases Non-Communicable Diseases
Can spread from person to person Cannot spread from person to person
Caused by pathogens (bacteria, virus, fungi, etc.) Caused by deficiency, lifestyle, genetics, or allergies
Examples: Tuberculosis, Malaria, Chickenpox Examples: Diabetes, Heart disease, Obesity
Preventable by vaccination and hygiene Managed through diet, exercise, and medication

Communicable (Infectious) Diseases

These diseases are caused by pathogens — microscopic organisms like bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, and worms. They spread from an infected person to a healthy person through various routes.

How Do Communicable Diseases Spread?

Five Routes of Transmission:

  1. Direct Contact: Touching infected person's skin or body fluids
    • Example: Ringworm, Athlete's foot
  2. Droplet Infection (Air): Through sneezing, coughing, or talking
    • Example: Common cold, Influenza, Tuberculosis
  3. Contaminated Water: Drinking or bathing in unsafe water
    • Example: Cholera, Typhoid, Dysentery
  4. Contaminated Food: Eating spoiled or unhygienic food
    • Example: Food poisoning, Hepatitis A
  5. Vector Transmission: Through disease-carrying animals (insects)
    • Example: Malaria (mosquito), Dengue (mosquito), Plague (fleas)

Parasitic Diseases

Protozoan Diseases:

  • Malaria
    • Caused by: Plasmodium protozoan
    • Vector: Female Anopheles mosquito
    • Symptoms: Recurring fever, chills, sweating (fever spikes every 48-72 hours)
    • Prevention: Mosquito nets, insecticides, antimalarial drugs
  • Dysentery (Amoebic)
    • Caused by: Entamoeba histolytica protozoan
    • Transmission: Contaminated water/food
    • Symptoms: Severe diarrhea with blood and mucus, abdominal pain

Fungal Diseases:

  • Ringworm — Fungal infection of skin, not actually a worm!
    • Spread by direct contact or shared items
    • Circular rash with itching
  • Athlete's Foot — Fungal infection between toes
    • Thrives in warm, moist environments
    • Itching, cracking, peeling skin

Worm Diseases:

  • Ascariasis (Roundworm)
    • Parasite: Ascaris lumbricoides
    • Transmission: Contaminated food/water
    • Symptoms: Abdominal pain, malnutrition, weakness
  • Elephantiasis (Filarial Worm)
    • Parasite: Wuchereria bancrofti
    • Vector: Culex mosquito
    • Symptoms: Severe swelling of limbs (can become massive)
    • Endemic in tropical regions

Non-Communicable (Non-Infectious) Diseases

These diseases cannot spread from one person to another. They develop due to deficiency, lifestyle choices, genetics, or allergies.

Deficiency Diseases

Caused by lack of essential nutrients:

Disease Vitamin/Mineral Deficiency Symptoms Food Sources
Scurvy Vitamin C Bleeding gums, poor wound healing, weakness Citrus fruits, tomatoes, spinach, guava
Rickets Vitamin D Weak bones, bowing of legs, tooth decay Sunlight, milk, egg yolk, fish
Anaemia Iron Pale skin, weakness, shortness of breath Spinach, beans, red meat, fortified cereals
Goitre Iodine Swollen thyroid gland in neck Iodized salt, seafood, seaweed
Night Blindness Vitamin A Cannot see in dim light Carrots, sweet potato, pumpkin, egg
Beriberi Vitamin B1 (Thiamine) Nerve damage, muscle weakness, heart problems Whole grains, peanuts, pork, seeds
Allergies

An allergy is an exaggerated immune response to specific substances called allergens.

Common Allergens:

  • Dust particles
  • Pollen (hay fever)
  • Pet fur
  • Food (nuts, shellfish, milk)
  • Insect stings
  • Mold
  • Medications

Allergic Reactions:

  • Mild: Itching, rash, sneezing, watery eyes
  • Moderate: Swelling, hives, difficulty breathing
  • Severe: Anaphylaxis (life-threatening)

Management:

  • Identify and avoid allergens
  • Use antihistamine medications
  • Keep epinephrine auto-injector for severe allergies
Lifestyle Diseases

These develop due to unhealthy living habits:

Diabetes

  • Blood glucose levels too high (hyperglycemia)
  • Causes: Obesity, lack of exercise, high sugar diet, family history
  • Symptoms: Frequent urination, thirst, weakness, weight loss
  • Management: Diet control, exercise, insulin, regular monitoring

Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

  • Blood pressure consistently above 140/90 mmHg
  • Causes: High salt intake, obesity, stress, lack of exercise
  • Often called "silent killer" — usually no symptoms until severe
  • Management: Low salt diet, exercise, weight loss, medication

Obesity

  • Excess body weight (BMI > 30)
  • Causes: Overeating, sedentary lifestyle, junk food
  • Leads to: Diabetes, heart disease, hypertension, joint problems
  • Management: Balanced diet, regular exercise, behavioral changes

Heart Disease

  • Weakening of heart muscle or blocked arteries
  • Causes: High blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, obesity
  • Symptoms: Chest pain, shortness of breath, fatigue
  • Prevention: Healthy diet, exercise, stress management, avoid smoking

Cancer

  • Uncontrolled growth of abnormal cells
  • Risk factors: Smoking, alcohol, sun exposure, family history, age
  • Early detection and treatment improve survival rates
Genetic/Hereditary Diseases

Diseases passed from parents to children through genes:

  • Cystic Fibrosis — Thick mucus in lungs and digestive system
  • Sickle Cell Disease — Abnormal red blood cells
  • Hemophilia — Blood doesn't clot properly
  • Colour Blindness — Cannot distinguish certain colors
  • Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy — Progressive muscle weakness

Note: These cannot be "caught" but are inherited in the genes.

Prevention of Diseases

The best strategy is prevention — stopping disease before it starts. Here's how:

Personal & Domestic Hygiene

Daily Personal Practices:

  • Handwashing: Before eating, after toilet, after playing — kills pathogens
  • Bathing: Daily shower/bath removes dirt and germs
  • Clean Clothes: Change daily, especially undergarments
  • Dental Care: Brush twice daily, floss, regular checkups
  • Nail Care: Trim nails short, keep them clean
  • Hair Hygiene: Regular washing, brushing

Food Hygiene:

  • Eat freshly cooked food
  • Keep raw and cooked food separate
  • Use clean utensils and cooking vessels
  • Drink boiled/filtered water only
  • Wash fruits and vegetables before eating
  • Avoid street food and unhygienic restaurants
Public Health & Sanitation

Government/Community Measures:

  • Clean Water Supply: Chlorination and testing
  • Sewage Treatment: Proper disposal of wastewater
  • Waste Management: Regular collection and landfilling
  • Insect Control: Spraying pesticides to eliminate vectors
  • Public Toilets: Clean, accessible facilities for all
  • Health Education: Campaigns about hygiene and disease prevention
Vaccination/Immunization

What is Vaccination?

A medical procedure where weakened or dead pathogens (or their parts) are introduced into the body to stimulate the immune system.

Historical Milestone

Edward Jenner (1798) developed the first vaccine against smallpox using cowpox virus. This was a revolutionary discovery that saved millions of lives.

How Vaccines Work:

  1. Weakened/dead pathogen injected into body
  2. Immune system recognizes it as foreign
  3. Produces antibodies against the pathogen
  4. Creates memory B cells and T cells
  5. Next time real pathogen enters, immune system is ready
  6. Disease is prevented or minimized

Benefits of Vaccination:

  • Individual Protection: Vaccinated person doesn't get the disease
  • Community/Herd Immunity: When 70-90% vaccinated, disease can't spread
  • Eradication: Smallpox completely eradicated (1980)
  • Safe & Effective: Few side effects, high success rate

Major Vaccines (India's Routine Immunization Schedule):

Vaccine Protects Against Age Given
BCG Tuberculosis (TB) At birth
OPV/IPV Poliomyelitis (Polio) 6 weeks - 5 years (boosters)
DPT Diphtheria, Pertussis (Whooping cough), Tetanus 6 weeks - 4.5 years
MMR Measles, Mumps, Rubella 9 months - 15 months (booster at 4-6 years)
Hepatitis B Hepatitis B virus infection At birth, 6 weeks, 10 weeks, 14 weeks
Influenza Influenza (Flu) Annually from 6 months
Pulse Polio Programme (India)

A nationwide campaign launched in 1995 where all children under 5 years receive OPV vaccine on designated days. This has brought polio cases down from 100,000+ (1988) to near zero!

Exercise & Rest

Physical Exercise:

  • Recommended: 30 minutes of moderate exercise daily (or 60 minutes for children)
  • Benefits: Stronger muscles and bones, better heart health, weight control, stress relief
  • Types: Walking, running, sports, swimming, cycling, dancing, yoga

Sleep & Rest:

  • Recommended: 7-9 hours for adults, 8-10 hours for children
  • Why important:
    • Body repairs and grows during sleep
    • Memory consolidation (learning)
    • Immune system strengthens
    • Emotional regulation and stress relief
  • Sleep Tips: Consistent schedule, dark room, no screens before bed, comfortable mattress
First Aid Basics

First Aid: Immediate care given to an injured or ill person before medical help arrives.

ABC of First Aid:

  1. A — Airway: Keep airways open, position victim on side if unconscious
  2. B — Breathing: Check if victim is breathing; give CPR if needed
  3. C — Circulation: Check for pulse, stop bleeding if any

Common First Aid Situations:

Minor Cuts & Wounds:
  • Wash with clean water and soap
  • Apply antibiotic ointment
  • Cover with sterile bandage
  • Change dressing daily
Burns:
  • Cool under running water (10-20 minutes)
  • Remove tight clothing (unless stuck)
  • Apply antibiotic ointment and sterile dressing
  • Don't apply ice directly or use butter/oil
  • Seek medical help for severe burns
Fractures & Sprains:
  • RICE Method:
    • Rest — Immobilize the injured area
    • Ice — Apply for 15-20 minutes every 2 hours
    • Compression — Wrap with elastic bandage
    • Elevation — Keep above heart level
  • Use sling or splint for immobilization
  • Seek X-ray confirmation and doctor's advice
Snake Bites:
  • Stay calm (panic increases heart rate, spreading venom)
  • Wash bite with soap and water
  • Immobilize the bitten limb
  • Apply pressure bandage (not tourniquet)
  • Seek medical help immediately — Antivenom must be given early
Insect Stings/Bee Stings:
  • Remove stinger if visible (scrape off, don't pinch)
  • Wash with soap and water
  • Apply ice to reduce swelling
  • Use antihistamine cream or tablet
  • Avoid scratching to prevent infection
  • Seek emergency help if allergic (anaphylaxis)

When to Seek Medical Help:

  • Unconsciousness
  • Severe bleeding
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Chest pain
  • Signs of poisoning/snake bite
  • Severe burns
  • Suspected fractures
  • Anaphylaxis (swelling, breathing difficulty after sting/allergy)

  • Vaccination coverage >95% has virtually eradicated polio globally
  • Breakthrough infections are extremely rare (0.1-1% despite exposure)

Case-Based Questions (CBSE 2025-26 Pattern)

Case Study 1: Vaccination Campaign Impact
Passage: In a rural area of Madhya Pradesh, health officials conducted a vaccination drive. They vaccinated 85% of children against measles, polio, and diphtheria over 6 months. Before the drive, the area had 150 cases of measles annually. After the drive, measles cases dropped to just 8 cases in the next year. Similar patterns were seen for other vaccinated diseases.
(i) Why did the number of measles cases drop significantly despite some children not being vaccinated?
Answer: This is due to Herd Immunity. When 85% of the population is vaccinated, the disease cannot find enough susceptible hosts to spread. Even unvaccinated children are protected indirectly because the virus has limited opportunity to circulate. The vaccinated children act as a barrier, preventing the virus from reaching unvaccinated individuals.
(ii) Name the pathogens responsible for measles and polio, and classify them.
Answer:
  • Measles: Causative agent = Measles virus (Paramyxovirus); Classification = Viral disease
  • Polio: Causative agent = Poliovirus (Picornavirus); Classification = Viral disease
Both are RNA viruses that cause acute infections of the respiratory and digestive systems respectively.
(iii) What precautions should the health officials take to achieve 95% vaccination coverage in future campaigns?
Answer:
  • Conduct health awareness programs to counter vaccine hesitancy and myths
  • Identify and reach remote villages with mobile vaccination units
  • Schedule vaccination drives at convenient times and locations
  • Maintain cold chain facilities to keep vaccines potent
  • Train healthcare workers in vaccine administration
  • Keep proper records of vaccinated children for follow-up
  • Partner with local leaders and schools to increase community participation
Case Study 2: Balanced Diet and Deficiency Disease Prevention
Passage: Seema, age 12, complained of extreme tiredness, pale skin, and inability to concentrate in studies. Her doctor performed blood tests and found her hemoglobin levels were 8 g/dL (normal is 12-16 g/dL). The doctor prescribed iron supplements and recommended dietary changes including spinach, eggs, and fortified cereals. Her mother noted the family's diet was mainly rice and wheat without much protein or green vegetables.
(i) Identify the deficiency disease Seema is suffering from and name the missing nutrient.
Answer:
  • Disease: Anaemia (Iron Deficiency Anaemia)
  • Missing Nutrient: Iron (Fe)
  • Cause: Inadequate dietary iron intake
(ii) Why is Seema experiencing tiredness and inability to concentrate? Explain the physiological basis.
Answer:
  • Role of Iron: Iron is essential for hemoglobin synthesis in red blood cells
  • Low Hemoglobin: Reduced hemoglobin means fewer oxygen-carrying molecules in blood
  • Tissue Oxygenation: Brain and muscles don't receive sufficient oxygen
  • Symptoms:
    • Tiredness/Fatigue = muscles lack energy due to poor oxygenation
    • Poor Concentration = brain cells aren't getting enough oxygen to function optimally
    • Pale Skin = reduced blood flow and hemoglobin
(iii) Design a balanced daily diet plan for Seema that prevents this deficiency and supports overall health.

Breakfast (300 cal): Whole wheat bread (2 slices) + egg (1) + tomato + orange juice

Mid-morning (150 cal): Banana + groundnut butter

Lunch (550 cal): Rice (1 bowl) + lentil curry + spinach curry + cucumber salad + milk

Afternoon Snack (150 cal): Fortified cereal + milk

Dinner (550 cal): Wheat roti (2) + chicken/fish + green beans + carrot + yogurt

Key Features:

  • Iron-rich foods: Spinach, fortified cereals, eggs, meat
  • Vitamin C foods: Citrus fruits, tomatoes (enhances iron absorption)

Case Study 3: Disease Outbreak in Slum Colony
Passage: During monsoon season, a slum colony in Mumbai reported multiple cases of cholera, typhoid, malaria, and dengue within a 2-week period. 150 residents fell ill, 20 needed hospitalization, and 2 deaths were reported. The colony lacked proper sewage, had contaminated water supply, stagnant water pools, no drainage system, and poor personal hygiene. The health department immediately deployed medical teams, distributed oral rehydration salts (ORS), and started awareness campaigns.
(i) Classify the diseases mentioned (cholera, typhoid, malaria, dengue) based on their transmission routes.
Answer:

Water-Borne Diseases:

  • Cholera — Vibrio cholerae via contaminated water
  • Typhoid — Salmonella typhi via contaminated food/water

Vector-Borne Diseases:

  • Malaria — Plasmodium via female Anopheles mosquito
  • Dengue — Dengue virus via Aedes mosquito
(ii) Why did all four diseases spread rapidly in this colony simultaneously?
Answer:
  • Poor Sanitation: No sewage → contaminated water spread cholera and typhoid bacteria
  • Overcrowding: Close proximity → easy droplet transmission (if any respiratory cases)
  • Stagnant Water: Breeding grounds for Anopheles (malaria) and Aedes (dengue) mosquitoes
  • Weak Immunity: Malnutrition common in slums → immunodeficiency
  • No Vaccination: Low immunization rates meant population was susceptible
  • Rainy Season: Monsoon provided ideal conditions for vector breeding and water contamination
  • Limited Healthcare: No early intervention → diseases spread unchecked
(iii) As a health official, propose a comprehensive prevention strategy for this colony.
Short-Term Measures (Immediate Response):
  • Distribute ORS and provide medical care to affected residents
  • Quarantine severely ill patients to prevent further spread
  • Spray insecticides to kill mosquito vectors
  • Distribute mosquito nets (for malaria prevention)

Long-Term Prevention (Infrastructure):

  • Install proper sewage treatment system
  • Provide clean drinking water via government supply or bore wells
  • Build drainage system to eliminate stagnant water
  • Construct public toilets
  • Establish waste collection and disposal system

Health Promotion:

  • Conduct vaccination drives for polio, DPT, MMR, hepatitis B
  • Health education on hygiene, safe water, food safety
  • Establish primary health center with trained healthcare workers
  • Nutritional supplementation for children and pregnant women
  • Regular health checkups and surveillance

Remediation Guide: Struggling with Concepts?

Concept: Communicable vs Non-Communicable Diseases

Common Confusion: Students think all diseases spread from person to person.

Quick Fix: Ask yourself: "Can I catch this disease by being near someone with it?"

  • YES? It's communicable (TB, chickenpox, cholera)
  • NO? It's non-communicable (diabetes, anaemia, cancer)

Pro Tip: Non-communicable diseases usually have these causes: lack of nutrients (deficiency), bad habits (lifestyle), family history (genetic), or body's wrong reaction (allergy). None of these involve catching from someone!

Concept: Why Antibiotics Don't Work Against Viruses

Common Confusion: Antibiotics should kill all germs, including viruses.

Quick Fix: Understand the difference in structure:

  • Bacteria: Cell wall, cytoplasm, ribosomes, DNA — many unique features
  • Virus: Just protein coat + genetic material — inside host cell

The Key: Antibiotics target bacterial cell walls or ribosomes. Viruses hide inside your cells using YOUR ribosomes. Antibiotics can't attack the virus without also killing your own cells!

Analogy: If a thief locks himself in your room, you can't spray poison gas to kill the thief — you'd poison yourself too!

Concept: How Vaccines Create Immunity

Common Confusion: Vaccines inject the disease.

Quick Fix: Vaccines use WEAK or DEAD pathogens, not strong ones.

  • Weak pathogen → triggers immune response without causing disease
  • Body learns to recognize the pathogen
  • Next time real pathogen attacks → immune system is prepared!

Analogy: It's like a fire drill at school. You practice what to do in a real fire so you're prepared when it actually happens!

Concept: Transmission Routes (5 Ways)

Common Confusion: All diseases spread the same way.

Memory Trick: Think of "DCFVW"

  • D = Direct contact (touching, scratching)
  • C = Cough/sneeze (droplets in air)
  • F = Food (contaminated eating)
  • V = (Contaminated) Water
  • W = Vector (animal like mosquito)

Practice: Pick any disease and identify its route!

Concept: Deficiency Diseases

Common Confusion: Difficulty remembering which nutrient causes which disease.

Memory Trick: Connect disease to symptoms:

  • Scurvy: Vitamin C ← Sailors on ships (no fresh fruit) got scurvy
  • Night Blindness: Vitamin A ← "A" for eyes, "A" for vision
  • Rickets: Vitamin D ← "D" for bones (D is needed with calcium)
  • Anaemia: Iron ← Low iron = less red blood = pale
  • Goitre: Iodine ← Neck swelling (thyroid needs iodine)
  • Beriberi: B1 ← Nerve problems (B vitamins for nerves)
Concept: What Makes You "Healthy"?

Common Confusion: Health = just absence of disease.

Quick Fix: Remember the three pillars (P-M-S):

  • P = Physical (body functioning, no disease)
  • M = Mental (happy, relaxed, good memory)
  • S = Social (good friends, feels accepted)

Real-Life Check: A person with no disease (physical) but depressed (mental) is NOT fully healthy. All three matter!
