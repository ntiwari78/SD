Work and Energy

Class 9 · Chapter 10 · Physics · CBSE 2025-26
The Physics Hook: You push a wall and get tired — but did you do any 'work' in the physics sense? Surprisingly, no! In physics, work has a very specific meaning, and understanding it unlocks the powerful concept of energy.

1. Work: Force Meets Displacement

What is Work?

In everyday language, "work" means any activity that requires effort. But in physics, work has a precise definition: Work is done when a force acts on an object and causes displacement in the direction of the force.

When force and displacement are in the same direction:
$$W = F \times s$$

In the general case (angle θ between force and displacement):
$$W = F \times s \times \cos\theta$$

SI Unit

The SI unit of work is the Joule (J), where:

$$1 \text{ Joule} = 1 \text{ Newton} \times 1 \text{ meter} = 1 \text{ N·m}$$

Types of Work

  • Positive Work: When force and displacement are in the same direction. Example: Pushing a box forward.
  • Negative Work: When force and displacement are in opposite directions. Example: Friction opposing motion.
  • Zero Work: When force is perpendicular to displacement, or when there is no displacement. Example: Carrying a bag horizontally (gravity perpendicular to motion).

Real-World Examples

  • Pushing a box across the floor — Positive work (force and displacement same direction)
  • Friction stopping a sliding block — Negative work (friction opposes motion)
  • Carrying a heavy bag horizontally — Zero work by gravity (gravity perpendicular to displacement)
  • Pushing a wall — Zero work (no displacement occurs)

Example 1: A Porter Lifting Luggage

Problem: A porter lifts luggage of mass 15 kg to a height of 1.5 m. Calculate the work done. (Take g = 10 m/s²)

Solution:

The force required is the weight: F = mg = 15 × 10 = 150 N

Displacement is vertical: s = 1.5 m

$$W = F \times s = 150 \times 1.5 = 225 \text{ J}$$

Answer: The work done is 225 Joules.

2. Energy: The Ability to Do Work

Understanding Energy

Energy is the capacity of a system to do work. An object possessing energy can exert forces on other objects and do work on them. Energy is measured in the same units as work: Joules (J).

Forms of Energy

  • Kinetic Energy: Energy due to motion
  • Potential Energy: Energy due to position or configuration
  • Thermal/Heat Energy: Energy due to temperature
  • Chemical Energy: Energy stored in chemical bonds
  • Electrical Energy: Energy of moving electrons
  • Nuclear Energy: Energy from atomic nuclei
  • Light Energy: Energy carried by electromagnetic radiation
  • Sound Energy: Energy of vibrating matter

In this chapter, we focus on mechanical energy — the combination of kinetic and potential energy.

3. Kinetic Energy: Energy of Motion

What is Kinetic Energy?

Kinetic energy is the energy possessed by an object due to its motion. Any moving object has kinetic energy.

$$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
where m = mass (kg), v = velocity (m/s)

Key Properties of Kinetic Energy

  • Always positive or zero (never negative)
  • Depends on both mass and velocity
  • Directly proportional to mass: Double the mass → Double the KE
  • Proportional to v²: Double the velocity → 4 times the KE (since KE ∝ v²)
  • A moving car has much more KE than a moving ball at the same speed due to mass

Work-Energy Theorem

The work-energy theorem states that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

$$W_{\text{net}} = \Delta KE = KE_f - KE_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2$$

This means if you do positive work on an object, its kinetic energy increases (it speeds up). If you do negative work, its kinetic energy decreases (it slows down).

Example 2: Comparing Kinetic Energies

Problem: Calculate the kinetic energy of a 0.5 kg ball moving at:

  1. 10 m/s
  2. 20 m/s

Compare the two energies.

Solution:

At v₁ = 10 m/s:

$$KE_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.5 \times (10)^2 = 0.25 \times 100 = 25 \text{ J}$$

At v₂ = 20 m/s:

$$KE_2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.5 \times (20)^2 = 0.25 \times 400 = 100 \text{ J}$$

Comparison: When velocity doubles (10 → 20 m/s), kinetic energy becomes 4 times (25 → 100 J), confirming KE ∝ v².

4. Potential Energy: Energy of Position

What is Potential Energy?

Potential energy is the energy stored in an object due to its position or configuration. It represents the capacity to do work based on where the object is located.

Gravitational Potential Energy

The energy stored in an object at a certain height above the ground:

$$PE = mgh$$
where m = mass (kg), g = acceleration due to gravity (10 m/s²), h = height above reference level (m)

Important Points About PE

  • Relative concept: PE depends on the chosen reference level. The zero level is arbitrary.
  • Can be negative: If the object is below the reference level
  • Increases with height: Higher position = Greater PE
  • Water in a dam: Has high PE due to elevation
  • A stretched bow: Has elastic PE in the stretched string
  • A compressed spring: Has elastic PE

Other Forms of Potential Energy

  • Elastic Potential Energy: Stored in springs, stretched materials. PE = ½kx² (where k is spring constant, x is extension)
  • Chemical Potential Energy: Stored in chemical bonds of molecules

Example 3: Height and Potential Energy

Problem: A 2 kg object is placed at three different heights:

  • h₁ = 5 m (on a building)
  • h₂ = 0 m (on the ground — reference level)
  • h₃ = -3 m (in a well below reference level)

Calculate PE at each position. (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution:

$$PE_1 = mgh_1 = 2 \times 10 \times 5 = 100 \text{ J}$$ $$PE_2 = mgh_2 = 2 \times 10 \times 0 = 0 \text{ J}$$ $$PE_3 = mgh_3 = 2 \times 10 \times (-3) = -60 \text{ J}$$

5. Law of Conservation of Energy

The Fundamental Law

The law of conservation of energy states: Energy can neither be created nor destroyed; it can only be transformed from one form to another. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant.

In Mechanical Systems

For a system where only conservative forces act (like gravity), the total mechanical energy remains constant:

$$E_{\text{total}} = KE + PE = \text{constant}$$

Example: A Falling Ball

Consider a ball dropped from a height h:

  • At the top: PE = mgh, KE = 0, Total E = mgh
  • Halfway down: PE = mgh/2, KE = mgh/2, Total E = mgh
  • At the bottom: PE = 0, KE = mgh (since ½mv² = mgh), Total E = mgh

Throughout the fall, PE converts to KE, but the total energy remains mgh.

Example: A Pendulum

A pendulum continuously converts energy:

  • At the highest point: All PE, KE = 0
  • At the lowest point: All KE, PE = 0
  • In between: Mix of both PE and KE

The total energy (PE + KE) remains constant throughout the motion, ignoring air resistance and friction.

Energy Transformations in Nature

  • Chemical → Heat: Burning fuel releases thermal energy
  • Chemical → Mechanical: Food energy makes muscles move
  • Mechanical → Electrical: Hydroelectric dams convert water's PE to electricity
  • Heat → Mechanical: Steam engines convert thermal energy to motion
  • Electrical → Light: Light bulbs convert electricity to light

Example 4: A Ball Thrown Upward

Problem: A 1 kg ball is thrown upward with an initial velocity of 20 m/s. At what height will its velocity become 10 m/s? (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution: Using conservation of energy at initial position and at height h:

$$\frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh$$ $$\frac{1}{2}(20)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(10)^2 + 10h$$ $$200 = 50 + 10h$$ $$h = 15 \text{ m}$$

Answer: The velocity will be 10 m/s at a height of 15 m.

6. Power: Rate of Doing Work

What is Power?

Power is the rate at which work is done, or the rate at which energy is transferred or transformed.

$$P = \frac{W}{t}$$
where W = work done (J), t = time taken (s)

SI Unit of Power

The SI unit of power is the Watt (W):

$$1 \text{ Watt} = 1 \text{ Joule/second} = 1 \text{ J/s}$$

Other Units of Power

  • Horsepower (hp): 1 hp = 746 W (used for engines)
  • Kilowatt (kW): 1 kW = 1000 W
  • Megawatt (MW): 1 MW = 10⁶ W

Power in Terms of Force and Velocity

Power can also be expressed as:

$$P = F \times v$$
where F = force (N), v = velocity (m/s)

This shows that for the same work, more power is needed if it's done in less time.

Commercial Unit of Energy

In electricity billing, energy is measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh):

$$1 \text{ kWh} = 1000 \text{ W} \times 3600 \text{ s} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$$

For example, if you use a 1000 W (1 kW) device for 1 hour, you consume 1 kWh of energy.

Example 5: Power of a Climbing Person

Problem: A 40 kg person climbs a set of stairs of height 8 m in 10 seconds. Calculate the power developed. (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution:

First, find the work done (against gravity):

$$W = mgh = 40 \times 10 \times 8 = 3200 \text{ J}$$

Now, calculate power:

$$P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{3200}{10} = 320 \text{ W}$$

Answer: The power developed is 320 W.

Example 6: Power with Velocity

Problem: A car engine provides a constant force of 2000 N. If the car travels at 25 m/s, what is the power of the engine?

Solution:

$$P = F \times v = 2000 \times 25 = 50,000 \text{ W} = 50 \text{ kW}$$

Answer: The engine power is 50 kW (or 67 hp).

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS Question 1: Real-World Energy Transformation

Question: A hydroelectric dam holds water at a height. Explain how the potential energy of water is ultimately converted into electrical energy that reaches homes. List all the energy transformations involved.

Answer Guide: Gravitational PE of elevated water → Kinetic energy of falling/flowing water → Mechanical energy of turbine rotation → Electrical energy in generator → Electrical energy transmitted through wires → Heat/light energy in home appliances. This demonstrates that energy conservation applies across multiple transformations.

HOTS Question 2: Why Do We Need Power Ratings?

Question: Two machines do the same work (e.g., lift 1000 kg by 10 m), but Machine A takes 10 seconds and Machine B takes 20 seconds. Both do 100,000 J of work, yet they have different power ratings. Why is power more useful than just work for comparing machines?

Answer Guide: Power tells us the rate of energy transfer. Machine A (10 kW) is twice as powerful as Machine B (5 kW), meaning it's more efficient and productive. In real applications, the rate matters: a fast elevator (high power) is more practical than a slow one (low power) even if both can reach the same height.

HOTS Question 3: Energy Loss and Efficiency

Question: In real life, a ball dropped from height h doesn't bounce back to the same height. Explain what happens to the "lost" energy and why this doesn't violate the law of conservation of energy.

Answer Guide: The lost gravitational PE doesn't disappear; it's converted into other forms: sound energy (the bounce sound), heat (internal energy of ball and ground, vibrations), and deformation energy. The total energy is still conserved—it's just that not all of it returns as mechanical (kinetic/potential) energy. This introduces the concept of irreversible processes and entropy.

Case-Based Learning Questions

Case Study 1: The Athlete's Training

Scenario: A runner trains by climbing stairs. On Day 1, she climbs 50 steps (each 20 cm high) in 25 seconds. On Day 2, she climbs the same stairs in 20 seconds. Her mass is 50 kg.

Questions:

  1. Calculate the work done on both days. (Is it different?)
  2. Calculate the power developed on each day.
  3. Which day shows better athletic performance? Why?
  4. If she wants to double her power, what could she do?

Solution Guide: Work is the same both days (W = mgh = 50 × 10 × 10 = 5000 J). But power differs: Day 1: P = 200 W, Day 2: P = 250 W. Day 2 is better (higher power = more efficient). To double power, she could climb faster, climb higher stairs, or climb more stairs in the same time.

Case Study 2: Energy at the Playground

Scenario: A child (mass 30 kg) plays on a swing. At the lowest point, the swing has a speed of 8 m/s. At the highest point, the speed becomes zero.

Questions:

  1. Calculate the kinetic energy at the lowest point.
  2. If all this KE converts to PE, find the maximum height reached by the child above the lowest point.
  3. Explain why the child needs to push the swing periodically to keep it moving.

Solution Guide: KE_min = ½ × 30 × 8² = 960 J. Height = KE/(mg) = 960/(30 × 10) = 3.2 m. The child must push because energy is lost to air resistance, friction at the pivot, and sound, preventing perpetual motion without input.

Case Study 3: Home Electricity Usage

Scenario: A family uses the following appliances daily:

  • Light bulb: 100 W for 5 hours
  • Ceiling fan: 60 W for 8 hours
  • Water heater: 1500 W for 1 hour

Questions:

  1. Calculate the energy consumed by each appliance in Joules and kWh.
  2. Which appliance consumes the most energy?
  3. If electricity costs Rs. 5 per kWh, calculate the daily bill.

Solution Guide: Light: 500 Wh = 0.5 kWh, Fan: 480 Wh = 0.48 kWh, Heater: 1500 Wh = 1.5 kWh. Total = 2.48 kWh. Daily cost = 2.48 × 5 = Rs. 12.40. Heater uses most energy despite shorter running time due to high power.

Glossary: Key Terms

Work (W)
The product of force and displacement in the direction of the force. Measured in Joules. Can be positive, negative, or zero.
Energy
The capacity of a system to do work. Forms include kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical, electrical, nuclear, light, and sound energy.
Kinetic Energy (KE)
The energy possessed by an object due to its motion. Formula: KE = ½mv². Depends on mass and the square of velocity.
Potential Energy (PE)
Energy stored in an object due to its position or configuration. Gravitational PE = mgh. Relative to a chosen reference level.
Work-Energy Theorem
States that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy: W_net = ΔKE.
Law of Conservation of Energy
Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transformed from one form to another. Total energy in an isolated system remains constant.
Power (P)
The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. Formula: P = W/t. SI unit: Watt (W) = J/s.
Joule (J)
SI unit of energy and work. 1 J = 1 N·m (one newton-meter).
Watt (W)
SI unit of power. 1 W = 1 J/s (one joule per second).
Horsepower (hp)
A traditional unit of power. 1 hp = 746 W. Used for engines and motors.
Kilowatt-hour (kWh)
A commercial unit of energy. 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J. Used for billing electricity.
Reference Level
An arbitrary zero point chosen for measuring potential energy. PE is relative to this level.
Displacement
The change in position of an object in a specific direction. A vector quantity with both magnitude and direction.
Conservative Force
A force (like gravity) where work done is independent of the path; mechanical energy is conserved.
Elastic Potential Energy
Energy stored in stretched or compressed elastic materials like springs. Formula: PE = ½kx².

Need Extra Help?

Common Misconceptions & Clarifications

Misconception 1: "Work is always done when I exert a force."

Clarification: Work is only done if there is displacement in the direction of the force. Pushing a wall with all your might does zero work because there is no displacement. Carrying a heavy bag horizontally involves zero work by gravity (perpendicular to motion), even though you feel tired (muscular work is different from physics work).

Misconception 2: "An object at rest has no energy."

Clarification: A stationary object at height has potential energy. An object at rest at ground level has zero KE and zero PE (relative to that level), but it still possesses internal energy (thermal, chemical). Energy comes in many forms.

Misconception 3: "Higher power means more work done."

Clarification: Power is the rate of doing work, not the amount. A low-power device working for a long time can do as much work as a high-power device working briefly. Power tells us how quickly work is done, not how much.

Misconception 4: "Energy disappears when there's friction."

Clarification: Friction converts mechanical energy into heat. The energy doesn't vanish; it transforms. This is why objects get hot when there's friction. The total energy (mechanical + thermal) is conserved.

Misconception 5: "PE is always positive."

Clarification: PE is relative to the chosen reference level. An object below the reference level has negative PE. This is just a choice of zero point and doesn't affect energy differences.

Tips for Success

  • Always identify the reference level for PE calculations.
  • Check the angle between force and displacement for work: W = Fs cos θ
  • Remember KE ∝ v²: Small changes in velocity cause large changes in KE.
  • Use conservation of energy whenever possible—it's often easier than using equations of motion.
  • Units matter: Always convert to SI units (J, W, m/s) before calculating.
  • Draw diagrams to visualize forces, displacement, and energy transformations.
