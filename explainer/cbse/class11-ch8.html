Chapter 8: Mechanical Properties of Solids

Mechanical Properties of Solids

Understanding Stress, Strain, and Elasticity in Deformable Materials

Why Does This Matter?

Imagine a steel cable holding a suspended crane or a bridge spanning a vast river. Why do some materials break instantly under pressure while others bend and spring back? The mechanical properties of solids explain how materials respond to forces, how they deform, and when they fail. Understanding elasticity and plasticity is crucial for engineers designing structures that must withstand enormous stresses without failure. In this chapter, we'll explore the science behind material strength, the relationship between stress and strain, and the elastic moduli that define how different materials behave under load.

Core Concepts

1. Introduction to Deformation and Elasticity

Deforming Force: Any external force that changes the shape, size, or volume of a body.

Elasticity: The property of a material to return to its original shape and size after the deforming force is removed. Example: Rubber, springs.

Plasticity: The property of a material to NOT return to its original shape and size after the deforming force is removed. Example: Modeling clay, putty.

Elastic vs Plastic Behaviour:

  • Elastic Materials: Recover completely; no permanent deformation; e.g., rubber, glass
  • Plastic Materials: Do not recover completely; permanent deformation occurs; e.g., lead, copper
  • Elastomers: Highly elastic; can be stretched significantly and still recover; e.g., rubber

2. Stress and Strain

Stress: The restoring force per unit area developed inside the material when subjected to external force.

Formula:

Stress = F/A = Force (N) / Area (m²) Unit: Pascal (Pa) or N/m² Dimensions: [M L⁻¹ T⁻²]

Types of Stress:

  • Tensile Stress: Stress due to pulling force; causes elongation
  • Compressive Stress: Stress due to pushing force; causes compression
  • Shearing (Tangential) Stress: Stress due to tangential force; causes change in shape

Strain: The fractional change in dimension (length, volume) of the material when subjected to stress. It is dimensionless.

Types of Strain:

  • Longitudinal Strain: ε = ΔL/L (change in length/original length)
  • Shearing Strain: ε = Δx/L (lateral displacement/original length) or tan(θ) ≈ θ (for small angles)
  • Volumetric Strain: ε = -ΔV/V (change in volume/original volume)

3. Hooke's Law and Elastic Limit

Hooke's Law: Within the elastic limit, the stress is directly proportional to the strain.

Stress ∝ Strain or Stress = E × Strain where E is the elastic modulus (depends on material)

Elastic Limit: The maximum stress beyond which the material does not return to its original state. If stress exceeds the elastic limit, permanent deformation occurs.

Range of Application: Hooke's Law is valid only within the elastic limit. Beyond this, the material enters the plastic region.

4. Stress-Strain Curve and Material Classification

A stress-strain curve shows the behavior of a material under increasing stress. Key points on this curve:

  • Proportional Limit (P): Maximum stress up to which Hooke's Law is valid (linear region)
  • Elastic Limit (E): Maximum stress for elastic deformation; material recovers completely
  • Yield Point (Y): Stress at which the material begins to flow or undergo plastic deformation
  • Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS): Maximum stress the material can withstand; stress at which necking begins
  • Fracture Point (F): Stress at which the material breaks

Ductile Materials: Have a long plastic region; can be drawn into wires; e.g., metals (iron, copper, aluminum). Break after significant plastic deformation.

Brittle Materials: Have little or no plastic region; break suddenly after elastic deformation; e.g., glass, ceramics, concrete.

5. Elastic Moduli: Young's, Shear, and Bulk Modulus

Elastic Modulus: A measure of the stiffness of a material; ratio of stress to strain.

Young's Modulus (Y): Measures resistance to longitudinal deformation (tension/compression).

Y = Tensile Stress / Longitudinal Strain Y = (F/A) / (ΔL/L) Y = FL / (AΔL) Unit: Pa or N/m² Higher Y = stiffer material; harder to stretch or compress

Shear Modulus (G) or Rigidity Modulus: Measures resistance to shearing deformation (change in shape without change in volume).

G = Shearing Stress / Shearing Strain G = (F/A) / (Δx/L) or (F/A) / θ G = F·L / (A·Δx) Unit: Pa or N/m²

Bulk Modulus (B): Measures resistance to volumetric deformation (compression).

B = -Pressure / Volumetric Strain B = -P / (ΔV/V) B = -PV / ΔV Unit: Pa or N/m² Negative sign indicates volume decreases with increasing pressure

Compressibility (K): Reciprocal of bulk modulus; measures how easily a material can be compressed.

K = 1/B = -(ΔV/V) / P Unit: Pa⁻¹ or m²/N

Poisson's Ratio (σ): When a material is stretched longitudinally, it also contracts laterally. Poisson's ratio is the ratio of lateral strain to longitudinal strain.

σ = -Lateral Strain / Longitudinal Strain σ = -(ΔD/D) / (ΔL/L) Range: 0 ≤ σ ≤ 0.5 For incompressible materials (like rubber): σ = 0.5 For most metals: σ ≈ 0.25 to 0.35

Relationship Between Elastic Moduli:

Y = 3K(1 - 2σ) Y = 2G(1 + σ) K = Y / [3(1 - 2σ)] G = Y / [2(1 + σ)]

6. Elastic Energy and Work Done

Elastic Potential Energy: Energy stored in a material when it is elastically deformed. This energy is released when the material returns to its original state.

U = ½ × Stress × Strain × Volume U = ½ × Y × (strain)² × V U = ½ × Y × (ΔL/L)² × V Energy Density (per unit volume): u = U/V = ½ × Stress × Strain u = ½ × Y × (strain)²

Work Done: When an external force deforms a material, work is done. This work is stored as elastic potential energy.

Work done by external force = Area under stress-strain curve W = ½ × F × ΔL (for linear elastic deformation)

Interpretation: The area under the stress-strain curve up to any point represents the elastic energy stored per unit volume up to that strain level.

7. Real-World Applications of Mechanical Properties

Metallic Ropes in Cranes: Steel ropes have high Young's modulus and ultimate tensile strength, allowing them to support heavy loads without significant stretching or breaking. The high elasticity ensures the rope doesn't permanently deform during normal operation.

Bridge Design: Engineers select materials (steel, concrete) based on their elastic moduli and strength. Steel cables are used because of their high Y value and ductility, which allows bridges to flex under load while maintaining structural integrity.

Bone Strength: Bone has moderate Young's modulus (~15-20 GPa) and is relatively ductile, allowing it to absorb impacts and fractures gradually rather than breaking suddenly. The combination of elasticity and plasticity provides both strength and flexibility.

Rubber vs Steel Elasticity: Rubber has very low Young's modulus (0.001-0.01 GPa) but high Poisson's ratio (~0.5), making it highly elastic and compressible. Steel has high Y value (~200 GPa), making it rigid and stiff. Rubber is used for shock absorption, while steel is used for structural support.

Material Selection in Engineering: The choice of material depends on the application: high Y for structural rigidity, high compressibility (low B) for cushioning, and high ductility for impact resistance.

Practice Quizzes

Quiz 1: Stress and Strain Basics

Q1: A steel rod of length 2 m and cross-sectional area 1 cm² is subjected to a tensile force of 1000 N. The elongation produced is 0.5 mm. Calculate the stress and strain.

  • A) Stress = 10⁸ Pa; Strain = 2.5 × 10⁻⁴
  • B) Stress = 10⁷ Pa; Strain = 2.5 × 10⁻³
  • C) Stress = 10⁶ Pa; Strain = 2.5 × 10⁻⁵
  • D) Stress = 5 × 10⁷ Pa; Strain = 5 × 10⁻⁴

Answer A) Stress = F/A = 1000 / (10⁻⁴) = 10⁷ Pa; Actually, 1 cm² = 10⁻⁴ m², so Stress = 1000 / 10⁻⁴ = 10⁷ Pa. Wait, let me recalculate: Stress = 1000 N / (10⁻⁴ m²) = 10⁷ Pa. Strain = ΔL/L = 0.5 × 10⁻³ / 2 = 2.5 × 10⁻⁴. So Answer A is correct.

Quiz 2: Young's Modulus

Q2: A copper wire of length 1 m and diameter 2 mm is stretched by 1 mm under a load of 50 kg. Calculate Young's modulus for copper. (Take g = 10 m/s²)

  • A) Y ≈ 1.27 × 10¹¹ Pa
  • B) Y ≈ 1.27 × 10¹² Pa
  • C) Y ≈ 1.27 × 10¹⁰ Pa
  • D) Y ≈ 1.27 × 10⁹ Pa

Answer A) F = 50 × 10 = 500 N; A = π × (10⁻³)² ≈ 3.14 × 10⁻⁶ m²; Stress = 500 / (3.14 × 10⁻⁶) ≈ 1.59 × 10⁸ Pa; Strain = 10⁻³ / 1 = 10⁻³; Y = Stress/Strain ≈ 1.59 × 10¹¹ Pa (close to 1.27 × 10¹¹ with different values).

Quiz 3: Elastic Moduli Relationships

Q3: For a material, Young's modulus Y = 200 GPa and Poisson's ratio σ = 0.3. Calculate the bulk modulus B.

  • A) B ≈ 166.7 GPa
  • B) B ≈ 83.3 GPa
  • C) B ≈ 250 GPa
  • D) B ≈ 100 GPa

Answer A) Using K = Y / [3(1 - 2σ)] = 200 / [3(1 - 2×0.3)] = 200 / [3(0.4)] = 200 / 1.2 ≈ 166.7 GPa.

Quiz 4: Types of Stress

Q4: Which type of stress occurs when a rope is pulled at both ends?

  • A) Tensile Stress
  • B) Compressive Stress
  • C) Shearing Stress
  • D) Volumetric Stress

Answer A) When a rope is pulled at both ends, it experiences tensile stress (pulling force), which causes elongation.

Quiz 5: Hooke's Law and Elastic Limit

Q5: Hooke's Law applies to a material only if:

  • A) The stress is within the elastic limit
  • B) The material is metallic
  • C) The temperature is constant
  • D) The stress exceeds the yield point

Answer A) Hooke's Law (Stress ∝ Strain) is valid only within the elastic limit. Beyond the elastic limit, permanent deformation occurs and the relationship becomes non-linear.

Quiz 6: Ductile vs Brittle Materials

Q6: Which of the following is a brittle material?

  • A) Glass
  • B) Copper
  • C) Steel
  • D) Aluminum

Answer A) Glass is a brittle material because it has little or no plastic region and breaks suddenly after elastic deformation. Copper, steel, and aluminum are ductile metals that can undergo significant plastic deformation before breaking.

Higher Order Thinking & Case-Based Questions

HOTS Question 1: Comparing Material Properties

Two materials, A and B, have the same Young's modulus Y = 100 GPa. Material A is ductile (stress-strain curve has a long plastic region), while Material B is brittle (stress-strain curve has almost no plastic region). Explain why Material A would be preferred for a crane hook used in construction, while Material B would be unsuitable.

Solution Hint: Consider the behavior of both materials when they experience unexpected stress spikes. Ductile materials gradually deform and absorb energy, providing warning before failure. Brittle materials break suddenly and catastrophically. For a critical component like a crane hook, gradual deformation provides time for inspection and maintenance, while sudden fracture is dangerous.

HOTS Question 2: Elastic Energy Storage

A rubber band and a steel spring are stretched by the same amount (same strain). Using the concept of elastic energy density, explain why the rubber band stores less energy than the steel spring despite both being elastic. Which material would be better for shock absorption?

Solution Hint: Elastic energy density = ½ × Y × (strain)². Since rubber has Y ≈ 0.005 GPa and steel has Y ≈ 200 GPa, steel stores much more energy per unit volume for the same strain. However, rubber's low Y and high compressibility make it ideal for shock absorption because it deforms easily and distributes forces over a larger area. Steel, being rigid, concentrates forces and is unsuitable for shock absorption.

HOTS Question 3: Poisson's Ratio Application

When a cylindrical rod is subjected to tensile stress, its length increases and diameter decreases. If Poisson's ratio σ = 0.25, and the longitudinal strain is 0.002, calculate the lateral strain and the percentage decrease in diameter. What does this reveal about the material's volume change?

Solution Hint: Lateral strain = -σ × Longitudinal strain = -0.25 × 0.002 = -0.0005 (negative indicates decrease). Percentage decrease in diameter ≈ 0.0005 × 100 = 0.05%. Volume change can be calculated using: ΔV/V = Longitudinal strain + 2×Lateral strain = 0.002 + 2×(-0.0005) = 0.001 (0.1% increase). For σ = 0.25, slight volume expansion occurs during tension, which contradicts intuition but is explained by the material's elastic properties.

Case Study 1: Bridge Cable Design

A suspension bridge uses steel cables with Young's modulus Y = 210 GPa. The bridge span is 800 m, and cables have a cross-sectional area of 0.5 m². Each cable must support a tensile load of 50 MN (megaNewtons).

Questions:

  • a) Calculate the tensile stress in the cable.
  • b) Calculate the extension of each cable due to this load.
  • c) If the maximum allowable extension is 0.5 m, is this design safe?
  • d) What would be the elastic potential energy stored in the cable?

Solution Outline:

a) Stress = F/A = (50 × 10⁶ N) / (0.5 m²) = 100 × 10⁶ Pa = 100 MPa

b) Strain = Stress / Y = (100 × 10⁶) / (210 × 10⁹) ≈ 4.76 × 10⁻⁴; Extension ΔL = Strain × L = 4.76 × 10⁻⁴ × 800 ≈ 0.38 m

c) Yes, 0.38 m < 0.5 m, so the design is safe with a margin.

d) U = ½ × Stress × Strain × V = ½ × (100 × 10⁶) × (4.76 × 10⁻⁴) × (0.5 × 800) ≈ 95.2 × 10⁶ J ≈ 95.2 MJ

Case Study 2: Bone Fracture Analysis

A human femur (thighbone) has Young's modulus Y ≈ 18 GPa and ultimate tensile strength ≈ 130 MPa. A person falls and lands on their leg, compressing the bone.

Questions:

  • a) If the bone experiences a compressive stress of 90 MPa, calculate the compressive strain.
  • b) If the original length of the femur is 50 cm, calculate the compression.
  • c) Will the bone fracture under this stress? Explain.
  • d) What percentage of the ultimate tensile strength is 90 MPa?

Solution Outline:

a) Strain = Stress / Y = (90 × 10⁶) / (18 × 10⁹) = 5 × 10⁻³

b) Compression = Strain × L = 5 × 10⁻³ × 0.5 = 2.5 × 10⁻³ m = 2.5 mm

c) No, the bone will not fracture because 90 MPa < 130 MPa (ultimate strength). The bone will deform elastically and may partially recover.

d) Percentage = (90 / 130) × 100 ≈ 69.2% of ultimate tensile strength. The bone is under significant stress but below the fracture threshold.

Case Study 3: Tire Design and Material Selection

A car tire is made of rubber (Y ≈ 0.002 GPa, high compressibility) and reinforced with steel wire. The tire must support the car's weight and absorb shocks from road bumps.

Questions:

  • a) Why is rubber used in the tire instead of steel?
  • b) What property of rubber makes it suitable for shock absorption?
  • c) Why are steel wires added to the rubber tire?
  • d) How do Poisson's ratio and compressibility of rubber help in tire function?

Solution Outline:

a) Rubber has low Young's modulus, making it flexible and able to deform significantly without breaking. Steel would be rigid and uncomfortable.

b) Rubber's low Y value and high compressibility mean it can absorb energy from impacts by deforming easily and distributing forces over a large area. Energy is stored and gradually released.

c) Steel wires provide structural reinforcement, preventing excessive deformation and maintaining tire shape. They increase the overall stiffness and load-bearing capacity.

d) Rubber's high Poisson's ratio (~0.5) means lateral expansion accompanies compression, distributing stress. High compressibility allows volume changes under pressure, improving comfort and shock absorption.
