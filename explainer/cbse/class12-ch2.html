Electrostatic Potential and Capacitance

Chapter 2: Understanding Electric Potential Energy and Charge Storage in Depth

Why Does This Matter?

Imagine holding a charged balloon near your hair—it rises! Behind this magic is the concept of electric potential, the invisible field that stores energy. When you charge your phone, capacitors inside silently store energy in electric fields. From lightning rods protecting buildings to the touchscreen responding to your finger, electrostatic potential and capacitors are everywhere. This chapter reveals how charges create potential differences, how energy gets stored, and why some materials (dielectrics) make capacitors work better—unlocking the physics of modern electronics. Understanding electrostatic potential is crucial for designing power systems, protecting against electrical hazards, and developing next-generation energy storage solutions. The concepts in this chapter form the foundation of everything from semiconductor devices to high-voltage transmission systems.

Core Concepts in Detail

1. Electric Potential Fundamentals

Electric potential (V) is the work done per unit charge to bring a test charge from infinity to a point. For a point charge: V = kq/r. For a system of charges or continuous distribution: V = -∫E·dr. The potential due to a dipole depends on angle and distance, exhibiting angular dependence. Electric potential is a scalar quantity, making superposition straightforward. Potential is always defined with respect to a reference point, typically infinity or ground. The SI unit is the Volt (V), where 1 V = 1 J/C.

2. Equipotential Surfaces & Visualization

Equipotential surfaces are imaginary surfaces where all points have the same electric potential. The electric field is always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential surface. For point charges, equipotential surfaces are spheres; for uniform fields, they are planes perpendicular to the field direction. Equipotential surfaces help visualize potential distribution and are crucial for understanding field topology and charge motion.

3. Potential Energy of Charge Systems

The electric potential energy of two point charges is U = kq₁q₂/r. For a dipole in an external field: U = -p·E = -pE cos(θ), where p is the dipole moment. Minimum energy occurs when the dipole aligns with the field (θ = 0). Total potential energy of a system is the sum of pair potentials. Potential energy can be positive (repulsive, unstable) or negative (attractive, stable).

4. Electric Field from Potential

Electric field is the negative gradient of potential: E = -dV/dr (in 1D). In 3D: E = -∇V = -(∂V/∂x, ∂V/∂y, ∂V/∂z). The electric field points from high to low potential. Field magnitude is proportional to the potential gradient. This relation is fundamental—it connects force (field) to energy (potential). The negative sign indicates field direction opposes increasing potential.

5. Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

Inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium: electric field E = 0, potential is constant. All excess charge resides on the conductor's surface. The surface is an equipotential, and the field just outside is perpendicular to the surface. Conductors shield their interior from external electric fields (electrostatic screening). This principle is crucial for protecting sensitive electronics and understanding electromagnetic shielding.

6. Capacitors & Capacitance Definition

Capacitance C = Q/V is the ratio of charge stored to potential difference. For a parallel-plate capacitor: C = ε₀A/d (where A is plate area, d is separation, ε₀ is permittivity of free space). In series: 1/C_total = Σ(1/Cᵢ). In parallel: C_total = ΣCᵢ. Capacitors are charge storage devices fundamental to electronics. Capacitance depends only on geometry and material, not on voltage or charge.

7. Dielectrics & Polarization Mechanisms

Dielectric materials increase capacitance by a factor K (relative permittivity): C = Kε₀A/d. Dielectrics contain bound charges that polarize in response to the electric field. Polarization reduces the net field inside the dielectric. The dielectric constant K varies with material (e.g., K = 1 for vacuum, K ≈ 80 for water, K ≈ 6 for mica). Higher K means stronger polarization response.

8. Energy Storage in Capacitors

Energy stored: U = ½CV² = Q²/2C = ½QV (all equivalent forms). Energy density in the field: u = ½ε₀E² (vacuum) or u = ½Kε₀E² (with dielectric). Total energy is the integral of energy density over volume. This energy comes from work done to charge the capacitor and is released when discharged—crucial for energy storage applications and power delivery systems.

9. Van de Graaff Generator & High-Voltage Applications

A Van de Graaff generator uses a moving belt to transport charge to a metal dome, building up high voltage. The large radius of the dome minimizes the electric field at its surface (since E = kQ/r²), allowing enormous charges to accumulate without breakdown. Demonstrates principles of charge distribution and electrostatic potential on conductors. Important for demonstrating electrostatic principles and generating high voltages safely.

Deep Dive: Electric Potential from First Principles

The concept of electric potential emerges naturally from the work-energy theorem. When we move a test charge q₀ in an electric field E, the field does work on the charge. The work done by the electric field from point A to point B is:

$$W = \int_A^B \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{l} = q_0 \int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l}$$

Since the electric force is conservative, this work is independent of path. We define the potential difference as the work per unit charge:

$$V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l}$$

Taking the reference potential at infinity as zero, we get V(r) = kq/r for a point charge. The negative sign reflects that the electric field points from high to low potential. This relationship is fundamental— it connects the field (which describes forces) to potential (which describes energy). The integral form shows that potential is path-independent (conservative field property), which simplifies many calculations.

Example: Potential of a Dipole

For a dipole with charges +q at origin and -q at distance d, the potential at a point far away is: $$V(r, \theta) = \frac{k \vec{p} \cdot \hat{r}}{r^2} = \frac{kp \cos\theta}{r^2}$$ where p = qd is the dipole moment and θ is angle from dipole axis. This shows the potential depends on angle— maximum on the axis where the dipole moment points, zero perpendicular to it. This angular dependence is crucial in molecular physics and chemistry, explaining intermolecular forces and molecular structure.

Deep Dive: Conductors and Electrostatic Equilibrium

A conductor in electrostatic equilibrium exhibits remarkable properties that make it invaluable in practical applications. The key principle is that free charges (electrons in metals) redistribute themselves to eliminate any internal electric field. This happens almost instantaneously (nanoseconds) after charge is applied, leading to complete shielding of the interior.

Key Properties of Conductors in Equilibrium:
  • Electric field inside: E = 0 (absolutely)
  • Potential inside: V = constant (uniform)
  • Surface is an equipotential (all points same V)
  • All excess charge on surface (none inside)
  • Field just outside: perpendicular to surface
  • Charge density on curved surfaces: higher at sharper points

The field just outside a conductor's surface is given by E = σ/ε₀, where σ is the surface charge density. This relationship comes from Gauss's law and is independent of the specific shape of the conductor. For a conductor held at potential V, the potential is constant throughout the conductor. The higher charge density at sharp points (like on a lightning rod tip) means field concentration, which can trigger breakdown if field exceeds dielectric strength.

Practical Application: Lightning Rods

Lightning rods work by providing a high-conductivity path to ground and by concentrating field at their sharp tip. The sharp geometry increases field concentration, potentially triggering corona discharge (ionization at the tip) that creates a conductive channel to the cloud before a full lightning strike occurs. The low resistance path (copper rod: ρ ≈ 10⁻⁸ Ω·m) ensures safe charge flow to ground rather than through the building.

Deep Dive: Understanding Dielectrics—Polarization and Breakdown

When a dielectric material is placed in an electric field, its molecules respond by polarizing. This polarization can occur in two ways: induced polarization (electron clouds shift relative to nuclei) and permanent polarization (molecules with permanent dipole moments align with the field). The distinction is crucial for understanding material behavior.

Microscopic Polarization Mechanism

Consider a simple model: a positive charge +q and negative charge -q (bound to atom) separated by distance d. In an external field E₀, the positive charge experiences force qE₀ in the field direction, and the negative experiences force -qE₀ opposite. This creates a separation, increasing d, which generates an internal field E_ind opposing E₀. At equilibrium, the net field inside is E = E₀ - E_ind, reduced by a factor K. This reduction is why dielectrics increase capacitance.

The relative permittivity (dielectric constant) K is defined as the ratio of capacitance with dielectric to capacitance without:

$$K = \frac{C_{dielectric}}{C_{vacuum}} = \frac{E_0}{E_{net}}$$

Different materials have different K values based on their molecular structure: water (K ≈ 80) due to strong hydrogen bonding and molecular dipoles; vacuum (K = 1) by definition; mica (K ≈ 6); ceramic (K ≈ 1000+). The dielectric strength (breakdown field) is another critical property—it determines the maximum field a material can withstand before catastrophic breakdown and ionization occur. When field exceeds this limit, the dielectric loses its insulating properties and conducts, often causing permanent damage.

Critical Point: Dielectric strength is absolute. Exceeding it causes breakdown—the material suddenly becomes conductive and may be destroyed. Design margins are essential in all capacitor applications.

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Problems

HOTS 1: Analyzing Complex Potential Distribution

Question: Three point charges (+q, -2q, +q) are placed at vertices of an equilateral triangle with side length a. (a) Calculate the total electric potential at the center of the triangle. (b) Is this configuration stable or unstable for a test charge placed at the center? (c) Use energy arguments to justify your answer. (d) What happens if we slightly displace the test charge?

Solution Approach: By symmetry, the two +q charges are equidistant from center at distance a/√3. Calculate V_total = kq/(a/√3) + k(-2q)/(a/√3) + kq/(a/√3) = (kq√3/a)(1 - 2 + 1) = 0. Even though V = 0, analyze stability using potential energy gradient: U = qV_external. For stability, consider second derivative of potential (potential curvature). A test charge in potential minimum is stable; at a saddle point is unstable. This is a saddle point—unstable! Small displacement causes the charge to accelerate away.

HOTS 2: Dielectric Breakdown and Practical Limits

Question: A parallel-plate capacitor with dielectric (K = 5) has dielectric breakdown field strength E_b = 3 × 10⁶ V/m. Plate separation d = 2 mm. (a) Calculate maximum voltage it can withstand. (b) Compare with an identical air-gap capacitor (K = 1, same E_b). (c) If both are connected to 4 kV power supply, which breaks down first and why? (d) What is the charge stored at maximum voltage in each case if plate area A = 100 cm²?

Solution Approach: (a) V_max = E_b × d = 3 × 10⁶ × 0.002 = 6 kV (b) Both have same V_max = 6 kV (breakdown field same for air and dielectric material in this problem) (c) Neither breaks down at 4 kV—both safe at this voltage (d) With dielectric: C = Kε₀A/d = 5 × 8.85×10⁻¹² × 0.01 / 0.002 = 2.2 × 10⁻⁹ F = 2.2 nF Q = CV = 2.2 × 10⁻⁹ × 4000 = 8.8 × 10⁻⁶ C = 8.8 μC Without dielectric: C = ε₀A/d = 4.4 × 10⁻¹⁰ F = 0.44 nF Q = 0.44 × 10⁻⁹ × 4000 = 1.76 × 10⁻⁶ C = 1.76 μC The dielectric capacitor stores 5× more charge at the same voltage!

HOTS 3: Energy Dissipation in Capacitor Charging

Question: A capacitor (C = 10 μF) is charged by connecting across a battery (V = 100 V) through a resistor (R = 100 Ω). (a) Calculate total energy supplied by battery, energy stored in capacitor, and energy dissipated in resistor. (b) Prove that ½CV² is always dissipated regardless of R. (c) Calculate the time constant τ = RC and energy dissipation rate at t = 0 and at t = τ. (d) Why is this energy "wasted"? Can it be minimized?

Solution Approach: (a) Energy from battery = ∫V·I dt = ∫V dQ = VQ_final = V(CV) = CV² = 100 × (10×10⁻⁶ × 100) = 0.1 J Energy stored in capacitor = ½CV² = 0.05 J Energy dissipated = 0.1 - 0.05 = 0.05 J (b) In RC circuit, I(t) = (V/R)e^(-t/RC). Power dissipated: P = I²R = (V²/R)e^(-2t/RC) Total dissipated = ∫₀^∞ I²R dt = ∫₀^∞ (V²/R)e^(-2t/RC) dt = (V²/R) × RC/2 = ½CV² This is independent of R! Fast or slow charging dissipates same energy. (c) τ = RC = 100 × 10 × 10⁻⁶ = 10⁻³ s = 1 ms At t = 0: P = I₀²R = (V/R)² × R = V²/R = 10000/100 = 100 W At t = τ: I = I₀e⁻¹ = (V/R)e⁻¹, so P = (V²/R)e⁻² ≈ 100 × 0.135 ≈ 13.5 W (d) This energy is "wasted" as heat in resistance. It cannot be minimized below ½CV² regardless of charging method. However, faster charging (smaller RC) spreads dissipation over shorter time, reducing peak temperature rise in circuits.

Case-Based Questions: Real-World Applications

Case 1: Touchscreen Technology (Capacitive Sensing)

Scenario: Modern smartphone touchscreens use capacitive sensing. When your finger touches the screen, it changes the local capacitance, which the device detects. The screen acts as a parallel-plate capacitor with a conductive layer (indium tin oxide) on glass. When touched, the human body acts as a conductor, altering the electric field distribution. The device measures capacitance changes of picofarads (pF) to detect touch location and pressure.

Questions:
(a) Why is the human body effective in changing the capacitance? Explain in terms of induced charges and field distribution.
(b) If the baseline capacitance is 100 pF and increases by 5% upon touch, with operating voltage 5 V, how much additional charge flows? What does this tell us about detection sensitivity?
(c) Explain why a capacitive screen doesn't work with regular gloves but works with "smart gloves." What property of the material is crucial? Compare conductivities.
(d) Design a system to distinguish between a finger touch and a stylus touch using capacitance measurements. Hint: Consider spatial distribution of capacitance changes.

Model Answers:
(a) The human body has conductivity σ ≈ 1 S/m (seawater-like), allowing free charges to redistribute. This effectively increases the effective capacitor plate size or reduces the effective separation distance. The body acts as a conductor with σ ≈ 10⁶ ·S/m compared to air σ ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ S/m.
(b) ΔQ = ΔC × V = (0.05 × 100 pF) × 5 V = 5 pF × 5 V = 25 × 10⁻¹² C = 25 pC. Such tiny charge flows indicate sensors must be extremely sensitive. The device measures frequency changes in an oscillator circuit— as capacitance changes, oscillation frequency shifts. This is more practical than measuring absolute charge flow.
(c) Regular gloves are insulators: σ ≈ 10⁻¹⁴ S/m for leather, σ ≈ 10⁻¹⁸ S/m for cotton. This prevents charge redistribution and capacitance change. Smart gloves have conductive threads: σ ≈ 10⁷ S/m, similar to metallic conductivity. The threads form a conductive network allowing touch detection while retaining glove functionality.
(d) A finger creates a broad, distributed capacitance increase (large contact area, gradual gradient from contact to non-contact region). A stylus tip (small conductive area) creates a sharp, localized capacitance spike. By analyzing the spatial distribution of capacitance change—using multiple sensors and interpolating—the device can distinguish. This enables pressure-sensitive drawing on styluses: harder pressure means larger contact area, larger capacitance increase.

Case 2: Atmospheric Electricity and Lightning Protection

Scenario: During a thunderstorm, the cloud-ground system acts as a giant capacitor. Charge separation in clouds creates potential differences exceeding 100 million volts. When the electric field exceeds air's breakdown strength (~3 × 10⁶ V/m), ionization occurs, creating an ionized conducting path—lightning. Modern buildings use lightning protection systems involving conductors, grounding, and surge protection. Understanding the physics helps design safe systems.

Questions:
(a) If cloud-ground separation is 500 m and breakdown field is 3 × 10⁶ V/m, calculate the minimum voltage required for lightning initiation. What does this reveal about thunderstorm intensity?
(b) Explain why tall buildings (like skyscrapers) attract lightning in terms of electric field concentration and potential gradients. Use Coulomb's law and field from geometry.
(c) How does a lightning rod (grounded conductor) prevent damage? Use concepts of electric field, conductivity, equipotential surfaces, and current flow to justify why it works.
(d) Calculate the energy released in a typical lightning bolt: charge Q = 15 C transferred at average potential V = 400 MV. Compare to TNT explosive (4.184 MJ per ton). How much damage is this?
(e) Modern surge protectors use avalanche diodes. Explain why they are more effective than simple fuses for rapid protection.

Model Answers:
(a) V = E × d = 3 × 10⁶ V/m × 500 m = 1.5 × 10⁹ V = 1.5 GV. Such enormous voltages indicate intense charge separation— updrafts in thunderstorms can separate charges millions of times per second. This explains why some storms are particularly dangerous.
(b) Field concentrates at sharp points due to higher curvature: E = kq/r² becomes very large as r decreases (r is distance from point). A tall building extends this pointed region into the cloud, creating local field concentration. The stepped leader process starts where field first exceeds breakdown at ground level, which is at the building's sharp corner/antenna.
(c) The rod provides a low-resistance path: R_copper ≈ 10⁻⁸ Ω·m vs R_air ≈ 10¹⁶ Ω·m (many orders of magnitude difference). Current flows preferentially through the low-R path. Also, the rod extends conductive material upward, reducing effective distance to cloud and reducing local field strength, potentially preventing lightning initiation altogether. The rod connects building interior (through grounding conductor) to deep earth ground, dissipating charge safely.
(d) U = QV = 15 C × 400 × 10⁶ V = 6 × 10⁹ J = 6 GJ. Energy per ton TNT = 4.184 MJ = 4.184 × 10⁶ J. Equivalent TNT mass = 6 × 10⁹ / 4.184 × 10⁶ ≈ 1,400 tons of TNT. This enormous energy releases in microseconds, creating extreme temperatures and pressures. The explosive expansion of superheated air (thunder) and melting/vaporization of materials explain lightning damage.
(e) Avalanche diodes (transient voltage suppressors) respond in nanoseconds by entering reverse breakdown mode, shunting excessive voltage. This is much faster than fuse melting (microseconds) and limits overvoltage instantly, protecting sensitive components from damage.

Case 3: Medical Defibrillators (Cardiac Devices)

Scenario: Cardiac defibrillators deliver high-voltage electrical shocks (typically 4,000–8,000 V) using large capacitors to correct abnormal heart rhythms (fibrillation). The capacitor stores energy quickly and releases it in a controlled pulse through the patient's chest via electrodes. Modern defibrillators use biphasic waveforms—first positive pulse, then negative pulse—for improved effectiveness and reduced tissue damage. Understanding capacitor physics is essential for safe operation and device design.

Questions:
(a) If a defibrillator capacitor is 100 μF and charged to 6,000 V, calculate total energy delivered to patient. How does this compare to the energy released in TNT (as reference)?
(b) Why is capacitor technology preferred over direct current from a battery? Consider charge delivery speed and energy concentration.
(c) Explain the role of circuit resistance (including electrode contact resistance) in controlling the discharge pulse shape and duration. How does this affect patient safety?
(d) Calculate peak current if chest impedance is 50 Ω. How does current decay over a 10 ms pulse with RC time constant?
(e) Why do modern defibrillators use biphasic waveforms instead of monophasic? What advantage does reversing polarity provide?
(f) What happens to tissues during defibrillation? Explain electrical effects on cardiac cells.

Model Answers:
(a) U = ½CV² = ½ × 100 × 10⁻⁶ F × (6000 V)² = ½ × 10⁻⁴ × 36 × 10⁶ = 1.8 × 10³ = 1,800 J = 1.8 kJ. Energy per ton TNT = 4.184 MJ. This 1.8 kJ is about 0.0004 tons TNT equivalent, small by explosive standards but enormous for biological tissues.
(b) Capacitors discharge in microseconds to milliseconds, delivering peak currents in hundreds of amperes. Batteries cannot deliver such rapid high current—internal resistance limits current to milliamperes. The rapid energy release is essential: it must depolarize all cardiac cells simultaneously to break fibrillation. Slower current might not synchronize depolarization.
(c) Circuit resistance R (including contact resistance ≈ 20-100 Ω) acts in RC circuit: I(t) = (V₀/R)e^(-t/RC), V(t) = V₀e^(-t/RC). Higher R → longer time constant τ = RC → slower current decay. Longer pulse duration → more complete tissue depolarization but also more heat generation and tissue damage. Design balances these effects. Contact resistance must be minimized (electrode gel improves contact) to ensure proper current delivery.
(d) Peak current I₀ = V/R = 6000/50 = 120 A. Estimated circuit R ≈ 100 Ω total (electrode resistance ~50 Ω + internal circuit). τ = RC = 100 × 100 × 10⁻⁶ = 10 ms. After 10 ms (one time constant), I = I₀e⁻¹ ≈ 120 × 0.368 ≈ 44 A. After 20 ms (two time constants), I ≈ 120 × 0.135 ≈ 16 A. The exponential decay means most current flows in first 10-20 ms.
(e) Biphasic waveforms first deliver positive current, depolarizing cardiac cells. Then negative current follows, helping repolarization and reducing cumulative thermal damage. Monophasic delivers all positive current, increasing total energy dissipation and tissue heating. Biphasic is about 50% more energy-efficient—same defibrillation success at lower voltage/current, reducing skin burn.
(f) During defibrillation, the electrical pulse creates electric fields exceeding cell membrane breakdown threshold. This causes electroporation—transient opening of ion channels. All cells depolarize simultaneously, interrupting chaotic fibrillation signals. The cardiac pacemaker then regains control. Excessive current causes muscle tetany (sustained contraction), thermal burns, and arrhythmias, which is why current limiting is crucial.

Critical Insights & Problem-Solving Strategies

  • Potential is path-independent: Work done depends only on initial/final positions (conservative field). Use this to simplify calculations—choose the easiest path for integration. This saves tremendous effort!
  • Superposition principle applies: V_total = V₁ + V₂ + ... Potential is scalar (unlike field vectors), making superposition simpler and faster than adding E fields.
  • Field points from high to low potential: E = -dV/dr (negative sign is crucial). Field opposes increasing potential direction. This sign error is common—write it out every time.
  • Equipotential surfaces are key visualizations: Field perpendicular to equipotentials, no work along equipotential. Use equipotentials to understand field topology, charge distribution, and conductor shapes.
  • Conductor properties in equilibrium: E_inside = 0, V = constant, all charge on surface. Use these to eliminate unknowns in complex geometries and boundary conditions.
  • Capacitance depends ONLY on geometry and material: C is independent of V or Q. For given geometry, C is fixed—use this to find Q from V or vice versa without complex calculations.
  • Series capacitors: 1/C = Σ(1/Cᵢ): Series reduces capacitance below smallest value. Analogous to springs in series increasing stiffness. Remember: reciprocal formula!
  • Parallel capacitors: C = ΣCᵢ: Parallel adds capacitances directly. Opposite of resistor behavior (where series adds). This confusion is common—remember the difference!
  • Energy stored is real electromagnetic energy: U = ½CV² is energy stored in the field, not theoretical. It's released in discharge, powers defibrillators, and drives applications.
  • Dielectrics increase capacitance by factor K: They allow higher voltage for same field. K is material property, independent of geometry. Higher K means more effective polarization.
  • Energy dissipation in RC charging: Battery supplies CV², capacitor stores ½CV², difference ½CV² dissipated in resistance. This is independent of R—true regardless of charging speed! Thermal constraint, not electrical efficiency.
  • Watch sign conventions carefully: V_AB = V_A - V_B. Potential difference direction matters. Consistently define reference (usually ground). Sloppy with signs = wrong answer.
  • Breakdown is absolute limit: Dielectric strength E_max cannot be exceeded without catastrophic failure. Design safety margins are essential—never design capacitors at their limit!

Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

  • MISTAKE: Using E = +dV/dr (wrong sign) → Correct: E = -dV/dr. Field opposes increasing potential. Write the negative!
  • MISTAKE: Confusing potential V and potential energy U → V is per unit charge; U = qV is total. Know which you need before starting.
  • MISTAKE: Mixing up series/parallel capacitor formulas → Series: 1/C_eq = Σ(1/Cᵢ); Parallel: C_eq = ΣCᵢ. Opposite of resistors! Make a note.
  • MISTAKE: Assuming uniform field inside conductor → E = 0 exactly, not "approximately uniform". No field inside conductors.
  • MISTAKE: Forgetting dielectric constant in energy → u = ½Kε₀E² with dielectric, not ½ε₀E². The K factor is crucial for correct answers.
  • MISTAKE: Integrating from 0 to r instead of r to ∞ → Potential reference is infinity (V_∞ = 0). Integrate from r to ∞ for potential!
  • MISTAKE: Applying Q = CV before understanding geometry → Capacitance depends on geometry first! Always find C from geometry, then use Q = CV.
  • MISTAKE: Neglecting internal resistance in RC circuits → Discharge rate depends on RC time constant. Cannot ignore R's effect on pulse shape.
  • MISTAKE: Assuming capacitor voltage = battery voltage immediately → Capacitor charges exponentially: V(t) = V_battery(1 - e^(-t/RC)). Takes time!
  • MISTAKE: Confusing energy dissipated with circuit resistance value → ½CV² energy dissipates regardless of R (fast or slow). Energy is not R-dependent!
