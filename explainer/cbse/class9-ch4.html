Structure of the Atom

"For centuries, people thought atoms were the smallest things possible — tiny, solid, unbreakable balls. Then scientists started smashing them apart, and what they found inside changed everything we know about matter."

Charged Particles in Matter

Matter is made up of atoms, but atoms themselves are not the simplest things. Inside every atom lie smaller, charged particles. When you rub a glass rod with silk, it gets charged — why? Because electrons, which are negatively charged particles, transfer from one object to another. This shows us that atoms contain charged particles, and understanding these particles is the key to understanding the structure of atoms.

Just as a country contains cities, which contain neighborhoods, which contain houses — atoms contain subatomic particles, organized in specific ways.
When you walk across a carpet and touch a doorknob, the spark you see is millions of electrons jumping from you to the metal. Your body was charged!

Thomson's Model of the Atom (1897)

In 1897, J.J. Thomson discovered the electron through brilliant cathode ray experiments. He passed electricity through nearly empty glass tubes and observed a mysterious glow. By studying how these rays behaved in electric and magnetic fields, he concluded they were made of negatively charged particles — electrons.

Cathode Ray Tube Experiment

Thomson used a vacuum tube with two metal electrodes. When high voltage was applied, a stream of particles traveled from the negative electrode (cathode) to the positive electrode (anode), creating a fluorescent glow. By deflecting these rays with magnets and electric fields, Thomson discovered they were identical particles with a fixed charge-to-mass ratio — the electrons.

Based on his discovery, Thomson proposed the "plum pudding" or "watermelon" model of the atom.

Imagine a watermelon (the positive charge) with seeds (electrons) embedded throughout. The entire atom is this sphere of positive charge, with electrons randomly scattered inside it, held in place by electrical attraction.
Learn More: Why "Plum Pudding"?

In Victorian England, plum pudding was a dessert with raisins (the "plums") embedded in a cake. Thomson's model resembled this — electrons (raisins) embedded in a sphere of positive charge (pudding). The name stuck because it helped people visualize the model.

J.J. Thomson won the Nobel Prize in 1906 for discovering the electron. Later, his son George Thomson won the Nobel Prize for proving electrons are waves!

Limitations of Thomson's Model

  • Couldn't explain why electrons don't collapse into the positive charge
  • Couldn't account for the nucleus and its concentration of positive charge
  • Later experiments disproved this model completely

Rutherford's Model of the Atom (1911)

In 1911, Ernest Rutherford performed one of the most important experiments in science. He took Thomson's model and tested it with a brilliant experiment using alpha particles.

The Gold Foil Experiment

Setup: Rutherford's team, led by Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden, bombarded an extremely thin sheet of gold foil (just 1000 atoms thick!) with alpha particles from a radioactive source. Around the foil, they placed a fluorescent screen to detect where the alpha particles went.

Observations:

  • Most alpha particles (99%) passed straight through the gold foil without deflection
  • Some particles deflected at small angles — bending slightly from their path
  • Very few particles (1 in 20,000) bounced straight back! Rutherford famously said this was "as if you fired a bullet at tissue paper and it came back and hit you"

Why was this shocking? If atoms were like Thomson's plum pudding (uniform positive charge throughout), alpha particles should have been deflected more evenly. The fact that most passed straight through and very few bounced back suggested something completely different.

Rutherford's Conclusions

1Most of the atom is empty space. If the atom were solid, most particles would be deflected.
2All the positive charge is concentrated in a tiny, dense center — called the nucleus.
3Electrons revolve around the nucleus in orbits, like planets around the sun.
Imagine a cricket stadium: the nucleus is a marble sitting at the center of the field, and electrons are tiny insects flying far away on the bleachers. The vast majority of the stadium is empty space — just like the atom!
The nucleus is incredibly dense. One teaspoon of nuclear material would weigh about 5 million tons on Earth! It's denser than any material we can create.

Rutherford's Model — Strengths and Limitations

Strengths

Explained the results of the gold foil experiment perfectly and showed that atoms had internal structure with a nucleus at the center.

Limitations

The Stability Problem: According to classical physics (electrodynamics), an orbiting electron should constantly lose energy as radiation and spiral into the nucleus in a fraction of a second. But atoms are stable — they don't collapse! Rutherford's model couldn't explain why atoms are stable.

Bohr's Model of the Atom (1913)

In 1913, Danish physicist Niels Bohr solved the puzzle of atomic stability. He proposed that electrons don't orbit the nucleus in any orbit — instead, they can only occupy discrete energy levels or shells.

Key Principles of Bohr's Model

Principle 1: Discrete Orbits

Electrons can only orbit at specific distances from the nucleus. These are like rungs on a ladder — an electron can be on rung 1, 2, 3, or 4, but NOT in between.

Principle 2: Fixed Energy

Each orbit has a fixed energy. As long as an electron stays in one orbit, it does NOT radiate energy. It's stable!

Principle 3: Energy Transitions

Energy is absorbed or released only when an electron jumps from one orbit to another. When an electron jumps to a lower orbit, it releases energy as light. When it absorbs energy, it jumps to a higher orbit.

Think of electrons like students in a school building. There are specific classrooms (K, L, M, N shells), and students only stay in classrooms, never in the hallways. When a student moves from one class to another, energy is involved. While in a classroom, they're perfectly stable and content.

Electron Shells and Capacities

Bohr named the shells after letters: K, L, M, N (or numbers: 1, 2, 3, 4). The maximum number of electrons in each shell is given by the formula:

Maximum electrons in nth shell = $$2n^2$$

Where n is the shell number (1, 2, 3, 4...)

K Shell (n=1): $$2(1)^2 = 2$$ electrons maximum
L Shell (n=2): $$2(2)^2 = 8$$ electrons maximum
M Shell (n=3): $$2(3)^2 = 18$$ electrons maximum
N Shell (n=4): $$2(4)^2 = 32$$ electrons maximum
Niels Bohr's model was a huge leap forward, but it was later refined by quantum mechanics. Modern atomic models are much more complex — electrons don't really "orbit" but exist in probability clouds called orbitals. Still, Bohr's model is taught because it's intuitive and explains a lot!

Neutrons and the Nucleus

Thomson, Rutherford, and Bohr had all described the electron and shown that atoms had a nucleus with positive charge. But what was this positive charge? And what else was in the nucleus?

In 1919, Rutherford discovered the proton — a positively charged particle roughly equal in mass to an electron but with opposite charge. But there was a mystery: if an atom's mass came only from protons and electrons, the math didn't work out. Hydrogen had one proton but was twice as heavy as it should be!

For 13 years, scientists couldn't figure out where the missing mass was. In 1932, James Chadwick finally discovered the neutron — a particle with no charge and mass almost equal to a proton. He won the Nobel Prize for this discovery!

The Nucleus: Protons and Neutrons

The nucleus contains two types of particles:

Particle Charge Mass (relative) Location
Proton +1 (positive) 1 unit Nucleus
Neutron 0 (neutral) 1 unit Nucleus
Electron -1 (negative) 1/1836 unit Shells around nucleus
1Protons and neutrons together are called nucleons.
2The nucleus is incredibly small — if an atom were a football stadium, the nucleus would be a marble at the center!
3Protons provide the positive charge that holds the electrons in the atom.
Neutrons are slightly heavier than protons, but we usually consider them equal in mass for chemistry. Neutrons are crucial — they provide nuclear stability. Too few neutrons and the nucleus falls apart (radioactive decay); too many and it's also unstable.

How Are Electrons Distributed? Electronic Configuration

Now that we know atoms have electrons in shells, the big question is: how are electrons distributed among these shells? The answer is simple: electrons fill shells from the inside out, starting with the innermost shell (K), then moving to L, M, N, and so on.

Rules for Electronic Configuration

1Electrons fill the lowest energy shell first (K shell), then L, then M, then N.
2A shell must be completely filled before the next shell starts filling (except for the outermost shell, which can have up to 8 electrons).
3The outermost shell can have a maximum of 8 electrons (with some exceptions for the first shell, which can have max 2).

Electronic Configuration Examples

Hydrogen (H, atomic number 1):
K(1) — One electron in the K shell
Carbon (C, atomic number 6):
K(2), L(4) — K shell full with 2, L shell has 4 electrons
Nitrogen (N, atomic number 7):
K(2), L(5) — K shell full with 2, L shell has 5 electrons
Oxygen (O, atomic number 8):
K(2), L(6) — K shell full with 2, L shell has 6 electrons
Sodium (Na, atomic number 11):
K(2), L(8), M(1) — K and L full, M has 1 electron
Chlorine (Cl, atomic number 17):
K(2), L(8), M(7) — K and L full, M has 7 electrons
Calcium (Ca, atomic number 20):
K(2), L(8), M(8), N(2) — K and L full, M full, N has 2 electrons
Think of electrons like parking spaces. The K lot is small (2 spaces), L lot is bigger (8 spaces), M lot is even bigger (18 spaces). You fill the K lot completely before anyone parks in L lot. Only when all inner lots are full does someone park in the next lot.

Valence Electrons and Chemical Properties

The valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost shell. These electrons determine how an atom reacts chemically. Atoms with full outer shells (like Noble gases with 8 electrons in outermost shell) are very stable and don't react easily.

Valency: Rules for Combining Power

Valency is the combining capacity of an element — how many bonds it forms.

  • If valence electrons ≤ 4: Valency = number of valence electrons
  • If valence electrons > 4: Valency = 8 - number of valence electrons

Examples:

  • Sodium (Na): 1 valence electron → Valency = 1 → Forms Na⁺
  • Chlorine (Cl): 7 valence electrons → Valency = 8 - 7 = 1 → Forms Cl⁻
  • Oxygen (O): 6 valence electrons → Valency = 8 - 6 = 2 → Forms O²⁻
  • Carbon (C): 4 valence electrons → Valency = 4 → Forms 4 bonds
Noble gases like Helium, Neon, and Argon have full valence shells (2 or 8 electrons), which is why they don't react with anything. They're perfectly satisfied with their electron configuration and have no reason to bond!

Atomic Number and Mass Number

To identify atoms and distinguish between different types, scientists use two key numbers:

Atomic Number (Z)

1The atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in the nucleus.

In a neutral atom, the atomic number also equals the number of electrons. For example, carbon has atomic number 6, so it has 6 protons and 6 electrons.

Mass Number (A)

2The mass number (A) is the total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus.

The mass number tells us the total mass of the nucleus (roughly, since electrons are so light). For example, if an atom has 11 protons and 12 neutrons, its mass number is 23.

Notation

We write the atomic number and mass number using this notation:

$$^{A}_{Z}X$$

Where X is the element symbol, A is mass number (top-left), Z is atomic number (bottom-left)

$$^{23}_{11}Na$$ (Sodium-23):
Z = 11 (11 protons, 11 electrons)
A = 23 (total nucleons)
Neutrons = A - Z = 23 - 11 = 12
$$^{35}_{17}Cl$$ (Chlorine-35):
Z = 17 (17 protons, 17 electrons)
A = 35 (total nucleons)
Neutrons = A - Z = 35 - 17 = 18
$$^{12}_{6}C$$ (Carbon-12):
Z = 6 (6 protons, 6 electrons)
A = 12 (total nucleons)
Neutrons = A - Z = 12 - 6 = 6

Key Relation

$$\text{Number of Neutrons} = A - Z$$
Scientists use mass number to identify specific versions of elements. When you see "Carbon-14" or "U-235," the number is the mass number. This notation came from mass spectrometry, where particles are separated by their mass.

Isotopes

What if two atoms have the same element (same atomic number) but different mass numbers? They would have the same number of protons and electrons, but different numbers of neutrons. These are called isotopes.

Definition and Examples

1Isotopes are atoms of the SAME element (same atomic number Z) but with DIFFERENT mass numbers (different numbers of neutrons).

Isotopes of Hydrogen

Hydrogen has three natural isotopes:

Protium (¹H): 1 proton, 0 neutrons. The most common form of hydrogen (>99.9%).
Deuterium (²H or D): 1 proton, 1 neutron. Also called "heavy hydrogen." Makes up about 0.015% of natural hydrogen.
Tritium (³H or T): 1 proton, 2 neutrons. Radioactive and rare in nature, but produced in laboratories.

Other Common Isotopes

Carbon Isotopes: $$^{12}_{6}C$$ (12 neutrons), $$^{13}_{6}C$$ (13 neutrons), $$^{14}_{6}C$$ (14 neutrons, radioactive)
Chlorine Isotopes: $$^{35}_{17}Cl$$ (18 neutrons), $$^{37}_{17}Cl$$ (20 neutrons)
Oxygen Isotopes: $$^{16}_{8}O$$ (8 neutrons, 99.8%), $$^{18}_{8}O$$ (10 neutrons, 0.2%)

Chemical vs. Physical Properties of Isotopes

1Chemical properties are THE SAME because they depend on electrons and valence electrons, which are the same for all isotopes of an element.
2Physical properties are DIFFERENT because they depend on atomic mass, which varies with the number of neutrons.
Isotopes are like different models of the same car: a Honda Civic 1.5L and a Honda Civic 2.0L. They have the same design (same element), but different engines (different numbers of neutrons). They drive similarly (same chemistry), but have different speed and power (different physics).

Applications of Isotopes

Carbon-14 Dating

C-14 is radioactive and decays over time. By measuring the C-14 content in ancient objects, scientists can determine their age (dating artifacts, fossils, etc.). Accurate for up to 60,000 years!

Medical Treatments

Cobalt-60 is used to treat cancer (radiotherapy). Iodine-131 treats thyroid disease. These radioactive isotopes kill cancer cells or help regulate the thyroid.

Nuclear Energy

Uranium-235 is used in nuclear power plants to generate electricity. Its nuclei split (fission) and release tremendous energy.

Tracers

Radioactive isotopes can be used to "tag" molecules and track their movement in the body or environment. Doctors inject radioactive glucose to detect cancer.

Disproving Dalton's Theory

Dalton's atomic theory stated: "All atoms of an element are identical." But isotopes disprove this! Atoms of the same element can have different masses because they have different numbers of neutrons. This was a major revision to atomic theory when isotopes were discovered.

The Sun powers itself through nuclear fusion of hydrogen isotopes (deuterium and tritium). Every second, the Sun converts 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium, releasing enough energy to power all of human civilization billions of times over!

Isobars

Just as isotopes are atoms of the same element with different mass numbers, there's another category of atoms worth knowing: isobars.

Definition

1Isobars are atoms of DIFFERENT elements that have the SAME mass number (same total nucleons) but different atomic numbers (different numbers of protons and neutrons).

Examples of Isobars

$$^{40}_{18}Ar$$ and $$^{40}_{20}Ca$$:
Both have mass number 40, but Argon has 18 protons (Z=18) while Calcium has 20 protons (Z=20). They're different elements with the same mass!
$$^{14}_{6}C$$ and $$^{14}_{7}N$$:
Both have mass number 14, but Carbon has 6 protons while Nitrogen has 7 protons. Both have 14 nucleons total, but different compositions.

Key Differences

Element Same element Different elements
Atomic Number (Z) Same (same protons) Different (different protons)
Mass Number (A) Different (different neutrons) Same (same total nucleons)
Example C-12 and C-14 Ar-40 and Ca-40

Important Formulas

$$\text{Max electrons} = 2n^2$$

Relationship Between Atomic Number, Mass Number, and Neutrons

$$A = Z + n$$

Where A = mass number, Z = atomic number, n = number of neutrons

Number of Neutrons

$$n = A - Z$$

Atomic Number Definition

Z = Number of protons = Number of electrons (in neutral atom)

Valency Rules

  • If valence electrons ≤ 4: Valency = valence electrons
  • If valence electrons > 4: Valency = 8 - valence electrons

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

Why did Rutherford use gold foil in his experiment, and could he have used aluminum instead?
Answer Hint

Rutherford used gold foil because: (1) Gold is extremely malleable — it can be beaten into an extremely thin sheet (0.0004 mm), allowing alpha particles to pass through with minimal material; (2) Gold is dense enough to observe significant scattering events; (3) Gold is chemically inert (doesn't react). Aluminum could not have been used because: (1) Aluminum is not as malleable — difficult to beat into foil thin enough; (2) Aluminum is less dense than gold, so fewer scattering events would occur; (3) Aluminum reacts with atmospheric oxygen, forming oxide layers. The thinness of the foil was crucial — too thick and all particles would be scattered; too thick and the nucleus wouldn't deflect them as clearly.

An element has 2 electrons in K shell, 8 electrons in L shell, and 3 electrons in M shell. Identify it and predict its valency and the formula of its oxide.
Answer Hint

Identification: Total electrons = 2 + 8 + 3 = 13. Atomic number = 13. This is Aluminum (Al).
Electronic configuration: K(2), L(8), M(3)
Valency: M shell (valence shell) has 3 electrons. Since ≤ 4, valency = 3
Oxide formula: Aluminum forms Al³⁺ ions (loses 3 electrons). Oxygen forms O²⁻ ions (gains 2 electrons). To balance charges: 2 Al³⁺ requires 3 O²⁻. Formula = Al₂O₃ (aluminum oxide)

Explain why isotopes have the same chemical properties but different physical properties.
Answer Hint

Same Chemical Properties: Chemical properties depend on the valence electrons (outermost shell electrons) and how atoms bond. Isotopes of the same element have identical electronic configurations and the same number of valence electrons. Therefore, they form the same bonds and have identical chemical properties.
Different Physical Properties: Physical properties depend on atomic mass, density, melting point, boiling point, etc. Isotopes have different numbers of neutrons, giving them different atomic masses. For example, Deuterium (H-2) is twice as heavy as Protium (H-1), so it has different density, boiling point, and other physical properties. Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 have different masses, making C-14 radioactive while C-12 is stable.

Case-Based Studies

Case Study 1: Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment

Setup: Rutherford bombarded a thin gold foil (1 μm thick) with alpha particles. Here are the observations:

  • 99% of alpha particles passed through undeflected
  • 0.9% deflected at angles up to 90°
  • 0.1% bounced back (deflected > 90°)

Questions:

  1. What do these observations tell us about atomic structure?
  2. If the atom were solid (as Thomson suggested), what would happen to the alpha particles?
  3. Why was the backscattering of alpha particles so remarkable?
Answers

Q1: The observations indicate: (a) Most of the atom is empty space (99% pass through), (b) Positive charge is concentrated at the center (nucleus) because only particles hitting the nucleus bounce back, (c) The nucleus is very small (few backscatters) but very dense.
Q2: If the atom were solid, most alpha particles would be deflected significantly. Few would pass through undeflected, contradicting observations.
Q3: Backscattering revealed a massive, dense, positively charged nucleus. Alpha particles (positive) hitting the nucleus head-on experience strong repulsion and bounce backward — proving the nucleus exists and concentrates all the positive charge.

Case Study 2: Electronic Configurations and Chemical Bonding

Data: Three elements X, Y, and Z have the following configurations:

  • Element X: K(2), L(8), M(1)
  • Element Y: K(2), L(8), M(7)
  • Element Z: K(2), L(6)

Questions:

  1. Identify X, Y, and Z
  2. Calculate the valency of each
  3. What compound is formed when X and Y react?
  4. Write the electronic configuration of the ions formed
Answers

Q1: X = Sodium (Na, Z=11); Y = Chlorine (Cl, Z=17); Z = Oxygen (O, Z=8)
Q2: Valency of Na = 1; Valency of Cl = 1; Valency of O = 2
Q3: Compound = NaCl (sodium chloride / table salt)
Q4: Na⁺ = K(2), L(8); Cl⁻ = K(2), L(8), M(8); O²⁻ = K(2), L(8)

Case Study 3: Medical Applications of Isotopes

Scenario: Three radioactive isotopes are used in medicine:

  • Cobalt-60 (⁶⁰Co): Used for cancer treatment (radiotherapy)
  • Iodine-131 (¹³¹I): Used to treat thyroid diseases
  • Technetium-99m (⁹⁹ᵐTc): Used for diagnostic imaging

Questions:

  1. Why is the same element behaving differently when it's an isotope?
  2. Why are these radioactive isotopes suitable for medical use while stable isotopes are not?
  3. What is the relationship between atomic number and mass number for ⁶⁰Co (Z=27) and ¹³¹I (Z=53)?
Answers

Q1: Isotopes of the same element have identical electronic configurations (same element), but different numbers of neutrons. The chemical behavior is the same, but the nuclear stability varies. Radioactive isotopes emit radiation, which is harmful to cancer cells but can be controlled medically.
Q2: Radioactive isotopes emit particles (alpha, beta) and energy (gamma rays), which can kill rapidly dividing cancer cells or be used for imaging. Stable isotopes don't emit radiation, so they can't be detected or used therapeutically.
Q3: For ⁶⁰Co: Neutrons = 60 - 27 = 33; For ¹³¹I: Neutrons = 131 - 53 = 78. Both have different numbers of neutrons from their stable forms, making them unstable and radioactive.

Glossary of Key Terms

Atom: The smallest unit of an element that retains the properties of that element.
Electron: A negatively charged subatomic particle found in shells around the nucleus.
Proton: A positively charged subatomic particle found in the nucleus.
Neutron: A neutral (uncharged) subatomic particle found in the nucleus.
Nucleus: The small, dense, positively charged center of an atom containing protons and neutrons.
Nucleon: A collective term for protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
Shell (Energy Level): A fixed orbital path where electrons can revolve around the nucleus without radiating energy.
Valence Electrons: The electrons in the outermost shell of an atom that determine chemical properties.
Valency: The combining capacity of an atom — the number of electrons it needs to lose, gain, or share to achieve a stable configuration.
Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus, which defines the element.
Mass Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
Isotope: Atoms of the same element (same Z) with different mass numbers (different number of neutrons).
Isobar: Atoms of different elements (different Z) with the same mass number (same total nucleons).
Electronic Configuration: The arrangement of electrons in the various shells of an atom.
Plum Pudding Model: Thomson's model of the atom, picturing it as a sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded throughout.
Gold Foil Experiment: Rutherford's landmark experiment that revealed the nuclear structure of atoms.

Remediation Guide

Common Misconceptions & Fixes

Misconception: "Electrons orbit the nucleus like planets around the sun"
Correction: In modern quantum mechanics, electrons don't have definite orbits. They exist in probability clouds called orbitals. Bohr's model is useful for learning but is simplified.
Misconception: "Isotopes are different elements"
Correction: No! Isotopes are atoms of the SAME element with different mass numbers. They have the same atomic number (same protons/electrons) but different neutrons.
Misconception: "Atomic number and mass number are the same"
Correction: NO! Z = protons only. A = protons + neutrons. A - Z = neutrons. They're completely different.
Misconception: "Valence = number of valence electrons always"
Correction: Only if valence electrons ≤ 4! If > 4, valency = 8 - valence electrons. Oxygen (6 valence) has valency 2, not 6!
Misconception: "Isobars are the same as isotopes"
Correction: NO! Isotopes = same element, different A. Isobars = different elements, same A. Ar-40 and Ca-40 are isobars.
