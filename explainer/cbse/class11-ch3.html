Motion in a Plane

Chapter 3 | NCERT Class 11 Physics

Why This Matters

Have you ever wondered why a cricket ball follows a curved path when bowled, or how satellites orbit Earth in circular paths? The answer lies in understanding motion in more than one dimension. Motion in a plane extends our knowledge from one-dimensional motion to two dimensions, allowing us to describe and predict the behavior of projectiles, rotating objects, and countless everyday phenomena.

From calculating the perfect arc of a basketball shot to understanding the orbital mechanics of space missions, the concepts in this chapter form the foundation of classical mechanics. We'll explore how objects move when forces act in different directions simultaneously.

1. Scalars and Vectors

Understanding the Basics

Physical quantities in physics can be classified into two main categories:

Scalars

A scalar is a physical quantity that has only magnitude (size or value) and no direction. It can be completely described by a single number and a unit.

Examples of Scalars:

  • Mass: 75 kg (just a number)
  • Temperature: 25°C (just a number)
  • Speed: 60 km/h (magnitude only)
  • Distance: 100 m (total path length)
  • Time: 5 seconds
  • Energy: 500 J
Did You Know? Speed is a scalar, but velocity is not! This is because velocity requires direction. A car traveling at 60 km/h north is different from one traveling at 60 km/h south.

Vectors

A vector is a physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction. It requires both components to be fully described.

Examples of Vectors:

  • Displacement: 100 m north (distance + direction)
  • Velocity: 60 km/h northeast (speed + direction)
  • Force: 50 N upward
  • Acceleration: 10 m/s² horizontally
  • Weight: 735 N downward
  • Momentum: 100 kg·m/s in a specific direction

Representation of Vectors

Vectors are typically represented using arrow notation:

\(\vec{A}\) or \(\mathbf{A}\) = represents a vector
\(|\vec{A}|\) or \(A\) = magnitude of the vector

Graphically, vectors are drawn as arrows where:

  • The arrow's length represents the magnitude
  • The arrow's direction shows the direction of the vector
  • The tail of the arrow is at the starting point
  • The head/tip points toward the direction
Did You Know? In navigation, vectors are essential. Pilots and sailors must consider both speed and direction (wind vectors, current vectors) to reach their destination.

2. Vector Operations

Multiplication by a Scalar

When a vector is multiplied by a scalar (a simple number), its magnitude is multiplied by that scalar, but its direction remains the same.

If \(\vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j}\) and \(k\) is a scalar:

\(k\vec{A} = kA_x\hat{i} + kA_y\hat{j}\)

Example: If velocity is 5 m/s east, then 2 times this velocity is 10 m/s east.

Vector Addition: Triangle Law and Parallelogram Law

When two vectors are added, we cannot simply add their magnitudes. We must consider their directions.

Triangle Law of Vector Addition:

If two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are represented as two sides of a triangle taken in the same order, their sum \(\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}\) is represented by the third side of the triangle.

Did You Know? Vector addition is commutative: \(\vec{A} + \vec{B} = \vec{B} + \vec{A}\). The order doesn't matter!

Parallelogram Law of Vector Addition:

If two vectors are represented as two adjacent sides of a parallelogram (in magnitude and direction), their resultant is represented by the diagonal of the parallelogram from the common vertex.

Magnitude of resultant: \(R = \sqrt{A^2 + B^2 + 2AB\cos\theta}\)

where \(\theta\) is the angle between vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\)

Resolution of Vectors into Components

A vector can be broken down into its perpendicular components. In a 2D coordinate system:

\(A_x = A \cos\theta\)
\(A_y = A \sin\theta\)

where \(\theta\) is the angle from the x-axis

Magnitude from components:

\(|\vec{A}| = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}\)

Worked Example: Vector Components

Problem: A force of 100 N is applied at an angle of 30° to the horizontal. Find its horizontal and vertical components.
Solution:
Given: \(F = 100\) N, \(\theta = 30°\)

Horizontal component: \(F_x = F\cos\theta = 100 \times \cos 30° = 100 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 50\sqrt{3}\) N

Vertical component: \(F_y = F\sin\theta = 100 \times \sin 30° = 100 \times \frac{1}{2} = 50\) N
Answer: \(F_x = 86.6\) N (horizontal), \(F_y = 50\) N (vertical)

Unit Vectors

Unit vectors are vectors with magnitude 1, used to specify direction. In a 2D Cartesian coordinate system:

  • \(\hat{i}\) = unit vector along positive x-axis
  • \(\hat{j}\) = unit vector along positive y-axis
  • \(\hat{k}\) = unit vector along positive z-axis (for 3D)

Properties:

  • \(|\hat{i}| = |\hat{j}| = |\hat{k}| = 1\)
  • \(\hat{i} \cdot \hat{i} = 1\), \(\hat{i} \cdot \hat{j} = 0\) (perpendicular)
  • Any vector can be expressed as: \(\vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j}\)
Vector representation: \(\vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j}\)

where \(A_x\) and \(A_y\) are the components along x and y axes

Dot Product (Scalar Product)

The dot product of two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is a scalar quantity.

\(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB\cos\theta\)

OR in component form:
\(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_xB_x + A_yB_y\)

Properties:

  • If \(\theta = 0°\): \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB\) (vectors in same direction)
  • If \(\theta = 90°\): \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = 0\) (perpendicular vectors)
  • If \(\theta = 180°\): \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = -AB\) (opposite direction)
  • Dot product is commutative: \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A}\)

Cross Product (Vector Product)

The cross product of two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is another vector perpendicular to both.

\(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = AB\sin\theta \, \hat{n}\)

where \(\hat{n}\) is the unit vector perpendicular to the plane containing \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\)

Determinant Method (for 2D vectors with z-component 0):

\(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ A_x & A_y & 0 \\ B_x & B_y & 0 \end{vmatrix} = (A_xB_y - A_yB_x)\hat{k}\)

Properties:

  • Cross product is NOT commutative: \(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = -\vec{B} \times \vec{A}\)
  • If \(\theta = 0°\): \(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = 0\) (parallel vectors)
  • If \(\theta = 90°\): \(|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = AB\) (maximum)
  • Direction: Right-hand rule determines the direction of the result
Did You Know? The cross product is used to calculate torque (rotational force): \(\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}\). This is why it's essential in rotational motion!

3. Motion in a Plane

Position Vector

The position of an object in a 2D plane is described by the position vector:

\(\vec{r} = x\hat{i} + y\hat{j}\)

where \(x\) and \(y\) are the coordinates of the object.

Displacement

Displacement is the change in position and is a vector quantity:

\(\Delta\vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i = \Delta x\hat{i} + \Delta y\hat{j}\)

Magnitude of displacement: \(|\Delta\vec{r}| = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}\)

Velocity

Velocity is the rate of change of position with respect to time:

\(\vec{v} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = v_x\hat{i} + v_y\hat{j}\)

Components of velocity:

  • \(v_x = \frac{dx}{dt}\) = component along x-axis
  • \(v_y = \frac{dy}{dt}\) = component along y-axis

Magnitude of velocity (speed):

\(v = |\vec{v}| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}\)

Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time:

\(\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = a_x\hat{i} + a_y\hat{j}\)

Components of acceleration:

  • \(a_x = \frac{dv_x}{dt}\) = component along x-axis
  • \(a_y = \frac{dv_y}{dt}\) = component along y-axis
Did You Know? In motion in a plane, you can analyze the motion independently along x and y axes. This is called the principle of independence of motion!

4. Projectile Motion

What is Projectile Motion?

Projectile motion occurs when an object is thrown or launched into the air and moves under the influence of gravity alone. The object follows a parabolic path. Classic examples include:

  • A ball thrown horizontally from a cliff
  • A cricket ball hit at an angle
  • A stone thrown from the ground at an angle
  • A water fountain creating an arc
PROJECTILE MOTION TRAJECTORY H (max height) | 90°| * | * * | * * | * * | * * | * * | * * 45°----|-------*-------------*--------R (max range) | * * | * * | * * 0°--*---------------------* θ (launch angle) Note: Max range occurs at θ = 45°

Key Assumptions

  • Air resistance is negligible
  • The acceleration due to gravity is constant (\(g = 9.8\) m/s² or 10 m/s²)
  • The motion can be analyzed independently in x and y directions
  • The Earth is flat (valid for small heights)

Equations of Motion: Horizontal and Vertical Components

Horizontal Motion (constant velocity):

\(x = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t\)

\(v_x = v_0 \cos\theta\) (remains constant)

Vertical Motion (uniformly accelerated):

\(y = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\)

\(v_y = v_0 \sin\theta - gt\)

where \(v_0\) is the initial velocity, \(\theta\) is the launch angle, and \(g\) is acceleration due to gravity.

Important Results in Projectile Motion

Time of Flight (T): Time taken to complete the projectile motion

\(T = \frac{2v_0 \sin\theta}{g}\)

Maximum Height (H): The highest point reached by the projectile

\(H = \frac{v_0^2 \sin^2\theta}{2g}\)

Horizontal Range (R): Maximum horizontal distance covered

\(R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}\)

Important Note: Maximum range occurs when \(\theta = 45°\) (since \(\sin 90° = 1\) is maximum)

\(R_{max} = \frac{v_0^2}{g}\) (when \(\theta = 45°\))

Equation of Path (Trajectory)

The trajectory of a projectile (the path it follows) is parabolic:

\(y = x\tan\theta - \frac{gx^2}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}\)

This equation shows that:

  • The path is a parabola
  • The shape depends on the initial angle and velocity
  • Higher initial velocity → flatter parabola, greater range
  • Greater angle → higher trajectory, greater height

Worked Example 1: Projectile Motion

Problem: A cricket ball is projected at an angle of 45° with an initial velocity of 20 m/s. Calculate:
(a) Time of flight
(b) Maximum height
(c) Horizontal range
Solution:
Given: \(v_0 = 20\) m/s, \(\theta = 45°\), \(g = 10\) m/s²

(a) Time of Flight:
\(T = \frac{2v_0\sin\theta}{g} = \frac{2 \times 20 \times \sin 45°}{10}\)
\(T = \frac{2 \times 20 \times 0.707}{10} = \frac{28.28}{10} = 2.828\) s ≈ 2.83 s

(b) Maximum Height:
\(H = \frac{v_0^2\sin^2\theta}{2g} = \frac{(20)^2 \times \sin^2 45°}{2 \times 10}\)
\(H = \frac{400 \times 0.5}{20} = \frac{200}{20} = 10\) m

(c) Horizontal Range:
\(R = \frac{v_0^2\sin 2\theta}{g} = \frac{(20)^2 \times \sin 90°}{10}\)
\(R = \frac{400 \times 1}{10} = 40\) m
Answers: (a) T = 2.83 s, (b) H = 10 m, (c) R = 40 m

Worked Example 2: Projectile from Height

Problem: A projectile is launched horizontally with velocity 30 m/s from a height of 45 m. Calculate:
(a) Time to reach the ground
(b) Horizontal distance covered
(c) Final velocity (magnitude and direction)
Solution:
Given: \(v_0 = 30\) m/s (horizontal), \(h = 45\) m, \(\theta = 0°\), \(g = 10\) m/s²

(a) Time to reach ground:
Using \(y = v_{0y}t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) where \(y = -45\) m (downward)
\(-45 = 0 - \frac{1}{2}(10)t^2\)
\(t^2 = 9\), so \(t = 3\) s

(b) Horizontal distance:
\(x = v_0 \times t = 30 \times 3 = 90\) m

(c) Final velocity:
\(v_x = 30\) m/s (constant)
\(v_y = 0 - gt = 0 - 10(3) = -30\) m/s
\(v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} = \sqrt{900 + 900} = \sqrt{1800} ≈ 42.4\) m/s
Direction: \(\tan\phi = \frac{v_y}{v_x} = \frac{30}{30} = 1\), so \(\phi = 45°\) below horizontal
Answers: (a) t = 3 s, (b) x = 90 m, (c) v ≈ 42.4 m/s at 45° below horizontal
Did You Know? A projectile shot at 30° has the same range as one shot at 60° (for the same initial velocity). This is because \(\sin(2×30°) = \sin(2×60°) = \sin 120° = \sin 60°\). These are called complementary angles!

5. Uniform Circular Motion

What is Circular Motion?

Circular motion occurs when an object moves in a circular path. When the speed remains constant but direction keeps changing, it's called uniform circular motion.

Real-world examples:

  • Earth revolving around the Sun
  • A stone tied to a string and swung in a circle
  • A car taking a circular turn at constant speed
  • Electrons orbiting the nucleus in atoms
  • A Ferris wheel moving at constant speed

Angular Displacement and Angular Velocity

Period (T): Time taken to complete one full revolution

Frequency (ν): Number of revolutions per unit time

\(\nu = \frac{1}{T}\)

Angular Velocity (ω): Rate of change of angular displacement

\(\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi\nu\)

Relationship between linear velocity and angular velocity:

\(v = \omega r\)

where \(r\) is the radius of the circular path.

Centripetal Acceleration

Even though the speed is constant in uniform circular motion, the direction is constantly changing. This change in direction requires acceleration, called centripetal acceleration.

\(a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r\)

Important Properties of Centripetal Acceleration:

  • Always directed toward the center of the circle
  • Perpendicular to the velocity at every instant
  • Has constant magnitude (for uniform circular motion)
  • Always acts inward (toward center)
UNIFORM CIRCULAR MOTION ↑ v (velocity) | [Object moving] * / \ | | r ac→ | O | (radius) | | \ / \_/ ac = v²/r (toward center O) v = ωr (tangent to circle) Key: Velocity is ALWAYS tangent to circle Acceleration is ALWAYS toward center

Centripetal Force

To maintain circular motion, a force must act toward the center. This is the centripetal force:

\(F_c = ma_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = m\omega^2r\)

Sources of Centripetal Force:

  • Tension in a string (stone in circular motion)
  • Normal force from the road (car taking a turn)
  • Friction between tires and road (car on circular track)
  • Gravitational force (planets orbiting stars)
  • Electrical force (electrons orbiting nucleus)
Did You Know? There is NO "centrifugal force" pulling outward! What we feel is actually our inertia trying to keep us moving in a straight line while the circular path pulls us inward.

Worked Example 1: Circular Motion Basics

Problem: A stone is attached to a string of length 2 m and is whirled in a horizontal circle. If the stone completes 5 revolutions in 10 seconds, calculate:
(a) Period and frequency
(b) Angular velocity
(c) Linear velocity
(d) Centripetal acceleration
Solution:
Given: \(r = 2\) m, 5 revolutions in 10 s

(a) Period and Frequency:
Period = Time for 1 revolution = \(\frac{10}{5} = 2\) s
Frequency = \(\nu = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5\) Hz

(b) Angular Velocity:
\(\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = \frac{2\pi}{2} = \pi\) rad/s ≈ 3.14 rad/s

(c) Linear Velocity:
\(v = \omega r = \pi \times 2 = 2\pi\) m/s ≈ 6.28 m/s

(d) Centripetal Acceleration:
\(a_c = \omega^2 r = (\pi)^2 \times 2 = 2\pi^2\) m/s² ≈ 19.74 m/s²
OR \(a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{(2\pi)^2}{2} = 2\pi^2\) m/s²
Answers: (a) T = 2 s, ν = 0.5 Hz, (b) ω ≈ 3.14 rad/s, (c) v ≈ 6.28 m/s, (d) ac ≈ 19.74 m/s²

Worked Example 2: Centripetal Force

Problem: A car of mass 1000 kg takes a circular turn of radius 50 m at a speed of 20 m/s. Calculate:
(a) Centripetal acceleration required
(b) Centripetal force required
(c) If friction provides this force, what is the minimum coefficient of friction needed?
Solution:
Given: \(m = 1000\) kg, \(r = 50\) m, \(v = 20\) m/s, \(g = 10\) m/s²

(a) Centripetal Acceleration:
\(a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{(20)^2}{50} = \frac{400}{50} = 8\) m/s²

(b) Centripetal Force:
\(F_c = ma_c = 1000 \times 8 = 8000\) N

(c) Minimum Coefficient of Friction:
The friction force provides centripetal force: \(f = \mu N = \mu mg\)
\(\mu mg = F_c\)
\(\mu \times 1000 \times 10 = 8000\)
\(\mu = \frac{8000}{10000} = 0.8\)
Answers: (a) ac = 8 m/s², (b) Fc = 8000 N, (c) μ = 0.8

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS 1: Why is it not possible to add a vector and a scalar?

A vector has both magnitude and direction, while a scalar has only magnitude. They are fundamentally different types of quantities. Addition requires both quantities to have the same dimensionality. A vector requires a direction specification, while a scalar doesn't. Mathematically, you cannot add 5 m/s (velocity vector) and 3 kg (mass scalar) because they represent different physical properties. However, you can multiply a scalar with a vector!

HOTS 2: A projectile has maximum height at a certain point during its motion. What are the horizontal and vertical components of its velocity at this point, and why?

At maximum height, the vertical component of velocity (vy) is ZERO, while the horizontal component (vx) remains unchanged. This is because at maximum height, the projectile momentarily stops moving upward before starting to fall. The horizontal velocity remains constant throughout (no horizontal acceleration), but vertical velocity becomes zero at the turning point. This is why vy = v₀ sin θ - gt = 0 at max height, giving t = v₀ sin θ / g.

HOTS 3: In uniform circular motion, the speed is constant but the object still has acceleration. Explain this apparent paradox.

This is not a paradox! Acceleration is defined as the rate of change of velocity (not speed). Velocity is a vector with both magnitude and direction. In circular motion, while the magnitude (speed) stays constant, the direction changes continuously. Since velocity direction changes, there IS acceleration, even though speed is constant. This acceleration (centripetal acceleration) is directed toward the center and is responsible for the continuous change in direction. This fundamental insight shows why vectors are necessary in physics!

Cheat Sheet & Formula Reference

\(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = AB\sin\theta\) — Cross product magnitude
\(R = \sqrt{A^2 + B^2 + 2AB\cos\theta}\) — Resultant of two vectors

\(\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = a_x\hat{i} + a_y\hat{j}\) — Acceleration
\(\Delta\vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i\) — Displacement

\(x = v_0\cos\theta \cdot t\) — Horizontal position
\(y = v_0\sin\theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) — Vertical position
\(v_x = v_0\cos\theta\) — Horizontal velocity (constant)
\(v_y = v_0\sin\theta - gt\) — Vertical velocity
\(T = \frac{2v_0\sin\theta}{g}\) — Time of flight
\(H = \frac{v_0^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}\) — Maximum height

\(T = \frac{1}{\nu}\) — Period and frequency relation

\(v = \omega r\) — Linear and angular velocity relation
\(a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r\) — Centripetal acceleration
\(F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r\) — Centripetal force

Key Glossary

Scalar: Physical quantity with magnitude only, no direction (e.g., mass, temperature, speed).
Vector: Physical quantity with both magnitude and direction (e.g., displacement, velocity, force).
Displacement: Change in position of an object, a vector quantity with magnitude and direction.
Velocity: Rate of change of displacement with respect to time; a vector quantity.
Acceleration: Rate of change of velocity with respect to time; a vector quantity.
Projectile Motion: Motion of an object under gravity alone (ignoring air resistance).
Range: Maximum horizontal distance covered by a projectile during its flight.
Trajectory: Path traced by a moving object, parabolic for projectiles.
Circular Motion: Motion of an object along a circular path at constant or variable speed.
Centripetal Force: Force directed toward the center, required to maintain circular motion.
Angular Velocity: Rate of change of angular displacement, ω = 2π/T.
Period: Time taken by an object to complete one full revolution in circular motion.
