Chapter 5: Work, Energy and Power

Understanding the fundamental concepts of work, kinetic and potential energy, and power in mechanics

Why Study This Chapter?

When you lift a book, push a cart, or throw a ball, you're doing work. Understanding work and energy helps us analyze why objects move, how fast they move, and what happens when they collide. Power tells us how quickly we can do work. These concepts are crucial for everything from designing machines to understanding collisions in real-world scenarios like car crashes and sports.

Core Concepts

1. Work (W)

Work is done when a force causes a displacement in the direction of the force. It quantifies the energy transfer when a force acts on an object.

Work Formula: W = F⃗ · d⃗ = Fd cos θ

Where:

  • F = magnitude of force (N)
  • d = displacement (m)
  • θ = angle between force and displacement
  • Unit: Joule (J) = kg·m²/s²

Types of Work:

  • Positive Work (0° ≤ θ < 90°): Force and displacement in same direction. Object gains energy.
  • Negative Work (90° < θ ≤ 180°): Force opposes displacement. Object loses energy.
  • Zero Work (θ = 90°): Force perpendicular to displacement. No energy transfer. Example: carrying a book horizontally (gravity does zero work).
Work Done by Variable Force: W = ∫ F·dx = Area under F-x curve [Force on vertical axis, displacement on horizontal] Example: Spring force increases linearly with extension

Example 1: Constant Force

A force of 50 N pulls a box 10 m at 30° above horizontal. Find work done.

Solution: W = Fd cos θ = 50 × 10 × cos(30°) = 500 × 0.866 = 433 J

2. Kinetic Energy (KE)

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. Every moving object possesses kinetic energy.

Kinetic Energy Formula: KE = ½mv²

Where:

  • m = mass (kg)
  • v = velocity (m/s)
  • KE depends on mass and square of velocity

Work-Energy Theorem

The net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy:

W_net = ΔKE = KE_f - KE_i = ½m(v_f² - v_i²)

Derivation:

From Newton's second law: F = ma

Work: W = F·s = m·a·s

Using v² = u² + 2as: a·s = (v² - u²)/2

Therefore: W = m(v² - u²)/2 = ½mv² - ½mu² = ΔKE

Example 2: Work-Energy Theorem

A 2 kg object accelerates from 3 m/s to 8 m/s. Find the net work done.

Solution:

KE_i = ½(2)(3)² = 9 J

KE_f = ½(2)(8)² = 64 J

W_net = 64 - 9 = 55 J

3. Work Done by Variable Force

When force changes with displacement, we integrate to find total work.

W = ∫₀ˢ F(x)dx

This represents the area under the force-displacement curve.

Spring Force (Special Case)

For a spring: F = -kx (Hooke's law)

Work done to stretch or compress by x:

W = ½kx²

This is the elastic potential energy stored in the spring.

Example 3: Spring Force

A spring with k = 200 N/m is stretched 0.5 m. Find work done.

Solution: W = ½kx² = ½(200)(0.5)² = 100(0.25) = 25 J

4. Potential Energy (PE)

Potential energy is stored energy due to position or configuration. It arises from conservative forces.

Conservative vs Non-Conservative Forces:

  • Conservative Forces: Work done is path-independent. Examples: gravity, spring force, electric force.
  • Non-Conservative Forces: Work depends on path. Examples: friction, air resistance.

Gravitational Potential Energy

U_grav = mgh

Where:

  • m = mass (kg)
  • g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
  • h = height above reference point (m)

Elastic (Spring) Potential Energy

U_spring = ½kx²

Where:

  • k = spring constant (N/m)
  • x = displacement from equilibrium (m)

Relation Between Force and Potential Energy

F = -dU/dx

Force is the negative gradient of potential energy. This shows force always acts to decrease potential energy.

Example 4: Potential Energy

A 5 kg object is at height 20 m. Find gravitational PE relative to ground.

Solution: U = mgh = 5 × 9.8 × 20 = 980 J

5. Conservation of Mechanical Energy

In a system with only conservative forces, total mechanical energy remains constant.

E = KE + PE = constant KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f ½mv_i² + U_i = ½mv_f² + U_f

Conditions for Conservation:

  • Only conservative forces do work
  • No friction or air resistance
  • No energy loss to heat or other forms
Energy Conservation in Free Fall: At height h: KE = 0, PE = mgh, E = mgh At height h/2: KE = ½mgh, PE = ½mgh, E = mgh At ground: KE = mgh, PE = 0, E = mgh (Total energy E remains constant)

Example 5: Free Fall

A 2 kg ball is dropped from 10 m. Find velocity at 5 m height (ignoring air resistance).

Solution:

Initial: KE_i = 0, PE_i = mgh = 2(10)(10) = 200 J

At 5 m: PE_f = 2(10)(5) = 100 J

By conservation: 200 = ½(2)v² + 100

100 = v²

v = 10 m/s

6. Different Forms of Energy

Energy exists in many forms and can be converted from one to another.

Form of Energy Description Examples
Heat (Thermal) Energy due to random motion of particles Friction, combustion, sun
Chemical Energy stored in chemical bonds Food, fuel, batteries
Electrical Energy of moving charges Lightning, circuits, motors
Nuclear Energy from nuclear reactions Radioactive decay, nuclear power
Mechanical Kinetic + Potential energy Moving objects, springs

Mass-Energy Equivalence

E = mc²

Einstein's equation showing mass can be converted to energy. A tiny amount of mass contains enormous energy.

Example: 1 kg of mass = 9 × 10¹⁶ J (equivalent to 21.5 megatons TNT)

7. Power (P)

Power is the rate of doing work or the rate of energy transfer.

P = W/t = ΔE/Δt P = F⃗ · v⃗ = Fv cos θ

Where:

  • W = work done (J)
  • t = time (s)
  • Unit: Watt (W) = J/s = kg·m²/s³

Instantaneous Power

Power at a specific instant: P = dW/dt

Average Power

Power over a time interval: P_avg = W_total / t_total

Common Power Units:

  • 1 Watt (W) = 1 J/s
  • 1 Kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W
  • 1 Horsepower (HP) = 746 W
  • 1 Megawatt (MW) = 10⁶ W

Example 6: Power Calculation

A motor lifts 100 kg by 5 m in 10 seconds. Find power.

Solution:

Work = mgh = 100 × 10 × 5 = 5000 J

Power = W/t = 5000/10 = 500 W = 0.67 HP

8. Collisions

A collision is an event where two or more bodies exert forces on each other for a relatively short time. During collision, momentum is always conserved.

Types of Collisions:

A. Elastic Collisions

Both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved. Objects bounce apart.

Conservation of Momentum: m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = m₁v₁' + m₂v₂' Conservation of KE: ½m₁v₁² + ½m₂v₂² = ½m₁v₁'² + ½m₂v₂'²

1D Elastic Collision Formula (Derived)

v₁' = ((m₁ - m₂)/(m₁ + m₂))v₁ + (2m₂/(m₁ + m₂))v₂ v₂' = (2m₁/(m₁ + m₂))v₁ + ((m₂ - m₁)/(m₁ + m₂))v₂

Example 7: Elastic Collision (Equal Masses)

Two objects of equal mass (1 kg each) collide elastically. First has velocity 5 m/s, second at rest.

Solution:

Using formulas with m₁ = m₂ = m:

v₁' = 0 (first object stops)

v₂' = 5 m/s (second object moves with original velocity)

Result: Velocities are exchanged!

B. Inelastic Collisions

Only momentum is conserved. Kinetic energy is lost to deformation, heat, sound, etc.

Perfectly Inelastic Collision

Objects stick together after collision.

m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = (m₁ + m₂)v' v' = (m₁v₁ + m₂v₂)/(m₁ + m₂)

Example 8: Perfectly Inelastic Collision

A 2 kg ball (5 m/s) collides and sticks with a 3 kg ball (0 m/s).

Solution:

v' = (2×5 + 3×0)/(2+3) = 10/5 = 2 m/s

Both objects move together at 2 m/s after collision.

Coefficient of Restitution (e)

Measures the elasticity of a collision.

e = (v₂' - v₁')/(v₁ - v₂)

Where:

  • e = 1: Perfectly elastic collision
  • 0 < e < 1: Partially elastic collision
  • e = 0: Perfectly inelastic collision
Relative Velocity: The coefficient of restitution compares the relative velocity after and before collision.

C. 2D (Oblique) Collisions

In 2D collisions, momentum is conserved separately in x and y directions.

x-direction: m₁v₁ₓ + m₂v₂ₓ = m₁v₁ₓ' + m₂v₂ₓ' y-direction: m₁v₁ᵧ + m₂v₂ᵧ = m₁v₁ᵧ' + m₂v₂ᵧ'
Collision Analysis Steps:
  1. Identify collision type (elastic, inelastic, or perfectly inelastic)
  2. Apply conservation of momentum
  3. For elastic, also apply conservation of kinetic energy
  4. Solve simultaneously for final velocities

Example 9: Elastic Collision with Different Masses

1 kg object at 6 m/s collides elastically with 2 kg object at 0 m/s. Find final velocities.

Solution:

Using v₁' = ((1-2)/(1+2))×6 + 0 = -2 m/s (bounces back)

Using v₂' = (2×1/(1+2))×6 = 4 m/s (moves forward)

Check momentum: 1×6 = 1×(-2) + 2×4 = 6 ✓

Check energy: ½(1)(36) = ½(1)(4) + ½(2)(16) = 18 J ✓

Special Cases in Collisions:

  • Both objects at rest: No collision (relative velocity = 0)
  • Moving in same direction: If faster catches slower, collision possible
  • Head-on collision: 1D collision with opposite velocities
  • Glancing blow: Objects don't collide head-on; 2D analysis needed
Elastic vs Inelastic Collision: Elastic (1kg at 4 m/s hits 2kg at 0): Before: [1kg→] [2kg] After: [1kg↓] [2kg→→] Objects separate, KE conserved Inelastic (1kg at 4 m/s sticks to 2kg at 0): Before: [1kg→] [2kg] After: [1kg+2kg→] Objects stick together, KE lost

Quick Knowledge Check

Q1: Work Concept (name="q5-1")

A force of 20 N is applied on an object at an angle of 60° to the direction of motion. If the object moves 5 m, the work done is:

A) 50 J
B) 100 J
C) 150 J
D) 200 J
Correct Answer: A) 50 J

W = Fd cos θ = 20 × 5 × cos(60°) = 100 × 0.5 = 50 J

Q2: Kinetic Energy (name="q5-2")

If the velocity of an object is doubled, its kinetic energy becomes:

A) 2 times
B) 4 times
C) 8 times
D) Same
Correct Answer: B) 4 times

KE = ½mv². If v → 2v, then KE = ½m(2v)² = 4(½mv²) = 4 × KE₀

Kinetic energy depends on v², so doubling velocity quadruples kinetic energy.

Q3: Conservation of Energy (name="q5-3")

A ball is dropped from a height of 10 m. Ignoring air resistance, its speed at 5 m height is (g = 10 m/s²):

A) 5 m/s
B) 10 m/s
C) 15 m/s
D) 20 m/s
Correct Answer: B) 10 m/s

Using energy conservation: mgh₁ = ½mv² + mgh₂

gh₁ = ½v² + gh₂

g(h₁ - h₂) = ½v²

10 × 5 = ½v²

v² = 100, so v = 10 m/s

Q4: Spring Potential Energy (name="q5-4")

If a spring with spring constant 100 N/m is compressed by 0.2 m, the elastic potential energy stored is:

A) 1 J
B) 2 J
C) 3 J
D) 4 J
Correct Answer: B) 2 J

U = ½kx² = ½ × 100 × (0.2)² = 50 × 0.04 = 2 J

Q5: Power Calculation (name="q5-5")

A machine does 6000 J of work in 30 seconds. Its power is:

A) 200 W
B) 180000 W
C) 300 W
D) 100 W
Correct Answer: A) 200 W

P = W/t = 6000/30 = 200 W

Q6: Collision Type (name="q5-6")

In a collision, momentum is always conserved but kinetic energy may not be. This is characteristic of:

A) Elastic collision
B) Inelastic collision
C) Perfectly inelastic collision
D) Both B and C
Correct Answer: D) Both B and C

Inelastic collisions (including perfectly inelastic) conserve momentum but not kinetic energy. Some KE is converted to heat, sound, deformation, etc.

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

HOTS 1: Energy Conservation with Variable Forces

Question: A block slides down a curved surface (frictionless). The surface has varying slope. Using the work-energy theorem and conservation of energy, prove that the final speed depends only on the vertical height difference, not on the shape of the path.

Hint: Consider that work done by gravity depends only on vertical displacement, not path taken.

Solution Approach:

  • Identify that gravity is a conservative force
  • Gravitational work W = mgh (height difference only)
  • By work-energy theorem: W = ΔKE
  • mgh = ½m(v_f² - v_i²)
  • Final velocity depends only on Δh, regardless of path shape
  • This demonstrates path independence of conservative forces

HOTS 2: Collision Analysis with Energy Loss

Question: Two cars of equal mass m collide. Car 1 approaches at velocity v, Car 2 is stationary. After collision, they stick together. Calculate: (a) Final velocity, (b) Kinetic energy before collision, (c) Kinetic energy after collision, (d) Energy loss percentage.

Solution:

  • Part (a): Using momentum conservation: mv + 0 = 2m·v', so v' = v/2
  • Part (b): KE_initial = ½mv² + 0 = ½mv²
  • Part (c): KE_final = ½(2m)(v/2)² = ¼mv²
  • Part (d): Energy loss = ½mv² - ¼mv² = ¼mv²
  • Energy loss percentage = (¼mv²)/(½mv²) × 100% = 50%
  • Insight: Perfectly inelastic collision always loses energy (except special case of stationary objects)

HOTS 3: Power and Efficiency

Question: An electric motor rated 1000 W lifts a 50 kg load at constant velocity. (a) What is the maximum height the load can be lifted in 10 seconds? (b) If the actual height reached is 15 m, what is the efficiency of the motor?

Solution:

  • Part (a): P = W/t, so W = Pt = 1000 × 10 = 10,000 J
  • mgh = 10,000
  • 50 × 10 × h = 10,000
  • h_max = 20 m (in ideal case with 100% efficiency)
  • Part (b): Actual work = mgh = 50 × 10 × 15 = 7500 J
  • Efficiency = (Useful output / Total input) × 100% = (7500/10000) × 100% = 75%
  • Insight: Real motors always have efficiency < 100% due to friction and other losses

Case-Based Questions

Case Study 1: Roller Coaster Dynamics

Scenario: A roller coaster cart of mass 500 kg starts from rest at height 30 m. Assume friction is negligible.

Questions:

  1. Using conservation of energy, find the speed at the bottom (h = 0)
  2. At what height will the speed be exactly half of the bottom speed?
  3. If a brake applies an average friction force of 2000 N as the cart descends from 30 m to 0, find actual speed at bottom
  4. Calculate the work done by friction in part (c)

Solutions:

  1. Using mgh = ½mv²: v = √(2gh) = √(2×10×30) = √600 = 24.5 m/s
  2. For v' = v/2: mgh' = ½m(v/2)² = ½m(v²/4) = ½m(2gh)/4 = mgh/4, so h' = 7.5 m
  3. Work by gravity = 500 × 10 × 30 = 150,000 J
  4. Work by friction = -2000 × 30 = -60,000 J
  5. Net work = 150,000 - 60,000 = 90,000 J
  6. ½mv² = 90,000, so v = √(360) = 19 m/s

Case Study 2: Car Collision Analysis

Scenario: A car A (mass 1000 kg) traveling at 20 m/s collides with stationary car B (mass 1500 kg). After collision, the cars move together.

Questions:

  1. Find the velocity of the combined mass after collision
  2. Calculate kinetic energy before and after collision
  3. What percentage of kinetic energy is lost?
  4. Is this collision elastic or inelastic? Justify.

Solutions:

  1. Momentum conservation: 1000(20) + 1500(0) = (1000+1500)v'
  2. 20,000 = 2500v', so v' = 8 m/s
  3. KE_before = ½(1000)(20)² = 200,000 J
  4. KE_after = ½(2500)(8)² = 80,000 J
  5. Energy loss = 200,000 - 80,000 = 120,000 J
  6. Percentage loss = (120,000/200,000) × 100% = 60%
  7. This is a perfectly inelastic collision (bodies stick together and KE is not conserved)

Case Study 3: Elastic Collision in Sports

Scenario: In a tennis game, a ball (0.06 kg) hits a wall elastically with velocity 20 m/s and rebounds with velocity -20 m/s (opposite direction).

Questions:

  1. Calculate the change in kinetic energy of the ball
  2. Calculate the change in momentum of the ball
  3. Why is kinetic energy unchanged even though momentum changes?
  4. If the collision lasts 0.01 s, find the average force exerted by wall on ball

Solutions:

  1. KE_initial = ½(0.06)(20)² = 12 J
  2. KE_final = ½(0.06)(20)² = 12 J
  3. ΔKE = 0 J (elastic collision conserves KE)
  4. p_initial = 0.06 × 20 = 1.2 kg·m/s
  5. p_final = 0.06 × (-20) = -1.2 kg·m/s
  6. Δp = -1.2 - 1.2 = -2.4 kg·m/s (change in momentum)
  7. KE depends on v² (magnitude squared), so it's same regardless of direction. But momentum is a vector, so direction matters.
  8. F·Δt = Δp, so F = -2.4/0.01 = -240 N (negative = toward ball)

Quick Reference: Cheat Sheet

Work: W = F·d cos θ = F⃗·d⃗

Unit: Joule (J). Positive when force aids motion.

Kinetic Energy: KE = ½mv²

Energy due to motion. Depends on v².

Work-Energy Theorem: W_net = ΔKE = KE_f - KE_i

Net work equals change in kinetic energy.

Variable Force Work: W = ∫ F(x)dx

Area under F-x graph.

Spring Potential Energy: U_spring = ½kx²

k = spring constant, x = displacement from equilibrium.

Gravitational Potential Energy: U_grav = mgh

h = height above reference level.

Force from Potential Energy: F = -dU/dx

Force is negative gradient of PE.

Conservation of Mechanical Energy: E = KE + PE = constant ½mv₁² + U₁ = ½mv₂² + U₂

Valid only for conservative forces (no friction).

Power: P = W/t P = F⃗·v⃗ = Fv cos θ

Unit: Watt (W) = J/s. 1 HP = 746 W.

Mass-Energy Equivalence: E = mc²

Tiny mass → enormous energy.

Elastic Collision (1D): v₁' = ((m₁-m₂)/(m₁+m₂))v₁ + (2m₂/(m₁+m₂))v₂ v₂' = (2m₁/(m₁+m₂))v₁ + ((m₂-m₁)/(m₁+m₂))v₂

Both momentum and KE conserved.

Perfectly Inelastic Collision: v' = (m₁v₁ + m₂v₂)/(m₁ + m₂)

Objects stick together. Only momentum conserved.

Coefficient of Restitution: e = (v₂' - v₁')/(v₁ - v₂)

e = 1 (elastic), 0 < e < 1 (inelastic), e = 0 (perfectly inelastic)

Conservation of Momentum: m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = m₁v₁' + m₂v₂' (always)

Always true in collisions, regardless of type.

Important Special Cases:
  • Equal mass elastic collision (one at rest): Velocities exchange
  • Much heavier object hits light one: Light rebounds, heavy continues
  • Light object hits heavy one: Light bounces back, heavy barely moves
Energy Forms:

Mechanical (KE + PE), Heat, Chemical, Electrical, Nuclear, Radiant

Quick Calculation Tips:
  • For free fall: v² = 2gh (from energy conservation)
  • Stopping distance proportional to v²
  • Power = Force × Velocity (when force parallel to motion)
  • Work-energy approach often faster than kinematics
