Chapter 4: Moving Charges and Magnetism

1. Magnetic Force on Moving Charge

When a charged particle moves in a magnetic field, it experiences a force perpendicular to both its velocity and the magnetic field. This forms the foundation of electromagnetism.

Magnetic Force (Lorentz Force)

The force on a moving charged particle in a magnetic field is given by the Lorentz force equation:

F = q(E + v × B)

Where:

  • F = Force vector (Newtons)
  • q = Charge (Coulombs)
  • E = Electric field (V/m)
  • v = Velocity of charge (m/s)
  • B = Magnetic field (Tesla)
  • × = Cross product

Magnetic Force Only (When E = 0)

F = qv × B or F = qvB sin(θ)

The force is maximum when θ = 90° (v perpendicular to B) and zero when θ = 0° (v parallel to B).

Key Properties:
  • Magnetic force is always perpendicular to velocity
  • Magnetic force does no work (velocity magnitude remains constant)
  • Direction determined by right-hand rule: fingers point along v, curl towards B, thumb points along F
  • Force is zero for stationary charges

2. Motion in Magnetic Field

Circular Motion

When a charged particle enters perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, it undergoes circular motion with the Lorentz force providing centripetal force.

qvB = mv²/r Radius: r = mv/(qB)

Radius of circular path depends on:

  • Mass of particle (m) - directly proportional
  • Velocity (v) - directly proportional
  • Charge (q) - inversely proportional
  • Magnetic field (B) - inversely proportional

Period and Frequency

Period: T = 2πm/(qB) Frequency: f = qB/(2πm) Angular Frequency: ω = qB/m (Cyclotron frequency)

Note: Period and frequency are independent of velocity and radius!

Helical Motion

When a particle has both perpendicular and parallel velocity components to B:

  • Perpendicular component causes circular motion
  • Parallel component remains unchanged (no force in that direction)
  • Results in helical/spiral path
Pitch = v_parallel × T = v_parallel × 2πm/(qB) Radius of helix = m(v_perpendicular)/(qB)

Cyclotron

A device that accelerates charged particles to high energies using alternating electric fields in a magnetic field.

Cyclotron Frequency: ω = qB/m Resonance Condition: Applied AC frequency = qB/(2πm)

Working Principle:

  • Particle spirals outward as it gains energy
  • Frequency of AC voltage matches cyclotron frequency for synchronization
  • Each time particle crosses gap, it gains energy qV
  • Radius increases with each acceleration: r = √(2mKE)/(qB)
Circular motion: Center at instantaneous center of curvature
Helical motion: Combination of circular and linear paths
Cyclotron: Particle spirals outward in magnetic field with RF acceleration

3. Biot-Savart Law and Magnetic Field Calculations

Biot-Savart Law

The fundamental law for calculating magnetic field due to current-carrying conductors:

dB = (μ₀I/4π) × (dl × r̂)/r² or in magnitude: dB = (μ₀I/4π) × (sin(θ)/r²) dl

Where:

  • μ₀ = Permeability of free space = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A
  • I = Current (Amperes)
  • dl = Current element vector
  • r = Distance from element to field point
  • r̂ = Unit vector along r
  • θ = Angle between dl and r
Direction (Right-Hand Rule): Point thumb along current direction, curl fingers - direction of curl gives magnetic field direction.

Magnetic Field of Straight Wire

B = (μ₀I/2πr)

For a long straight current-carrying wire:

  • Field magnitude depends only on current and distance
  • Field lines form concentric circles around the wire
  • Field is inversely proportional to distance (1/r relationship)

Magnetic Field of Circular Loop

At center: B_center = μ₀I/(2R) At axial point (distance x from center): B = μ₀IR²/(2(R² + x²)^(3/2))

Special Cases:

  • At center (x = 0): B = μ₀I/(2R)
  • Far away (x >> R): B ≈ μ₀2πR²I/(4πx³) = μ₀(2m)/(4πx³) where m = πR²I
  • Field on axis is maximum at center and decreases symmetrically
Straight wire: Circular field lines around wire
Circular loop: Field stronger at center, weaker at ends
Superposition: Add individual dB vectors using Biot-Savart

4. Ampere's Circuital Law

Ampere's Law Statement

The line integral of magnetic field around a closed loop equals μ₀ times the enclosed current:

∮ B·dl = μ₀I_enclosed

Where:

  • ∮ B·dl = Line integral around closed loop
  • μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A
  • I_enclosed = Total current passing through loop
Application Conditions:
  • Symmetry is essential for easy application
  • Choose Amperian loop matching field geometry
  • Field must be tangent to loop at constant magnitude
  • Can't be used if field varies unpredictably

Magnetic Field Inside a Solenoid

A solenoid is a coil of wire wound tightly in a helical pattern:

Inside solenoid: B = μ₀nI Outside solenoid: B ≈ 0

Where:

  • n = Number of turns per unit length (turns/meter)
  • I = Current (Amperes)

Derivation using Ampere's Law:

  • Choose rectangular Amperian loop with one side inside and one outside
  • Only inside contribution matters (B·dl on inside, 0 on outside)
  • ∮ B·dl = B·(length inside) = Bl
  • Enclosed current = nIl, so B = μ₀nI

Magnetic Field in a Toroid

A toroid is a solenoid bent into a doughnut shape:

Inside toroid at radius r: B = μ₀NI/(2πr) Outside toroid: B = 0

Where:

  • N = Total number of turns
  • r = Distance from center of toroid

Note: Field varies with radius (unlike solenoid where it's uniform)

Solenoid: Uniform field inside (parallel lines), zero outside
Toroid: Circular field lines, non-uniform magnitude
Amperian loop: Choose circle for toroid, rectangle for solenoid

5. Force Between Parallel Currents and Definition of Ampere

Force Between Two Parallel Wires

Two parallel current-carrying wires exert magnetic forces on each other:

Force per unit length: F/L = (μ₀I₁I₂)/(2πd)

Where:

  • F = Force (Newtons)
  • L = Length of wire (meters)
  • I₁, I₂ = Currents in wires (Amperes)
  • d = Distance between wires (meters)

Direction:

  • Parallel currents attract each other
  • Antiparallel currents repel each other

Definition of Ampere (SI Unit of Current)

The ampere is defined based on the force between parallel currents:

One ampere is the current that, when flowing in two parallel wires separated by 1 meter, produces a force of 2 × 10⁻⁷ N per meter of length.

Mathematical Definition:

F/L = 2 × 10⁻⁷ (μ₀I₁I₂)/(2πd) = 2 × 10⁻⁷ when I₁ = I₂ = 1 A and d = 1 m This gives: μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A
Fundamental Importance:
  • This definition links mechanical force to electrical current
  • Historically crucial for standardizing electrical measurements
  • Forms basis of electromagnetic unit systems
  • Used in precision current measurement

6. Torque on Current Loop in Magnetic Field

Torque Formula

A current-carrying loop experiences a torque when placed in a magnetic field:

τ = nIAB sin(θ) or τ = m × B

Where:

  • τ = Torque (N·m)
  • n = Number of turns
  • I = Current (Amperes)
  • A = Area of loop (m²)
  • B = Magnetic field strength (Tesla)
  • θ = Angle between normal to plane and B field
  • m = nIA = Magnetic moment

Magnetic Moment

Magnetic moment: m = nIA Direction: Perpendicular to loop plane (right-hand rule)

Physical Meaning:

  • Quantifies strength and orientation of current loop's magnetic property
  • Similar to electric dipole moment for current loops
  • Unit: A·m² (Ampere-meter squared)

Torque Analysis

Maximum and Minimum Torque:

  • Maximum: τ_max = nIAB when θ = 90° (plane parallel to B)
  • Zero: τ = 0 when θ = 0° or 180° (plane perpendicular to B)

Equilibrium Position:

  • Stable equilibrium when θ = 0° (magnetic moment aligned with field)
  • Unstable equilibrium when θ = 180° (moment opposite to field)

Work Done:

W = -m·B = -mB cos(θ) Potential Energy: U = -m·B·cos(θ)
Vector Form:
  • τ = m × B (torque is cross product of moment and field)
  • Tendency to align magnetic moment with field
  • Same principle used in electric motors

7. Moving Coil Galvanometer

Principle and Construction

A galvanometer is a sensitive instrument for detecting and measuring small electric currents, based on torque experienced by a current loop in a magnetic field.

Main Components:

  • Rectangular coil: Mounted on a frame in a radial magnetic field
  • Permanent magnet: Provides uniform magnetic field (typically pole pieces create radial field)
  • Spring: Provides restoring torque (torsional spring or suspension wire)
  • Pointer and scale: Indicates deflection
  • Damping: Air damping or magnetic damping prevents oscillation

Working Principle

Deflecting torque: τ_d = nIAB Restoring torque: τ_r = k·θ (where k is spring constant, θ is angle) At equilibrium: nIAB = k·θ Therefore: θ = (nAB/k)·I

Mechanism:

  • Current flows through coil placed in radial magnetic field
  • Two vertical sides of coil experience opposite forces (F = BIL)
  • Forces create clockwise/counterclockwise torque
  • Coil rotates until spring restoring torque balances magnetic torque
  • Deflection angle is proportional to current

Sensitivity

Sensitivity: S = θ/I = nAB/k or S = 1/I_g where I_g is galvanometer constant (full-scale deflection current)

Factors Increasing Sensitivity:

  • Increase number of turns (n)
  • Increase coil area (A)
  • Increase magnetic field strength (B)
  • Decrease spring constant (k) - weaker spring

Conversion to Ammeter

An ammeter measures current. To convert galvanometer to ammeter, connect a shunt resistance in parallel:

Shunt resistance: S = (I_g·G)/(I - I_g) = G/(n - 1) where n = I/I_g = multiplying factor G = galvanometer resistance

Purpose:

  • Most current bypasses galvanometer through shunt
  • Only fraction of total current goes through galvanometer
  • Protects delicate galvanometer from large currents
  • Allows measurement of larger currents

Conversion to Voltmeter

A voltmeter measures potential difference. To convert galvanometer to voltmeter, connect a high resistance (multiplier) in series:

Multiplier resistance: R = (V - V_g)/I_g = (V/I_g) - G where V = desired full-scale voltage V_g = galvanometer full-scale voltage = I_g·G

Purpose:

  • High resistance limits current through galvanometer
  • Allows measurement of higher voltages
  • Protects galvanometer from excessive current
  • Voltmeter has high resistance (approximately 1000 Ω per Volt)
Property Galvanometer Ammeter Voltmeter
Function Detect current Measure current Measure voltage
Connection Series Series Parallel
Internal Resistance ~100 Ω (moderate) Very low (~0.01 Ω) Very high (~1000 Ω)
Modification None Shunt in parallel Multiplier in series
Ideal Property Zero resistance Zero resistance Infinite resistance
Important Design Considerations:
  • Shunt resistance should be low to minimize voltage drop
  • Multiplier resistance should be high to limit current
  • Damping prevents overshooting and oscillations
  • Radial magnetic field ensures constant field strength near coil

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

HOTS-1: Cyclotron Paradox

Why doesn't a particle accelerate indefinitely in a cyclotron? If the frequency of the applied AC voltage doesn't match the cyclotron frequency, what happens? Derive the condition for continuous acceleration and explain what happens when particles approach relativistic speeds.

Answer Hints: Non-relativistic derivation assumes constant mass. At relativistic speeds, effective mass increases, so ω = qB/m changes. This causes particles to go out of phase with the accelerating voltage. Maximum kinetic energy: KE_max = q²B²R²/(2m_eff)

HOTS-2: Toroid vs Solenoid Design

Why is a toroid more efficient than a solenoid for certain applications? Analyze the magnetic field distribution, energy storage, and leakage. For the same number of turns and current, how do the fields compare at different radial distances?

Answer Hints: Solenoid: uniform field inside, total energy = (B²/2μ₀)·A·l. Toroid: non-uniform field B∝1/r, energy stored is concentrated near the core. Toroid has no field leakage outside, making it suitable for transformers and inductors.

HOTS-3: Magnetic Focusing and Deflection

In cathode ray tubes and mass spectrometers, magnetic fields deflect charged particles. Design a system where different masses separate while all having the same kinetic energy. How would you arrange the magnetic field for maximum separation? What happens to the trajectory if the field is non-uniform?

Answer Hints: Circular motion with r = √(2mKE)/(qB). For same KE: r ∝ √m. Stronger B gives smaller r (deflection). Non-uniform fields cause complex trajectories; radial field approximation used in mass spectrometers.

Case-Based Questions (Application Scenarios)

Case Study 1: Earth's Magnetic Field and Aurora

Solar wind particles (electrons and protons) enter Earth's magnetic field with velocities ~400 km/s. The dipole field strength near poles is ~60 μT, and near equator ~30 μT. Explain how particles follow helical paths, why the aurora occurs near poles, and calculate the gyroradius of a 1 keV electron in Earth's field.

Calculation: For electron: KE = 1 keV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁶ J, v = √(2KE/m) ≈ 1.88 × 10⁷ m/s. At equator B = 30 μT: r = mv/(eB) = (9.11×10⁻³¹ × 1.88×10⁷)/(1.6×10⁻¹⁹ × 30×10⁻⁶) ≈ 35.5 m

Case Study 2: Tokamak Fusion Reactor Design

In a tokamak, hot plasma (100 million K) is confined by a toroidal magnetic field (5-10 T) and a poloidal field. Explain how magnetic confinement works. If a deuteron (q = e, m ≈ 2m_p) has thermal energy 3kT/2, calculate its gyroradius at 5 T (assume T = 10⁸ K).

Key Concept: Particles follow helical paths along field lines. Collision-driven transport is minimized by large gyroradius compared to device size. Higher B and T increase confinement time.

Case Study 3: Hall Effect Sensor Applications

A Hall effect sensor uses the deflection of charge carriers by a magnetic field perpendicular to current flow. In a semiconductor strip carrying current I = 10 A with B = 0.1 T perpendicular, explain the physics and calculate the Hall voltage for carrier density n = 10²⁸ m⁻³ and strip width w = 5 mm.

Formula: Hall coefficient R_H = 1/(ne). Hall voltage V_H = (R_H × I × B)/d where d is thickness. Applications: current sensing, position detection, magnetic field measurement.

Summary

Chapter 4 on Moving Charges and Magnetism covers the fundamental principles of electromagnetism and their practical applications. Key concepts include:

  • The Lorentz force law describing magnetic force on moving charges
  • Circular and helical motion of charged particles in magnetic fields
  • Fundamental laws for calculating magnetic fields (Biot-Savart and Ampere's laws)
  • Practical configurations like solenoids and toroids
  • Forces between current-carrying conductors
  • Torque on current loops and magnetic moments
  • Moving coil galvanometers and their conversion to ammeters and voltmeters

These principles form the foundation for numerous technological applications from power generation to medical imaging and particle acceleration.
