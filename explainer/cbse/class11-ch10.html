Chapter 10: Thermal Properties of Matter - Complete Study Guide

Thermal Properties of Matter

Understanding heat, temperature, thermal expansion, calorimetry, phase changes, and heat transfer mechanisms. This chapter explores how matter responds to temperature changes and how thermal energy is exchanged between systems and surroundings.

Why This Matters?

Why do metal railway tracks have gaps? Why does boiling water feel hot but steam can scald you worse? Why is water unique—denser at 4°C than at 0°C? These phenomena are explained through thermal properties of matter. Understanding thermal expansion, specific heat, latent heat, and heat transfer is crucial for engineering applications, meteorology, climate science, cooking, refrigeration, and even understanding why the Earth's climate is changing. This chapter forms the foundation for thermodynamics.

1. Temperature & Heat: Thermal Equilibrium & Scales

Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

If two systems are separately in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This fundamental principle allows us to define temperature and use thermometers.

Thermal Equilibrium

Two systems reach thermal equilibrium when they have the same temperature and no net heat flow occurs between them. At the molecular level, the average kinetic energy of molecules is equal.

Temperature Scales

Celsius to Kelvin: T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15
Celsius to Fahrenheit: T(°F) = (9/5)T(°C) + 32
Fahrenheit to Kelvin: T(K) = (T(°F) - 32) × 5/9 + 273.15

Kelvin (K): SI unit; absolute zero is 0 K (−273.15°C). Used in scientific calculations.

Celsius (°C): Water freezes at 0°C, boils at 100°C at 1 atm pressure.

Fahrenheit (°F): Water freezes at 32°F, boils at 212°F. Mainly used in USA.

Example: Convert 25°C to Kelvin and Fahrenheit.
T(K) = 25 + 273.15 = 298.15 K
T(°F) = (9/5) × 25 + 32 = 45 + 32 = 77°F

Heat vs Temperature

Temperature: Measure of average kinetic energy of molecules (intensive property). Measured in K, °C, or °F.

Heat (Q): Transfer of thermal energy between systems due to temperature difference (extensive property). Measured in Joules (J) or Calories (cal). 1 cal = 4.186 J.

2. Thermal Expansion: Linear, Area, and Volume

Most substances expand when heated. The change in dimensions depends on the temperature change and the material's coefficient of thermal expansion.

Linear Thermal Expansion

ΔL = αL₀ΔT
L = L₀(1 + αΔT)

where α is the coefficient of linear thermal expansion, L₀ is the original length, and ΔT is the temperature change.

Coefficient of Linear Expansion (α): Represents the fractional change in length per degree temperature change. Units: K⁻¹ or °C⁻¹.

Example: An iron rod of length 1 m at 20°C is heated to 70°C. If α = 12 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹, find the change in length.
ΔL = αL₀ΔT = 12 × 10⁻⁶ × 1 × (70 - 20) = 12 × 10⁻⁶ × 50 = 6 × 10⁻⁴ m = 0.6 mm

Area (Superficial) Thermal Expansion

ΔA = 2αA₀ΔT
β = 2α (Coefficient of area expansion)
A = A₀(1 + βΔT)

Volume (Cubical) Thermal Expansion

ΔV = 3αV₀ΔT
γ = 3α (Coefficient of volume expansion)
V = V₀(1 + γΔT)

Anomalous Expansion of Water

Water exhibits anomalous (abnormal) expansion behavior:

  • Water contracts from 0°C to 4°C (negative coefficient of expansion)
  • Maximum density occurs at 4°C (1 g/mL)
  • From 4°C to 0°C and above 4°C, water expands (positive coefficient)
  • Ice (frozen water) is less dense than water, so it floats
  • This prevents water bodies from freezing solid, allowing aquatic life to survive
Key Point: Thermal expansion causes stress in materials. This is why railway tracks have expansion gaps, concrete has gaps, and why bimetallic strips work in thermostats.

3. Specific Heat Capacity: Energy & Temperature Change

Specific heat capacity is the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C).

Heat Energy Formula

Q = mcΔT

where:

  • Q = heat energy absorbed or released (Joules)
  • m = mass of substance (kg)
  • c = specific heat capacity (J/kg·K)
  • ΔT = change in temperature (K or °C)

Specific Heat Capacity of Common Substances

Substance Specific Heat (J/kg·K) Notes
Water 4186 Highest among common substances; slow to heat/cool
Ice 2100 Less than water
Steam 1400 Gas phase; lower capacity
Aluminum 900 Metal; good heat conductor
Iron 450 Denser metal
Copper 385 Excellent conductor
Sand 840 Heats quickly; useful in deserts analogy

Why Water Has High Specific Heat?

Water molecules form hydrogen bonds, requiring significant energy to break. This means water can absorb or release large amounts of heat with small temperature changes—crucial for climate regulation.

Molar Specific Heat

C = Mc
where C is molar specific heat (J/mol·K) and M is molar mass (g/mol)
Example: How much heat is needed to raise the temperature of 2 kg of water from 20°C to 80°C?
Q = mcΔT = 2 × 4186 × (80 - 20) = 2 × 4186 × 60 = 502,320 J ≈ 502.3 kJ

4. Calorimetry: Heat Exchange Principle

Calorimetry is the measurement of heat released or absorbed during physical or chemical processes. It's based on the principle of conservation of energy.

Principle of Calorimetry

Heat lost by hot objects = Heat gained by cold objects
Q_released = Q_absorbed

In an isolated system (calorimeter), total heat change is zero:

ΣQ = 0
m₁c₁(T₁ - T_final) = m₂c₂(T_final - T₂)

Calorimeter (Heat Capacity)

A calorimeter is an insulated container used to measure heat exchanges. Real calorimeters have finite heat capacity (water equivalent), meaning the calorimeter itself absorbs/releases heat.

Q_lost = Q_absorbed + Q_calorimeter
m₁c₁ΔT₁ = m₂c₂ΔT₂ + m_cal × c_cal × ΔT_cal

Water Equivalent

The heat capacity of a calorimeter is often expressed as its "water equivalent"—the mass of water that would have the same heat capacity.

Example: 500 g of iron at 100°C is dropped into 200 g of water at 25°C. If c_iron = 450 J/kg·K and c_water = 4186 J/kg·K, find the final temperature (assume no heat loss to surroundings).
Heat lost by iron = Heat gained by water
0.5 × 450 × (100 - T_f) = 0.2 × 4186 × (T_f - 25)
225(100 - T_f) = 837.2(T_f - 25)
22500 - 225T_f = 837.2T_f - 20,930
43,430 = 1062.2T_f
T_f ≈ 40.9°C
Key Application: Calorimetry is used to determine calorific values of fuels, specific heat capacities of unknown substances, and heat released in chemical reactions.

5. Change of State: Melting, Boiling & Latent Heat

A change of state is the transformation from one phase (solid, liquid, gas) to another. This occurs at constant temperature but requires/releases significant energy (latent heat).

Latent Heat

Latent heat is the amount of heat required to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without changing its temperature. It represents energy used to break/form intermolecular bonds.

Q = mL
where L is latent heat (J/kg)

Types of Latent Heat

  • Latent Heat of Fusion (L_f): Heat needed to convert solid to liquid at constant temperature.
  • Latent Heat of Vaporization (L_v): Heat needed to convert liquid to gas at constant temperature.
  • Latent Heat of Sublimation (L_s): Heat needed to convert solid directly to gas (rare, but dry ice does this).

Latent Heat Values for Water (Standard Values)

Process Latent Heat Value at 1 atm
Melting (ice → water) L_f (fusion) 334 kJ/kg (334,000 J/kg)
Freezing (water → ice) −L_f −334 kJ/kg
Vaporization (water → steam) L_v 2256 kJ/kg (2,256,000 J/kg)
Condensation (steam → water) −L_v −2256 kJ/kg
Sublimation (ice → vapor) L_s 2590 kJ/kg

Phase Diagram & Melting/Boiling Points

Melting Point: Temperature at which solid converts to liquid (at constant pressure). For water: 0°C at 1 atm.

Boiling Point: Temperature at which liquid converts to gas (vapor pressure = atmospheric pressure). For water: 100°C at 1 atm.

Effect of Pressure on Melting & Boiling Points

  • Melting Point: For most substances, increases with pressure. Exception: water (ice melts under pressure due to anomalous expansion).
  • Boiling Point: Increases with pressure. At higher pressure, liquid molecules need more energy (higher temperature) to escape to gas phase.

Regelation

The phenomenon where ice melts under pressure and refreezes when pressure is removed. This is why ice skating works—the blade creates localized pressure that melts the ice surface.

Triple Point

The unique condition where solid, liquid, and gas phases of a substance exist in equilibrium. For water: 0.01°C (273.16 K) at 611.7 Pa.

Clausius-Clapeyron Equation (Advanced)

Describes how vapor pressure changes with temperature, determining boiling point at different pressures.

Example: How much heat is needed to convert 500 g of ice at 0°C to water at 0°C?
Q = mL_f = 0.5 × 334,000 = 167,000 J = 167 kJ

How much heat to vaporize 100 g of water at 100°C?
Q = mL_v = 0.1 × 2,256,000 = 225,600 J = 225.6 kJ
Key Point: Vaporization requires much more heat than melting (for water: L_v ≈ 6.75 × L_f), explaining why steam burns are more severe than contact with hot water.

6. Heat Transfer: Conduction, Convection & Radiation

Heat transfer is the movement of thermal energy from regions of higher temperature to lower temperature. Three mechanisms: conduction, convection, and radiation.

A. Thermal Conduction

Heat transfer through a material without bulk motion of the material. Occurs in solids, liquids, and gases when there's a temperature difference.

Fourier's Law of Thermal Conduction

Q/t = K × A × (T₁ - T₂) / L
H = KA(T₁ - T₂)/L (Heat Transfer Rate)

where:

  • Q/t or H = heat transfer rate (watts, W = J/s)
  • K = thermal conductivity (W/m·K)
  • A = cross-sectional area (m²)
  • T₁ - T₂ = temperature difference (K)
  • L = thickness of material (m)

Thermal Conductivity (K) of Common Materials

Material Thermal Conductivity (W/m·K) Classification
Silver 430 Excellent conductor
Copper 400 Excellent conductor
Aluminum 237 Good conductor
Iron 79 Moderate conductor
Brick 0.7 Poor conductor (insulator)
Wood 0.12 Poor conductor (insulator)
Air (still) 0.024 Very poor (excellent insulator)
Water 0.6 Poor (liquid)

Thermal Resistance

R = L / (K × A)
H = ΔT / R

Series Thermal Resistance: For multiple layers:

R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
H = ΔT_total / (R₁ + R₂ + ...)

Parallel Thermal Resistance: For parallel paths:

1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ...
Example: A copper rod of length 1 m and cross-sectional area 10 cm² has one end at 100°C and the other at 0°C. Find the heat transfer rate. (K_copper = 400 W/m·K)
H = KA(T₁ - T₂)/L = 400 × (10 × 10⁻⁴) × (100 - 0) / 1
H = 400 × 0.001 × 100 = 40 W

B. Thermal Convection

Heat transfer through bulk motion of fluids (liquids or gases). Molecules move from one location to another, carrying thermal energy.

Types of Convection

  • Natural Convection: Movement due to density differences caused by temperature variations. Hot fluid rises, cool fluid sinks. Example: hot air rising in a room.
  • Forced Convection: Movement driven by external means (fans, pumps). Example: air conditioning systems, car radiators.

Newton's Law of Cooling (Convection)

H = hA(T_surface - T_fluid)
where h is convection heat transfer coefficient (W/m²·K)

Convection Examples in Nature

  • Sea Breezes: Land heats faster than ocean. Hot air over land rises, cool ocean air rushes in (sea breeze). At night, reverse occurs (land breeze).
  • Atmospheric Circulation: Convection drives global wind patterns.
  • Ocean Currents: Temperature differences drive circulation.

C. Thermal Radiation

Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves without requiring a medium. All objects at temperature above absolute zero emit radiation.

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

E = σAT⁴
where σ = 5.67 × 10⁻⁸ W/m²·K⁴ (Stefan-Boltzmann constant)

Power radiated is proportional to the fourth power of absolute temperature!

Wien's Displacement Law

λ_max × T = b
where b = 2.898 × 10⁻³ m·K (Wien's constant)

The wavelength of maximum emission intensity is inversely proportional to temperature. As temperature increases, the peak shifts to shorter wavelengths (bluer colors).

Newton's Law of Cooling (Radiation)

dT/dt = -k(T - T₀)
or T(t) = T₀ + (T_initial - T₀)e^(-kt)

For cooling bodies in air, the rate of temperature decrease is proportional to the temperature difference with surroundings. Valid when convection and radiation are important.

Blackbody Radiation

Blackbody: An ideal surface that absorbs all incident radiation and emits the maximum possible radiation at each temperature. Real objects have lower emissivity (ε):

E = εσAT⁴
where ε is emissivity (0 < ε ≤ 1)
  • Black surface: ε ≈ 1 (absorbs/emits well)
  • White surface: ε ≈ 0 (reflects, poor absorber)
  • Shiny metal: ε ≈ 0.1 (poor radiator)

Radiation Examples

  • The Sun: Radiates as a blackbody with T ≈ 5800 K. Peak emission in visible range.
  • Earth: Receives solar radiation; radiates in infrared. Greenhouse gases trap this radiation.
  • Thermal Cameras: Detect infrared radiation.
  • Night Vision: Infrared heat detection.
Example: A blackbody at 5800 K radiates from a surface area of 1 m². Find the total power radiated.
E = σAT⁴ = 5.67 × 10⁻⁸ × 1 × (5800)⁴ = 5.67 × 10⁻⁸ × 1.136 × 10¹⁵ = 6.44 × 10⁷ W

Find the wavelength of maximum emission.
λ_max = b / T = 2.898 × 10⁻³ / 5800 = 5.0 × 10⁻⁷ m = 500 nm (green light)

Comparison of Heat Transfer Methods

Method Medium Required Mechanism Speed
Conduction Yes (direct contact) Molecular vibration/electron movement Slow in most materials
Convection Yes (fluid required) Bulk fluid movement Moderate to fast
Radiation No (vacuum possible) Electromagnetic waves Instant (speed of light)

Quick Concept Quizzes

Q1: What is the temperature in Kelvin if it's 25°C?
Answer: B) 298.15 K
T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15 = 25 + 273.15 = 298.15 K
Q2: A rod expands by 2 mm when heated from 20°C to 120°C. If original length was 1 m, what is the coefficient of linear expansion?
Answer: A) 2 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹
ΔL = αL₀ΔT → 2×10⁻³ = α × 1 × (120-20)
α = 2×10⁻³ / 100 = 2 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹
Q3: Which substance has the highest specific heat capacity among those commonly used?
Answer: C) Water (4186 J/kg·K)
Water has the highest specific heat capacity due to hydrogen bonding, making it ideal for thermal regulation and cooling systems.
Q4: The latent heat of vaporization of water is 2256 kJ/kg. How much heat is needed to convert 100 g of water at 100°C to steam at 100°C?
Answer: A) 225.6 kJ
Q = mL_v = 0.1 × 2256 = 225.6 kJ
Q5: Which heat transfer method does NOT require a medium?
Answer: C) Radiation
Radiation transfers heat through electromagnetic waves and can occur in a vacuum. Conduction requires direct contact, convection requires fluid movement.
Q6: A blackbody at temperature T radiates power E. If temperature is doubled to 2T, what happens to the radiated power?
Answer: C) Power increases 16-fold (16E)
Stefan-Boltzmann law: E = σAT⁴. If T → 2T, then E_new = σA(2T)⁴ = 16σAT⁴ = 16E. Power is proportional to T⁴.

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) & Case-Based Questions

HOTS-1: Explain why water at 4°C is denser than water at 0°C and 8°C. What are the ecological implications of this anomalous expansion?

Answer:

Water exhibits anomalous (negative) thermal expansion between 0°C and 4°C. This occurs because:

  • Below 4°C, hydrogen bonding creates a crystalline-like structure with more space between molecules.
  • The density decreases as temperature decreases from 4°C to 0°C.
  • Maximum density occurs at 4°C, where hydrogen bonding is optimally balanced.

Ecological Implications:

  • Lakes freeze from the top down, creating an insulating ice layer (less dense ice floats).
  • Aquatic organisms can survive winter under the ice in liquid water below 4°C.
  • If ice were denser than water, water bodies would freeze solid, killing most aquatic life.
  • This is a critical adaptation that allows life in temperate regions.
HOTS-2: Design a thermal insulation system for a spacecraft. Discuss the roles of conduction, convection, and radiation. Why is a vacuum-based insulation most effective?

Answer:

In space, a spacecraft experiences extreme temperature variations (sunlit side: +120°C, shadow side: −100°C). An effective insulation design must minimize heat transfer through all three methods:

  • Conduction Barrier: Use materials with low thermal conductivity (K < 0.1 W/m·K) like foam, aerogel, or multi-layer insulation.
  • Convection Elimination: Convection cannot occur in vacuum (no fluid), so this is automatically eliminated in space.
  • Radiation Control: Use reflective coatings on outer surface (high emissivity absorbers outside, low emissivity reflectors inside) to reduce radiative heat transfer using Stefan-Boltzmann law.
  • Vacuum Benefit: Vacuum eliminates conduction and convection pathways. Only radiation remains, making it the dominant heat loss mechanism that must be controlled with reflective surfaces.
  • Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI): Alternating layers of reflective material and low-conductivity spacers in vacuum provide maximum insulation.

This design principle is crucial for satellite and spacecraft thermal management.

HOTS-3: A thermos flask is designed with vacuum between two walls. Explain how this design minimizes heat loss and why it's more effective than just using a material with low thermal conductivity.

Answer:

A thermos (vacuum flask) uses vacuum insulation, which is superior to solid materials because:

  • Conduction Elimination: Vacuum has no atoms/molecules to conduct heat. Even excellent insulators like wood (K = 0.12) or air (K = 0.024) still allow some conduction. Vacuum eliminates this entirely.
  • Convection Elimination: No fluid means no convection currents. Solid materials cannot prevent convection if they're porous.
  • Radiation Minimization: Reflective coatings on the vacuum walls reflect radiation back, reducing radiative heat transfer (follows Stefan-Boltzmann law with reduced emissivity).
  • Comparison: A solid material insulator must allow some heat conduction through it. A vacuum barrier has zero thermal conductivity (K = 0) through the vacuum region itself.
  • Effectiveness: Thermos flasks can keep hot drinks hot for hours (or cold drinks cold) because the vacuum essentially creates an impenetrable thermal barrier. Only radiation at the thin glass seals and contact points allows minimal heat escape.

Real-World Benefit: A modern vacuum flask can keep coffee hot for 24+ hours, demonstrating the power of eliminating conduction and convection pathways.

Case-Based-1: Urban Heat Island Effect. In a city, the temperature is often 5-10°C higher than surrounding rural areas. Explain this using concepts of thermal properties, specific heat capacity, and heat transfer mechanisms.

Answer:

The urban heat island (UHI) effect results from multiple thermal factors:

Role of Materials and Specific Heat:

  • Cities replace vegetation (high specific heat, evaporative cooling) with concrete and asphalt (lower specific heat, high thermal conductivity).
  • Concrete has c ≈ 840 J/kg·K (vs. grass/soil with evaporation effects). This means concrete heats quickly under sunlight but cannot shed heat as effectively through evaporation.
  • Dark asphalt has high emissivity and absorbs more solar radiation (follows Stefan-Boltzmann law with high emissivity).

Heat Transfer Mechanisms:

  • Radiation Absorption: Dark urban surfaces absorb more solar radiation. High emissivity (ε ≈ 0.95) means they absorb and re-radiate heat efficiently, heating the air.
  • Reduced Convection Cooling: Buildings block wind, reducing forced convection cooling. Natural convection is less effective in dense urban areas.
  • Reduced Evaporation: Loss of vegetation and water bodies reduces latent heat loss through evaporation. Trees have high specific heat and lose heat through transpiration (Q = mL for phase change of water vapor).
  • Heat from Activities: Vehicle engines, air conditioning units, and human activities release additional heat (anthropogenic heat).

Mitigation Strategies:

  • Use reflective (white) roofs and pavements (low emissivity) to reduce radiation absorption.
  • Plant trees and vegetation to increase evaporative cooling and reduce direct heating.
  • Create water bodies and green spaces (high specific heat, evaporative cooling).
  • Improve air circulation (forced convection cooling).
Case-Based-2: Industrial Boiler Efficiency. A factory uses a boiler to heat water for steam production. Heat is lost through the boiler walls via conduction, convection, and radiation. Design improvements to minimize energy waste and explain using thermal formulas.

Answer:

Boiler efficiency is critical for industrial operations. Heat loss minimization involves all three transfer mechanisms:

Conduction Loss Prevention:

  • Current Issue: Q/t = KA(T_inside - T_outside)/L. Reduce this by:
  • Increase insulation thickness (L), using materials with low K (e.g., fiberglass K ≈ 0.04 W/m·K instead of bare steel K ≈ 50 W/m·K).
  • Formula shows Q/t ∝ 1/L, so doubling insulation thickness halves conduction loss.
  • Use composite insulation: rock wool (K ≈ 0.04) + outer reflective layer.

Radiation Loss Prevention:

  • Blackbody radiation loss: E = εσAT⁴. Reduce by:
  • Use low-emissivity reflective coatings (ε ≈ 0.1) on outer surfaces instead of bare metal (ε ≈ 0.95).
  • Reducing surface emissivity by 10× reduces radiation loss by 10×.
  • For high-temperature boilers (T = 500 K), radiation becomes significant: E = ε × 5.67×10⁻⁸ × A × (500)⁴.

Convection Loss Prevention:

  • Reduce convection: H = hA(T_surface - T_air). Minimize by:
  • Install wind barriers or covers to reduce forced convection coefficient h.
  • Improve insulation to keep surface temperature low (T_surface ↓).
  • Natural convection (h ≈ 5-25 W/m²·K) < Forced convection (h ≈ 25-250 W/m²·K), so still significant.

Design Recommendations:

  • Install 10 cm thick insulation (e.g., mineral wool) instead of 2 cm → reduces conduction by 5×.
  • Apply reflective ceramic coating → reduces radiation loss by 10×.
  • Add wind barriers → reduces convection losses by 30-50%.
  • Recover waste steam and condensation heat (latent heat recovery).
  • Result: Overall efficiency improvement of 10-15%, saving 10-15% of fuel costs annually for industrial boilers.
Case-Based-3: Climate Change & Greenhouse Effect. Explain how the Stefan-Boltzmann law and Wien's law help understand Earth's energy balance and the greenhouse gas effect.

Answer:

Earth's climate is governed by radiative heat balance—understanding Stefan-Boltzmann and Wien's laws is crucial:

Solar Input (Stefan-Boltzmann Law):

  • Sun's surface temperature: T_sun ≈ 5800 K
  • Sun's radiated power: E_sun = σA_sun T_sun⁴ = 5.67×10⁻⁸ × A_sun × (5800)⁴
  • Earth receives approximately 1361 W/m² solar irradiance (solar constant).
  • Peak wavelength: λ_max = b/T = 2.898×10⁻³ / 5800 = 5.0×10⁻⁷ m = 500 nm (visible light).

Earth's Radiation (Stefan-Boltzmann & Wien's Laws):

  • Earth's average temperature: T_Earth ≈ 288 K (15°C)
  • Earth's radiated power: E_Earth = σA_Earth T_Earth⁴ = 5.67×10⁻⁸ × A × (288)⁴
  • Peak emission wavelength: λ_max = 2.898×10⁻³ / 288 = 1.0×10⁻⁵ m = 10 μm (infrared).
  • Earth radiates in infrared because T is much lower than the Sun.

Energy Balance Without Atmosphere:

  • Incoming solar energy (visible) = Outgoing Earth radiation (infrared)
  • Without greenhouse gases, Earth would be much colder (T ≈ 255 K or −18°C).

Greenhouse Effect (With Atmosphere):

  • Greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, H₂O) are transparent to visible light (allow solar input) but opaque to infrared (block Earth's radiation).
  • This creates a radiative imbalance: More energy is absorbed by Earth than escapes.
  • Result: Earth warms (T increases) to restore balance.
  • From Stefan-Boltzmann law: If T increases by ΔT, energy radiated increases by E ∝ (T+ΔT)⁴ ≈ T⁴ + 4T³ΔT (for small ΔT).
  • A modest temperature increase (1.5-4°C in next century) can represent a large energy imbalance due to T⁴ dependence.

Predictions & Implications:

  • Doubling CO₂ concentration → ~1.5-4.5°C warming (depending on feedback mechanisms).
  • Small temperature increase causes large energy flux changes due to Stefan-Boltzmann law's T⁴ dependence.
  • This heating drives polar ice melt, sea-level rise, and weather pattern changes.
  • Mitigation: Reduce greenhouse gas emissions to restore radiative balance.
