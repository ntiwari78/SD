What is Wave Optics?

Wave optics, also known as physical optics, explains optical phenomena in terms of the wave nature of light. It accounts for interference, diffraction, and polarization phenomena that cannot be explained by ray optics (geometrical optics).

Key Point: While ray optics treats light as straight-line rays, wave optics considers light as an electromagnetic wave with wavelength, frequency, and phase properties.

Main Topics Covered

  • Huygens' Principle: Wavefront construction and understanding reflection/refraction
  • Interference: Superposition of waves from coherent sources (Young's double slit)
  • Diffraction: Bending of light around obstacles (single slit diffraction)
  • Resolving Power: Ability of optical instruments to distinguish between closely spaced objects
  • Polarisation: Orientation of vibrations in light waves and polaroids

Importance in Physics

Wave optics is fundamental to understanding modern optical instruments, laser technology, fiber optics, X-ray crystallography, and quantum mechanics. The principles of interference and diffraction are universally applicable to all wave phenomena.

Huygens' Principle: Wavefronts and Secondary Wavelets

Definition of Huygens' Principle

Huygens' principle states that every point on a wavefront acts as a secondary source of wavelets that spread out in all directions at the same speed as the original wave. The new wavefront is the envelope of all these secondary wavelets.

Huygens' Principle: "All points on a wavefront can be considered as secondary sources of spherical wavelets, and the envelope of these wavelets forms the new wavefront."

Wavefronts

A wavefront is the locus of all points vibrating in the same phase. Different types of wavefronts include:

  • Plane Wavefront: Parallel planes perpendicular to the direction of propagation (from distant sources or parallel beams)
  • Spherical Wavefront: Concentric spheres expanding from a point source
  • Cylindrical Wavefront: Concentric cylinders expanding from a linear source

Secondary Wavelets

When a wavefront reaches a point, that point becomes a source of secondary wavelets that are small spheres expanding outward at the speed of light. The envelope (tangent to all secondary wavelets) forms the next wavefront.

Example: Consider a plane wave traveling in a medium. Each point on the wavefront becomes a source of spherical secondary wavelets. After time Δt, these wavelets have expanded by distance cΔt. The envelope of all these spheres is perpendicular to the original direction, giving a new plane wavefront.

Reflection from Huygens' Principle

When a wave reflects from a plane surface:

  1. The first point of the wavefront touches the reflecting surface and acts as a secondary source
  2. Secondary wavelets spread from this point into the incident medium
  3. As the entire wavefront contacts the surface, all points become secondary sources
  4. The envelope of the reflected wavelets gives the reflected wavefront
Law of Reflection (from Huygens'):
Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection
Both measured from the normal to the surface

Refraction from Huygens' Principle

When a wave passes from one medium to another with different speeds:

  1. The wavefront hits the interface at angle i (angle of incidence)
  2. The first point of the wavefront contacts the interface and acts as secondary source in medium 2
  3. The wavefront travels distance c₁t in medium 1 while traveling c₂t in medium 2 in the same time t
  4. The new wavefront makes angle r with the normal (angle of refraction)
Snell's Law (from Huygens'):
sin i / sin r = c₁ / c₂ = n₂ / n₁
n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
where n = c/v is the refractive index

Advantages of Huygens' Principle

  • Provides a geometric method to find new wavefronts
  • Explains both reflection and refraction laws uniformly
  • Accounts for wave bending around obstacles
  • Foundation for understanding diffraction

Coherent and Incoherent Light Sources

What is Coherence?

Coherence refers to the phase relationship between waves. Two or more waves are coherent if they maintain a constant phase difference over time, allowing constructive and destructive interference patterns to form and remain stable.

Coherent Sources

Coherent sources are light sources that produce waves with a constant phase difference (independent of time).

Characteristics of Coherent Sources:
  • Constant phase difference between waves
  • Same frequency of oscillation
  • Phase difference doesn't change with time
  • Produce stable, stationary interference patterns
  • Can be from the same source (split into two)

Incoherent Sources

Incoherent sources are light sources that produce waves with a random or constantly changing phase difference.

Characteristics of Incoherent Sources:
  • Random phase difference that changes rapidly
  • Phase difference changes many times per second
  • No stable interference pattern
  • Intensity is simply the sum of individual intensities
  • Different sources (like two separate lamps)

Differences Between Coherent and Incoherent Sources

Property Coherent Sources Incoherent Sources
Phase Difference Constant Randomly varying
Frequency Same May differ
Interference Pattern Stable and sharp No pattern or rapidly changing
Visibility High (V ≈ 1) Low or zero
Examples Laser, split parts of same source Two separate incandescent bulbs

How to Create Coherent Sources

Since natural light from independent sources is incoherent, coherent sources are created by splitting light from a single source:

  • Young's Double Slit: Single source → two slits (coherent sources)
  • Lloyd's Mirror: Real source + virtual image from plane mirror
  • Fresnel's Double Mirror: Two mirrors at small angle create two virtual sources
  • Lasers: Emit naturally coherent light

Intensity from Coherent vs Incoherent Sources

Coherent Sources: I_total = I₁ + I₂ + 2√(I₁I₂)cos(φ) (includes interference term)

Incoherent Sources: I_total = I₁ + I₂ (no interference)

Important: Why can't two independent bulbs show interference? Because the phase difference changes billions of times per second, and our eyes/detectors cannot observe such rapid fluctuations. The average effect is simple addition of intensities.

Young's Double Slit Experiment: Interference

Historical Background

In 1801, Thomas Young demonstrated that light exhibits wave properties by passing light through two narrow slits and observing an interference pattern. This experiment provided one of the first direct evidences for the wave nature of light.

Setup of Young's Double Slit Experiment

  • A monochromatic light source (single wavelength λ)
  • Two narrow slits S₁ and S₂ separated by distance d (slit separation)
  • A screen at distance D from the slits (D >> d)
  • Observation point P on the screen at distance y from the central axis

Path Difference

For a point P on the screen, the path difference between light from the two slits is:

Path Difference:
Δ = S₂P - S₁P
For small angle approximation:
Δ = d sin θ
where θ is the angle from the central axis
Also: Δ = d × y / D
(where y is distance from center on screen)

Conditions for Interference

Constructive Interference (Bright Fringes)

When light waves from two slits arrive in phase at point P, they interfere constructively.

Condition for Bright Fringes:
d sin θ = n λ
where n = 0, ±1, ±2, ±3, ... (order of interference)
n = 0: Central bright fringe (maximum intensity)
n ≠ 0: Higher order bright fringes

Destructive Interference (Dark Fringes)

When light waves from two slits arrive out of phase by π, they interfere destructively.

Condition for Dark Fringes:
d sin θ = (n + 1/2) λ
where n = 0, 1, 2, 3, ...
This produces minima (zero intensity) on the screen

Fringe Width (Band Width)

The fringe width β is the distance between two adjacent bright fringes (or dark fringes).

Fringe Width Formula:
β = λD / d
where:
λ = wavelength of light
D = distance between slits and screen
d = separation between two slits

Characteristics of Fringe Width

  • Directly proportional to λ: Larger wavelength → larger fringe width
  • Directly proportional to D: Greater distance → larger fringe width
  • Inversely proportional to d: Greater slit separation → smaller fringe width
  • All fringes equally spaced (for small angles)
  • Same width for bright and dark fringes

Intensity Distribution in Young's Double Slit

Intensity Formula

Intensity at point P:
I = 4I₀ cos²(φ/2)
where:
I₀ = intensity from each slit
φ = phase difference = (2π/λ) × path difference
φ = (2πd sin θ) / λ

Intensity at Bright Fringes

For nth bright fringe where d sin θ = nλ:

φ = 2πn (multiple of 2π)
I_max = 4I₀ cos²(πn) = 4I₀

Intensity at Dark Fringes

For dark fringes where d sin θ = (n + 1/2)λ:

φ = (2n + 1)π (odd multiple of π)
I_min = 4I₀ cos²((2n+1)π/2) = 0

Intensity at Central Point

At the center (θ = 0), path difference = 0
φ = 0
I_center = 4I₀ cos²(0) = 4I₀ (maximum)

Visibility of Fringes

The visibility V indicates the contrast between bright and dark fringes:

Visibility:
V = (I_max - I_min) / (I_max + I_min)
For equal intensity sources: V = 1 (perfect visibility)

Effects on Fringe Pattern

Change Effect on Fringes
Increase wavelength λ Fringes spread apart (larger β)
Increase distance D Fringes spread apart (larger β)
Increase slit separation d Fringes come closer (smaller β)
Use white light instead of monochromatic Central fringe colored, outer fringes overlapping
Decrease slit width More visible fringes, higher intensity
Example Calculation:
If λ = 600 nm, D = 1 m, d = 2 mm
β = λD/d = (600 × 10⁻⁹ × 1) / (2 × 10⁻³)
β = 3 × 10⁻⁴ m = 0.3 mm = 3 mm (if calculations vary, check units!)

Applications of Young's Double Slit

  • Measurement of wavelength: λ = βd/D
  • Determination of slit separation: d = λD/β
  • Understanding wave interference: Direct proof of wave nature

Single Slit Diffraction

What is Diffraction?

Diffraction is the bending of light around the edges of obstacles or apertures. When light passes through a single narrow slit, it doesn't produce a simple geometrical shadow. Instead, it spreads out and creates a pattern of bright and dark bands on a screen. This phenomenon cannot be explained by ray optics and requires the wave theory of light.

Comparison with Interference

  • Interference: Superposition of coherent waves from two or more sources
  • Diffraction: Spreading of waves from a single source through an aperture or past an edge

Single Slit Diffraction Pattern

When monochromatic light passes through a single narrow slit:

  • A bright central maximum forms at the center
  • Alternating bright and dark bands (minima) appear on either side
  • The intensity decreases as you move away from the center
  • The central maximum is much broader and more intense than secondary maxima

Conditions for Minima (Dark Fringes)

The first dark fringe occurs when light from one edge of the slit travels one wavelength more than light from the opposite edge:

Condition for Dark Fringes (Minima):
a sin θ = n λ
where:
a = width of the slit
θ = angle from the center
λ = wavelength of light
n = 1, 2, 3, ... (order of minima)

Missing Orders in Single Slit Diffraction

Unlike Young's double slit, single slit diffraction shows "missing orders" where secondary maxima appear instead of orders that coincide with the diffraction envelope's minima.

Central Maximum Width

The central maximum extends from the first minimum on one side to the first minimum on the other side.

Width of Central Maximum:
From a sin θ = λ for first minimum:
For small angles: sin θ ≈ tan θ ≈ θ
Angular width = 2λ/a
Linear width on screen = 2λD/a
where D = distance of screen from slit

Intensity Distribution in Single Slit

The intensity distribution is more complex than in double slit, given by:

Intensity in Single Slit Diffraction:
I = I₀ (sin β / β)²
where β = (πa sin θ) / λ
I₀ = intensity at the center

Characteristics of Single Slit Pattern

  • Central maximum: Very broad and most intense
  • Secondary maxima: Alternate with minima, much weaker than central maximum
  • Symmetric pattern: About the central maximum
  • No maximum exactly at minima positions: Unlike double slit

Effects of Slit Width on Diffraction

Change Effect
Decrease slit width (a) Central maximum becomes wider
Increase slit width (a) Central maximum becomes narrower (more like ray optics)
Increase wavelength Pattern becomes wider
Increase distance D Pattern becomes larger but same angular width

Diffraction vs Ray Optics

Ray optics predicts that if light passes through a slit, the shadow of the slit would be a sharp edge with the same width as the slit. However, due to diffraction, the light spreads beyond the geometrical limits, and a diffraction pattern appears instead.

Example: A slit of width 0.1 mm with red light (λ = 650 nm) placed at distance 1 m from the screen.
Central maximum width = 2λD/a = 2 × (650 × 10⁻⁹) × 1 / (0.1 × 10⁻³)
= 1.3 × 10⁻² m = 1.3 cm

Fraunhofer vs Fresnel Diffraction

  • Fraunhofer Diffraction: Parallel rays incident on slit (source and screen far away), simpler pattern
  • Fresnel Diffraction: Spherical wavefront (source or screen close), more complex pattern

Resolving Power: Rayleigh Criterion

What is Resolving Power?

Resolving power is the ability of an optical instrument (like a microscope or telescope) to distinguish between two closely spaced objects as separate entities. The better the resolving power, the closer together two objects can be and still appear as distinct.

The Rayleigh Criterion

According to the Rayleigh criterion, two point sources are just resolved when the central maximum of the diffraction pattern of one source coincides with the first minimum of the diffraction pattern of the other source.

Rayleigh Criterion: Two objects are just resolved when separated by a distance equal to the radius of the central maximum of their diffraction patterns.

Minimum Resolvable Angle

For a circular aperture of diameter D observing objects at wavelength λ:

Minimum Resolvable Angle:
θ_min = 1.22λ / D
where:
1.22 is the constant for the first minimum of circular aperture diffraction
λ = wavelength of light
D = diameter of aperture

Resolving Power of a Microscope

A microscope resolves two nearby points with the help of the objective lens, which collects the diffracted light.

Resolving Power of Microscope:
RP = 2n sin θ / λ
where:
n = refractive index of medium between object and lens
θ = half angle of the cone of light from object
λ = wavelength of light used
Higher RP means better resolution

Minimum Resolvable Distance (Microscope)

The minimum distance δ between two points that can be resolved:

Minimum Resolvable Distance:
δ = λ / (2n sin θ)
This is the reciprocal of resolving power

Resolving Power of a Telescope

A telescope resolves two distant stars or objects based on the diameter of its objective lens.

Resolving Power of Telescope:
RP = D / (1.22λ)
where:
D = diameter of the objective lens
λ = wavelength of light
1.22λ/D = minimum resolvable angle

Methods to Improve Resolving Power

For Microscope

  • Use shorter wavelength light: UV light instead of visible light
  • Use immersion medium: Increase refractive index n (oil immersion microscopy)
  • Increase numerical aperture: RP ∝ 2n sin θ
  • Electron microscopy: Use electrons with very short wavelength (0.01 nm)

For Telescope

  • Increase objective diameter: Larger lens/mirror collects more light
  • Use shorter wavelength: UV telescopes have better resolution
  • Space telescopes: No atmospheric distortion (Hubble Space Telescope)

Limiting Factor of Wavelength

The wavelength of light is a fundamental limit to how finely two objects can be resolved with optical instruments. This is why:

  • Optical microscopes cannot resolve structures smaller than ~200 nm
  • Electron microscopes can resolve structures at atomic scales (0.1 nm)
  • Radio telescopes have poor angular resolution (large wavelength)
Important: The diffraction limit is the fundamental constraint on how much detail an optical system can resolve. This is why we need electron microscopes for atomic-scale imaging and why larger telescopes can see more distant, smaller objects in space.

Comparison: Microscope vs Telescope

Property Microscope Telescope
Purpose Magnify nearby small objects Magnify distant objects
RP Formula 2n sin θ / λ D / (1.22λ)
To improve RP Reduce λ, increase n, increase θ Increase D, reduce λ
Improvement Method Oil immersion, UV light Larger aperture, space telescope

Polarisation of Light

What is Polarisation?

Polarization is the phenomenon in which the vibrations of light waves are restricted to a single plane perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Unpolarized light (natural light) has vibrations in all directions perpendicular to propagation. Polarized light has vibrations restricted to one plane.

Types of Light

Unpolarized (Natural) Light

Light from ordinary sources (sun, incandescent bulbs, fluorescent lamps) consists of waves vibrating in all directions perpendicular to propagation, with random and rapidly changing phase relationships.

Linearly Polarized Light

Light in which the electric field vector vibrates in a single plane (one direction). This plane is called the plane of polarization.

Partially Polarized Light

Light containing a mixture of unpolarized and linearly polarized components.

Methods of Polarization

1. Polarization by Reflection (Brewster's Law)

When unpolarized light reflects from a transparent medium (like water or glass), the reflected light becomes partially polarized. At a specific angle called Brewster's angle, the reflected light is completely polarized.

Brewster's Angle:
tan θ_B = n₂ / n₁
For reflection at air-glass interface: tan θ_B = n
where n = refractive index of the denser medium
At Brewster's angle, reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular

2. Polarization by Refraction

When light refracts through certain crystals (like Iceland spar/calcite), it splits into two rays with perpendicular polarization directions.

3. Polarization by Scattering

When light scatters off molecules (like in the sky), the scattered light becomes partially polarized. This is why the sky appears blue and polarized.

4. Polarization by Absorption (Polaroids)

Special materials called polaroids contain parallel chains of molecules that allow vibrations in one direction to pass while absorbing vibrations in the perpendicular direction.

Malus' Law

When polarized light passes through a polarizer (or analyzer), the intensity of the transmitted light depends on the angle between the incident polarization and the transmission axis.

Malus' Law:
I = I₀ cos²θ
where:
I₀ = intensity of incident polarized light
θ = angle between polarization direction and transmission axis
I = intensity of transmitted light

Applications of Malus' Law

  • θ = 0°: I = I₀ (maximum transmission, axes parallel)
  • θ = 45°: I = I₀/2 (half maximum transmission)
  • θ = 90°: I = 0 (no transmission, axes perpendicular/crossed polarizers)

Polaroid and Polarizer

A polaroid is a sheet of material containing long-chain molecules aligned in one direction. When light passes through:

  • Vibrations parallel to chains → absorbed
  • Vibrations perpendicular to chains → transmitted (in theory)
  • Unpolarized light → becomes partially polarized, intensity reduces to ~50%
Two Crossed Polarizers: When two polarizers are placed with their transmission axes perpendicular (crossed), no light passes through, regardless of intensity (I = I₀ cos²90° = 0).

Practical Applications of Polarisation

1. Reducing Glare

Sunglasses with polaroid lenses block the horizontally polarized light reflected from water and road surfaces, reducing glare.

2. LCD Displays

Liquid crystal displays use polarization principles. The liquid crystals rotate the plane of polarization based on applied electric field.

3. Photography

Polarizing filters enhance contrast in sky and water photographs by eliminating reflected polarized light.

4. 3D Movies

Two slightly different images are projected with perpendicular polarizations. Viewers wear polaroid glasses that allow each eye to see only the intended image.

5. Optical Isolators

Used in lasers and fiber optics to allow light to travel in one direction only.

6. Stress Analysis

Transparent materials under stress become double refracting. Viewed under crossed polarizers, stress patterns become visible (photoelasticity).

Double Refraction (Birefringence)

Some crystals (like calcite) have two different refractive indices for different polarizations. Light splits into two rays:

  • Ordinary ray (o-ray): Follows Snell's law, vibrates perpendicular to principal axis
  • Extraordinary ray (e-ray): Doesn't follow Snell's law, vibrates in principal axis

Optical Rotation

Some substances (like sugar solution) rotate the plane of polarization of light passing through them. This rotation is proportional to the concentration and path length.

Optical Rotation:
θ = α × c × l
where:
θ = angle of rotation
α = specific rotation (constant for substance)
c = concentration
l = path length
Key Point: Polarization is a unique property of transverse waves. It provides evidence that light is a transverse wave, not a longitudinal wave like sound.
