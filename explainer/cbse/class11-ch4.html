Laws of Motion

Newton's Three Laws • Momentum • Forces • Circular Motion Dynamics • Problem Solving

Why Do Objects Move?

Imagine a hockey puck sliding on frictionless ice versus a hockey puck on concrete. The ice puck continues moving seemingly forever, while the concrete one stops quickly. Why?

For centuries, Aristotle thought a force was needed to keep things moving. But Galileo realized the truth: friction was stopping the puck, not the absence of force. Newton transformed this insight into his laws of motion—the foundation of classical mechanics.

In this chapter, you'll learn why rockets accelerate, how to analyze forces on inclined planes, why we need seatbelts, and how satellites orbit without falling to Earth. Everything traces back to three elegantly simple laws.

Aristotle's Fallacy and Galileo's Insight

The Ancient Misconception

Aristotle (384-322 BCE) observed that objects naturally come to rest. His conclusion: a continuous force is needed to keep an object moving. This seemed obvious—push a box, it stops when you stop pushing.

Aristotle's Law (WRONG): Force is required to maintain motion. An object at rest is the "natural state."

This belief persisted for nearly 2000 years because everyday experience seemed to confirm it. Every moving object we observe eventually stops.

Galileo's Revolutionary Idea (1600s)

Galileo Galilei performed thought experiments with inclined planes and asked: "What if we remove obstacles?" He imagined a frictionless surface and realized:

Galileo's Law of Inertia: An object in motion will continue moving at constant velocity indefinitely unless acted upon by an external force. Objects naturally resist changes in their state of motion—this is inertia.

Friction wasn't a fundamental property of motion; it was just an external force. On a frictionless surface, no force is needed to keep something moving!

Example: A hockey puck on ice (low friction) travels much farther than one on concrete (high friction) when given the same initial push. Both are obeying the same law—friction is the only difference.

Why We Don't See This in Daily Life

Friction is ubiquitous. Air resistance, ground friction, and other dissipative forces constantly slow objects. We never see a truly frictionless scenario naturally. Galileo's genius was imagining what would happen without these hidden forces.

Newton's First Law: Law of Inertia

Statement

Newton's First Law: Every body continues in a state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force.

This formalizes Galileo's insight. Two key points:

  • Rest and uniform motion are equivalent: Both mean zero acceleration. From a physics standpoint, they're the same "state."
  • Uniform motion = constant velocity: The object moves in a straight line at unchanging speed.

Inertial Frames of Reference

Newton's laws are valid only in inertial frames—reference frames that are either at rest or moving with constant velocity. In non-inertial (accelerating) frames, fictitious forces appear (like the centrifugal force you feel in a turning car).

Inertial Frame: A frame where Newton's first law holds. An observer in an inertial frame experiences no unexplained forces due to the frame's motion.

Inertia and Mass

Inertia is the property of an object to resist changes in its motion. Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia. A more massive object has more inertia—it's harder to accelerate.

Example: A bowling ball and a tennis ball rolling toward you at the same speed. It's easier to stop the tennis ball (less inertia) than the bowling ball (more inertia). The bowling ball "wants" to keep moving more stubbornly.

Practical Consequences

  • Seatbelts: Your body wants to continue forward when a car brakes. A seatbelt applies a force to change your motion with the car.
  • Sliding in a turn: Your car turns, but your body continues straight (inertia). To turn with the car, friction must push you sideways.
  • Pushing a stationary car: It takes more force to start moving a car (overcome static friction and inertia) than to keep it moving.

Newton's Second Law: F = ma

Statement and Formulation

Newton's Second Law: The net force on an object is equal to its mass times its acceleration.
$$\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}$$

This is the workhorse equation of mechanics. It tells us:

  • Acceleration is proportional to force: Double the force → double the acceleration (for constant mass).
  • Acceleration is inversely proportional to mass: Double the mass → half the acceleration (for constant force).
  • Vectors matter: Force and acceleration point in the same direction.
  • Net force: We sum all forces. If net force = 0, acceleration = 0 (Newton's first law is a special case!).

Momentum and Force as Rate of Change

Newton originally stated his second law in terms of momentum:

$$\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$

Where momentum is:

$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$

Why is this form important? It's more general. When mass is constant, F = dp/dt reduces to F = ma. But for rockets (losing fuel) or relativistic speeds (mass changes), this form is essential. Force is the rate of change of momentum.

Units of Force

Newton (N): The unit of force. 1 N = 1 kg·m/s². A force that accelerates 1 kg at 1 m/s².

Weight is a force: W = mg. On Earth (g ≈ 10 m/s²), a 1 kg object weighs about 10 N.

Impulse and Momentum

Integrating the second law over time gives impulse:

$$J = \vec{F}\Delta t = \Delta\vec{p}$$

Impulse (J) is force multiplied by the time it acts. It equals the change in momentum.

Impulse-Momentum Theorem: The impulse from a force equals the change in momentum produced.
Example: A car crash. Airbags increase the time over which the car's momentum changes (longer Δt), so the force required is smaller. F = Δp/Δt. Longer time → smaller force → less injury.

Worked Example 1: Acceleration on a Horizontal Surface

Problem:

A 2 kg block is pushed with a force of 10 N on a frictionless surface. What is its acceleration?

Given: m = 2 kg, F = 10 N, no friction
Find: a = ?
Solution: F = ma → a = F/m = 10/2 = 5 m/s²
Interpretation: The block speeds up at 5 m/s every second. After 2 seconds, its velocity is 10 m/s.

Worked Example 2: Momentum Change

Problem:

A 1500 kg car moving at 20 m/s brakes. If the braking force is 6000 N, how long does it take to stop?

Given: m = 1500 kg, v₀ = 20 m/s, v = 0 m/s (final), F = -6000 N (opposes motion)
Find: t = ?
Momentum change: Δp = m(v - v₀) = 1500(0 - 20) = -30,000 kg·m/s
Impulse: J = FΔt → -30,000 = -6000 × t → t = 5 seconds

Newton's Third Law: Action-Reaction

Statement

Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. If object A exerts a force on object B, then B exerts an equal and opposite force on A.
$$\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}$$

The forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

Critical Insight: Different Bodies

CRUCIAL: Action and reaction forces act on different objects. They don't cancel out! If they did, nothing could ever accelerate.

Why action-reaction don't cancel: They act on different bodies. Only forces on the same body can be added and potentially cancel.
Wrong thinking: "If I push a wall with force F, the wall pushes back with force F. They cancel, so nothing moves."

Right thinking: The wall pushes on me, not on itself. I push on the wall, not on myself. These are different pairs. The wall may not move (ground friction holds it), but I feel the reaction force.

Examples of Action-Reaction Pairs

1. Walking

Action: You push backward on the ground with your feet.
Reaction: The ground pushes forward on you (friction).
Result: You accelerate forward.

2. Rocket Propulsion

Action: The rocket pushes hot gases backward (exhaust).
Reaction: The gases push the rocket forward.
Result: The rocket accelerates upward, even in the vacuum of space where there's no "air to push against."

3. Gun Recoil

Action: The gun pushes the bullet forward.
Reaction: The bullet pushes the gun backward.
Result: Both accelerate in opposite directions. The bullet goes far (small mass, large force), the gun recoils (large mass, same force).

4. Jumping

Action: Your legs push down on Earth.
Reaction: Earth pushes up on you.
Result: You accelerate upward. (Earth accelerates downward too, but its enormous mass makes this imperceptible.)

Worked Example: Recoil Velocity

Problem:

A 0.05 kg bullet is fired from a 2 kg gun. If the bullet's velocity is 500 m/s, what is the gun's recoil velocity?

Given: m_bullet = 0.05 kg, m_gun = 2 kg, v_bullet = 500 m/s
Use conservation of momentum (next section!): m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = 0 (initial momentum = 0)
Solve: 0.05 × 500 + 2 × v_gun = 0 → 25 + 2v_gun = 0 → v_gun = -12.5 m/s
Result: The gun recoils backward at 12.5 m/s. The negative sign indicates opposite direction.

Conservation of Momentum

Statement

Law of Conservation of Momentum: If no external force acts on a system, the total momentum of the system remains constant.
$$\vec{p}_{\text{initial}} = \vec{p}_{\text{final}}$$
$$\sum m_i v_i = \text{constant}$$

Derivation from Newton's Third Law

Consider two objects colliding. The forces are action-reaction pairs:

  • Object 1 exerts force F₁₂ on object 2.
  • Object 2 exerts force F₂₁ on object 1, where F₂₁ = -F₁₂.

Applying Newton's second law to each:

  • For object 1: F₂₁ = m₁a₁ = m₁(dv₁/dt)
  • For object 2: F₁₂ = m₂a₂ = m₂(dv₂/dt)

Since F₂₁ = -F₁₂:

m₁(dv₁/dt) = -m₂(dv₂/dt)

d(m₁v₁)/dt = -d(m₂v₂)/dt

d(m₁v₁ + m₂v₂)/dt = 0

Total momentum is constant! The total momentum before and after any interaction is the same (if no external forces).

Examples

1. Gun and Bullet

Before: Both at rest. Total momentum = 0.
After: Bullet moves forward, gun recoils backward.
Conservation: m_bullet × v_bullet + m_gun × v_gun = 0 (always).

2. Rocket Propulsion

Before: Rocket + fuel at rest. Total momentum = 0.
After: Rocket moves up, exhaust moves down.
Conservation: m_rocket × v_rocket + m_exhaust × v_exhaust = 0 (in rocket's frame initially).

Rockets work even in space because they don't push against air—they push against their own exhaust. The momentum of the exhaust and rocket must be equal and opposite.

3. Collision

Before: Two cars collide. Momentum = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂.
After: They stick together. Combined momentum = (m₁ + m₂)v_final.
Conservation: m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = (m₁ + m₂)v_final.

Worked Example 1: Inelastic Collision

Problem:

Car A (1000 kg, 20 m/s) collides with Car B (1500 kg, at rest) and they stick together. Find their final velocity.

Given: m_A = 1000 kg, v_A = 20 m/s, m_B = 1500 kg, v_B = 0 m/s
Find: v_final = ?
Momentum before: p_i = 1000 × 20 + 1500 × 0 = 20,000 kg·m/s
Momentum after: p_f = (1000 + 1500) × v_final = 2500 × v_final
Conservation: 20,000 = 2500 × v_final → v_final = 8 m/s
Result: Both cars move forward at 8 m/s after collision.

Worked Example 2: Explosion

Problem:

A 4 kg object initially at rest explodes into two pieces: 1 kg moving at 12 m/s to the right, and 3 kg moving to the left. Find the velocity of the 3 kg piece.

Given: m_total = 4 kg, at rest. After: m₁ = 1 kg, v₁ = 12 m/s; m₂ = 3 kg, v₂ = ?
Momentum before: p_i = 0
Momentum after: p_f = 1 × 12 + 3 × v₂ = 12 + 3v₂
Conservation: 0 = 12 + 3v₂ → v₂ = -4 m/s
Result: The 3 kg piece moves at 4 m/s to the left (negative sign indicates leftward direction).

Equilibrium of a Particle

Static Equilibrium

Equilibrium: A particle is in equilibrium when the net force on it is zero. This means either:
  • The particle is at rest (static equilibrium), or
  • The particle moves with constant velocity (dynamic equilibrium).
$$\sum \vec{F} = 0$$
$$\vec{F}_1 + \vec{F}_2 + \vec{F}_3 + \ldots = 0$$

In component form (x-y plane):

  • ΣF_x = 0
  • ΣF_y = 0

Lami's Theorem

For a particle in equilibrium under three coplanar forces, Lami's theorem relates the magnitudes to the angles between them:

$$\frac{F_1}{\sin\alpha} = \frac{F_2}{\sin\beta} = \frac{F_3}{\sin\gamma}$$

Where α, β, γ are the angles between the forces (not the angles with the axes).

Worked Example: Three Forces in Equilibrium

Problem:

A picture frame hangs from two ropes. Each rope makes an angle of 30° with the vertical. If the frame weighs 20 N, find the tension in each rope.

Given: Weight W = 20 N downward, two ropes at 30° from vertical (60° from horizontal)
Three forces: T₁ (left rope), T₂ (right rope), W (weight). By symmetry, T₁ = T₂ = T.
Vertical equilibrium: T cos(30°) + T cos(30°) = W
Solve: 2T × (√3/2) = 20 → T√3 = 20 → T = 20/√3 ≈ 11.5 N
Result: Each rope has tension of approximately 11.5 N.

Common Forces in Mechanics

1. Weight (Gravitational Force)

$$W = mg$$

Weight is the force of gravity on an object. On Earth, g ≈ 9.8 m/s² (often approximated as 10 m/s²). Weight always acts downward.

Important: Mass and weight are different. Mass is intrinsic (same everywhere); weight depends on gravity (6 N on the Moon vs. 60 N on Earth for a 6 kg object).

2. Normal Force (Contact Force)

The normal force is the force exerted by a surface to support an object perpendicular to the surface. It prevents objects from "sinking" into the surface.

Normal force is always perpendicular to the surface. It's a reaction force—the object pushes down, the surface pushes up.
Free Body Diagram: For a block on a horizontal table:
  • Weight W = mg acts downward (center of block).
  • Normal force N acts upward (perpendicular to surface).
  • If the block is at rest: N = W.
  • If the block is accelerating vertically: N = mg ± ma (depending on direction).
Example: In an elevator accelerating upward, you feel heavier because N > W. In an elevator accelerating downward, you feel lighter because N < W.

3. Tension (Pulling Force)

Tension is the pulling force exerted by a rope, string, or cable on an object. It acts along the length of the rope, pulling the object.

Assumptions: In typical problems, we assume:
  • Ropes are massless (or negligible mass).
  • Ropes are inextensible (don't stretch).
  • Tension is uniform throughout the rope.

4. Spring Force (Hooke's Law)

$$F = -kx$$

Where:

  • k = spring constant (N/m), measuring spring stiffness.
  • x = displacement from equilibrium.
  • Negative sign = restoring force (opposes displacement).
Hooke's Law: The force exerted by a spring is proportional to how much it's stretched or compressed, and it always acts to restore the spring to its equilibrium length.
Example: A spring with k = 100 N/m stretched by 0.1 m exerts a restoring force of F = -100 × 0.1 = -10 N (pulling back toward equilibrium).

5. Friction

Friction opposes relative motion (kinetic friction) or prevents motion (static friction).

Static Friction

$$f_s \leq \mu_s N$$

Static friction can be anything from 0 to μ_s N, depending on the applied force. It adjusts to balance other forces (up to a limit).

Kinetic Friction

$$f_k = \mu_k N$$

Once an object is sliding, kinetic friction is constant and proportional to the normal force. μ_k is the coefficient of kinetic friction.

Key fact: μ_s > μ_k always. It's easier to keep something sliding than to start it sliding.

Angle of Friction and Angle of Repose

If an object is on an inclined plane at angle θ to the horizontal:

  • Angle of friction: θ_f where tan(θ_f) = μ_s. This is the maximum angle at which a static object remains at rest.
  • Angle of repose: The same as angle of friction for static friction at the verge of sliding.
$$\tan\theta_f = \mu_s$$
Free Body Diagram on Inclined Plane: For an object on an incline at angle θ:
  • Weight W = mg acts vertically downward.
  • Component parallel to plane: mg sin(θ) (down the plane).
  • Component perpendicular to plane: mg cos(θ) (into the plane).
  • Normal force N = mg cos(θ) (perpendicular to plane, outward).
  • Friction f acts up the plane (if object tends to slide down).

Worked Example: Block on Inclined Plane

Problem:

A 5 kg block rests on a 30° incline. Coefficient of static friction is 0.4. Will the block slide?

Given: m = 5 kg, θ = 30°, μ_s = 0.4, g = 10 m/s²
Weight components: mg sin(30°) = 5 × 10 × 0.5 = 25 N (down plane)
Normal force: N = mg cos(30°) = 5 × 10 × (√3/2) ≈ 43.3 N
Max static friction: f_s,max = μ_s N = 0.4 × 43.3 ≈ 17.3 N
Comparison: 25 N > 17.3 N, so the block WILL slide down the plane.

Types of Friction

  • Static friction: Before an object moves. Acts when applied force is less than maximum static friction.
  • Kinetic (sliding) friction: When an object slides across a surface. Constant magnitude μ_k N.
  • Rolling friction: When an object rolls without slipping. Much smaller than sliding friction (different physics—involves rotation).

Free Body Diagrams (FBDs)

What is a Free Body Diagram?

A Free Body Diagram is a sketch showing all forces acting on a single object (the "body"), with the object isolated from its surroundings. It's the first and most important step in solving mechanics problems.

How to Draw a Free Body Diagram

  1. Identify the body: Choose one object and isolate it mentally from everything else.
  2. Draw the object: Represent it as a point or simple shape (dot, block, sphere).
  3. Identify all forces: Ask "what is touching this object or acting on it from a distance?"
    • Gravity (weight, always downward).
    • Normal forces (surfaces in contact).
    • Tension (ropes, strings).
    • Friction (surfaces in contact, opposes motion/tendency).
    • Applied forces (pushes, pulls).
    • Spring forces (if attached to springs).
  4. Draw force vectors: Arrows representing forces, starting from the body.
    • Arrow length represents magnitude (roughly).
    • Arrow direction shows force direction.
    • Label each force (W, N, T, f, etc.).
  5. Choose a coordinate system: Draw x and y axes for reference.
  6. Solve using ΣF = ma: Apply Newton's second law in component form.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Including reaction forces on the wrong body: If drawing the FBD of the ball, don't include the force the ball exerts on the ground. Include only forces on the ball.
  • Forgetting the normal force: If an object is in contact with a surface, always include N.
  • Assuming tension equals weight: Tension varies with acceleration. Only T = W when a = 0.
  • Wrong friction direction: Friction opposes the direction of motion or the direction the object would move if friction weren't present.

FBD Example 1: Block on a Table with Applied Force

Scenario: A 2 kg block is pushed horizontally across a table with force F = 10 N. Coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.3. Draw the FBD and find the acceleration.

Forces identified:
  • W = mg: Weight = 2 × 10 = 20 N downward (acts at center).
  • N: Normal force from table, upward, at contact surface.
  • F = 10 N: Applied force, horizontal (rightward).
  • f_k: Kinetic friction, horizontal (leftward, opposes motion).
Equations:
  • Vertical: N - W = 0 → N = 20 N
  • Friction: f_k = μ_k N = 0.3 × 20 = 6 N
  • Horizontal: F - f_k = ma → 10 - 6 = 2 × a → a = 2 m/s²

FBD Example 2: Hanging Mass

Scenario: A 3 kg mass hangs from a rope attached to a ceiling. The rope can support a maximum tension of 50 N. If you pull downward on the mass with force F, at what value of F does the rope break?

Forces identified:
  • W = mg: Weight = 3 × 10 = 30 N downward.
  • T: Tension from rope, upward.
  • F: Applied downward force.
Equation:
  • Vertical: T - W - F = ma (taking upward as positive)
  • At breaking point: T = 50 N, and we can assume a → 0 (break is instant)
  • 50 - 30 - F = 0 → F = 20 N
Answer: The rope breaks when F = 20 N (rope tension reaches 50 N).

FBD Example 3: Two Connected Objects (Atwood Machine)

Scenario: Two masses connected by a string over a pulley. m₁ = 3 kg, m₂ = 2 kg. Assume m₁ is heavier. Find acceleration and tension.

Key insight: Both masses accelerate with the same magnitude (rope constraint), but in opposite directions.

FBD for m₁ (moving downward):
  • Weight W₁ = 30 N downward.
  • Tension T upward.
  • Equation: W₁ - T = m₁a → 30 - T = 3a
FBD for m₂ (moving upward):
  • Weight W₂ = 20 N downward.
  • Tension T upward.
  • Equation: T - W₂ = m₂a → T - 20 = 2a
Solve:
  • From m₂ equation: T = 20 + 2a
  • Substitute into m₁ equation: 30 - (20 + 2a) = 3a → 10 = 5a → a = 2 m/s²
  • Then: T = 20 + 2(2) = 24 N

Circular Motion Dynamics

Centripetal Acceleration and Force

For an object moving in a circle at constant speed v and radius r, the acceleration is directed toward the center (centripetal):

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r$$

Where ω = v/r is the angular velocity (rad/s).

The force required to maintain circular motion is:

$$F_c = m\frac{v^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r$$
Centripetal force is always directed toward the center of the circle. It's provided by whatever force is available: tension, normal force, friction, gravity, etc.
Examples of centripetal force sources:
  • Circular motion of a planet: Gravity provides centripetal force.
  • Car turning: Friction between tires and road provides centripetal force.
  • Satellite orbiting: Gravity pulls satellite toward Earth's center.
  • Ball on a string: Tension in the string provides centripetal force.

Banking of Roads

On a banked road, the normal force has a horizontal component pointing toward the center, reducing reliance on friction.

Ideal banking angle θ: The angle at which a road should be tilted so that at a specific speed, gravity and the normal force together provide exactly the needed centripetal force (no friction needed).
$$\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$

Derivation of Banking Formula

Consider a car on a banked road at angle θ (tilted inward). Forces:

  • Weight W = mg (downward).
  • Normal force N (perpendicular to road surface).

Components of N:

  • Vertical: N cos(θ) (balances weight if no friction).
  • Horizontal: N sin(θ) (provides centripetal force).

Equations:

  • Vertical: N cos(θ) = mg
  • Horizontal (centripetal): N sin(θ) = mv²/r

Divide the second by the first:

tan(θ) = v²/(rg)

Example: A road is banked at 30° for an ideal speed of 20 m/s on a 100 m radius curve (g = 10 m/s²). Check: tan(30°) ≈ 0.577 = 400/(100 × 10) = 0.4. (Note: This doesn't match perfectly in reality due to air resistance and other factors, but the principle is correct.)

Conical Pendulum

A mass m attached to a string of length L, rotating in a horizontal circle at angle θ from vertical. The string makes angle θ with the vertical.

Forces on the mass:
  • Weight W = mg (downward).
  • Tension T (along the string).
Components of T:
  • Vertical: T cos(θ) = mg (balances weight).
  • Horizontal: T sin(θ) = m(v²/r) (provides centripetal force).
Radius of circle: r = L sin(θ).
$$T\cos\theta = mg$$ $$T\sin\theta = m\frac{v^2}{r} = m\frac{v^2}{L\sin\theta}$$

Worked Example: Conical Pendulum

Problem:

A 0.5 kg mass is attached to a 1 m string and rotates in a horizontal circle, with the string making 30° with vertical. Find the tension and speed of rotation.

Given: m = 0.5 kg, L = 1 m, θ = 30°, g = 10 m/s²
Find tension: T cos(30°) = mg → T × (√3/2) = 0.5 × 10 → T = 5/(√3/2) = 10/√3 ≈ 5.77 N
Radius of circle: r = L sin(30°) = 1 × 0.5 = 0.5 m
Find speed: T sin(30°) = mv²/r → 5.77 × 0.5 = 0.5 × v²/0.5 → 2.89 = v² → v ≈ 1.7 m/s

Worked Example: Car on Circular Track

Problem:

A 1500 kg car moves on a horizontal circular track of radius 50 m at 15 m/s. What is the minimum coefficient of friction needed to prevent skidding?

Given: m = 1500 kg, r = 50 m, v = 15 m/s, g = 10 m/s²
Required centripetal force: F_c = mv²/r = 1500 × 15²/50 = 1500 × 225/50 = 6750 N
Normal force: N = mg = 1500 × 10 = 15,000 N (on horizontal surface)
Maximum friction: f_max = μN → 6750 = μ × 15,000 → μ = 6750/15,000 = 0.45
Result: Minimum coefficient of friction is 0.45.

Problem Solving: Applications and Techniques

Strategy for Solving Force and Motion Problems

  1. Read carefully: Identify what's given and what's asked.
  2. Draw a free body diagram (FBD): For each object, identify all forces.
  3. Choose a coordinate system: Usually x-horizontal, y-vertical (or along/perpendicular to motion).
  4. Resolve forces into components: Use sin and cos if forces are at angles.
  5. Apply Newton's second law: ΣF_x = ma_x and ΣF_y = ma_y separately.
  6. Identify constraints: For connected objects (strings, pulleys), accelerations may be related.
  7. Solve the system of equations: Use algebra to find unknowns.
  8. Check your answer: Does it make physical sense? Right units? Right magnitude?

Application 1: Atwood Machine

Problem:

Two masses m₁ = 4 kg and m₂ = 2 kg are connected by a light string over a frictionless pulley. Initially at rest. Find the acceleration and tension.

Given: m₁ = 4 kg, m₂ = 2 kg, g = 10 m/s²
FBD for m₁ (heavier, moves down): W₁ - T = m₁a → 40 - T = 4a
FBD for m₂ (lighter, moves up): T - W₂ = m₂a → T - 20 = 2a
Add equations: 40 - 20 = 4a + 2a → 20 = 6a → a = 10/3 ≈ 3.33 m/s²
Find tension: T - 20 = 2 × (10/3) → T = 20 + 20/3 = 80/3 ≈ 26.67 N

Application 2: Pulley System with Friction

Problem:

A block (m₁ = 5 kg) on a horizontal surface is pulled by a string connected over a pulley to a hanging mass (m₂ = 3 kg). Coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.2. Find acceleration.

Given: m₁ = 5 kg, m₂ = 3 kg, μ_k = 0.2, g = 10 m/s²
FBD for m₁ (on surface):
  • Normal force N₁ = m₁g = 50 N
  • Friction f = μ_k N₁ = 0.2 × 50 = 10 N (opposes motion)
  • Equation: T - f = m₁a → T - 10 = 5a
FBD for m₂ (hanging): m₂g - T = m₂a → 30 - T = 3a
Add equations: 30 - 10 = 5a + 3a → 20 = 8a → a = 2.5 m/s²
Find tension: T - 10 = 5 × 2.5 → T = 22.5 N

Application 3: Inclined Plane with Friction

Problem:

A 10 kg block slides down a 37° incline. Coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.3. Find the acceleration.

Given: m = 10 kg, θ = 37°, μ_k = 0.3, g = 10 m/s²
Weight components:
  • Parallel to incline: mg sin(37°) = 10 × 10 × 0.6 = 60 N (down)
  • Perpendicular to incline: mg cos(37°) = 10 × 10 × 0.8 = 80 N (into incline)
Normal force: N = mg cos(37°) = 80 N
Friction: f = μ_k N = 0.3 × 80 = 24 N (up the incline)
Net force down incline: F_net = 60 - 24 = 36 N
Acceleration: a = F_net/m = 36/10 = 3.6 m/s²

Application 4: Connected Masses Over Pulley with Friction

Problem:

Block A (2 kg) on a table connected via string over pulley to block B (3 kg) hanging. Friction coefficient between A and table is 0.25. Find acceleration and tension.

Given: m_A = 2 kg, m_B = 3 kg, μ = 0.25, g = 10 m/s²
FBD for A:
  • Normal force N = m_A g = 20 N
  • Friction f = 0.25 × 20 = 5 N
  • Equation: T - f = m_A a → T - 5 = 2a
FBD for B: m_B g - T = m_B a → 30 - T = 3a
Add equations: 30 - 5 = 5a → a = 5 m/s²
Find tension: T = 5 + 2 × 5 = 15 N

Application 5: Vertical Circular Motion

Problem:

A 0.2 kg ball swings on a 0.5 m string in a vertical circle. At the top of the circle, the tension is 2 N. Find the speed at the top.

Given: m = 0.2 kg, L = 0.5 m, T_top = 2 N, g = 10 m/s²
At the top of the circle:
  • Both tension and weight point toward center (downward).
  • Centripetal force: F_c = T + mg = T + 0.2 × 10 = 2 + 2 = 4 N
Solve for speed: F_c = mv²/r → 4 = 0.2 × v²/0.5 → 4 = 0.4v² → v² = 10 → v ≈ 3.16 m/s

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

These questions require deeper understanding and analysis. Use the concepts you've learned to solve novel problems.

H1: Elevator Paradox

Scenario: You stand on a scale in an elevator. The scale reads your "apparent weight." When the elevator is stationary, it reads 700 N. When the elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s², the scale reads 840 N. When it accelerates downward at 2 m/s², it reads 560 N.

Questions:

  1. Find your actual mass.
  2. Explain physically why the scale reading changes even though gravity on you hasn't changed.
  3. At what acceleration would the scale read zero? What would this mean physically?

Solution hints: The scale reads the normal force. Use F_net = ma. When a = 0, N = mg. When a ≠ 0, N = m(g ± a).

H2: Rope Breaking Problem

Scenario: You're hoisting a 50 kg crate using a rope with a breaking strength of 600 N. You want to lift it as fast as possible without breaking the rope.

Questions:

  1. What is the maximum acceleration you can achieve?
  2. If you pull with constant force, will the crate's acceleration change? Why or why not?
  3. Compare lifting on Earth (g = 10 m/s²) vs. on the Moon (g = 1.6 m/s²). How does this affect the maximum acceleration?

Solution hints: T = m(g + a). At breaking point, T = 600 N. Solve for max a.

H3: Friction and Limiting Angle

Scenario: A cylindrical cup filled with water sits on a rotating platform. As the rotation speed increases, water climbs the walls. At a certain critical speed, the cup tips over instead of the water spilling.

Questions:

  1. Why does water climb the walls as rotation speed increases?
  2. What is the minimum coefficient of static friction needed to prevent the cup from sliding outward at angular velocity ω on a platform of radius r?
  3. If the cup has radius R and height h, what's the condition for the cup to tip before the water spills?

Solution hints: In the rotating frame, centrifugal force (fictitious) pushes outward. Real force (friction) must balance it. Tipping occurs when the center of mass's horizontal displacement exceeds R/2.

Quick Reference: Cheat Sheet

Newton's Three Laws

1st Law (Inertia)

An object at rest stays at rest; one in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by external force. ΣF = 0 → a = 0.

2nd Law (F = ma)

$$\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$$ Net force equals mass times acceleration. Same direction.

3rd Law (Action-Reaction)

For every action, equal and opposite reaction. Act on different bodies. $$\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}$$

Key Equations

Momentum

$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$ $$\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$

Impulse

$$J = \vec{F}\Delta t = \Delta\vec{p}$$ Change in momentum over time.

Conservation of Momentum

$$\sum p_i = \text{constant (if } F_{ext} = 0)$$

Forces

Weight

$$W = mg$$ Always downward. g ≈ 9.8 m/s² on Earth.

Friction

Static: $$f_s \leq \mu_s N$$ Kinetic: $$f_k = \mu_k N$$ $$\mu_s > \mu_k$$ always.

Spring Force

$$F = -kx$$ Restoring force, proportional to displacement.

Circular Motion

Centripetal Acceleration

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r$$ Always toward center.

Centripetal Force

$$F_c = m\frac{v^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r$$ Directed toward center of circle.

Banking Angle

$$\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$ Ideal angle for speed v on radius r.

Units & Constants

SI Units

Force: 1 N = 1 kg·m/s²
Momentum: kg·m/s
Impulse: N·s = kg·m/s

Common Values

g (Earth) ≈ 9.8 or 10 m/s²
g (Moon) ≈ 1.6 m/s²
μ_steel (kinetic) ≈ 0.6

Problem-Solving Checklist

  • ✓ Read carefully and identify given/unknowns.
  • ✓ Draw Free Body Diagram for each object.
  • ✓ Choose coordinate system (usually x-y).
  • ✓ Resolve all forces into components.
  • ✓ Apply ΣF_x = ma_x and ΣF_y = ma_y.
  • ✓ Identify constraints (connected objects, pulley, etc.).
  • ✓ Solve system of equations.
  • ✓ Verify units and reasonableness of answer.
