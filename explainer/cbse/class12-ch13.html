Chapter 13: Nuclei - Complete Study Guide

Nuclear Structure and Size

Nucleus Composition and Size

The nucleus is composed of protons and neutrons (nucleons). Its size follows an empirical formula:

R = R₀A^(1/3)

Where:
R = Nuclear radius
R₀ = 1.2 fm (Fermi, 1 fm = 10⁻¹⁵ m)
A = Mass number (total nucleons)
Key Insight: The nuclear density is approximately constant and equals ~2.3×10¹⁷ kg/m³, which is enormous compared to normal matter densities (~1000 kg/m³).

Atomic Mass Unit

1 u = 931.5 MeV/c²

This conversion factor is essential for calculating binding energy and mass defects.

Nuclear Radius

Increases with mass number A. For A=64: R ≈ 5 fm

Nuclear Volume

V = (4/3)πR³ ∝ A, so volume is proportional to nucleons

Mass Defect and Binding Energy

Mass Defect

The difference between the sum of masses of constituent nucleons and the actual nuclear mass:

Δm = [Zmp + (A-Z)mn] - mnucleus

Where:
Z = Atomic number (protons)
A-Z = Number of neutrons
mp = Mass of proton
mn = Mass of neutron

Binding Energy

Energy required to completely disassemble a nucleus:

BE = Δmc²

This energy is released when nucleons combine to form a nucleus.
Binding Energy per Nucleon (BE/A): This curve peaks at Fe-56 (~8.8 MeV/nucleon), making iron the most stable nucleus. Nuclei lighter than Fe release energy when fused; heavier nuclei release energy when split (fission).

Stability Criterion

Higher BE/A indicates greater stability. Most stable elements are near iron.

Energy Units

1 eV = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J; 1 MeV = 10⁶ eV

Nuclear Forces

Characteristics of Nuclear Forces

The force holding nucleons together has unique properties:

Property Description
Range Very short range (~2 fm). Negligible beyond 3 fm.
Charge Independence Force between two protons ≈ force between two neutrons ≈ force between proton and neutron
Saturation Each nucleon interacts strongly with only a few nearby nucleons, not all nucleons
Strength About 100 times stronger than Coulomb force at nuclear distances
Saturation Effect: This explains why BE/A remains roughly constant for heavier nuclei. A nucleon in the interior can only interact with nearby nucleons, not the entire nucleus.

Radioactivity and Decay

Types of Radioactive Decay

Alpha (α) Decay

Process: ²³⁸U → ²³⁴Th + ⁴He

Emission of helium nucleus (2 protons, 2 neutrons). Reduces A by 4, Z by 2.

Beta (β⁻) Decay

Process: ¹⁴C → ¹⁴N + e⁻ + ν̄

Neutron converts to proton. Increases Z by 1, A unchanged.

Beta (β⁺) Decay

Process: Proton converts to neutron. Decreases Z by 1, A unchanged.

Gamma (γ) Decay

Process: Emission of high-energy photons. No change in A or Z, only excitation energy released.

Radioactive Decay Laws

N(t) = N₀e^(-λt)

Where:
N(t) = Number of nuclei remaining at time t
N₀ = Initial number of nuclei
λ = Decay constant (probability per unit time)
Half-life: T₁/₂ = ln(2)/λ = 0.693/λ

Time for half of the sample to decay.
Activity: A = λN

Number of decays per unit time (measured in Becquerel or Curie).
1 Bq = 1 decay/s
1 Ci = 3.7×10¹⁰ decays/s
Decay Series: Heavy unstable nuclei undergo successive decays (uranium series, thorium series, etc.) producing various daughter products until a stable nucleus is reached.

Nuclear Fission

Fission Process

Splitting of a heavy nucleus into lighter fragments:

²³⁵U + n → Fragment₁ + Fragment₂ + 2-3n + 200 MeV (approx.)

Example: ²³⁵U + n → ⁹²Kr + ¹⁴¹Ba + 3n + energy
Energy Release: ~200 MeV per fission event. This enormous energy (compared to chemical reactions ~eV) comes from the conversion of mass to energy.

Chain Reaction and Critical Mass

Each fission releases 2-3 neutrons that can trigger more fissions:

Condition Description
Subcritical Mass too small; neutrons escape before causing fission
Critical One neutron per fission causes another fission (sustained reaction)
Supercritical More than one neutron per fission causes other fissions (explosive)
Critical Mass: Minimum mass needed for sustained chain reaction. For U-235 with ordinary moderation: ~1 kg.

Applications

Nuclear Reactors

Controlled fission for electricity generation. Control rods regulate reaction rate.

Nuclear Weapons

Uncontrolled chain reaction. Supercritical mass assembled rapidly.

Nuclear Fusion

Fusion Reaction

Combining of light nuclei to form heavier nucleus:

4 ¹H → ⁴He + 2e⁺ + 2νₑ + 26.7 MeV

Overall reaction in stars (pp chain at stellar core temperatures)
Energy Release: ~26.7 MeV for hydrogen fusion to helium. Higher energy per nucleon ratio makes it highly efficient.

Conditions for Fusion

Requirement Reason
Very High Temperature To overcome Coulomb repulsion between positive nuclei (~10⁷ K)
High Density To increase collision probability
Confinement Magnetic or inertial confinement to maintain conditions

Fusion in Stars and Universe

The pp chain (Proton-Proton chain): Primary energy source in stars like our Sun.

Step 1: ¹H + ¹H → ²H + e⁺ + νₑ
Step 2: ²H + ¹H → ³He + γ
Step 3: ³He + ³He → ⁴He + 2 ¹H + γ

Net: 4 ¹H → ⁴He + energy (~26.7 MeV)
Stellar Energy: The Sun converts ~600 million tons of hydrogen per second into helium, releasing energy that sustains all life on Earth. This process has been ongoing for ~5 billion years and will continue for ~5 billion more.

Controlled Fusion

Magnetic Confinement

Uses strong magnetic fields (tokamaks, stellarators) to confine hot plasma

Inertial Confinement

Uses high-intensity lasers or particle beams to compress fuel rapidly

Assessment Quizzes

Quiz 1: Nuclear Structure Basics

Q1: A nucleus has mass number A = 27. If R₀ = 1.2 fm, what is its approximate radius?
A) 3.0 fm
B) 3.6 fm
C) 4.2 fm
D) 5.0 fm

Answer: B) 3.6 fm [Using R = R₀A^(1/3) = 1.2 × 3 = 3.6 fm]

Quiz 2: Binding Energy

Q2: The binding energy per nucleon curve peaks at:
A) Helium-4
B) Iron-56
C) Uranium-235
D) Carbon-12

Answer: B) Iron-56 [Fe-56 has maximum binding energy per nucleon (~8.8 MeV), making it the most stable nucleus]

Quiz 3: Radioactive Decay

Q3: A radioactive sample has 1000 nuclei with half-life of 10 days. How many nuclei remain after 30 days?
A) 500
B) 250
C) 125
D) 62.5

Answer: C) 125 [After 30 days = 3 half-lives; 1000 → 500 → 250 → 125]

Quiz 4: Nuclear Fission

Q4: What is the approximate energy released per U-235 fission event?
A) 2 MeV
B) 50 MeV
C) 200 MeV
D) 2000 MeV

Answer: C) 200 MeV [Typical fission energy is approximately 200 MeV]

Quiz 5: Nuclear Fusion

Q5: What is the primary fusion reaction occurring in the Sun?
A) Deuterium + Tritium → Helium
B) 4 Protons → Helium + energy
C) Carbon-12 + Proton → Nitrogen-13
D) Boron-11 + Proton → Carbon-12

Answer: B) 4 Protons → Helium + energy [This is the pp chain that powers the Sun]

Quiz 6: Decay Constants

Q6: If λ = 0.1 s⁻¹, what is the half-life of the radioactive substance?
A) 6.93 s
B) 69.3 s
C) 10 s
D) 100 s

Answer: A) 6.93 s [T₁/₂ = 0.693/λ = 0.693/0.1 = 6.93 s]

Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS)

HOTS 1: Why is Iron-56 the Most Stable Nucleus?

Scenario: Consider a nucleus with 56 nucleons. Why doesn't it undergo fission despite having significant internal energy?

Analysis: Iron-56 sits at the peak of the binding energy per nucleon curve. This means it requires the most energy to disassemble on a per-nucleon basis. Further fission would produce lighter nuclei with lower total binding energy (less tightly bound), requiring energy input. Conversely, fusion of lighter nuclei to form Fe-56 releases energy. The saturation property of nuclear forces ensures that adding more nucleons beyond this point becomes energetically unfavorable.

HOTS 2: Chain Reaction Criticality Analysis

Scenario: Why does a nuclear reaction suddenly become explosive when crossing the critical mass threshold?

Analysis: Below critical mass, neutrons escape the sample faster than fission creates new ones (neutron leakage). The reaction rate decreases exponentially. At critical mass, the neutron multiplication factor equals 1 (each fission produces exactly one daughter fission). Beyond critical mass, neutrons accumulate exponentially: 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 16... This exponential growth occurs in microseconds, releasing enormous energy nearly instantaneously, causing an explosion.

HOTS 3: Fusion Requirements vs Fission Requirements

Scenario: Why does fusion require extreme temperatures (~10⁷ K) while fission only requires a neutron?

Analysis: Fusion involves overcoming the Coulomb repulsion barrier between positively charged nuclei. For example, two protons repel each other strongly until they come within ~1 fm where nuclear attraction dominates. The temperature must be such that thermal energy (kT) approaches the barrier height (~1 MeV). Fission of U-235 is different: a thermal neutron is absorbed, adding binding energy that destabilizes the nucleus (it becomes deformed and elongated). The Coulomb repulsion overcomes surface tension, causing fission. No extreme conditions are needed.

Case-Based Problems

Case Study 1: Radon Gas Decay in Homes

Background: Radon (Rn-222) is a radioactive gas that seeps into homes from soil. It has a half-life of 3.8 days. A room contains radon with initial activity of 400 Bq.

Questions:

  1. Calculate the decay constant λ for Rn-222.
  2. What is the activity after 7.6 days (two half-lives)?
  3. How long until the activity drops to 50 Bq?

Solutions:
1) λ = 0.693/T₁/₂ = 0.693/(3.8×24×3600 s) = 2.1×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹
2) After two half-lives: A = 400/4 = 100 Bq
3) Use N(t) = N₀e^(-λt): 50 = 400e^(-λt) → t = 9.0 days (about 2.4 half-lives)

Case Study 2: Energy from Nuclear Fusion in the Sun

Background: The Sun's core fuses ~620 million tons of hydrogen per second. Each fusion of 4 protons into helium releases 26.7 MeV.

Questions:

  1. How many fusion reactions occur per second in the Sun's core?
  2. Calculate the power output from fusion (in watts).
  3. If this continues for another 5 billion years, how much total hydrogen will be consumed?

Solutions:
1) 620×10⁹ kg/s ÷ (4×1.67×10⁻²⁷ kg) = 9.3×10³⁷ reactions/s
2) Power = 9.3×10³⁷ × 26.7 MeV × 1.6×10⁻¹³ J/MeV ≈ 3.8×10²⁶ W (actual: 3.8×10²⁶ W)
3) 620×10⁹ kg/s × 3600 s/hr × 24 hr/day × 365.25 day/year × 5×10⁹ years ≈ 10²⁰ kg

Case Study 3: Nuclear Reactor Design

Background: A nuclear reactor uses U-235 enriched to 3% (typical for power reactors). Each fission releases ~200 MeV. The reactor operates at 1000 MW thermal power.

Questions:

  1. How many fission reactions per second are needed for 1000 MW output?
  2. How much mass is converted to energy per day (E=mc²)?
  3. Why is enrichment needed, rather than using natural uranium (0.7% U-235)?

Solutions:
1) 10⁹ W ÷ (200 MeV × 1.6×10⁻¹³ J/MeV) = 3.1×10¹⁹ fissions/s
2) Energy per day = 10⁹ W × 86400 s = 8.64×10¹⁶ J; mass = 8.64×10¹⁶ J ÷ (3×10⁸)² kg ≈ 1 kg
3) Natural uranium cannot sustain a chain reaction without enrichment because too many neutrons are absorbed by non-fissile U-238 without causing fission. Enriched fuel increases the probability of neutrons hitting U-235, allowing critical mass to be achieved.
