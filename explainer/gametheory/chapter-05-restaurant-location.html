Chapter 5: Restaurant Location Games

Example — Spatial Competition with Variable Demand

🍣 The Sushi War: When Competition Creates Customers

Two sushi restaurant chains — Ann's and Beth's — are about to open on a small island of interconnected towns. Unlike the doctor game from Chapter 4, where every patient must see a doctor regardless of location, here demand is elastic. People only eat sushi if a restaurant is nearby.

Key Insight: When restaurants spread apart, the total number of customers increases (each captures their own neighborhood). When they cluster together, they cannibalize each other's market. This creates a fascinating game where selfish behavior often aligns with social welfare — a sharp contrast to the doctor game!

Three Key Scenarios

Scenario 1: Same Town

Both restaurants open in town X.

What happens: They split town X's residents 50/50. Residents in adjacent towns visit X at half frequency, splitting that half between both restaurants.

Each restaurant gets: $$\frac{1}{2} + \frac{d(x)}{4}$$ where d(x) is the degree of town X (number of neighbors).

Example: If town X has 4 neighboring towns, each restaurant serves: 0.5 + 1 = 1.5 town-equivalents.

Scenario 2: Adjacent Towns

Ann's restaurant in town X, Beth's in a neighboring town Y.

What happens: There's partial overlap — residents in towns adjacent to both X and Y can reach either restaurant. Each restaurant owns their home town but competes for the overlap zones.

Ann gets: $$\frac{1}{2} + \frac{d(x)}{2} - \frac{n(x,y)}{4}$$ and Beth gets: $$\frac{1}{2} + \frac{d(y)}{2} - \frac{n(x,y)}{4}$$

where n(x,y) = number of common neighbors of X and Y.

Example: If X has degree 3, Y has degree 3, and they share 1 common neighbor: Ann gets 0.5 + 1.5 - 0.25 = 1.75.

Scenario 3: Far Apart

Ann and Beth locate in non-adjacent towns X and Y.

What happens: No direct overlap. Each restaurant captures its own neighborhood completely. Total customers are maximized!

Ann gets: $$1 + \frac{d(x)}{2} - \frac{n(x,y)}{4}$$ and Beth gets: $$1 + \frac{d(y)}{2} - \frac{n(x,y)}{4}$$

Example: X and Y both degree 2, no common neighbors: Ann gets 1 + 1 - 0 = 2 town-equivalents.

The "Invisible Hand" Insight

Unlike doctor games, selfish restaurant placement tends to also be good for society. When both Ann and Beth seek maximum payoff, they often spread out, which simultaneously maximizes the total customers served. This is a case where competition and social welfare align naturally — though this property depends heavily on the town graph's structure.

📐 Formalizing Spatial Competition

Game Definition

$$\Gamma = \langle \{Ann, Beth\}, V, u \rangle$$

where V is the set of vertices (towns), and each player picks a vertex to place their restaurant.

Payoff Formulas

Let:

  • $$d(x)$$ = degree of vertex x (number of neighboring towns)
  • $$n(x,y)$$ = number of common neighbors of x and y
The payoff function depends on the relationship between Ann's location $$x$$ and Beth's location $$y$$:

Same location (x = y): $$u_{\text{Ann}}(x, x) = u_{\text{Beth}}(x, x) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{d(x)}{4}$$

Adjacent (x ∼ y): $$u_{\text{Ann}}(x, y) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{d(x)}{2} - \frac{n(x,y)}{4}$$ $$u_{\text{Beth}}(x, y) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{d(y)}{2} - \frac{n(x,y)}{4}$$

Non-adjacent (x ≁ y): $$u_{\text{Ann}}(x, y) = 1 + \frac{d(x)}{2} - \frac{n(x,y)}{4}$$ $$u_{\text{Beth}}(x, y) = 1 + \frac{d(y)}{2} - \frac{n(x,y)}{4}$$

Competition Penalty Function

We can unify these cases with a "competition penalty" function g(x,y) that represents the reduction in payoff due to proximity:

$$g(x,y) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2} + \frac{d(x)}{4} & \text{if } x = y \text{ (full overlap)} \\ \frac{1}{2} + \frac{n(x,y)}{4} & \text{if } x \sim y \text{ (adjacent)} \\ \frac{n(x,y)}{4} & \text{if } x \not\sim y \text{ (non-adjacent)} \end{cases}$$

Key Theorems

Theorem 1: Maximin Security

Claim: The maximin move for any player is to locate at a vertex with maximum degree Δ.

Why: The worst case for Ann is when Beth co-locates with her, giving payoff ½ + d(x)/4. To maximize this worst-case payoff, Ann should choose the vertex with the highest degree.

Security Level: $$v_i = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\Delta}{4}$$

Theorem 2: Existence of Pure Nash Equilibrium (Prisner 2011)

Claim: Every simultaneous restaurant location game on any finite graph has at least one pure Nash equilibrium.

Proof sketch: Consider the best-response function. Any finite digraph of best responses must contain a cycle (by the pigeonhole principle applied to acyclic chains). Any cycle in the best-response digraph yields at least one NE pair through a careful analysis of mutual best responses.

Implication: Unlike many games with potential mixed-strategy intricacy, restaurant location games always have a "clean" pure NE.

Theorem 3: Non-Constant-Sum Structure

Claim: Restaurant location games are NOT constant-sum. Total payoff varies with location choices.

Proof: Consider two towns X and Y both with degree 2, non-adjacent.

  • If Ann and Beth co-locate in X: Each gets 0.5 + 0.5 = 1. Total = 2.
  • If they locate in X and Y (non-adjacent): Each gets 1 + 0.5 = 1.5. Total = 3.

The game is variable-sum, with total payoff depending on strategic choices.

Nash Equilibrium Discovery via Best Response

To find all pure Nash equilibria:

  1. For each vertex a, find all best responses for Ann (vertices that maximize her payoff against Beth's choice).
  2. For each vertex b, find all best responses for Beth (vertices that maximize her payoff against Ann's choice).
  3. A pair (a, b) is a NE if and only if a is a best response to b AND b is a best response to a.
  4. Alternatively, follow the best-response digraph: if you encounter a cycle, every edge pair in that cycle corresponds to a NE.

Why Not Constant-Sum?

In the doctor game, demand is fixed: N patients must see doctors. Total payoff is always N, regardless of location choices. Here, demand is elastic: if restaurants are far apart, more people eat sushi. If they cluster, fewer people bother. This fundamental difference makes the game's incentive structure qualitatively different from zero-sum competition.

Python Reference Code

import numpy as np
from itertools import combinations

class RestaurantLocationGame:
    def __init__(self, n_vertices, edges):
        self.n = n_vertices
        self.adj = [set() for _ in range(n_vertices)]
        for u, v in edges:
            self.adj[u].add(v)
            self.adj[v].add(u)

    def degree(self, v):
        """Return the degree (number of neighbors) of vertex v."""
        return len(self.adj[v])

    def common_neighbors(self, x, y):
        """Return the number of common neighbors between x and y."""
        return len(self.adj[x] & self.adj[y])

    def are_adjacent(self, x, y):
        """Check if vertices x and y are adjacent."""
        return y in self.adj[x]

    def payoff(self, ann, beth):
        """Compute payoff for Ann and Beth given their locations."""
        dx = self.degree(ann)
        dy = self.degree(beth)
        nxy = self.common_neighbors(ann, beth)

        if ann == beth:
            p = 0.5 + dx / 4
            return (p, p)
        elif self.are_adjacent(ann, beth):
            ann_payoff = 0.5 + dx/2 - nxy/4
            beth_payoff = 0.5 + dy/2 - nxy/4
            return (ann_payoff, beth_payoff)
        else:
            ann_payoff = 1 + dx/2 - nxy/4
            beth_payoff = 1 + dy/2 - nxy/4
            return (ann_payoff, beth_payoff)

    def payoff_matrix(self):
        """Compute the full n x n payoff matrix."""
        matrix = {}
        for a in range(self.n):
            for b in range(self.n):
                matrix[(a,b)] = self.payoff(a, b)
        return matrix

    def find_nash_equilibria(self):
        """Find all pure Nash equilibria."""
        ne = []
        matrix = self.payoff_matrix()

        for a in range(self.n):
            for b in range(self.n):
                ann_payoff = matrix[(a,b)][0]
                beth_payoff = matrix[(a,b)][1]

                ann_br = all(matrix[(a2,b)][0] <= ann_payoff + 1e-9
                            for a2 in range(self.n))
                beth_br = all(matrix[(a,b2)][1] <= beth_payoff + 1e-9
                             for b2 in range(self.n))

                if ann_br and beth_br:
                    ne.append((a, b))

        return ne

    def maximin_moves(self):
        """Find vertices with maximum degree (maximin strategies)."""
        max_deg = max(self.degree(v) for v in range(self.n))
        return [v for v in range(self.n) if self.degree(v) == max_deg]

    def security_level(self):
        """Compute the maximin security level."""
        max_deg = max(self.degree(v) for v in range(self.n))
        return 0.5 + max_deg / 4

# Example usage:
edges = [(0,1), (0,2), (0,6), (1,2), (1,4), (1,6), (2,3), (2,4),
         (3,4), (4,5), (5,6)]
game = RestaurantLocationGame(7, edges)

print("Nash Equilibria:", game.find_nash_equilibria())
print("Maximin Moves:", game.maximin_moves())
print("Security Level:", game.security_level())

🤔 Strategic Reflection

Test your understanding with these thought-provoking questions. Click each question to reveal the answer.

1. Why is the restaurant game NOT constant-sum, unlike the doctor game? +

In doctor games, demand is fixed: N patients must see doctors regardless of location. The total "value" generated is always N, so if Ann gains customers, Beth loses them. It's a zero-sum competition.

In restaurant games, demand is elastic: people only eat sushi if a restaurant is nearby. So the total number of sushi eaters depends on where restaurants locate. If Ann and Beth spread out, each captures their own neighborhood and more people total eat sushi. If they cluster together, they might cannibalize each other and fewer people bother eating sushi at all.

The key difference: Spacing restaurants increases the total pie; spacing doctors does not.

2. The invisible hand seems to work here — restaurants choosing selfishly also maximizes social welfare. Why might this break down? +

You're right that in many simple graphs, selfish placement naturally spreads restaurants apart, which maximizes total customers. This happens because a restaurant's own payoff increases when it spreads away from competitors.

However, this doesn't always hold on all graph structures. On certain topologies:

  • Highly clustered graphs: The Nash equilibrium might force both restaurants into a dense region because leaving that region exposes you to the competitor's dominance elsewhere.
  • Bottleneck structures: Certain vertices might be so critical that both players want them, and equilibrium clustering is unavoidable.
  • Asymmetric graphs: One player might gain more from clustering near high-degree vertices, even if it reduces total payoff.

The general principle is: decentralization is often good, but it's not guaranteed on all topologies.

3. Why are maximin moves always at maximum-degree vertices? +

The maximin strategy for Ann is to choose the location that maximizes her worst-case payoff (assuming Beth plays optimally against her).

Ann's worst case occurs when Beth co-locates with her (direct competition). If Ann is at vertex x, and Beth also chooses x, Ann gets payoff ½ + d(x)/4.

To maximize this worst case, Ann wants to choose x with the largest degree. A high-degree vertex means more neighboring towns, so even in the worst case (co-location), Ann still serves many customers.

Intuition: If you're forced to share a location, you want it to be in the busiest, most connected town possible.

Maximin Security Level: ½ + Δ/4, where Δ is the maximum degree in the graph.

4. The theorem says pure NE always exist. We proved this by following best-response chains. Why does finding a cycle guarantee a NE? +

A best-response chain follows the best response digraph: from vertex a, draw an arrow to a best response vertex for Ann against Beth at a, then switch roles, and repeat.

In a finite graph, any acyclic chain must eventually repeat a vertex. When it does, we've found a cycle in the best-response digraph.

Suppose the cycle is: a₀ → a₁ → a₂ → ... → aₖ → a₀, where aᵢ is a best response to aᵢ₋₁ for some player.

The key insight: at any pair in the cycle, each player is already playing a best response to the other. For example, if a₀ is a best response for Ann to a₁, and a₁ is a best response for Beth to a₂, then the payoff structure forces certain equalities. By analyzing these equalities across the cycle, we can show that every edge pair in the cycle actually satisfies the NE condition: each player has no incentive to deviate.

Conclusion: Cycles in the best-response digraph don't just hint at equilibria — they guarantee them.

5. Compare a path graph P₅ and a cycle C₅. How does adding the closing edge change equilibria and social welfare? +

Path Graph P₅ (vertices 1-2-3-4-5):

  • Vertex degrees: endpoints have d=1, center has d=2.
  • Maximin moves: vertex 3 (center, degree 2). Maximin payoff: 0.5 + 0.5 = 1.
  • Nash equilibria typically include placing restaurants at vertices 2 and 4 (symmetric spread with room between them).
  • Intuition: spreading avoids overlap; endpoints are weak (low degree).

Cycle Graph C₅ (vertices 1-2-3-4-5-1):

  • All vertices have degree 2 (symmetric!).
  • Maximin payoff: 0.5 + 0.5 = 1 (same as P₅, by coincidence).
  • More NE pairs exist due to symmetry. The closing edge creates more non-adjacent pairs.
  • Players have more flexibility; spreading is still preferred but easier to achieve.

Effect of the Closing Edge (5-1):

  • Payoff changes: Vertices 1 and 5 now have degree 2 instead of 1. Common neighbors increase.
  • Non-adjacent pairs increase: In P₅, there are few non-adjacent pairs; in C₅, every pair is non-adjacent except the 5 edge pairs. Non-adjacent pairs yield higher payoffs (1 + d(x)/2 instead of 0.5 + d(x)/2).
  • Social welfare: Generally increases because both players can achieve higher individual payoffs through spreading (the 1 + d(x)/2 option is now available more often).
  • NE characterization: C₅ has more Nash equilibria due to symmetry and more non-adjacent options; P₅ is more constrained.

Key Lesson: Graph topology fundamentally shapes the game's strategic landscape. Adding a single edge can create or destroy equilibria and change players' incentives.
