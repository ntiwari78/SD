🌉 The Feynman Bridge — What Is a Game, Really?

Imagine you're at a traffic intersection with no traffic lights. You're in your car, another driver is approaching from the left. You both need to decide — simultaneously — whether to go or wait. If you both go, crash. If you both wait, you sit there awkwardly. If one goes and the other waits, traffic flows.

Congratulations — you just played a game.

But before you reach for the Monopoly board, let's be clear: this isn't about fun. In mathematics, a "game" is about structure.

At that intersection, you're dealing with:

Players: You and the other driver.
Choices: Each of you can go or wait.
Outcome: Whatever combination of decisions results from your simultaneous choices.
Payoffs: Getting through quickly feels good. Crashing feels very bad. Sitting awkwardly is neutral.
Uncertainty: You don't know what the other person will do.

Here's the key insight: Game theory is the mathematics of decisions made by multiple minds, each trying to optimize their own outcome while predicting what others will do. It's different from optimization or puzzle-solving because you're not playing against nature or logic — you're playing against other rational agents who are trying to predict you at the same time.

Think of a game as a machine. You feed in decisions from all players, and it spits out consequences. The machine's blueprint is the rules. One run of the machine is a play. Each decision point where you make a choice is a position, and your choice is a move. And a strategy? That's your complete instruction manual — "if I see THIS, I do THAT" — for every possible situation you might encounter.

"What makes game theory different from puzzles or optimization is OTHER MINDS. You're not just solving a math problem — you're predicting what a rational person will do, knowing that they're trying to predict what YOU will do."

This chapter is the map legend. Before we explore territories like Nash Equilibrium or Backward Induction, we need to know what the symbols mean.

📐 The Mathematical Ascent — Formalizing the Game

2.1 Formal Definition of a Game

A game in strategic (normal) form is defined as a tuple:

$$\Gamma = \langle N, (S_i)_{i \in N}, (u_i)_{i \in N} \rangle$$

where:

$N = \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ is the finite set of players.

$S_i$ is the set of strategies (or actions) available to player $i$. A strategy $s_i \in S_i$ is a complete plan of action for player $i$ throughout the game.

$u_i : S_1 \times S_2 \times \cdots \times S_n \to \mathbb{R}$ is the payoff function for player $i$, mapping every possible outcome (strategy profile) to a real number representing player $i$'s "satisfaction" or "utility."

2.2 Key Definitions

Strategy Profile (Outcome)

A strategy profile is a tuple $s = (s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n) \in S_1 \times S_2 \times \cdots \times S_n$ where $s_i$ is the strategy chosen by player $i$. This profile fully specifies the outcome of the game.

Payoff

The payoff to player $i$ from outcome $s$ is the real number $u_i(s)$. Higher payoffs represent better outcomes for that player (though payoffs need not be comparable across players).

Zero-Sum Game

A game is zero-sum if for every strategy profile $s$, we have $$\sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i(s) = 0$$ This means one player's gain is exactly another's loss. In a two-player zero-sum game, $u_1(s) + u_2(s) = 0$ always.

General-Sum Game

A game where the total payoff $\sum_i u_i(s)$ may vary across different outcomes. Most real-world situations are general-sum: you can create value (both sides better off) or destroy it (both worse off).

Rational Behavior

Each player $i$ chooses a strategy $s_i$ to maximize their payoff $u_i(s)$, under the assumption that all other players are also acting rationally. This is the foundation of strategic reasoning in game theory.

🏛️ The Socratic Sandbox — Test Your Understanding

Below are five progressively challenging questions about game theory concepts from this chapter. Click on any question to reveal the answer.

1
You're playing poker online with 5 other players. How many players are in this game? Is it simultaneous or sequential? Does it have perfect information? Random moves? Is it zero-sum?
▼

Number of Players: 6 players (you + 5 others).

Timing: Mixed. Within each round, betting is sequential (players take turns). But the hand itself is dealt simultaneously (both cards appear at once). So it's primarily sequential.

Perfect Information: No, imperfect. Other players' hole cards are hidden until showdown. This is crucial — if everyone could see everyone's cards, optimal play would be trivial.

Random Moves: Yes, stochastic. The deck shuffle and card dealing introduce randomness. "Nature" makes moves.

Zero-Sum: Yes (mostly). In principle, what one player wins, others lose. But in reality, the house takes a rake, so it's technically general-sum (total shrinks due to fees).

2
An English auction: A painting is being auctioned. Bidders publicly announce prices. The price rises until only one bidder remains. Classify this game along all seven axes.
▼

Players: n bidders (varies).

Timing: Sequential. Bidders take turns raising the price. Each decision depends on previous bids.

Information: Perfect. All bids are public. Every bidder sees every previous offer.

Randomness: Deterministic. No randomness in the auction mechanism itself (though bidders' willingness to pay can seem "random").

Payoff Structure: General-sum. The total value is fixed (the painting's worth), but the distribution varies. Winner gets high value (gets the painting below their max bid). Losers get zero. Seller benefits. Not zero-sum.

Cooperation: Non-cooperative. Bidders cannot make binding agreements to rig bids (that's illegal collusion). They compete.

Information Completeness: Incomplete. Bidders don't know each other's true valuations. A rich collector and a speculator may value the same painting differently.

3
In rock-paper-scissors, your friend claims "I have a pure strategy that always wins." Why is this impossible? What does this tell us about the game's structure?
▼

Why It's Impossible: Suppose your friend always plays Rock. You simply play Paper every time and win. So there is no pure strategy that beats all others. Any pure strategy (Rock, Paper, or Scissors) can be countered.

What This Means: Rock-paper-scissors has no dominant strategy. The only way to guarantee long-term success is to randomize — play each option equally often (1/3 each). This is a mixed strategy, not a pure one.

The Deep Insight: This game foreshadows the concept of a Nash equilibrium, which often requires mixed strategies (randomization). Chapter 27 on Mixed Strategies will explore this formally. For now: sometimes, the optimal play is to play unpredictably.

4
Two rival coffee shops must decide their morning opening time: 6am, 7am, or 8am. Customers prefer the earlier-opening shop. If both open at the same time, they split customers equally. Model this as a game: define players, strategies, and payoffs. Is it zero-sum?
▼

Players: {Shop A, Shop B}

Strategies: For each shop: {6am, 7am, 8am}

Payoff Structure (one possible model):

If Shop A opens before Shop B: Shop A gets 70% of morning customers, Shop B gets 30%.
If both open at same time: Each gets 50%.
Total "morning customers" = 100 (constant).

Is it zero-sum? No, it's general-sum. The total payoff varies with the outcome. If both open at 8am, fewer early customers arrive (because some never go), so the total is say 80. If both open at 6am, more customers arrive (120). One player's gain is NOT exactly another's loss.

Insight: This is a sequential move game in practice: the first mover (who opens earlier) captures more value. It illustrates the importance of timing and the advantage of moving first.

5
Prisner writes that game theory "doesn't even acknowledge the existence of cheating." Why might this be both a strength AND a weakness of the mathematical model?
▼

STRENGTH: By assuming all players follow the rules, game theory avoids infinite regress and complexity. It can focus on rational strategic reasoning within a well-defined framework. This produces clean, mathematically tractable results. You can prove theorems (like the existence of Nash Equilibrium) without worrying about players breaking the rules.

WEAKNESS: Real-world "games" — business, politics, international relations — often involve rule-breaking, deception, and norm violation. A pure game-theoretic strategy derived assuming no cheating may fail catastrophically against an unscrupulous opponent. For example, a strategy that works in chess (which strictly enforces rules) might fail in poker or diplomacy, where bluffing and hidden information abound.

The Tension: This is the fundamental gap between model and reality. Game theory gives us the tools to analyze ideal competition, but we must always ask: How much do real players deviate from rationality? How much do they break the rules? This question haunts game theory throughout Prisner's book and remains an active research area.
