The Four-Card Game — Bluffing with Nuance

① ② ③ ④

The Feynman Bridge

You've mastered the simple two-card poker game from Chapter 31. Ann and Beth each got one card from {1, 2}, and the strategic landscape was clean: bluff rarely with 1, value-bet always with 2. Everything had a symmetry, a clarity.

Now imagine you walk into a real poker night. The dealer hands you a card from {1, 2, 3, 4}. Suddenly the world is richer and messier. You can't just divide hands into "bluff" and "value." What about 3? Is it strong or weak? Against which opponents?

The tic-tac-toe to chess analogy: In tic-tac-toe, every game looks the same after a few moves. There are only a few truly different positions. Chess, by contrast, explodes into countless branches. The number of possible games grows exponentially. Likewise, jumping from 2 card values to 4 seems small numerically, but strategically it's like moving from a children's game to a complex sport. Ann now has 2^4 = 16 pure strategies instead of 4. Beth has 16 as well. The normal form game is 16×16 instead of 4×4. And importantly, the middle cards (2 and 3) become zones of ambiguity. They're neither clearly strong nor clearly weak. This is where bluffing becomes an art.

Why Middle Cards Matter

In the 2-card game, there was no "middle ground." Card 1 was bad (bluff), card 2 was good (bet). In the 4-card game, cards 2 and 3 sit in a strategic grey zone. Against what distribution of opponent hands should you raise with 3? If the opponent is very tight (only raising with 4, and rarely with others), then 3 is a strong hand—you should raise and expect a fold or a weak call. But if the opponent raises with any card, then 3 becomes weak and you should fold or call cautiously.

The Nash equilibrium will answer this precisely: it will tell you the optimal mixing probabilities for cards 2 and 3, given the ratio n/m = 5/3. The equilibrium achieves a balance where neither player can improve by deviating.

The Parameters

S = 4: Four card values: 1 (weakest), 2, 3, 4 (strongest).

r = 4: Four copies of each value in the deck. Total deck size: 16 cards.

m = 3: Initial bet. Ann must decide whether to raise (bet an additional n − m = 2) or check.

n = 5: Raise bet. If Ann raises, the bet jumps to 5. Beth then decides to call (matching the 5) or fold.

The ratio n/m = 5/3 ≈ 1.667. This is the key parameter that drives the equilibrium structure. It's less than 2 (which was the threshold in the 2-card game), so we're in a regime where bluffing is "attractive"—there's a positive probability of raising with weak cards.

Your journey in this chapter is to understand how these four card values, combined with the specific betting structure, create a rich equilibrium with subtle bluffing opportunities and defensive strategies.

Socratic Sandbox

Explore deeper questions about the 4-card poker game by reading and reflecting on these prompts.

Q1: Why do middle card values create more strategic complexity?
▼

In the 2-card game, the strategic choice was binary: you either had the weak card (bluff) or the strong card (bet). But with 4 cards, there's a continuum. Card 1 is clearly weak, card 4 is clearly strong, but cards 2 and 3 are ambiguous. Their relative strength depends entirely on what you think the opponent has.

For example, if you're raising with card 3, is that a value bet (betting good hands) or a bluff (betting weak hands)? It depends on the opponent's hand distribution. Against an opponent who folds frequently, card 3 is valuable. Against one who calls frequently, card 3 is weak.

This ambiguity forces the equilibrium to use mixing on the middle cards. Ann will raise with card 3 some fraction of the time, not always or never. This mixture is precisely calibrated so that Beth is indifferent between calling and folding—a hallmark of Nash equilibrium. The complexity arises because the game must coordinate all four cards simultaneously; changing the raising probability on card 1 affects the optimal call probability on card 1 for Beth, which in turn affects Ann's incentives with card 2, and so on.

Q2: How does the n/m ratio determine whether bluffing is profitable?
▼

The ratio n/m = 5/3 is the crux. It tells you the "pot odds" that the opponent must overcome to make a call profitable.

If n/m is very large (e.g., n=10, m=1), then the pot odds are huge. You're only risking 1 to win potentially 10. In this case, the opponent should call frequently, and bluffing becomes unprofitable. Conversely, if n/m is close to 1 (e.g., n=1.1, m=1), the bet is tiny, and the opponent can afford to fold unless they have a strong hand. Bluffing becomes very profitable.

The number 5/3 ≈ 1.667 is in the "intermediate" range. It's less than 2 (which was the transition point in the 2-card game), so there is some positive probability of bluffing in equilibrium. With a bet size of 5 after checking-raising, the pot odds are 5-to-3, meaning Beth is risking 5 to win 3 + 3 = 6 (the initial bet plus Ann's raise). So Beth breaks even if she wins more than 5/(5+6) ≈ 45% of the time. This is achievable, so Beth should call sometimes, making bluffing viable for Ann.

Q3: Why might a pure strategy outperform Nash equilibrium against a specific opponent?
▼

The Nash equilibrium is a mutual best response—if the opponent is playing their equilibrium mix, you cannot do better by deviating. However, this assumes the opponent is actually playing that mix.

In reality, opponents have biases. Some always fold to a raise. Some always call. Some play too tight (raise too rarely). If you discover that your opponent calls too often, then a "pure bluff" strategy (raise with all hands) might do very well against them, even though it would be exploited by an equilibrium player.

For example, imagine you're facing an opponent who calls with probability 0.9 (very loose). The Nash-equilibrium mixing probabilities were designed assuming the opponent calls according to their equilibrium strategy. But if they call much more often, then your weak hands become more valuable as bluffs fail less often. A "value-heavy" pure strategy (raise with 3 and 4 only) might beat the loose opponent, despite being suboptimal against an equilibrium player.

This is why poker is dynamic: you must constantly adjust to your opponent's tendencies, not just play the mathematical equilibrium.

Q4: What changes when we go from 2 card values to 4? What stays the same?
▼

What changes:

  • The strategy space explodes from 4 to 16 pure strategies per player.
  • The equilibrium structure becomes richer: it may involve mixing on multiple card values (e.g., 1, 2, and 3), not just the extreme cards.
  • The middle cards (2, 3) now create distinct zones where the mixing probabilities must be calculated precisely.
  • The normal form game is 16×16 instead of 4×4, with many more payoff entries to compute.

What stays the same:

  • The core logic: a player raises/calls if they expect a positive payoff, and mixes if indifferent.
  • The role of the n/m ratio: it still determines the overall profitability of bluffing.
  • The existence of a Nash equilibrium: the game is still finite and symmetric, so an equilibrium exists.
  • The principle of indifference: in equilibrium, mixing probabilities are set so that the opponent is indifferent between their mixed actions.
  • The asymmetry: Ann (first mover) has an informational advantage and typically has a positive game value.
Q5: How do VNM insights apply to real hand range construction?
▼

In real poker, you don't have just 4 card values—you have hundreds of possible hands. But the VNM framework teaches you how to think about ranges and balance.

Range: Instead of "I have the 2," you think "I have a range of hands—some strong (pairs, high cards), some weak (low cards, misses)." The VNM game gives you the intellectual tool to reason about which hands should be in which part of the range.

Balance: The equilibrium teaches you that value bets (bets with strong hands that want to be called) and bluffs (bets with weak hands that want folds) should exist in a specific ratio. If you always bet strong hands and never bluff, opponents will fold to your bets. If you always bluff, opponents will call everything. The right balance—determined by pot odds and hand equity—is what makes you unpredictable and unexploitable.

Specific ratios: Professional poker players use the concept of "value:bluff ratio," often expressed as something like "2 value bets for every 1 bluff" or "3:1." This comes directly from game-theoretic thinking like VNM poker. The exact ratio depends on the bet size (the n/m ratio) and the probability of hands in your range.

By studying VNM poker, you're learning the foundational math that professional poker is built on.
