Feynman Bridge: The Roommate Alliance

Three Roommates Splitting Rent

Imagine three roommates—Ann, Beth, and Cindy—looking at a three-bedroom apartment. The landlord will offer different deals depending on which roommates sign the lease together. This is a coalition: any subset of players who band together to secure an advantage.

If all three sign, rent is $1,500. But the landlord hints: if only two sign, he'll discount it to $900 (because he can still fill the third bedroom at market rate separately). So Ann and Beth together guarantee themselves $900 savings on a two-bedroom. Cindy alone? She pays full price: nothing saved.

Why Coalitions Matter

Collective Bargaining Power: Two roommates negotiating together have more leverage than one. They can credibly walk away and find alternatives together.

The Core Concept: A "deal" (allocation of rent savings) is stable if no group of roommates wants to break away and negotiate separately. If Ann thinks she could do better by siding with Beth instead of the current arrangement, the current deal isn't stable—it's not in the Core.

The Shapley Value: A "fair" way to split the savings. Instead of arbitrary negotiation, it asks: "On average, how much does each roommate contribute?" Imagine bringing them in one at a time, in all possible orders, and averaging their marginal contribution.

Three Players, One Key Insight

With three players, coalitions are small and fragile. The "outside player"—the one not in the coalition—has bargaining power. They can threaten to offer a better deal to one coalition member, breaking up the alliance. This dynamic explains why coalitions form, dissolve, and reform.

The Shapley Value: Fair Division

The Shapley value is the average marginal contribution of a player across all possible orderings.

Shapley Value Formula:

For each player i, sum over all coalitions S not containing i, weighted by the probability that S formed just before i joined.

Computing Shapley Value for Ann

All 6 possible orderings (3! = 6), each with probability 1/6:

Order Join Sequence Marginal Contribution of Ann
1 Ann → Beth → Cindy v({Ann}) - v(∅) = 30 - 0 = 30
2 Ann → Cindy → Beth v({Ann}) - v(∅) = 30 - 0 = 30
3 Beth → Ann → Cindy v({Beth,Ann}) - v({Beth}) = 65 - 25 = 40
4 Beth → Cindy → Ann v({Beth,Cindy,Ann}) - v({Beth,Cindy}) = 100 - 55 = 45
5 Cindy → Ann → Beth v({Cindy,Ann}) - v({Cindy}) = 60 - 20 = 40
6 Cindy → Beth → Ann v({Cindy,Beth,Ann}) - v({Cindy,Beth}) = 100 - 55 = 45

φAnn = (30 + 30 + 40 + 45 + 40 + 45) / 6 = 230 / 6 ≈ 38.33

Core vs. Shapley Value

The Core: A set of feasible allocations. A point is in the Core if no coalition can block it.

The Shapley Value: A single point—one specific allocation that balances fairness based on average marginal contributions.

Relationship: The Shapley value often lies within the Core, but not always. When it does, it's a focal point for negotiation—a "natural" fair outcome.

Socratic Sandbox: Deeper Questions

Q1: Why is the grand coalition unstable even though it maximizes total payoff? ▼

The grand coalition earns 100 points total—the maximum. But individual payoffs within this coalition depend on internal negotiation. If the agreed split is (40, 35, 25), then Beth might think: "If I leave and form a 2-player coalition with Ann, we earn 65 together. We could split it 40-25, giving me 40 instead of 35. I'm better off defecting!"

The Core captures this: An allocation is stable only if no sub-coalition has incentive to break away. The grand coalition's allocation must respect all 2-player coalition constraints to be stable.

Q2: How does the Shapley value differ from an equal split? ▼

Equal Split: (100/3, 100/3, 100/3) ≈ (33.33, 33.33, 33.33). Everyone gets the same regardless of ability.

Shapley Value: (38.33, 33.33, 28.33). Weighted by average marginal contribution.

Why the difference? Ann is the strongest player (30 points solo vs. 25 for Beth, 20 for Cindy). When she joins a coalition, she often adds more value than others would. The Shapley value rewards her for that consistent strength while penalizing Cindy.

The Shapley value is efficient (sums to 100), individually rational (each player gets at least their solo payoff), and symmetric (treats identical players equally).

Q3: What happens when the Core is empty? ▼

Some games have no stable allocation. No matter how you split the total payoff, some coalition can block it by breaking away.

Example: Suppose v({A,B}) = 60, v({A,C}) = 60, v({B,C}) = 60, but v({A,B,C}) = 100. Then:

  • If x_A + x_B < 60, then {A,B} blocks (they can earn 60 together).
  • If x_A + x_C < 60, then {A,C} blocks.
  • If x_B + x_C < 60, then {B,C} blocks.

But x_A + x_B + x_A + x_C + x_B + x_C ≥ 180 while x_A + x_B + x_C = 100. Contradiction! The Core is empty.

Implication: In such games, coalitions form and dissolve cyclically. There's no rest point; the game is inherently unstable.

Q4: Why do outside players have bargaining power? ▼

Suppose Ann and Beth form a coalition and plan to split 65 points as (35, 30). Cindy, outside, observes this and offers: "Beth, I'll give you 35 if you team with me instead. Together we earn 55, so I'll take 20, you take 35."

Beth is indifferent (gets 35 either way), but she now has an outside offer to consider. Ann must either sweeten her offer to Beth or accept being left out.

The Mechanism: The outside player can bid away coalition members by offering them at least their current payoff plus a bit more. This "divide and conquer" strategy is only limited by what the outside player can actually afford (their value with a potential new coalition).

Q5: How does this model apply to real political coalitions? ▼

In a parliament, "payoff" can mean ministerial positions, committee seats, or legislative influence. Each party has a "power index" based on votes and negotiating strength—analogous to our coalition values.

Coalition Formation: A 51% coalition is stable only if the allocated ministries satisfy each party's outside option (what they could get by partnering with another coalition).

Shapley Value Application: Political scientists use Shapley-like indices (Banzhaf index, Shapley–Shubik index) to measure each party's "fair share" of power in voting bodies and coalitions. A party with 20% of votes may have only 10% of effective power if it's rarely pivotal in coalitions.

Real Challenge: Unlike our quiz show, political coalitions involve infinite strategic depth, shifting preferences, and agenda control. But the Core and Shapley value remain useful benchmarks for stability and fairness.
