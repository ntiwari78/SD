🎯 Feynman Bridge: The Intuition

Imagine playing chess, but after each move, a gust of wind randomly knocks over a piece. You still plan ahead, but now you can't just think about what your opponent will do — you also have to account for what random events might happen. That's exactly what sequential games with randomness are about.

Nature as the Third Player

In the sequential games we studied in Chapter 8, two players took turns making decisions. Every move was deterministic: if you decided to do X, then X happened. But in the real world, randomness intervenes. Markets crash without warning. Weather disrupts plans. Dice rolls determine outcomes.

The key insight: we can treat randomness as if it were another player. We call this player "Nature," and Nature makes moves according to probabilities, not strategy. When you hit a tennis ball, Nature (in the form of wind and gravity) randomly perturbs its trajectory. When you draw a card from a deck, Nature randomly determines which card emerges.

Once we model Nature as a probabilistic player, we can use the same backward induction technique from Chapter 8—but with one modification: instead of maximizing payoffs at random vertices, we compute expected payoffs as weighted averages.

Expected Payoffs Replace Certain Payoffs

Consider a simple bet: flip a fair coin. Heads, you win $100. Tails, you lose $50. What should you be willing to pay to enter this game?

Expected payoff = (1/2) × $100 + (1/2) × (−$50) = $25

Rational decision-makers use expected payoffs to evaluate situations with randomness. The backward induction algorithm remains the same—we just replace "payoff" with "expected payoff" at every random vertex.

Three Examples from the Textbook

1. Random NIM(n, p)

A pile of n stones. On your turn, you can remove 1 or 2 stones. But between each player's move, Nature randomly removes 0 or 1 stone (with probability p and 1−p). The player who can't move (stone pile empty) loses. Strategy depends on both the pile size and Nature's randomness!

2. Quiz Show Decision Problem

You're on a game show. There are 6 questions worth $1, $1, $2, $4, $8, $16. After each question you answer correctly, you can either:

  • Stop and keep your winnings
  • Risk it all on the next question

If you answer wrong, you lose everything. The probability of answering question n correctly is 1 - \frac{2}{9-n}. Backward induction reveals the surprising answer: you should stop before the highest-value question!

3. OH-NO Dice Game

Roll a die repeatedly. Each roll (2–6) adds that many dollars to your heap. Roll a 1 and you lose everything. You can stop anytime and keep what you've accumulated. What's the optimal strategy?

Through backward induction, we discover a critical threshold: stop whenever your heap reaches or exceeds $20. This magical number emerges from the expected value calculation, independent of any deeper strategy.

The Deep Structure: Kuhn's Theorem

In 1953, Harold Kuhn proved something remarkable: every finite perfect-information sequential game—even with randomness—has a unique solution computable by backward induction. This is the foundational result for analyzing games with incomplete information and chance events.

📐 Mathematical Ascent: Formalizing the Concepts

Extended Extensive Form

In an extensive-form game with randomness, the game tree contains three types of vertices:

Vertex Types:
  • Player vertices: A specific player chooses an action (outgoing arc)
  • Random vertices: Nature chooses, according to fixed probabilities on outgoing arcs
  • Terminal vertices: Game ends; payoffs are assigned to each player

At a random vertex V with successors s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_k having probabilities p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_k (where \sum p_i = 1):

$$E(V) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} p_i \cdot \text{value}(s_i)$$

At a player vertex, the player selects the successor that maximizes their expected payoff.

Backward Induction with Randomness

Algorithm:
  1. Find a vertex V with no assigned values but all successors have values.
  2. If V is a player vertex:
    • The player chooses the successor s^* that maximizes their expected payoff.
    • Copy the payoff vector at s^* to V.
  3. If V is a random vertex:
    • Compute the expected payoff as the weighted sum of successor values.
    • Assign this vector to V.
  4. Repeat until the root is reached.

Kuhn's Theorem (1953)

Theorem [K1953]: Every finite perfect-information sequential game—including those with random moves by Nature—has a unique solution (subgame-perfect equilibrium) that can be computed via backward induction using expected payoffs.

This theorem is the bedrock of game theory. It tells us that even when randomness is present, rational decision-making follows a consistent algorithm: optimize your expected payoff given what you know and what might happen.

OH-NO: The Critical Value Calculation

In the OH-NO dice game, let v(n) be the optimal value when your current heap is $n. At any state $n$, you choose between:

Stop: Keep $n$

Roll: Expected value = $$\frac{1}{6}(0 + (n+2) + (n+3) + (n+4) + (n+5) + (n+6))$$ where rolling a 1 (probability 1/6) gives $0, and rolling 2–6 (each probability 1/6) adds that amount.

Simplifying the rolling option:

$$\frac{1}{6}(5n + 20) = \frac{5n + 20}{6}$$

You roll if and only if:

$$\frac{5n + 20}{6} > n$$ $$5n + 20 > 6n$$ $$20 > n$$

Conclusion: Stop rolling when your heap reaches or exceeds $20. This critical value emerges purely from the mathematics of expectations—no deeper strategic reasoning needed.

Kelly Criterion: Optimal Betting Fraction

In repeated betting games with random outcomes, how much of your bankroll should you risk on each bet? The Kelly Criterion provides the answer:

Kelly Optimal Fraction:

$$f^* = \frac{p \cdot b - q}{b}$$ where:
  • p = probability of winning
  • q = 1 - p = probability of losing
  • b = net odds (profit on $1 bet)

The Kelly Criterion maximizes the long-run growth rate of wealth, specifically the expected value of log-wealth (geometric mean of returns). Betting more than f^* risks "gambler's ruin"; betting less leaves money on the table.

Example: You have a betting system that wins 55% of the time with even odds (b = 1):

$$f^* = \frac{0.55 \cdot 1 - 0.45}{1} = 0.10 = 10\%$$

You should bet 10% of your bankroll on each game. This strategy grows your wealth faster than any other fixed-fraction strategy in the long run.
