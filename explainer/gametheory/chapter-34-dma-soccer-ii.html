Chapter 34 — DMA Soccer II

Multi-Round Simultaneous Games with Imperfect Information

Feynman Bridge: Playing Soccer With a Blindfold

Imagine a championship soccer shootout, but with a twist: after each round, you get blindfolded before the next round starts. You can't remember whether the goalkeeper dove left or right. You can't recall your previous kicks. You and your opponent are playing chess moves that neither of you can see from the previous turn.

That's DMA Soccer II—a multi-round penalty kick game where information sets matter more than pure strategy.

The Setup

There are two players: the Kicker and the Goalkeeper. Each round (called a "third"), they play simultaneously:

  • Kicker chooses: Left, Center, or Right
  • Goalkeeper chooses: Left or Right
  • If they match, it's a save (no goal)
  • If they don't match, it's a goal

The game typically has three thirds (like three penalty kicks in a shootout), and players accumulate goals across all thirds.

The Blindfold: Information Sets

Here's where it gets interesting. After each third, two things can happen:

  • "Full Information" mode: Both players remember exactly what happened. The Kicker knows the Goalkeeper saved or not. The Goalkeeper knows where the Kicker aimed. Everyone can adapt.
  • "No Information" mode (the blindfold): Neither player knows what happened. They can't change strategy based on past outcomes. This creates an information set—a set of situations that look identical to the player, even though they're actually different.

In game theory, an information set is like a poker player not seeing other players' cards—you must choose an action without knowing which "version" of the game you're actually in. In DMA Soccer with no information, players treat all thirds identically because they can't distinguish them.

Resource Allocation: The (2,2) vs (3,1) Puzzle

Suppose you're the Kicker and you have 4 attempts to distribute across 3 thirds. You could:

  • (2,2,0): Play hard in thirds 1 and 2, skip third 3 entirely
  • (2,1,1): Spread evenly-ish
  • (3,1,0): Go all-in on the first third, hope for a big lead

This chapter reveals that how you split your resources matters differently depending on whether you know what happened before. With full information, you can react to the score. Without information, you're locked in to your initial commitment.

Why This Matters

DMA Soccer II teaches us that information is both a blessing and sometimes irrelevant. In some games, knowing the past helps you (classical game theory). In others, the structure of the game is so symmetric that information doesn't change the outcome. This mirrors real sports strategy: when should you "show your hand" early, and when should you keep your strategy hidden?

Mathematical Ascent: Equilibrium in Multi-Round Games

We'll build the mathematics of DMA Soccer II from the ground up: single-round payoff matrices, mixed strategy equilibria, backward induction, and expected value calculations.

Single-Round Payoff Matrix

In one third of play, the Kicker's payoff is the probability of scoring. The Goalkeeper's payoff is the probability of saving (= 1 − Kicker's payoff in symmetric zero-sum form).

The (2,2) vs (3,1) Resource Allocation Analysis

Suppose the Kicker has 4 "effort units" and must allocate them across 3 thirds. Consider two allocation schemes:

With Full Information: The (3,1,0) allocation (go hard in Third 1) allows the Kicker to build a lead and adjust in later rounds if needed. The expected value is typically higher because information allows responsive play.

With No Information: Both (2,2,0) and (3,1,0) lock in their payoff expectations from the start. The (3,1,0) strategy is riskier—if Third 1 fails badly, there's no recovery. The (2,2,0) strategy is safer, as it spreads risk. Depending on the distribution of the Kicker's effort across rounds, (2,2,0) may actually yield a higher expected payoff in some cases.

This reveals a deep principle: information and flexibility aren't always advantages; under certain structures, spreading effort can be superior because it reduces exposure to any single failure.

Socratic Sandbox: Deepen Your Understanding

Explore these questions to solidify your grasp of DMA Soccer II. Click each question to reveal the answer.

1
Why might having less information sometimes not hurt you in this game?
▼

In DMA Soccer, the structure of the game is highly symmetric and repetitive. If you don't know the past results, you can't do worse than committing to an optimal mixed strategy upfront. Without information, you're forced to use a strategy that works equally well in all circumstances—which is actually a strength in symmetric games.

Moreover, if your opponent also lacks information, they can't adapt against you. In many symmetric simultaneous games, information is a two-way street: knowing the past is only valuable if you can exploit it better than your opponent. If both players are in information sets where they can't distinguish outcomes, they both default to the same mixed equilibrium, and the advantage cancels out.

This is different from sequential games like chess, where information always helps because one player moves after seeing the other's move.

2
How does backward induction change when moves are simultaneous?
▼

Backward induction, classically, works in sequential games: you solve the last subgame, then work backward, knowing exactly what the previous player chose.

In DMA Soccer with simultaneous moves within each round, backward induction must be adapted. After each round, players may or may not have information about the other player's choice. If they do have information, you can use classical backward induction: solve the final round's equilibrium, then in the penultimate round, each player knows the Kicker's previous choice and the Goalkeeper's response, and they can adjust.

If players don't have information (no information mode), backward induction essentially collapses: you can't condition your strategy in round 2 on what happened in round 1 because you don't know it. Instead, you're solving the entire game as a single simultaneous-move game where all decisions are made at once.

The key insight: Information structure determines whether backward induction is possible. With information, it works. Without it, the game becomes a one-shot commitment.

3
Why does the (2,2) split sometimes outperform (3,1)?
▼

This question assumes a context where players are distributing resources (effort, attempts, or probability weight) across rounds.

The (3,1) allocation goes heavy in one round: put 3 units in Round 1, then 1 unit in Round 2. The risk is variance: if Round 1 goes badly, you can't recover. With no information mode especially, you're locked into a low payoff for Rounds 2–3.

The (2,2) allocation spreads the effort: 2 units in Round 1, 2 units in Round 2. This is more robust. Even if Round 1 fails, Round 2 still has full resources, and you have a chance to score.

Mathematically, under certain payoff functions—especially when outcomes in each round are stochastic—spreading effort reduces the variance of total payoff. In game-theoretic terms, (2,2) might yield a higher expected payoff than (3,1) if the game structure heavily penalizes "falling too far behind early."

This echoes the real-world principle: diversification reduces risk. In sports, overcommitting to one play or quarter can backfire; spreading your effort across the whole game is often safer.

4
What role do mixed strategies play in each third?
▼

Mixed strategies are essential in DMA Soccer because of the asymmetry: the Kicker has 3 options (L, C, R) while the Goalkeeper has only 2 (L, R).

If the Kicker used a pure strategy in every round—say, always kick left—the Goalkeeper would simply always dive left and never get scored on. Pure strategies are exploitable.

In equilibrium, the Kicker must randomize to keep the Goalkeeper guessing. The Goalkeeper's mixed strategy response ensures the Kicker is indifferent between their options, so the Kicker has no incentive to deviate.

Numerically, in the standard soccer game, the equilibrium often has the Kicker mixing roughly equally among L, C, and R (with perhaps higher weight on C, which is always a goal against a two-location defense). The Goalkeeper mixes between L and R (no pure choice dominates).

Each third, this equilibrium repeats (unless information allows players to adapt based on past outcomes). Mixed strategies are the hallmark of unpredictability in symmetric games—they're what prevents exploitation.

5
How does this model relate to real penalty shootouts?
▼

Real penalty shootouts in soccer are far more complex than DMA Soccer II, but the core insight applies: sequential learning and information structure matter enormously.

In a real shootout (e.g., FIFA World Cup), the kicker and goalkeeper see all previous kicks and responses. This creates full information. A goalkeeper can notice patterns (e.g., "This kicker always goes right under pressure"), and a kicker can see if the goalkeeper is favoring one side. Players adjust in real time.

DMA Soccer's "no information" mode is closer to a blind penalty kick where neither player has memory—implausible in real soccer, but useful theoretically to understand the value of information.

Real shootouts also involve psychological elements (fatigue, pressure, momentum) that game theory models abstract away. But the mathematical structure—mixed strategies, equilibrium play, the tension between predictability and exploitation—carries over. Coaches and players intuitively use mixed strategies in real shootouts, varying their target zones to avoid goalkeeper predictability.

DMA Soccer II is a simplified abstraction that reveals the deep principles: what equilibrium play looks like, why information matters, and how resource allocation and strategy interact over multiple rounds.
