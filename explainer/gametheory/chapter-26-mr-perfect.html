🏠 The Feynman Bridge: A House in a Hot Market

Imagine you and a rival buyer are both house hunting in a competitive real estate market. Each week, a new listing appears on the market. You both instantly see it and must decide immediately: make an offer or pass. There are only three weeks (rounds) available.

The Setup

Each new house is in one of three conditions:

  • Type 1: Your dream house (rarely appears, probability p₁)
  • Type 2: Really nice house (somewhat likely, probability p₂)
  • Type 3: Decent house (the rest, probability p₃)

If you both bid on the same house, it goes to coin flip—50/50 who gets it. If only you bid, you get it. If only your rival bids, they get it.

The Strategic Dilemma

This is not about emotion—it's about optimal strategy under competition. Consider:

  • Jump too early: You secure a "really nice" house in round 1, but your rival now has two more rounds to find their dream house. You end up happy, but they end up happier.
  • Wait too long: You hold out for the dream house that never comes, but your rival snapped up the nice house and now owns something solid. You end up alone (or in a bad house).
  • Coordinated risk: If both of you wait, neither of you gets anything in early rounds. But in round 3 (the last round), you both must decide on whatever shows up, knowing there's no next week.

This is where game theory reveals something stunning: What looks like a personal decision (my threshold for accepting a house) is actually a social equilibrium problem. The optimal strategy depends on the probability distribution of house quality and your rival's strategy.

The Key Insight

In a Nash equilibrium, each player's strategy is optimal given the other player's strategy. For the house-hunting game, this means: there is a quality threshold in each round such that if both buyers follow that threshold, neither wants to deviate.

That threshold is not determined by your personal preferences—it's determined by the market (the probability distribution) and the fact that both buyers face the same problem.

Three Critical Cases

The Nash equilibrium depends on the relationship between p₂ and p₃. We analyze three cases:

Case 1: p₂ + 2p₃ < 1

When Type 2 candidates are rare and Type 3 candidates are not very common, the optimal strategy is wait and hold out. Accepting early is unlikely to be in equilibrium because the probability of a better option next round is high enough to justify waiting.

Case 2: p₂ + 2p₃ > 1

When candidates of decent quality are frequent, both players prefer to accept relatively early. The equilibrium often involves accepting Type 2 candidates in early rounds.

Case 3: p₂ + 2p₃ = 1

This is the boundary case. The indifference point between waiting and accepting is balanced. Multiple equilibria may exist, including mixed-strategy equilibria.

🤔 Socratic Sandbox: Guided Reflection

Use these questions to deepen your understanding. Click any question to reveal a detailed answer.

1. Why does competition change your acceptance threshold?
▼

Without competition, you could afford to wait for a perfect candidate—if no one else is racing for them, you can pass on good ones and hold out. But with a rival buyer or suitor, passing on a good option means your opponent might take it instead.

This creates option scarcity. A Type 2 (very good) candidate in round 1 is no longer a personal decision about whether you like them—it's a strategic choice accounting for the probability your rival seizes them if you pass.

Game theory shows this mathematically: your equilibrium acceptance threshold depends on p₂ and p₃ (the market), not on your internal preferences alone. The presence of a competitor forces you to be more aggressive (accept earlier) than you would be alone.

2. How does the quality distribution (p₂, p₃) affect optimal strategy?
▼

The magic threshold is p₂ + 2p₃. This single number summarizes the entire probability landscape:

  • If p₂ + 2p₃ < 1: Type 3 candidates (okay) are rare relative to the expected value of waiting. You should reject early and hold out.
  • If p₂ + 2p₃ > 1: Decent candidates are common. You should accept them sooner because the risk of ending up alone is higher.
  • If p₂ + 2p₃ = 1: The indifference point. Multiple equilibria exist, and small changes in probabilities swing the strategy completely.

This teaches a profound lesson: optimal behavior is not universal—it adapts to the environment. The same person with the same preferences should behave differently in different markets.

3. Why might it be rational to accept an "okay" candidate early?
▼

Classical decision theory says: only accept an option if its payoff exceeds the expected payoff of future options. But in a competitive game, this logic breaks down.

An "okay" (Type 3) candidate in round 1 might have payoff 1, while the expected payoff of waiting is, say, 1.2 based on probabilities alone. Normally, you'd reject.

But with a competitor, you must account for conflict: your rival is also waiting for that better option. If you both wait, one of you gets a better option and one gets stuck alone. The risk of being the one who waits and finds nothing is often worth accepting the okay candidate now.

This is a key insight from game theory: strategic interdependence can make "suboptimal" choices optimal. You're not being irrational—you're accounting for your rival's behavior.

4. What happens at the boundary case p₂ + 2p₃ = 1?
▼

The boundary case is a critical juncture. It's the point where the game is perfectly balanced between two regimes:

  • Pure strategy equilibria: May not exist uniquely. Instead, there can be multiple equilibria, some involving mixed strategies (randomization).
  • Sensitivity: Tiny changes in p₂ or p₃ flip the equilibrium structure. This is why the boundary is called a "critical case."
  • Real-world interpretation: Markets that sit at this boundary are particularly unstable and unpredictable. Small perturbations lead to large strategic shifts.

In economic markets, this is often where you see the most interesting behavior—oscillations, cascades, and sudden tipping points.

5. How does this game model real-world competitive selection?
▼

"Waiting for Mr. Perfect" is a metaphor that extends far beyond dating:

  • Hiring: Two companies both want to recruit top talent. If you wait for the perfect candidate, your rival might hire them first.
  • Real estate: Competing buyers in a hot market must decide whether to bid aggressively now or hold out for a better property.
  • Venture capital: VCs compete for promising startups. Pass on a good one and a competitor invests instead.
  • Procurement: Suppliers with limited inventory. Buyers must decide when to lock in a deal.
  • Bankruptcy/Auction: Bidders competing in an auction must balance caution with missing out.

In all these cases, the same mathematical structure applies: simultaneous decisions, asymmetric information about future quality, and competition for a limited resource.

The Prisner model teaches that optimal behavior in these domains is not about personal preferences—it's about equilibrium strategy given the market structure.
