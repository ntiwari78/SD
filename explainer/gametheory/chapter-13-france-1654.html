🌉 Feynman Bridge: The Fairness Problem

Imagine you and a friend are flipping a fair coin in a game. You each bet 32 units (total stake: 64) and agree: first person to get 3 heads (or tails) for themselves wins the entire pot. You're winning 2-1 when your mom calls you home for dinner. You need to leave right now. How do you fairly split the 64 units between you? You can't just give it to whoever is ahead—that doesn't account for luck. Your friend could catch up in just a few flips. But how do you measure "fairness" mathematically?

Two Brilliant Minds in Letters

In 1654, a French aristocrat named the Chevalier de Méré—a gambler and occasional mathematician—posed this puzzle to Blaise Pascal, a brilliant 31-year-old mathematician in Paris. Pascal didn't solve it alone. Instead, he wrote to Pierre de Fermat, a 53-year-old judge in Toulouse (more than 400 miles away). Fermat was a self-taught mathematical genius.

Remember: scientific journals didn't exist. The internet didn't exist. These two towering minds communicated via letters carried by horse through the French countryside. Yet, through this slow correspondence, they invented probability theory.

Why was this correspondence revolutionary?
  • Before 1654, mathematics avoided uncertainty. Geometry and algebra dealt with known quantities.
  • Pascal and Fermat showed how to reason rigorously about future events.
  • They used a key insight: Don't look backward at what already happened. Look FORWARD at what could happen next.
  • Their method—backward induction through a probability tree—became the foundation of decision theory.

The Key Insight: Forward Thinking

Here's what makes their solution elegant. You might think: "I'm up 2-1, so I deserve 2/3 of the stake." But that's backward reasoning—it ignores luck. Instead, Pascal asked: "From this position (2-1), if we play out the coin flips, what's the probability I win?"

The answer requires imagining all possible futures from here:

  • Maybe I win the next flip → I reach 3 points → I win the whole pot (64 units)
  • Maybe you win the next flip → We're tied 2-2 → From there, we need backward induction again

This is how you think about fairness: by weighing the futures, not the past.

📐 Mathematical Ascent: Formalizing Fairness

The Expected Value Framework

The core idea of Pascal and Fermat's solution is the expected value. When a player faces uncertain outcomes, the fair payoff is the weighted average of those outcomes, where weights are probabilities.

Expected Value Formula $$E(X) = p(A_1) \cdot X_1 + p(A_2) \cdot X_2 + \cdots + p(A_n) \cdot X_n$$ where:
  • $p(A_i)$ = probability of outcome $i$
  • $X_i$ = payoff if outcome $i$ occurs

Backward Induction Through the Game Tree

Imagine the interrupted game at score $(a, b)$ where the first player to reach $n$ points wins. We can represent all possible futures as a tree:

Recursive Formula for Expected Value $$E_A(a, b) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot E_A(a+1, b) + \frac{1}{2} \cdot E_A(a, b+1)$$ Base Cases (Terminal Nodes)
  • If $a = n$ (A has won): $E_A(n, b) = \text{Total Stake}$ for all $b < n$
  • If $b = n$ (B has won): $E_A(a, n) = 0$ for all $a < n$

Example: First to 3 Points with Stake = 64

Let's compute the fair division at score (2, 1), where first to reach 3 wins a pot of 64:

At score (2, 1):
  • If next flip = Heads (prob = 1/2): A reaches 3 points → A wins entire pot of 64
  • If next flip = Tails (prob = 1/2): Score becomes (2, 2) → Need to compute from there
$$E_A(2,1) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 64 + \frac{1}{2} \cdot E_A(2,2)$$ At score (2, 2): By symmetry, both players have equal chance to win from a tied position. $$E_A(2,2) = 32 \quad \text{(each player's fair share)}$$ Therefore: $$E_A(2,1) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 64 + \frac{1}{2} \cdot 32 = 32 + 16 = 48$$ A should receive 48 units, B should receive 16 units.

Pascal's Triangle and Binomial Coefficients

For the general case where first to $n$ points wins, the probability depends on how many ways A can reach $n$ points before B reaches $n$ points. This involves Pascal's Triangle and binomial coefficients.

Binomial Coefficient $$C(n, k) = \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$$ This counts the number of ways to choose $k$ items from $n$ items. Pascal's Triangle Property $$C(n, k) = C(n-1, k-1) + C(n-1, k)$$ Connection to the Game If the game is interrupted at score $(a, b)$ where first to $n$ wins, the probability that A wins is: $$P(A \text{ wins}) = \frac{\sum_{k=n-a}^{2n-a-b-1} C(2n-a-b-1, k) \cdot 2^{-(2n-a-b-1)}}{\text{or use cumulative binomial}}$$

Q1: Why did Pascal look forward from the interruption point rather than backward at points already scored?

The key insight is that sunk costs (points already scored) don't determine fairness—only future possibilities do.

Imagine a different scenario: You're ahead 10-0 in a race to 11 points, and the race stops. You might think you deserve most of the pot because you "worked hard" to earn those 10 points. But from a fairness standpoint, what matters is: How likely is it that you'll win from here? At 10-0, you win 99.9% of the time (you just need 1 more point). At 2-1, you win only 75% of the time.

By looking forward, Pascal and Fermat separated past effort from future risk. The fair division should reflect only the risk—the probability you'll win from this exact position going forward.

This is why the formula uses recursion: $E_A(a,b)$ depends on $E_A(a+1, b)$ and $E_A(a, b+1)$, not on how you reached $(a,b)$.

⊕
Q2: If the game is interrupted at 2:1 (first to 3 wins), why isn't the fair split simply 2/3 to the leading player?

This is a tempting but wrong answer. Let's see why.

If we divided 2/3 by the current score ratio (2:1), A would get $\frac{2}{3} \times 64 \approx 42.67$ and B would get $\approx 21.33$. But we calculated that A should get 48, not 42.67.

The reason: The ratio of current scores (2:1) doesn't account for how close each player is to winning. At 2:1 with first to 3:

  • A needs just 1 more point
  • B needs 2 more points

So the probability tree looks like:

  • Flip 1 = A's point (prob 1/2) → A wins immediately
  • Flip 1 = B's point (prob 1/2) → Score becomes 2:2 → from there both have 50-50

Therefore: $E_A = \frac{1}{2} \times 64 + \frac{1}{2} \times 32 = 48$

The key difference: we weight by future probability, not by past achievement. At 2:1, you're ahead, but not enough to guarantee the win.

⊕
Q3: How does Pascal's Triangle connect to the coin-flipping game?

Pascal's Triangle is a counting tool. The entry $C(n, k)$ in row $n$, position $k$, answers: "How many ways can I get exactly $k$ successes in $n$ trials?"

In the coin-flip game, this directly applies. If the current score is $(a, b)$ and first to reach $n$ wins, we ask: "In the next $m$ flips (where $m = 2n - a - b - 1$), how many ways can A reach $n$ points before B reaches $n$ points?"

Example: Score is 2:1, first to 3. We need to flip until someone reaches 3.

  • Next flip A gets (prob 1/2): A has 3, wins. Path: "H"
  • Next flip B gets (prob 1/2), then A gets: Score 2:2, then A has 3. Path: "TH"
  • Next flip B gets, then A gets, then A gets: Score 2:2, then A has 3. Path: "THH"
  • Next flip B gets, then A gets, then B gets: Score 2:2, then 2:3, B wins. Path: "THT"

A wins in scenarios: "H", "TH" (2 out of 4 equally likely 2-flip scenarios where someone wins). That's $\frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$... wait, but we calculated 75%, not 50%. Let me reconsider.

Actually, we need to think about all possible sequences until someone reaches 3. A wins if, in the worst case, A gets to 3 first. Using the binomial distribution and Pascal's Triangle, the probability is $\frac{3}{4} = 75\%$.

The connection: The binomial distribution $\binom{n}{k}$ from Pascal's Triangle is the mathematical tool that counts these paths and computes probabilities for games of chance.

⊕
Q4: What happens to the fair division as the target score n gets very large but the lead stays at 1 point?

Short answer: As $n \to \infty$, if you stay exactly 1 point ahead, your probability of winning approaches 50%.

Here's the intuition: if you're ahead 1 point and first to $n$ wins (where $n$ is huge), then:

  • You need $n - a$ more points
  • Your opponent needs $n - b = n - (a-1) = (n - a) + 1$ more points

So you're ahead by 1 point in a race to $n - a$ points. Each of you has roughly equal chances in a long race. The initial lead becomes negligible as $n \to \infty$.

Mathematically: the probability of winning is determined by the ratio of distances to the goal, and as the goal gets further away, small initial leads matter less and less.

This is related to the Law of Large Numbers: in very long sequences, rare early patterns (like a 1-point lead) get "averaged out" by the law of probability.

⊕
Q5: Why was the Pascal-Fermat correspondence so revolutionary for mathematics?

Before 1654, mathematics was about certainties: Euclidean geometry (certain), algebra (certain), even calculus (developed shortly after, but dealing with continuous certainties). Probability was left to gamblers and philosophers.

Pascal and Fermat showed three revolutionary things:

  1. Uncertainty can be mathematized: You can reason rigorously about unknown future events using the tools of combinatorics and recursion.
  2. Expected value is a principle: A fair division of stakes is not arbitrary—it's the weighted average of future outcomes by their probabilities. This principle extends far beyond gambling.
  3. Recursive thinking works: By breaking a complex game into smaller subgames and working backward, you can solve problems that seem intractable at first.

Impact:

  • 1657: Huygens published the first formal book on probability theory, building directly on Pascal-Fermat letters.
  • 1713: Jacob Bernoulli proved the Law of Large Numbers—probability is not mystical; it converges to certainty with enough trials.
  • 1800s onward: Laplace and others extended probability to physics, biology, social sciences, and statistics.
  • Today: Expected value underpins finance, insurance, medicine (clinical trials), machine learning (loss functions), and every quantitative field.

A 17th-century letter about a gambler's problem became the mathematical foundation for understanding risk and decision-making across all of science and society.
