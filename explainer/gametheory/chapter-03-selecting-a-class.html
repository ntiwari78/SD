🌉 Chapter 3: Selecting a Class

How social networks determine stable outcomes when students must choose between two classes.

🎭 The Feynman Bridge — The Cafeteria Table Problem

Remember middle school? There was always that moment in the cafeteria: you walk in with your tray, scan the room, and your brain runs a lightning-fast algorithm — "Where do my friends sit? Where does my nemesis sit? Can I sit near Jamie without being stuck next to Alex?"

Now imagine this decision actually mattered for an entire semester. At Soap College, every student must register for either French 100 or Italian 100. Most students pick based on the language. But the drama queens and kings? They pick based on the people. They want their allies in the same room and their enemies in a different one.

Here's where it gets interesting. Imagine three friends — let's call them Adam, Beth, and Carl. Adam and Beth are close. Beth and Carl are close. But Adam and Carl? They can't stand each other. Now they must all choose: French or Italian.

If Adam picks French to be with Beth, Carl wants to pick French too (he likes Beth). But Adam hates that Carl showed up. So Adam switches to Italian. Now Carl follows because Beth might go too... and the dance never stops. There is no stable arrangement. Every time someone settles, someone else wants to move.

That's the "Outsider Problem" — and it's a situation where Nash Equilibrium simply doesn't exist in pure strategies. The social graph creates a strategic impossibility.

This chapter is about what happens when social networks meet game theory. The structure of who likes whom — the social graph — determines whether stable outcomes exist, and what they look like when they do.

The topology of social relationships determines whether stable equilibria can exist. Some social structures are inherently unstable.

📐 The Mathematical Ascent — Social Graphs as Games

2a. The Formal Model

We model the Class Selection Game as a strategic game with a social graph structure:

$$\Gamma_{\text{class}} = \langle N, S, (u_i)_{i \in N}, G, \epsilon \rangle$$ where:
  • $N = \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ — the set of players (drama queens/kings)
  • $S_i = \{F, I\}$ (French or Italian) for each player $i$
  • $G = (N, E^+, E^-)$ — the social graph with $E^+$ (like edges) and $E^-$ (dislike edges)
  • $\epsilon \in \{0, 1\}$ — the class preference bonus (epsilon)

The payoff function for player $i$ choosing class $c_i$ is:

$$u_i(c_1, \ldots, c_n) = 9 \cdot |\{j \in L_i^+ : c_j = c_i\}| - 9 \cdot |\{j \in L_i^- : c_j = c_i\}| + \epsilon_i(c_i)$$ where:
  • $L_i^+$ = set of people player $i$ likes
  • $L_i^-$ = set of people player $i$ dislikes
  • $\epsilon_i(c_i) = 1$ if $c_i$ is $i$'s preferred class, 0 otherwise

In simple terms: each player earns +9 points for each liked person in their class, −9 points for each disliked person in their class, and a small +1 bonus if they're in their preferred class.

2b. Three Classic Cases with Payoff Matrices

Case 1: "I Like You Both"

Adam (A) and Beth (B) like each other. Beth and Carl (C) like each other. A and C are indifferent. All three prefer French. The payoff matrices (with A's choice determining which matrix):

When A chooses French:
B/C C: French C: Italian
B: French A: 10, B: 19, C: 9 A: 10, B: 10, C: 1
B: Italian A: 1, B: 0, C: 0 A: 1, B: 9, C: 10
When A chooses Italian:
B/C C: French C: Italian
B: French A: 0, B: 10, C: 9 A: 0, B: 1, C: 1
B: Italian A: 9, B: 9, C: 0 A: 9, B: 18, C: 10

Nash Equilibria: All three in French (10, 19, 9) and all three in Italian (9, 18, 10). Both are stable — no player can improve by switching alone.

Case 2: "Disliking the Rival"

Adam and Beth like each other. Beth and Carl like each other. But Adam and Carl mutually dislike each other. A and B prefer French; C prefers Italian.

Result: The dislike between A and C acts as a "separator." Instead of clustering together, the payoff structure pushes A and B to stay in their preferred French class, while C ends up in Italian class. This is often a unique Nash equilibrium.

Case 3: "The Outsider"

Adam and Beth like each other. Both Adam and Beth dislike Carl. But Carl likes both A and B. Preferences: A and C prefer French; B prefers Italian.

Result: No pure Nash Equilibrium exists!

  • If A and B are in French, C wants to join them (he likes them). But then A and B are unhappy (C is present).
  • A or B switches to Italian to flee from C. Now C wants to follow (he likes them).
  • A and B keep trying to escape. Carl keeps chasing. The system cycles forever.
The "Outsider" topology—where one person pursues others who flee from them—creates a strategic cycle with no stable solution.

2c. The Social Graph Theorem

Key Insight: The existence of pure Nash equilibria is determined by the structure of the social graph. When the graph contains "pursuit loops" (where A likes B, B flees A, B likes C, C flees B, etc.), pure equilibria may not exist. The "Outsider" configuration — mutual like between two players, both disliking a third who likes both — is the minimal example of such instability.

2d. Larger Cases: The Computational Challenge

For $n$ players and $k$ classes:

  • Total possible assignments: $k^n$
  • For 5 players and 2 classes: $2^5 = 32$ assignments
  • For 6 players and 3 classes: $3^6 = 729$ assignments
  • Checking each: $n \cdot (k-1)$ deviation checks per assignment

At scale, exhaustive search becomes intractable. Instead, we use best response dynamics: players tentatively register, then update one-by-one to their best response until equilibrium is reached (if it exists). This often finds equilibria quickly without checking all $k^n$ possibilities.

3a. Python Implementation

Here's the complete Python code that powers the simulator logic: 

"""
Game Theory Through Examples — Chapter 3
The Class Selection Game Simulator

Implements:
- Payoff computation from social graph
- Exhaustive Nash equilibrium search
- Best response dynamics simulation
"""

import numpy as np
from itertools import product
from typing import List, Tuple, Dict, Set, Optional

class ClassSelectionGame:
    def __init__(self, players, likes, dislikes, preferences,
                 like_value=9, dislike_value=-9, pref_bonus=1):
        self.players = players
        self.n = len(players)
        self.likes = likes
        self.dislikes = dislikes
        self.preferences = preferences
        self.like_val = like_value
        self.dislike_val = dislike_value
        self.pref_bonus = pref_bonus

    def payoff(self, assignment, player_idx):
        my_class = assignment[player_idx]
        pay = 0

        for j in range(self.n):
            if j != player_idx and (player_idx, j) in self.likes:
                if assignment[j] == my_class:
                    pay += self.like_val

        for j in range(self.n):
            if j != player_idx and (player_idx, j) in self.dislikes:
                if assignment[j] == my_class:
                    pay += self.dislike_val

        if my_class == self.preferences.get(player_idx, -1):
            pay += self.pref_bonus

        return pay

    def all_payoffs(self, assignment):
        return tuple(self.payoff(assignment, i) for i in range(self.n))

    def best_response(self, assignment, player_idx):
        current = list(assignment)
        payoffs = []
        for c in [0, 1]:
            current[player_idx] = c
            payoffs.append(self.payoff(tuple(current), player_idx))

        if payoffs[0] > payoffs[1]:
            return 0
        elif payoffs[1] > payoffs[0]:
            return 1
        else:
            return assignment[player_idx]

    def is_nash_equilibrium(self, assignment):
        for i in range(self.n):
            br = self.best_response(assignment, i)
            if br != assignment[i]:
                return False
        return True

    def find_all_nash(self):
        equilibria = []
        for assignment in product([0, 1], repeat=self.n):
            if self.is_nash_equilibrium(assignment):
                labels = tuple("FRE" if c == 0 else "ITA" for c in assignment)
                payoffs = self.all_payoffs(assignment)
                equilibria.append({
                    "assignment": labels,
                    "payoffs": payoffs
                })
        return equilibria

    def best_response_dynamics(self, start=None, max_steps=100):
        if start is None:
            start = tuple(np.random.randint(0, 2, self.n))

        current = list(start)
        history = [tuple(current)]

        for step in range(max_steps):
            changed = False
            for i in range(self.n):
                br = self.best_response(tuple(current), i)
                if br != current[i]:
                    current[i] = br
                    changed = True
                    history.append(tuple(current))

            if not changed:
                return history, True

        return history, False

Q1: Pattern Recognition — Why do both Nash equilibria in "I Like You Both" have everyone in the same class?
▼

Beth is the social hub — she likes both Adam and Carl, and both like her back. In any split configuration, Beth is separated from at least one person she cares about. Since she gains +9 for each liked person in her class, she always benefits from moving to the class with the most of her liked ones.

Once Beth moves, the person left alone (either Adam or Carl) also wants to follow because they like Beth. So any split unravels into a cluster. This illustrates a network effect: the presence of a "hub player" with high degree (many social connections) creates gravitational pull toward clustering.

Lesson: The topology of the graph forces clustering when there's a central popular player.

Q2: The Outsider Paradox — Why does the Outsider case have NO Nash Equilibrium?
▼

The core issue is asymmetric directed edges. Carl likes Adam and Beth (pulling him toward them), but Adam and Beth dislike Carl (pushing them away from him). This creates a "pursuit dynamic":

  • If A and B cluster: C wants to join them (he likes them). But once he does, A and B suffer payoff −9 each and want to escape.
  • If A or B flees: C pursues them (he likes them). The fleeing player keeps running.
  • The cycle: No stable state emerges because the incentive structure forces perpetual motion.

Lesson: Unrequited social attraction (one-sided likes) creates strategic instability. Mutual relationships are stabilizing; asymmetric ones are destabilizing.

Q3: Modeling Challenge — Design a 4-player graph with exactly three Nash equilibria.
▼

Graph Structure: Players A and B like each other. Players C and D like each other. A and C mutually dislike each other (the "separator"). D is indifferent to A and B.

Preferences: A and B prefer French; C and D prefer Italian.

The Three Equilibria:

  • NE1: (F, F, I, I) — A and B in French (mutual like, preferences satisfied), C and D in Italian (mutual like, preferences satisfied). The A-C dislike is satisfied by separation.
  • NE2: (F, F, F, F) — All in French. A and B love it, C and D tolerate it because they're together, and the A-C dislike hurts but is offset by C-D mutual like.
  • NE3: (I, I, I, I) — All in Italian. Similar logic reversed.

The key: the dislike provides separation incentive (enabling NE1), while the within-group likes provide clustering incentive (enabling NE2 and NE3).

Q4: Scaling Computation — With 6 players and 3 classes, how many assignments are there, and why is exhaustive search hard?
▼

Total Assignments: $3^6 = 729$

Verification Cost: For each assignment, you must check if any player has incentive to switch to one of the other 2 classes. That's $6 \times 2 = 12$ deviation checks per assignment, totaling $729 \times 12 = 8,748$ payoff computations.

For larger games (e.g., 10 players, 3 classes), this explodes to $3^{10} = 59,049$ assignments. By hand, this is completely infeasible.

Solution: Use best response dynamics—a heuristic that often finds equilibria much faster by simulating players greedily updating in sequence. While not guaranteed to find all equilibria or to converge, it's computationally practical for large games.

Q5: Deep Thinking — How would asymmetric like/dislike intensities change equilibria?
▼

The model uses symmetric payoffs: +9 for likes, −9 for dislikes. In reality, people might like someone at +7 and dislike someone at −3. These asymmetries fundamentally change the game's equilibrium structure.

Weak dislikes, strong likes: If dislikes are weak (e.g., −3) and likes are strong (e.g., +9), the "cluster with friends" force dominates. Players tolerate minor irritation to be with friends. Result: More equilibria, more clustering.

Strong dislikes, weak likes: If dislikes are strong (e.g., −15) and likes are weak (e.g., +3), separation pressure dominates. Players flee enemies even if it means losing friends. Result: Fewer equilibria, more fragmentation.

Implication for the Outsider case: If we weaken Carl's like for A and B (from +9 to +2) while keeping A and B's dislike at −9, Carl becomes nearly indifferent and stops chasing. This enables equilibrium where A and B stay together unmolested.

Lesson: Equilibrium existence is fragile — small changes in payoff intensities can shift the game from having multiple equilibria to having none, or vice versa.
