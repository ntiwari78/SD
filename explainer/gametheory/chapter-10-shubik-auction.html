Chapter 10: Shubik Auction I

The Bridge: Intuition First

A Painting at the Flea Market

Imagine two friends, Ann and Beth, are bidding on a painting at an auction. The twist: whoever loses the auction also has to pay their final bid to the auctioneer—and gets nothing in return.

Ann thinks the painting is worth $25 to her. Beth thinks it's worth $35. The auctioneer says: "Bids go up in $10 increments. Ann, you start. If you bid $10, Beth can respond with $20 or pass. If someone passes, the other wins the painting, but the loser still pays their highest bid."

Ann bids $10. Beth could bid $20 (thinking: I win the painting for $20, and it's worth $35 to me, so net $15 gain!). But wait—if Beth bids $20, Ann could respond with $30...

Here's the key insight: whoever gets the "last" move—the chance to bid or pass when only one round remains—will always bid. The player who moves last knows the other player can't respond. So if Ann has the last move, she'll bid; if Beth has it, she'll bid.

The player who doesn't have the last move, knowing this, should just pass immediately and pay $0. The end player wins the item for $10, and the other player pays $0. The auctioneer gets nearly nothing despite both players paying!

This is the Shubik Auction paradox: a system designed to extract money from bidders often yields almost nothing. And if you remove the round limit? Bidding spirals infinitely in real life as players try to avoid being stuck as the loser.

The Mathematics: Backward Induction

Game Definition

The Shubik Auction is formally defined as SHUBIK AUCTION(A, B, n):

  • A: value of the item to Ann
  • B: value of the item to Beth
  • n: maximum number of rounds

Bids start at 10 and increment by 10. Ann moves first in each round. Whoever passes loses their highest bid and gets nothing. Whoever has the final bid in round n must decide: bid or pass?

Key Lemma: The Last Move Determines Everything

Claim: The player with the last move in round n always bids. The other player passes immediately.

Proof by backward induction:

Round n (final round): Whoever moves last in round n (must bid or pass) will bid at the current price, say $10k. They know the opponent can't respond. So they pay $10k and get the item worth A (or B) to them. Net payoff: A - 10k (or B - 10k), which is positive if A > 10k (or B > 10k). Since A, B ≥ 10 > 10 in most cases, they bid.

Round n-1: The non-end player knows that in round n, the end player will bid and win. So the non-end player's choice is: bid and risk going to round n, or pass and pay 0. Passing dominates bidding (they'd lose money by going further). So they pass.

Payoff Analysis by Parity

Case 1: n is even (Beth moves last)

Beth is the "end player" in round n. Ann, knowing this, passes in round 1 before any bid is made. Ann pays $0, Beth gets the item for $10, pays $10, net gain: B - 10. Beth's payoff: B - 10. Ann's payoff: 0.

Case 2: n is odd (Ann moves last)

Ann is the "end player." Beth passes in round 1 (before any bid). Ann bids $10 in round 1 uncontested, wins the item, pays $10. Ann's payoff: A - 10. Beth's payoff: 0.

The Dollar Auction Paradox

If we remove the round limit (n → ∞), the analysis breaks down. In real auctions with real people, bidding spirals: Ann bids $10, Beth bids $20, Ann bids $30... Why? Because if Ann passes after bidding $10, she loses $10 for nothing. So she'd rather bid $30 to "win back" her $10. But then Beth is in the same spot, and bids $40. This escalates until both players are bidding far above the item's value—a sunk cost fallacy in action.

The game-theoretic prediction (backward induction, infinite rounds) gives: whoever moves last in some distant round always bids, so the other player should pass immediately. But humans don't think that far ahead. We see our own money at stake and bid more to "save" it, creating the paradox.

# Shubik Auction Solver
def shubik_auction(A, B, n):
    """
    Solve SHUBIK AUCTION(A, B, n) via backward induction.
    Returns: (payoff_ann, payoff_beth, auctioneer_revenue)
    """
    # Determine end player
    ann_is_end_player = (n % 2 == 1)
    
    if ann_is_end_player:
        # Ann moves last: she bids 10, wins, pays 10
        # Beth passes in round 1, pays 0
        payoff_ann = A - 10
        payoff_beth = 0
        auctioneer_revenue = 10
    else:
        # Beth moves last: she bids 10, wins, pays 10
        # Ann passes in round 1, pays 0
        payoff_ann = 0
        payoff_beth = B - 10
        auctioneer_revenue = 10
    
    return payoff_ann, payoff_beth, auctioneer_revenue

The Questions: Think Deeper

These questions push beyond the mechanics of backward induction. They explore why the Shubik Auction is so paradoxical and what it reveals about human rationality, sunk costs, and auction design.

Q1: Why does the auctioneer earn so little despite both players paying? ▼

In equilibrium, one player passes immediately without bidding at all. Only one bid (of $10) is placed. So the auctioneer collects $10 from the loser who placed no bid—wait, that's backwards. Let me clarify:

Equilibrium outcome: One player bids $10 in round 1, the other passes immediately. The bidder wins the item and pays $10. The passer loses and pays $0. So auctioneer revenue = $10.

Why is this so little? Because backward induction forces one player to realize they'll eventually lose if they keep bidding. By passing early, they avoid any payment. The game is designed to extract money from both players, but the threat of both escalating bids actually frightens one player away entirely. Paradoxically, preventing bidding wars reduces total revenue!

Q2: What happens when the item is worth less than $20 to both players (A < 20, B < 20)? ▼
Q3: How does parity (odd/even n) determine who wins? ▼

If n is odd: Ann moves in round 1, then Beth, then Ann, etc. The last round is round n, and Ann moves last (since n is odd). So Ann is the "end player" and always bids. Ann's equilibrium payoff: A - 10. Beth's: 0.

If n is even: Following the same logic, Beth moves last (round n is even, so Beth moves in the final round). Beth is the end player. Beth's equilibrium payoff: B - 10. Ann's: 0.

Why this matters: The auctioneer, by setting n, can almost control who wins! By making n odd, the auctioneer favors Ann; by making it even, favors Beth. In real auctions, this would be a scandal.

Q4: Why do real people overbid in dollar auctions when theory says to pass? ▼

Game theory assumes players think infinitely ahead. Real humans don't. When Ann bids $10 and Beth considers responding:

  • Theory: Beth thinks, "I'll trace backward through all 9 rounds and see Ann wins. So I should pass now and pay $0."
  • Reality: Beth sees her $10 value on the line and thinks, "If I bid $20, I'll win and get $15 profit. Sure, Ann might bid higher... but I'll cross that bridge when I get there."

This is a sunk cost fallacy: Beth's earlier loss (from passing) feels painful, so she bids more to "save" that loss. Both players spiral, bidding $100 for a $50 item.

The takeaway: real auctions are not perfect backward induction machines. Human psychology—pride, loss aversion, competitive instinct—hijacks rational analysis.

Q5: How would the analysis change if the loser paid only half their bid? ▼

With this new rule, a failed bidder who bid up to price $p$ pays only $p/2$.

New equilibrium: The threat of losing money is reduced. More bidders are tempted to stay in the game longer. The non-end player is more likely to bid at least once before passing, since the penalty for losing is halved.

The end player still bids (same logic: final move, so they win). But now bidding may escalate further before the non-end player passes. Auctioneer revenue could increase from $10 to $20 or more.

Lesson: Auction rules have enormous effects. A small change in penalty incentivizes different behavior. This is why auction design is both an art and a science in economics.
