Chapter 37: Kuhn Poker

Feynman Bridge: The Essence of Poker

Imagine a simplified poker night with just three types of cards — low (Jack), medium (Queen), and high (King). You and a friend each draw one card from a shuffled deck. The fascinating question: should you ever bluff by betting big with a bad card? And should you ever fold a decent card when your opponent raises?

Kuhn Poker strips the ancient game to its mathematical essence: no hidden community cards, no multiple rounds of betting, no psychology beyond pure probability. Just two players, three card values, and a deceptively simple ruleset. The stunning answer that emerges from game theory is counterintuitive:

Bluff with your worst cards sometimes. Bet with your best always. But never bluff with middle cards.

Why? Because your opponent can reason through exactly what you just reasoned. If you always bluff low and never bluff middle, they can exploit you. The solution requires randomness — a carefully calibrated mix of deception and honesty that no human can consistently achieve, but which mathematics reveals with precision.

Q1: Why is bluffing with middle cards (Queen) a bad idea? ▼

Bluffing is a strategic deception: betting as though you have a strong hand when you don't. With a Queen (middle card), you're in an awkward position:

  • Against a King: If your opponent calls, you lose anyway—the Queen can't beat the King.
  • Against a Jack: You're already ahead! A bluff is unnecessary; a check or call maximizes your expected value against a weaker hand.

The Jack (low card) has everything to gain from bluffing: it's the worst hand, so deception is the only way to win. The King (high card) naturally values betting to extract value from weaker hands. But the Queen? It gains nothing from deception because it's neither weak (desperate) nor strong (dominant). The math confirms: in any equilibrium, the Queen's raise probability is strictly lower than the Jack's or King's.

Q2: How does the behavioral form (x, 0, 1, y, 1, z) capture optimal play? ▼

This notation describes the optimal behavioral strategy form for Ann, where the first three values are raise probabilities for Jack, Queen, King (first decision), and the last three are call probabilities for Jack, Queen, King (second decision).

Breaking it down:

  • x: Raise with Jack with probability x ∈ [0, 1] — bluff occasionally.
  • 0: Never raise with Queen — never bluff the middle card (as discussed above).
  • 1: Always raise with King — maximum value extraction.
  • y: Call with Jack with probability y ∈ [0, 1] — after checking with a Jack, sometimes call if Beth raises (mixed decision).
  • 1: Always call with Queen — after checking, the Queen is still good enough to call.
  • z: Call with King with probability z ∈ [0, 1] — after checking with a King, the decision is indifferent in equilibrium.

The specific values (x, y, z) are determined by making Ann indifferent between her mixed-strategy options and making Beth unable to exploit her. The form itself encodes the principle: be unpredictable with weak (Jack) and free (King) hands, but never deviate with the middle hand.

Q3: Why does Beth have a slight advantage (value = -0.02)? ▼

The game value of -0.02 means Ann's expected payoff (averaged across all possible card combinations and both players playing optimally) is slightly negative. Beth, moving second, has a tiny advantage.

Why? Information advantage. Beth moves second in most strategic situations:

  • If Ann raises, Beth sees this information and can decide whether to call or fold with full knowledge.
  • If Ann checks, Beth gets to decide whether to check or raise, controlling the initiative.

In typical games of perfect information, the second mover has an advantage because they can react to the first mover's revealed action. Here, that advantage is quantified as a -0.02 edge for Beth. It's small (less than 2% of the initial bet) because Kuhn Poker is highly symmetric: both players have the same card distribution, both can bluff, and both must mix their strategies. But the "last word" in most interactions goes slightly to Beth, tipping the scales in her favor.

Q4: What's the relationship between mixed and behavioral strategies here? ▼

In extensive-form games (where the order of moves matters), two representations of strategy exist:

  • Mixed strategy: A probability distribution over pure strategies. For Ann, this means mixing among 12 pure strategies (each specifying a complete action at every information set). Example: "play CCRFC• with probability 2/3 and RCR•C• with probability 1/3."
  • Behavioral strategy: Independent probabilities at each information set. For Ann: "raise with Jack at some probability, raise with Queen at some probability," etc.

By Kuhn's theorem (1953), for perfect recall games (where players remember all their past actions and information), any mixed strategy can be represented as a behavioral strategy with the same payoff. Conversely, any behavioral strategy induces a unique payoff distribution across pure strategy outcomes.

In this chapter, we convert between the two. The Nash equilibrium is first identified as a mixed strategy (2/3 of CCRFC• and 1/3 of RCR•C• for Ann), then converted to a behavioral strategy by analyzing the probability of reaching each information set. The beauty is that the behavioral form is more intuitive: it directly tells you "raise with King 100% of the time," without requiring you to construct hypothetical pure strategies.

Q5: How does the bluff-low-value-high principle extend to real poker? ▼

Kuhn Poker is a toy model, but its insights scale up remarkably well to real poker. The "bluff low, value-bet high, never bluff middle" principle appears consistently:

  • Bluff with trash hands: In Texas Hold'em, professional players bluff most aggressively with the worst hands (complete misses, hands that have no chance of winning without deception). A hand with "nothing" has nothing to lose.
  • Value-bet strong hands: Premium hands (top pairs, sets, straights) are bet and raised aggressively to extract maximum value. These have the best chance of winning.
  • Play marginal hands cautiously: Mediocre hands (middle pairs, weak top pairs, gutshot draws) are played more conservatively, with less bluffing, because they're in the "no-man's-land" between total air and nuts.

The reason? Bluffing works only if your opponent fears a value bet. If you bluff too wide (including middle hands), you become exploitable: a smart opponent calls more often and profits. The equilibrium "ranges" that top players develop through intuition or study reflect this principle directly.

Kuhn Poker proves mathematically why these intuitions are correct: the equilibrium is provably unique, and it mandates this structure. Deviations make you exploitable. This is why poker training emphasizes understanding ranges (the distribution of hands at each action) rather than memorizing specific decisions—ranges obey the bluff-low, value-high law.
