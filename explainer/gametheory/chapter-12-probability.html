Chapter 12: Theory 4 — Probability

Game Theory Through Examples by Erich Prisner

Probability is the mathematics of uncertainty. In games with randomness—from coin flips to dice rolls to card shuffles—probability tells us what to expect when we cannot know for certain what will happen.

The Bridge: Predicting the Unpredictable

Imagine you're in a casino. You roll a single die—can you predict the outcome? No. It's random. Every number from 1 to 6 is equally likely, and you have no idea which one will come up.

But here's the magic: if you roll that die 1,000 times, you'll see each number appear roughly 167 times. If you roll it 1,000,000 times, the pattern becomes even clearer. The unpredictable individual rolls create a perfectly predictable pattern over time.

This is probability: While we cannot predict one specific outcome, we can predict the long-run frequency of outcomes. Probability transforms individual randomness into collective certainty.

Why does game theory need probability? Many real games involve chance. Poker has randomness in card shuffles. Dice games have inherent randomness. Even in apparently deterministic games, uncertainty about what other players will do can be modeled with probability. Understanding expected value—what we win or lose on average—is crucial to making rational decisions in games with randomness.

In this chapter, you'll learn to quantify uncertainty, compute expected outcomes, and use probability trees to analyze complex random scenarios. You'll discover why the Law of Large Numbers works, how conditional probability reveals hidden patterns, and why famous "paradoxes" like the AIDS test example aren't paradoxes at all—just surprising applications of probability.

Mathematical Ascent

Foundations: Experiments, Events, and Probability

An experiment is any process with uncertain outcomes. Rolling a die, drawing a card, flipping a coin—all experiments. A simple event is one specific outcome (e.g., rolling a 3). An event is a collection of simple events (e.g., rolling an even number: {2, 4, 6}).

Probability Definition:
For an event A with equally likely simple events: $$p(A) = \frac{\text{number of favorable outcomes}}{{\text{total number of possible outcomes}}}$$

Probability always satisfies: 0 ≤ p(A) ≤ 1

Empirical vs. Theoretical Probability

Theoretical probability is calculated from known structures (a fair die has 6 equally likely outcomes, so P(rolling a 3) = 1/6). Empirical probability is observed frequency: if you roll a die 100 times and get 3 exactly 16 times, empirical probability = 16/100 = 0.16.

Law of Large Numbers: As the number of experiments increases, empirical probability converges to theoretical probability. The more trials, the closer your observed frequency gets to the true probability.

Expected Value: The Average Outcome

When a random variable X can take values $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ with probabilities $p(A_1), p(A_2), \ldots, p(A_n)$, the expected value is:

$$E(X) = p(A_1) \cdot X_1 + p(A_2) \cdot X_2 + \cdots + p(A_n) \cdot X_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p(A_i) \cdot X_i$$

Expected value is a weighted average—outcomes are weighted by their probability. It tells you, on average, what you expect to get if you repeat the experiment many times.

Example: The Envelope Game

You're offered a choice: pick an envelope containing $10, $100, or $1,000 with equal probability (1/3 each).

Expected Value:

$$E = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 10 + \frac{1}{3} \cdot 100 + \frac{1}{3} \cdot 1000 = \frac{1110}{3} = 370$$

You expect to win $370 on average. If you played this game 1,000 times, you'd expect to win about $370,000 total.

Example: Two Dice Sum

Roll two fair dice and sum them. What's the expected sum?

There are 36 equally likely outcomes. The sum ranges from 2 to 12. Computing the frequency of each sum and multiplying by probability:

$$E = \frac{1}{36}(2 \cdot 1 + 3 \cdot 2 + 4 \cdot 3 + \cdots + 12 \cdot 1) = \frac{252}{36} = 7$$

The expected sum is always 7, regardless of the number of rolls. This is the center of the distribution.

Conditional Probability and Bayes' Theorem

Conditional probability is the probability of event C given that event A has occurred, written as $p(C|A)$ (read "probability of C given A"):

$$p(C|A) = \frac{p(C \text{ and } A)}{p(A)}$$

The Product Rule follows directly:

$$p(A \text{ and } C) = p(A) \cdot p(C|A)$$

For independent events (one doesn't affect the other), $p(C|A) = p(C)$, so $p(A \text{ and } C) = p(A) \cdot p(C)$.

Probability Trees and Multistep Experiments

Many experiments happen in steps. A probability tree shows each possible path and its probability. At each branch, the probabilities of the outgoing branches must sum to 1. The probability of any complete path is the product of probabilities along that path.

Probability Tree Principle: In a multistep experiment, the probability of a specific sequence of outcomes is the product of the conditional probabilities at each step.

Measurement Scales

Random variables can be:

  • Nominal: Categories with no order (colors, teams, coin: H/T)
  • Ordinal: Ordered categories (rank 1st/2nd/3rd, satisfaction: low/medium/high)
  • Interval: Numeric with no natural zero (temperature in Celsius)
  • Cardinal/Ratio: Numeric with natural zero (money, time, distance)

The Notorious AIDS Test Example

A test for a rare disease has 97.7% accuracy. If you test positive, what's the probability you actually have the disease?

Intuitively, you might think: "97.7%, obviously!" But probability trees reveal the answer is much lower.

Assume:

  • Disease prevalence: 0.1% (1 in 1,000)
  • Test accuracy: 97.7% (sensitivity and specificity both ~97.7%)

Among 100,000 people:

  • 100 have the disease; test correctly identifies ~97
  • 99,900 don't have it; test incorrectly flags ~2,298 (false positives)

Of those who test positive (97 + 2,298 = 2,395):

$$p(\text{infected | positive}) = \frac{97}{2,395} \approx 4.05\%$$

Despite 97.7% test accuracy, a positive test means only ~4% chance you're infected! The disease is so rare that false positives outnumber true positives. This is the base rate fallacy.

Python Reference Code: Probability Simulations ▼ 
import random
import numpy as np
from collections import Counter

# Empirical Probability: Roll dice and count outcomes
def simulate_dice(num_dice, num_rolls):
    outcomes = []
    for _ in range(num_rolls):
        roll = sum(random.randint(1, 6) for _ in range(num_dice))
        outcomes.append(roll)
    return Counter(outcomes)

# Expected Value Calculation
def expected_value(outcomes, probabilities):
    return sum(o * p for o, p in zip(outcomes, probabilities))

# Probability Tree with Bayes' Theorem
def bayes_theorem(p_a, p_b_given_a, p_b_given_not_a):
    p_b = p_a * p_b_given_a + (1 - p_a) * p_b_given_not_a
    p_a_given_b = (p_b_given_a * p_a) / p_b
    return p_a_given_b

# Example: AIDS test
p_infected = 0.001  # Base rate
p_positive_if_infected = 0.977
p_positive_if_not_infected = 1 - 0.977

result = bayes_theorem(p_infected,
                       p_positive_if_infected,
                       p_positive_if_not_infected)
print(f"P(infected | positive) = {result:.4f}")  # Output: 0.0405

Q1 Why doesn't the Law of Large Numbers mean a coin "owes" you heads after many tails? ▼

This is the gambler's fallacy—a common misunderstanding. The Law of Large Numbers says that empirical probability converges to theoretical probability, but it does NOT say past outcomes must be "balanced out."

Here's why: Suppose you flip a coin 1,000 times and get 600 heads and 400 tails (unlikely, but possible). The empirical probability of heads is 0.60, which is far from 0.50. If you flip 1,000,000 more times and get roughly equal heads and tails (~500,000 each), the overall empirical probability becomes (600,000 + 500,000) / 2,000,000 = 0.5500, much closer to the theoretical 0.50.

The coin didn't "pay back" the early excess of heads; rather, the enormous number of new flips diluted the effect of the earlier imbalance. The next flip has probability 0.50 regardless of what happened before.

Q2 How can the expected value be a number that never actually occurs? ▼

Expected value is a theoretical average, not necessarily an observable outcome. Roll two dice and sum them: the expected value is 7. But you'll never roll a 7 from the "2 and 5" combination if you only roll once—you'll get a specific outcome like 3, 7, 11, etc. Yet over many rolls, the average approaches 7.

In the envelope game (outcomes: $10, $100, $1,000), the expected value is $370. You'll never actually get $370 from a single draw. But if you played 1,000 times and pooled all winnings, dividing by 1,000 would give you approximately $370.

Expected value is a long-run aggregate property, not a promise about any single trial.

Q3 Why is the AIDS test example so counterintuitive? What's really happening? ▼

The test is highly accurate (97.7%), yet a positive result means only ~4% chance of actual infection. This feels impossible—how can accuracy be divorced from the conclusion?

The insight is the base rate. The disease is extremely rare (0.1%). So even though the test rarely makes mistakes, "mistakes" in a huge population of uninfected people (99.9%) far outnumber correct identifications in the tiny infected population (0.1%).

Think of it this way: Among 100,000 people, 100 are actually infected. The test catches ~97 of them. But it also falsely flags ~2,298 of the 99,900 uninfected people. The 2,298 false positives swamp the 97 true positives.

This is why doctors often recommend a second test. A second positive test, given the first positive, has much higher conditional probability of true infection.

Q4 When does it matter whether we use a probability tree or a digraph? ▼

Probability trees show every path explicitly. They're intuitive and great for small problems, but can explode exponentially in complexity. A 10-step process with 2 outcomes at each step creates 2^10 = 1,024 leaf nodes.

Probability digraphs collapse equivalent states. If two different paths lead to the same state (e.g., "have the disease"), you can merge them in the digraph. This dramatically reduces complexity.

When to use each:

  • Trees: Simple problems (2-4 steps), when you need to see every path, or for teaching
  • Digraphs: Complex sequential processes, when states repeat, or when you need computational efficiency

In the AIDS test example, a digraph elegantly shows "Infected/Not Infected" and "Positive/Negative" tests, while a full tree would show multiple paths converging to the same final probability.

Q5 How does adding randomness change the analysis of simultaneous games? ▼

In a pure simultaneous game (Rock-Paper-Scissors), each player chooses a strategy without knowing others' choices. Adding randomness—whether from Nature or from player uncertainty—fundamentally changes the analysis.

Nature as a Player: Suppose Nature randomly assigns a state (e.g., "high demand" or "low demand") before players move. Each player's payoff now depends on: (1) their choice, (2) others' choices, and (3) Nature's random choice. We compute expected payoffs by averaging over all possible states.

Two Bars Example: Owners choose "Nice" or "Mean" behavior. Nature randomly assigns tourists to one bar or the other. An owner's expected payoff from being Nice is: p(tourists come here) × (payoff if Nice | tourists come) + (1 - p) × (payoff if Nice | tourists don't come).

Game Theory Impact: Randomness can change equilibrium play. In some games, adding uncertainty makes defection less attractive (because your payoff depends on Nature, not entirely on your control). It can also create new mixed-strategy equilibria.

This is why many applied games (auctions, resource allocation, contracts) explicitly model randomness and players' beliefs about distributions.
