⚽ The Feynman Bridge: Why Randomization Wins

Imagine a soccer penalty kick. The kicker must choose: shoot left or shoot right. The goalie must also choose: dive left or dive right.

The Predictability Problem: If the kicker always shoots left, the goalie will always dive left, and every shot gets saved. If the kicker always shoots right, same problem. Neither the "always-left" nor the "always-right" strategy works. The goalie learns your pattern and shuts you down.

But here's the genius insight: What if you randomize? What if you flip a fair coin before each penalty? Half the time left, half the time right. Now the goalie can't predict you. No matter which way they dive, they're wrong 50% of the time.

This is the core of mixed strategies. Instead of rigidly committing to one action, a player assigns probabilities to each possible action and plays according to that random distribution. The opponent cannot exploit a pure strategy against you because you are genuinely unpredictable.

The Strategic Advantage: Randomization is not weakness or indecision. It is strategic sophistication. By mixing your choices, you prevent your opponent from exploiting a fixed pattern. In games with no pure Nash equilibrium (like Rock-Paper-Scissors), mixed strategies provide the resolution: play each option with probability 1/3, and neither player can do better.

Key insight: Randomization must be genuinely random, not alternating or predictable. If your opponent can guess your next move, you've lost the advantage. True randomization makes you truly unpredictable.

📐 Mathematical Ascent
Mixed Strategy Definition

A mixed strategy for player $$i$$ is a probability distribution over their pure strategies. If player $$i$$ has $$k$$ pure strategies, a mixed strategy is:

$$\sigma_i = (p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_k) \quad \text{where} \quad p_j \geq 0 \text{ and } \sum_{j=1}^{k} p_j = 1$$

Each $$p_j$$ represents the probability of playing pure strategy $$j$$. The set of all mixed strategies is the probability simplex.

Expected Payoff Under Mixed Strategies

When both players play mixed strategies $$\sigma_1$$ and $$\sigma_2$$, player 1's expected payoff is:

$$u_1(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = \sum_{i,j} p_i q_j \cdot u_1(s_i, t_j)$$

where $$p_i$$ is the probability that player 1 plays strategy $$s_i$$, $$q_j$$ is the probability that player 2 plays strategy $$t_j$$, and $$u_1(s_i, t_j)$$ is the payoff to player 1 from pure strategy pair $$(s_i, t_j)$$.

Best Response to a Mixed Strategy

When the opponent plays a mixed strategy, the best response is a pure strategy that maximizes your expected payoff:

$$BR_i(\sigma_{-i}) = \arg\max_{s_i} \, u_i(s_i, \sigma_{-i})$$

Key fact: A player never needs to randomize in their best response. If multiple pure strategies tie for best, the player can mix among them; otherwise, a single pure strategy is uniquely best.

Support and Indifference Principle

The support of a mixed strategy $$\sigma_i$$ is the set of pure strategies played with positive probability:

$$\text{supp}(\sigma_i) = \{s \in S_i : \sigma_i(s) > 0\}$$

Indifference Principle (for Mixed Nash Equilibrium): If player $$i$$ mixes between strategies in their support in a Nash equilibrium, then all strategies in the support must yield the same expected payoff. Otherwise, player $$i$$ would play only the higher-payoff strategy.

This principle is the primary tool for computing mixed Nash equilibria in non-zero-sum games.

Nash's Existence Theorem

Theorem (Nash, 1950):

Every finite game (finite players, finite strategy sets) has at least one Nash equilibrium, possibly in mixed strategies.

$$\exists \, (\sigma_1^*, \sigma_2^*, \ldots, \sigma_n^*) \, : \, u_i(\sigma_i^*, \sigma_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(\sigma_i', \sigma_{-i}^*) \, \forall i, \sigma_i'$$

Profound implication: No matter how games are structured, a resolution always exists in the form of mixed equilibrium. This guarantees strategic stability.

Von Neumann's Minimax Theorem (Zero-Sum Games)

For a two-player zero-sum game:

$$\max_{\sigma_1} \min_{\sigma_2} u_1(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = \min_{\sigma_2} \max_{\sigma_1} u_1(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = v^*$$

The mixed value $$v^*$$ equals both players' mixed security levels (maximin payoff). In equilibrium, both players achieve exactly this value.

Mixed Security Level and Maximin Strategy

Player $$i$$'s mixed security level is their guaranteed payoff against any opponent strategy:

$$v_i = \max_{\sigma_i} \min_{\sigma_{-i}} u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})$$

The strategy $$\sigma_i^*$$ achieving this is called the maximin (or mixed security) strategy. For zero-sum games, the mixed Nash equilibrium strategy equals the maximin strategy.

Computing Mixed NE in 2×2 Games

For a 2×2 game with payoff matrix:

$$\begin{array}{c|cc} & L & R \\ \hline U & (a, a') & (b, b') \\ D & (c, c') & (d, d') \end{array}$$

To find a fully mixed Nash equilibrium (both players mix), use the indifference principle:

Player 1 mixes between U and D: Player 2 must make Player 1 indifferent.

$$q \cdot a + (1-q) \cdot c = q \cdot b + (1-q) \cdot d \quad \Rightarrow \quad q^* = \frac{d - c}{a - b - c + d}$$

Player 2 mixes between L and R: Player 1 must make Player 2 indifferent.

$$p \cdot a' + (1-p) \cdot b' = p \cdot c' + (1-p) \cdot d' \quad \Rightarrow \quad p^* = \frac{d' - b'}{a' - b' - c' + d'}$$

The mixed Nash equilibrium is $$(\sigma_1^*, \sigma_2^*)$$ with probabilities $$(p^*, 1-p^*)$$ and $$(q^*, 1-q^*)$$.

Brown's Fictitious Play

Brown's Fictitious Play is an iterative algorithm that converges to mixed Nash equilibria in 2-player zero-sum games:

  1. Start with arbitrary strategy for each player.
  2. Each round $$t$$: Each player observes opponent's empirical frequency of play through round $$t-1$$, and plays a best response to that frequency.
  3. Update empirical frequencies.
  4. Repeat.

Convergence result: In 2-player zero-sum games, the empirical frequencies of play converge to the mixed Nash equilibrium.

Interpretation: Players learn the equilibrium through repeated play and adaptation to observed opponent behavior.

Connection to Linear Programming

Finding a mixed Nash equilibrium can be formulated as a linear programming problem. For zero-sum games, the problem becomes:

$$\text{maximize} \quad v \quad \text{subject to} \quad A \sigma_1 \geq v \mathbf{1}, \quad \mathbf{1}^T \sigma_1 = 1, \quad \sigma_1 \geq 0$$

where $$A$$ is the payoff matrix. This is solved efficiently by modern LP solvers.

❓ Socratic Sandbox: 5 Essential Questions
Why must randomization be genuinely random, not just alternating?
⌄

The core answer: If your randomization is predictable—for example, always alternating left-right-left-right—then an observant opponent learns your pattern and exploits it perfectly. After one cycle, they know exactly where you'll go next.

Why genuine randomness matters: True randomization (like a fair coin flip) means no pattern exists. Your opponent cannot learn to predict you, no matter how many rounds they observe. Your action in round 1,000 is independent of your actions in rounds 1-999.

Game-theoretic consequence: In a mixed Nash equilibrium, your opponent is indifferent between their strategies—they can't do better against your random play. But if you alternate predictably, your opponent can distinguish between "cycles you complete" and "where you go next," breaking the indifference.

Example: In Rock-Paper-Scissors, if you play Rock-Paper-Scissors-Rock-Paper-Scissors on repeat, I learn your cycle and always play the counter: Paper-Scissors-Rock-Paper-Scissors-Rock, and I never lose. But if you flip a fair 3-sided die each round, I have no advantage.

Why does the indifference principle work for finding mixed Nash equilibria?
⌄

The logical structure: Suppose Player 1 is mixing between Up and Down in equilibrium, and Player 2 plays a mixed strategy. If Player 1 is mixing, then both Up and Down must be best responses. But a player never strictly wants to mix unless the strategies in the mix are tied in expected payoff.

Proof by contradiction: Suppose Up gives an expected payoff of 5 and Down gives 3. Player 1 would never mix; they'd play Up all the time. The only way Player 1 is willing to mix is if Up and Down give the same expected payoff.

How this helps solve: If Player 1 mixes 50-50 and Player 2 wants Player 1 to be indifferent, Player 2 must choose their mix such that Player 1's expected payoff from Up equals their expected payoff from Down. This gives us equations to solve for Player 2's mixing probability.

Example: In matching pennies, if Player 1 plays Heads with probability p, then Player 2 must make Player 1 indifferent between Heads and Tails. Player 2 does this by playing Heads and Tails with equal probability (1/2 each), so Player 1 gets +1 from either choice.

Why does every finite game have at least one Nash equilibrium (possibly mixed)?
⌄

Nash's profound theorem (1950): The existence of mixed Nash equilibrium is guaranteed by a topological fixed-point argument. At an intuitive level: imagine players can "tweak" their strategies. If everyone is at a strategy profile where no one has an incentive to tweak, they're in equilibrium.

Why no pure equilibrium can exist: Some games (like Rock-Paper-Scissors, Matching Pennies, or Penalty Kicks) have no pure Nash equilibrium because every pure profile has a player wanting to deviate. But the set of mixed strategies is much larger—it's the probability simplex, a continuous space.

The mathematical guarantee: Nash proved using Brouwer's fixed-point theorem that a continuous function from the probability simplex to itself must have a fixed point. The "best response correspondence" (mapping each strategy profile to best responses) is such a function, so an equilibrium always exists in mixed strategies.

Philosophical implication: No game is "without solution." Even if players clash on all pure outcomes, they can reach stable randomized behavior.

When are mixed strategies better than any pure strategy for a single player?
⌄

The surprising answer: For a single player in isolation, a mixed strategy can never be better than the best pure strategy. If a player could do better by mixing, they'd just play the best pure strategy and do even better.

But in equilibrium: A player might be indifferent between their pure strategies given opponent play, and thus willing to mix. This doesn't mean mixing is better than pure play; it means pure strategies are equally good (and worse than some alternative).

Why players mix in equilibrium: A player mixes in equilibrium because pure deviations are equally bad. If the opponent is playing a mixed strategy that makes the player indifferent, then deviating to any single pure strategy yields the same (or lower) payoff.

Example: In Rock-Paper-Scissors, if you play Scissors with probability 1/3, I'm indifferent between Rock, Paper, and Scissors—each gives expected payoff 0. I don't gain by playing pure Rock; I'm no better off. But I also don't gain by mixing. Mixing 1/3-1/3-1/3 is as good as pure play when you mix 1/3-1/3-1/3.

How does Brown's fictitious play connect learning to equilibrium?
⌄

Brown's learning algorithm: Each player assumes the opponent will play in the future as they have in the past. In round t, each player observes the opponent's history (rounds 1 to t-1) and plays a best response to the empirical frequency. Then update and repeat.

How it connects to equilibrium: In zero-sum games, this simple adaptive rule—best respond to past frequencies—causes empirical play frequencies to converge to the mixed Nash equilibrium. Neither player needs to know the game structure or the opponent's strategy; both just react to observed history.

Convergence insight: If Player 1's play frequencies converge to p* and Player 2's converge to q*, then at the limit, each player is playing a best response to a fixed distribution (the limit of the opponent's frequencies). This is precisely a Nash equilibrium.

Real-world implications: Brown's fictitious play is a plausible learning model. It doesn't require rationality or game-theoretic knowledge—just observation and myopic best response. Yet over time, players naturally coordinate on equilibrium play.

Example: In Matching Pennies, if players alternate observing and best-responding, both gradually learn to mix 50-50. By round 100, Player 1 has played Heads ~50 times and Tails ~50 times. Player 2 sees this equal distribution and is indifferent, supporting the equilibrium.
