The Feynman Bridge: Zero-Jargon Analogy

Imagine two kids standing 100 feet apart, facing each other on a playground. Each child has exactly one water balloon. The game is simple: they walk toward each other, and at any moment, either player can throw their balloon.

Here's the catch: the closer you are, the better your chance of hitting. At 100 feet away, you might only have a 10% chance of a direct hit. At 50 feet, maybe 20%. At 10 feet, 90%. But here's the problem—while you're walking closer to improve your aim, so is your opponent. If you wait too long, they get close enough to almost guarantee their hit, and you're drenched.

The key question: At what distance should YOU throw, given that your opponent is also thinking strategically about the same problem?

This is exactly the Duel problem in game theory. The water balloons are bullets. Walking closer is stepping forward. The hit probability increases with distance (or rather, decreases with distance for a real gun, but increases with number of steps in the model). And both players reason backward from the end: "If I reach step 3, my opponent will shoot because the probability is too high. So should I shoot at step 2?"

The mathematician's insight: There's a threshold—a specific distance at which you should switch from "keep walking" to "shoot now." And once you start shooting, you never stop. Why? Because if shooting is worth the risk at distance d, it's even better at distance d-1 (your opponent is closer, making you less likely to live anyway).

The fascinating part: this threshold depends on how much your accuracy improves with each step. In some versions of the game, both players wait until they're dangerously close. In others, they shoot immediately. The shape of the game's payoff structure determines the strategy.

  • SHOOT: Fire the bullet now. Win with probability p_k, lose with probability 1 - p_k (opponent will then get to shoot).

The Monotonicity Theorem

Claim: If shooting is optimal at step k, then shooting is optimal at all steps < k (i.e., when closer to the opponent).

Intuition: If you're willing to take a 20% shot at step 5, you should certainly take a 30% shot at step 3. The payoff from shooting only improves as you get closer.

Consequence: The optimal strategy is a threshold: "Step forward while at step ≥ k*, then shoot at step < k*."

Expected Payoffs

At equilibrium (subgame perfect), each player's expected payoff can be computed. For one-bullet duels, the player who moves first often has a disadvantage (counterintuitive!), because the second mover can respond to their choice. With two bullets, the first mover gains an advantage (more weapons).

Socratic Sandbox: Five Guiding Questions

Below are five questions designed to deepen your intuition about duels. Click each question to reveal a detailed explanation. Spend time thinking about your answer first, then compare with the revealed response.

Question 1
Why is the monotonicity property intuitive? Why can't a rational player say "I'll shoot at step 5, but step at step 3"? ▼

Monotonicity is intuitive because shooting becomes strictly better as you get closer. If you're indifferent between shooting at step 5 (p = 50%) or stepping to step 4 (hoping to shoot at p = 60%), then at step 3 (p = 30%), shooting is worse. But wait—you wouldn't be at step 3 if you planned to shoot at step 5, because that's irrational at step 4 or 3.

More precisely: if shooting at distance d gives you payoff X, then shooting at distance d-1 (closer) gives you payoff X' ≥ X because your hit probability improved. So if stepping was better at d, it's not better at d-1; you should shoot at d-1.

This is why optimal strategies have the form: "Step while at distance ≥ d*, shoot when closer."

Question 2
How does having a backup bullet (two bullets instead of one) change your strategic thinking? Why would you shoot earlier with two bullets? ▼

With one bullet, you must be conservative. If you shoot at p = 0.3 and miss, you lose immediately. The risk is catastrophic.

With two bullets, you can afford to gamble. Shoot at p = 0.3, even if you miss, you still get another try at p = 0.4 or higher. This dramatically changes the threshold—you become willing to take earlier, riskier shots because failure isn't final.

The two-bullet duel becomes more aggressive overall. Both players know the other has a backup, so waiting becomes even more dangerous. The result: the game often ends sooner, not because people are reckless, but because the payoff structure incentivizes earlier action when you have insurance.

Question 3
What's the real-world connection between duels and timing decisions in business, warfare, or negotiations? Can you think of an example? ▼

Business example (product launch timing): Two startups are developing competing products. The longer you wait before launching, the more polished and feature-rich your product (higher "hit probability"). But waiting gives your competitor time to reach market first. When should you launch? The answer depends on how much each extra month of development improves your chances relative to the risk of losing first-mover advantage.

Warfare example (military engagement): Two armies approach each other. Each general can choose to engage now (with current troop numbers and position quality) or advance closer (strengthen position, but opponent does too). When is the moment to engage? The duel model applies: there's a threshold distance/timing at which engagement becomes optimal.

Negotiation example (salary negotiation): You're negotiating salary. Each round of negotiation is like a step—you get closer to knowing the employer's limit, but they also get more information about your reservation price. When should you make your final demand (your "shot")? Too early, you leave money on the table. Too late, the employer walks away.

In all cases, the duel structure appears: risks increase with waiting, but so do potential rewards. The threshold depends on how much information, power, or resources you gain per unit of time.

Question 4
Why does increasing m (the accuracy increment) make both players shoot sooner and more aggressively? What changes in their reasoning? ▼

When m is small (e.g., 0.05), each step only marginally improves accuracy. A player at step 6 (60% accuracy) thinks: "If I step, my opponent gets to 70%. But if I shoot now, I only have a 60% chance. I'd rather step." This logic repeats until you're very close.

When m is large (e.g., 0.2), the math changes. At step 2 (40% accuracy), if you step, your opponent jumps to 60%. From their perspective, 60% is already high enough to shoot! So stepping at m = 0.2, step 2 means: your opponent shoots at step 3 with 60%, you lose with high probability. Better to shoot now with 40%.

The key insight: Large m compresses the decision window. Waiting becomes dangerous sooner because the opponent's accuracy threats grow faster. Both players rationally shift to earlier, more aggressive strategies—not out of aggression, but out of self-preservation.

This models real scenarios like arms races: if weapons improve quickly (high m), first-strike pressure mounts, and even rational actors preempt.

Question 5
How do duels relate to arms races and deterrence theory? What does the duel model reveal about the logic of escalation and strategic stability? ▼

The deterrence paradox: In the Cold War, both superpowers built more nuclear weapons ("stepping closer"), believing this would deter the other side. But from a duel perspective, this is self-defeating. If both players keep stepping (building weapons), they reach a threshold where one rational actor preempts (shoots first) to avoid being shot.

The arms race spiral: Each side's motivation is defensive ("we need more weapons to be safe"), but the outcome is offensive pressure. Both parties converge toward the threshold sooner. The duel model shows this is inevitable given the payoff structure—there's no way to escape via unilateral caution.

Strategic stability: The duel is unstable. Small exogenous changes (e.g., new technology that increases m) push both players toward shooting. Stability in deterrence requires institutional mechanisms like arms control treaties, communication channels, or restructuring the game itself (e.g., "no first strike" pledges that change payoffs).

The socioeconomic question: Is the duel parameter m controllable by society? If nations can negotiate limits on weapons improvements, they effectively cap m, keeping both sides in the "wait longer" regime. This is why arms control is not about generosity—it's about preventing the game's inevitable escalation.
