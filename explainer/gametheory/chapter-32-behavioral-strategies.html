Feynman Bridge

Intuition Without Jargon

Imagine you're planning a cross-country road trip. You want some spontaneity, so you're definitely going to randomize your choices somewhere. But there are two very different ways you could do it:

Approach 1: The Complete Itinerary Lottery (Mixed Strategy)
Before you leave home, you sit down and write out every possible complete itinerary — all the different routes, which gas stations you'll stop at, which hotels you'll sleep in. Every detail. Maybe you have 8 different complete itineraries. Then you randomly pick one (say, with a spinner) and follow it exactly. You're committing to the entire plan before you start.
Approach 2: The Intersection Decision (Behavioral Strategy)
Instead, you start driving. When you reach an intersection where you have a choice, you flip a coin (or roll dice) to decide which way to turn. At the next intersection later that day, you make another random choice. You're deciding locally, at each moment, with your own randomization rule. No giant pre-commitment.

Here's the magic: Kuhn's Theorem (1953) says that as long as you remember everywhere you've been and everything you've decided so far — that is, you have perfect recall — these two approaches lead to exactly the same set of possible journeys with the same probabilities. You end up visiting the same destinations in the same proportions either way.

But the second approach is more natural and practical. You don't need to plan your entire trip in advance. You just need a rule for what to do at each decision point. That's the power of behavioral strategies.

The twist: If you somehow forgot where you'd been (imperfect recall), the two approaches could give different results. But in almost all real games, players remember their own decisions, so Kuhn's theorem applies.

Mathematical Ascent

Formal Definitions and Conversions

Mixed Strategy
A mixed strategy for player i is a probability distribution over all of player i's complete pure strategies. In extensive form games, a pure strategy specifies an action for every information set the player might encounter.
Behavioral Strategy
A behavioral strategy for player i assigns a probability distribution over actions at each information set independently. Rather than committing to a complete plan, the player specifies a local randomization rule at every decision point.

Kuhn's Theorem (Perfect Recall)

The Theorem
If a player has perfect recall — meaning at every information set, the player knows all of their own previous choices and information — then for every mixed strategy, there exists a behaviorally equivalent strategy (one producing the same outcome distribution), and vice versa.
Perfect Recall Condition
A player has perfect recall if: whenever they are at an information set h and have taken a path of actions (a₁, a₂, ..., aₖ), they know exactly which actions they took and the sequence of choices that brought them to h. Most real games satisfy this condition; games with "absent-minded" players do not.

Worked Example: 2-Stage Game

Consider a simple game where Player 1 moves first, choosing Left (L) or Right (R). Based on Player 1's choice, Player 2 either observes it (perfect information) or doesn't (imperfect information). Then Player 2 moves.

Suppose Player 1 plays the mixed strategy: σ₁ = 0.6 L + 0.4 R. This means: "Play L with probability 0.6, play R with probability 0.4."

Socratic Sandbox

5 Guiding Questions

1. Why might a player prefer to think in terms of behavioral strategies rather than mixed strategies?
+

Behavioral strategies are more cognitively natural and computationally efficient. In mixed strategies, you must specify a probability for every possible complete plan (pure strategy), which grows exponentially with the game tree's size. Behavioral strategies only require specifying a probability distribution at each decision point — a vastly smaller space.

Additionally, behavioral strategies align with how humans actually make decisions: we often decide locally, at each moment, rather than committing to a complete pre-planned strategy. In game-theoretic applications like mechanism design, behavioral strategies are often more tractable and easier to analyze. And when perfect recall holds (the typical case), Kuhn's theorem guarantees they're equivalent to mixed strategies.

2. What goes wrong with the conversion when a player has imperfect recall?
+

When a player forgets their own past decisions, the information sets they face become "merged" — nodes that should be distinct are treated as indistinguishable. This breaks the conversion formulas. Specifically, the conditional probability formula β(a|h) = Σ{s: s(h)=a} σ(s) / Σ{s reaches h} σ(s) requires knowing which pure strategy reached which information set. If the player can't distinguish information sets (due to imperfect recall), this decomposition is invalid.

The classic example is the absent-minded driver: a driver at an intersection doesn't remember which intersection they're at. A mixed strategy over complete plans still makes sense (e.g., "with probability 0.3, always turn right"), but the behavioral interpretation fails — the driver can't condition on information they don't have. The two representations no longer produce equivalent outcome distributions.

3. Can two different mixed strategies correspond to the same behavioral strategy?
+

Yes, absolutely. This is a key insight: multiple mixed strategies can produce the same behavioral strategy. For example, suppose a mixed strategy puts zero probability on any pure strategy that involves playing Left then Up. Even so, Player 1's behavioral strategy at their information set might be 0.5 Left + 0.5 Right (because the conditional probability of playing Left, given that some pure strategy is played, is 0.5).

However, when perfect recall holds, Kuhn's theorem implies that if two mixed strategies produce the same behavioral strategy, they also produce the same outcome distribution. The behavioral representation is "coarser" — multiple mixed strategies map to the same behavioral strategy, but they're behaviorally indistinguishable.

4. In what sense does Kuhn's Theorem simplify the analysis of extensive form games?
+

Kuhn's theorem simplifies analysis in two ways. First, it validates working with behavioral strategies when they're easier. Since behavioral strategies are a smaller, more manageable space, equilibrium existence and computation become simpler. For instance, finding a Nash equilibrium in behavioral strategies is often easier than in mixed strategies.

Second, it guarantees equivalence: any result proven using behavioral strategies (with perfect recall) immediately applies to mixed strategy play and vice versa. You're not losing generality by working in one representation. This freedom to choose the most convenient representation is a major computational and analytical advantage. It also justifies why textbooks often focus on behavioral strategies in extensive form games—we don't sacrifice any essential information or equilibrium properties.

5. How does perfect recall relate to the concept of an information set?
+

Perfect recall ensures that a player's information sets are well-structured: two nodes are in the same information set only if the player truly cannot distinguish them based on their own past. Perfect recall prohibits situations where a player is at a node where they can't remember their own previous choice — which would lead to paradoxes in action labeling (how do you label an action if you don't know which information set you're in?).

Formally, perfect recall implies that information sets form a proper partition of nodes reachable by the player, such that no "path" through the game tree passes through two nodes in the same information set in a way that violates the player's memory. Without perfect recall, information sets can create situations where actions are undefined or multiply-valued, and conditional probabilities cannot be computed. Perfect recall is precisely the condition needed to make the conversion between mixed and behavioral strategies well-defined and invertible.
