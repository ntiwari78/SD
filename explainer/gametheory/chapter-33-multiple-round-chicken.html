The Food Truck Gambit

Imagine two rival food trucks operating in the same city. Each day, they independently decide where to set up: either the popular downtown spot (where foot traffic is high but competition is brutal) or a safer neighborhood location (less exciting, but steady customers).

Round 1: The First Day

If both go downtown: They crash into each other—literally competing for the same customers, cannibalizing revenue. Both lose money (crash payoff: $0 each).

If one goes downtown, the other to the neighborhood: The bold truck gets the prime location and makes $4, while the cautious truck makes $1. Clear win for boldness.

If both go to the neighborhood: They coexist peacefully, splitting the steady stream. Both make $3—a modest but stable outcome.

This is Chicken: every driver faces pressure to be bold (go downtown) but knows mutual boldness leads to disaster.

But What if They Meet Tomorrow Too?

Now here's the twist: they'll meet again tomorrow, and the day after. Yesterday's outcome matters.

If the first truck went downtown and won, does it have momentum? Reputation? Customers might expect it there again. The second truck has learned: "That driver is bold. Maybe I should be cautious." Or conversely: "I got humiliated yesterday. I need to prove I'm tough too."

The multi-round game creates reputational warfare. Being known as crazy (always going downtown) might deter the opponent. But it's also exhausting—if your rival learns you're predictable, they'll exploit it.

With three rounds, four rounds, or a hundred, the strategic depth explodes. Should you test your opponent's resolve early? Should you build a reputation or stay unpredictable? The decision tree branches exponentially.

Why This Matters

Chicken repeating is everywhere:

  • Arms races: Two nations must decide whether to escalate weapons. Yesterday's escalation affects today's threat perception.
  • Business competition: Two firms choosing between aggressive expansion (risky) or safe consolidation (stable). Rivals watch each move.
  • Negotiation standoffs: Labor and management repeatedly face deadlines. Each round shows who "blinks" first.
  • Cybersecurity: Attackers and defenders play repeated rounds, each learning and adapting.

The single-round Chicken has three Nash equilibria. But when you repeat it, something remarkable happens: the number of possible outcomes explodes exponentially. The backward induction method—analyzing from the last round backward to the first—reveals which equilibria are truly robust when players look ahead.

One intuitive SPE:

Guided Reflection

Explore these questions to deepen your intuition:

Why does having multiple equilibria in the subgames make backward induction more complex? ▼

In a single-round game, you find the Nash equilibria and you're done. In a multi-round game, each subgame also has multiple equilibria. Backward induction requires you to consider not just one subgame equilibrium, but all possible combinations. If each of 4 subgames has 3 equilibria, there are 3^4 = 81 possible ways to combine them—and each combination creates a different set of incentives for the earlier rounds. You must check which combinations lead to rational play in the earlier rounds. This combinatorial explosion is why multi-round games with multiple subgame equilibria are hard to analyze fully.

In repeated Chicken, can a player benefit from being known as "crazy" (always playing Straight/Dare)? ▼

Possibly, in the short term. If your rival believes you always Dare, they will always Swerve, giving you the (4, 1) outcome every round—a total payoff of 4N for N rounds. But this strategy has a major flaw: if both players are "crazy" and always Dare, the outcome is perpetual (0, 0) crashes. Moreover, in a truly repeated game with a known final round, backward induction may eliminate this strategy. And if the game truly continues indefinitely, a "crazy" strategy might invite retaliation that leaves both worse off. The equilibrium depends on beliefs: if your reputation is believed to be binding, you can exploit it. If the opponent thinks you might change, or if they can somehow prove you will crash together, your craziness is less valuable.

How does the number of possible equilibria grow as we add more rounds? ▼

It grows roughly exponentially. With 1 round, there are 3 Nash equilibria. With 2 rounds, there are up to 3^4 = 81 subgame-perfect equilibria (since we have 4 subgames after round 1, each with 3 equilibria). With 3 rounds, the count explodes further. More precisely: the number of subgames after round 1 is 4, and each becomes a 2-round subgame with ~81 SPE, so the 3-round game could have on the order of 81^4 possible equilibria, though the actual count of SPE may be lower if not all combinations are actually subgame-perfect. The key point: the space of equilibria explodes, making it hard to pin down unique predictions without additional assumptions about equilibrium selection.

Why might the mixed Nash equilibrium be the most likely outcome when players can't coordinate? ▼

The two pure-strategy equilibria of single-round Chicken are asymmetric: one player Swerves while the other Dares. If players don't know which role they'll take, or if they can't communicate and agree on who "gets" the (4, 1) outcome and who gets (1, 4), the mixed-strategy equilibrium becomes a focal point. Each player independently randomizes with the same probabilities. If both play D with probability 1/4, both face the same expected payoff (9/4 or 2.25), and neither has an incentive to deviate. In repeated games where players learn and adjust, play often converges to something close to the mixed equilibrium. It's robust because it doesn't require coordination or asymmetric roles.

How does multi-round Chicken relate to real-world situations like arms races or business competition? ▼

In an arms race, each nation repeatedly decides whether to escalate weapons development (Dare) or show restraint (Swerve). Mutual escalation is costly (both crash), but unilateral restraint leaves you vulnerable. History matters: if one nation escalated and won concessions, the other feels pressure to escalate next time to avoid future losses. In business, two firms compete on price or innovation. Aggressive tactics (price wars, R&D spending) can win market share but hurt profitability if both firms do it. The repeated nature means reputation develops: "Are you a aggressive competitor or cautious?" Rivals watch for patterns. Leaders who can credibly commit to "crazy" behavior (taking losses now to establish a fearsome reputation) may win later. But this is risky if the rival also decides to be crazy. Multi-round Chicken models the dynamic tension: each actor must balance immediate payoffs against the long-term impact of their actions on future interactions.
