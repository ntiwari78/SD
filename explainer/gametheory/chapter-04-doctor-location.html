Doctor Location Games

Where Should Two Competitors Locate?

CHAPTER 04 | EXAMPLE

🌉 The Feynman Bridge — Where Should You Open Your Shop?

Imagine a long beach with sunbathers spread evenly along it. Two ice cream vendors arrive. Where should each set up? If they're far apart, each gets the customers closest to them — nice and fair. But here's the twist: if vendor A is at the 1/4 mark and vendor B is at the 3/4 mark, A could inch toward the middle and steal some of B's customers. B responds by moving toward the middle too. They keep inching... until both are smack in the CENTER of the beach, back to back.

This is Hotelling's famous 'ice cream on the beach' result from 1929 — and it explains everything from why gas stations cluster at intersections to why political candidates sound the same.

But what if the beach isn't a straight line? What if it's a network of roads on an island? Now the geometry gets interesting. On a GRAPH, the shape of the network completely determines where rational competitors will locate — and whether a stable arrangement even exists.

In this chapter, two doctors choose where to set up practice on an island of connected towns. Patients always go to the nearest doctor. The doctors want to maximize their patient count. It sounds simple, but the structure of the road network creates surprising outcomes:

  • On some graphs, there are many stable locations (Nash equilibria)
  • On others, there is NO stable arrangement — every placement invites the other doctor to poach patients by relocating
  • When equilibria DO exist, they're often BAD for the public — both doctors cluster together instead of spreading out

This is the tension between competition and social good, played out on a map.

💡 Key Insight

In a constant-sum game on a graph, the TOPOLOGY of the network determines whether equilibria exist and how socially optimal they are. Competition doesn't always serve the public interest.

📐 The Mathematical Ascent — Graph Theory Meets Game Theory

2a. Formal Model

The Doctor Location Game on a graph $G = (V, E)$:

$$\Gamma_{\text{doc}} = \langle \{A, B\}, V, V, (u_A, u_B) \rangle$$
  • Players: Two doctors $\{A, B\}$
  • Strategy sets: $S_A = S_B = V$ (each doctor chooses a vertex)
  • Distance: $d(v, w)$ = length of shortest path between $v$ and $w$

Patient allocation for vertex $v$ when Ann is at $a$ and Beth is at $b$:

$$\text{visits}_A(v) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } d(v,a) < d(v,b) \\ 0 & \text{if } d(v,a) > d(v,b) \\ 0.5 & \text{if } d(v,a) = d(v,b) \end{cases}$$

Payoff: The number of towns each doctor serves (including half-towns):

$$u_A(a, b) = \sum_{v \in V} \text{visits}_A(v), \quad u_B(a,b) = n - u_A(a,b)$$

2b. Key Theorems

Theorem (Constant-Sum Property):
$$u_A(a,b) + u_B(a,b) = |V| = n \quad \forall (a,b)$$

This means we can view the game as zero-sum after subtracting $n/2$ from each payoff. Every patient Ann gains is a patient Beth loses.

Fact (NE Characterization):

In doctor location games, Nash equilibria are formed by every maximin move of Ann vs every maximin move of Beth, provided both security levels equal $n/2$. If security levels are below $n/2$, no pure NE exists.

Theorem (Trees — Branch-Weight):

For a tree $T$ with $n$ vertices, define the branch-weight of vertex $x$:

$$bw(x) = \max_{i} |T_i(x)|$$

where $T_i(x)$ are the connected components (branches) when $x$ is removed.

The maximin vertices are those minimizing $bw(x)$. They are either:

  • One vertex with $bw(x) < n/2$, or
  • Two adjacent vertices, each with $bw = n/2$

Medians (Jordan, 1869): Vertices minimizing distance sum $\sigma(x) = \sum_{v \in V} d(v,x)$ coincide with vertices minimizing branch-weight.

2d. Social Welfare Analysis

The "distance sum" measures social welfare — total distance from all vertices to the nearest doctor:

Comparison for 7-Vertex Graph:
  • Nash Equilibrium (both doctors at V6): Distance Sum = 19
  • Optimal Placement (doctors at V2 and V5): Distance Sum = 5

The NE is stable even though it's socially suboptimal. This is the median voter theorem applied to space — competitors cluster at the center because moving away unilaterally loses customers.

💻 Implementation Code

Here's a complete Python implementation of the doctor location game solver:

"""
Game Theory Through Examples — Chapter 4
The Doctor Location Game on Graphs

Implements the doctor location game where two doctors choose vertices
on an undirected graph. Patients go to the nearest doctor.

Features:
- BFS-based shortest path computation
- Payoff matrix generation for any graph
- Nash equilibrium finder (constant-sum game)
- Branch-weight computation for trees
- Social welfare (distance sum) calculator
- Median vertex finder
"""

import numpy as np
from collections import deque
from itertools import product as iter_product

class DoctorLocationGame:
    """Two-doctor location game on an undirected graph."""

    def __init__(self, n, edges):
        self.n = n
        self.adj = [[] for _ in range(n)]
        for u, v in edges:
            self.adj[u].append(v)
            self.adj[v].append(u)
        self.dist = self._all_pairs_bfs()

    def _bfs(self, start):
        """BFS from a single source."""
        dist = [-1] * self.n
        dist[start] = 0
        queue = deque([start])
        while queue:
            u = queue.popleft()
            for v in self.adj[u]:
                if dist[v] == -1:
                    dist[v] = dist[u] + 1
                    queue.append(v)
        return dist

    def _all_pairs_bfs(self):
        """Compute all-pairs shortest paths."""
        return [self._bfs(i) for i in range(self.n)]

    def payoff(self, ann_loc, beth_loc):
        """Compute payoffs for placement (ann_loc, beth_loc)."""
        ann_pay = 0.0
        for v in range(self.n):
            da = self.dist[ann_loc][v]
            db = self.dist[beth_loc][v]
            if da < db:
                ann_pay += 1
            elif da == db:
                ann_pay += 0.5
        return (ann_pay, self.n - ann_pay)

    def payoff_matrix(self):
        """Generate n×n payoff matrices."""
        A = np.zeros((self.n, self.n))
        B = np.zeros((self.n, self.n))
        for i in range(self.n):
            for j in range(self.n):
                a, b = self.payoff(i, j)
                A[i][j] = a
                B[i][j] = b
        return A, B

    def nash_equilibria(self):
        """Find all pure Nash equilibria."""
        A, B = self.payoff_matrix()
        equilibria = []
        for i in range(self.n):
            for j in range(self.n):
                if A[i][j] >= np.max(A[:, j]) and \
                   B[i][j] >= np.max(B[i, :]):
                    equilibria.append((i, j, A[i][j], B[i][j]))
        return equilibria

    def distance_sum(self, ann_loc, beth_loc):
        """Total distance from all vertices to nearest doctor."""
        total = 0
        for v in range(self.n):
            total += min(self.dist[ann_loc][v],
                        self.dist[beth_loc][v])
        return total

    def optimal_placement(self):
        """Find placement minimizing distance sum."""
        best = (None, None, float('inf'))
        for i in range(self.n):
            for j in range(i, self.n):
                ds = self.distance_sum(i, j)
                if ds < best[2]:
                    best = (i, j, ds)
        return best

# Example: 7-vertex graph from Prisner Ch. 4
graph7 = DoctorLocationGame(7, [
    (0, 1), (0, 4), (1, 2), (1, 4), (2, 3), (2, 6),
    (3, 4), (3, 5), (4, 5), (5, 6)
])

ne = graph7.nash_equilibria()
print(f"Nash Equilibria: {len(ne)} found")
for a, b, pa, pb in ne:
    print(f"  ({a+1}, {b+1}) → ({pa}, {pb})")

🏛️ The Socratic Sandbox — Location Strategy Puzzles

Test your understanding with these thought-provoking questions about doctor location games, spatial competition, and the relationship between equilibrium and social welfare.

Q1: Why is the Doctor Location Game Constant-Sum? ▼

The Setup: There are $n$ vertices with residents distributed across them. Every resident must visit the nearest doctor. When a doctor gains a patient, the other doctor loses exactly one patient (or 0.5 in a tie).

The Insight: This makes the game constant-sum: $u_A(a,b) + u_B(a,b) = n$ always. We can subtract $n/2$ from each payoff to convert it to a zero-sum game.

Consequence: From Chapter 2, we know that in zero-sum games, Nash equilibria are formed by pairs of maximin moves (provided both security levels equal $n/2$). There's no possibility for mutual gain or cooperation — the game is purely competitive. Every strategic advantage for Ann is a disadvantage for Beth.

Why This Matters: Unlike games with positive-sum payoffs (where cooperation could benefit both), here the doctors' interests are perfectly opposed. This makes the equilibrium concept especially clean and the strategic logic pure competition.

Q2: The Clustering Problem — Why Don't Doctors Spread Out? ▼

The Observation: In the 7-vertex graph, both doctors placing at vertex 6 (the median) is a Nash equilibrium. Both get 3.5 patients. But if they split to vertices 2 and 5, the total distance sum drops from 19 to 5 — society is much better off!

Why They Don't Separate: Nash equilibrium doesn't require social optimality. Let's check incentives:

  • Both at V6 (Nash): Ann gets 3.5, Beth gets 3.5
  • If Ann moves to V2 unilaterally: Beth stays at V6. Ann now gets fewer patients (Beth dominates the center). Ann's payoff drops.
  • If Beth moves to V5 unilaterally: Ann stays at V6. Beth now gets fewer patients. Beth's payoff drops.

The Coordination Failure: Neither doctor wants to be the first to move away from the center. This is the median voter theorem applied to space. The center is the battleground — moving away seems like surrender. Both doctors would prefer mutual separation to both being at the center, BUT neither can achieve this unilaterally.

Policy Implication: This is why governments sometimes regulate business locations (pharmacy zoning, hospital placement) — competitive markets naturally produce excessive clustering, harming geographic coverage.

Q3: Tree Branch-Weights and the Median ▼

The Challenge: On a path graph 1-2-3-4-5, compute the branch-weight of each vertex. Which are the maximin moves? What are the Nash equilibria?

Solution — Branch-Weights: When we remove each vertex, we get branches:

  • Remove V1: branches {2-3-4-5} (size 4). So $bw(1) = 4$
  • Remove V2: branches {1} and {3-4-5} (sizes 1, 3). So $bw(2) = 3$
  • Remove V3: branches {1-2} and {4-5} (sizes 2, 2). So $bw(3) = 2$
  • Remove V4: branches {1-2-3} and {5} (sizes 3, 1). So $bw(4) = 3$
  • Remove V5: branches {1-2-3-4} (size 4). So $bw(5) = 4$

Maximin Vertices: Minimize $bw$: only V3 has $bw(3) = 2 < n/2 = 2.5$. So V3 is the unique maximin vertex with security level $n - 2 = 3 > n/2$. ✓

Nash Equilibria: Only (V3, V3). Both doctors at the center. This is optimal for society AND a stable equilibrium. This is the monopoly case — if only one doctor exists, they'd locate at the median. With two, they both do!

Moral: Trees are special — the unique minimizer of branch-weight is also the median. On paths, the center is both strategically optimal and socially optimal.

Q4: Designing a Graph with No Nash Equilibrium ▼

The Challenge: Create a graph with 6 vertices that has NO pure Nash equilibrium for the doctor location game. What structural property does it have?

Solution — A 6-Cycle: Consider the cycle graph 1-2-3-4-5-6-1.

Why No NE? By symmetry, every vertex has the same security level. For a 6-cycle, we can verify that every vertex has security level 2 (or close to it), which is less than $n/2 = 3$. This means:

  • No vertex is a maximin move with security $\geq 3$
  • For any placement, at least one doctor can improve by relocating to a more "opposite" vertex
  • The symmetry of the cycle means neither player can find a stable position

Structural Pattern: Graphs without NE tend to be:

  • Too symmetric: Every location looks equivalent (hard to break ties)
  • Too spread out: No central vertex where both doctors want to concentrate
  • Small and balanced: Few vertices, many far from any center

Contrast: Graphs WITH NE (like stars, trees with clear centers) have a natural "attracting" location where both doctors are content to be.

Q5: Market Competition and Public Policy — A Deep Dive ▼

The Observation: Prisner notes that for trees, the Nash equilibrium locations (medians) would be perfect if there were only ONE doctor. But with TWO doctors, the equilibrium is almost always suboptimal for society.

Why One Doctor is Optimal: A monopolist maximizes her profit by locating at the median — this minimizes the average distance patients must travel. This is also what a social planner would want! One doctor at the median is both privately optimal AND socially optimal.

Why Two Doctors Fail: When a second doctor enters, both race to the center (the median), trying to capture the most patients. This duplicates coverage at the median while leaving peripheral towns underserved. The equilibrium is stable (neither wants to unilaterally move away), but it's terrible for society.

The Deeper Lesson: This reveals a fundamental tension between competition and efficiency in spatial markets:

  • Price competition: Often drives prices down, benefiting consumers (efficient)
  • Spatial competition: Often drives clustering, harming geographic coverage (inefficient)

Policy Implications:

  • Healthcare: Governments regulate hospital and clinic locations to ensure rural coverage
  • Pharmacies: Some regions restrict new pharmacy licenses in areas with adequate coverage
  • Retail: Zoning laws prevent excessive commercial clustering in city centers
  • Schools: Public school placement tries to balance geographic equity, not just efficient centers

Conclusion: Competition doesn't automatically produce socially optimal outcomes when location is the strategic variable. Sometimes intervention is needed to achieve geographic balance and serve underserved populations.
