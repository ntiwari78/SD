🎯Feynman Bridge: The Unintuitive World of Sequential Games

Imagine this scenario: three contestants—Ann, Beth, and Cindy—stand on a quiz show stage. They face a brutally difficult multiple-choice question with five possible answers. None of them knows which answer is correct. They take turns deciding: do you answer now and risk the penalty if you're wrong, or do you wait and let the quiz master reveal a wrong answer to the next person?

Intuitively, going first seems like an advantage. But game theory reveals something counterintuitive: the first player often has the worst expected payoff. Why? Because waiting is valuable when you're clueless. Each wrong answer that's eliminated gives you better odds on your next turn.

The "More May Be Less" Paradox: Here's where it gets truly strange. Imagine you increase the prize money from $750 to $810. You'd think everyone would be happier, right? Wrong. In some parameter ranges, Ann's and Beth's expected payoffs actually DECREASE when the prize goes up. The reason: higher prize money changes Cindy's optimal strategy (she starts answering instead of waiting), which indirectly hurts the players before her. This is a fundamental property of non-cooperative games without side payments.
Information Asymmetry: What if one player secretly knows that one of the five answers is wrong? This knowledge advantage creates a complex game where a player with extra information faces a dilemma: should they use their advantage immediately, or wait and preserve it? The probability that they maintain their advantage decreases each time another player waits and an answer is eliminated by chance.

In this chapter, we'll explore:

  • The extensive form game tree with backward induction to find optimal strategies
  • Expected payoff calculations for different parameter values
  • The paradox where increasing payoffs makes players worse off
  • Information advantages and how they propagate through sequential decisions

❓Socratic Sandbox: Questions and Insights

Expand each question to explore the deeper logic behind sequential games and information asymmetry.

Q1: Why does the second player (Beth) typically have the highest expected payoff? ⌄

The second player occupies a "sweet spot" in the game tree. Unlike the first player (Ann), Beth gets to see whether Ann answered or waited. If Ann answers and loses, the quiz master reveals a wrong answer, improving Beth's odds. If Ann waits, the quiz master still reveals an answer, and Beth can decide with slightly better information.

The second player also has an advantage over the third player (Cindy): if Beth answers, Cindy faces worse odds. This positioning—able to react to the first player but with options still available—is game-theoretically optimal for maximizing expected payoff.

Intuition: Information and optionality matter more than going first. Beth gets information about Ann's choice (directly or indirectly) while maintaining her own options.

Q2: How can increasing the prize money make players worse off? Isn't more money always better? ⌄

This is the classic "More May Be Less" paradox in non-cooperative games. The key insight: increasing the prize doesn't just benefit all players equally. It changes incentives, which changes strategies, which can hurt some players indirectly.

Example: In SHOW(750, 400), suppose at this prize level, Cindy optimally waits (hoping to answer later with fewer choices). When the prize increases to $810, Cindy's calculation changes: the extra $60 makes answering immediately more attractive (maybe worth the risk now). If Cindy switches to answering, Ann and Beth face a very different game—Cindy is eliminating herself before they get a chance to benefit from waiting.

Mathematically: Let be Cindy's decision threshold. When n crosses this threshold, Cindy's strategy flips. Ann and Beth's expected payoffs depend on Cindy's strategy, so they can decrease when Cindy changes.

Why it's important: In non-cooperative games without binding agreements or side payments, collective rationality and individual rationality diverge. Everyone playing optimally for themselves can yield worse payoffs for everyone than some cooperatively agreed outcome.

Q3: Why does the 'More May Be Less' paradox disappear if players can cooperate? ⌄

Cooperation enables binding agreements and side payments. Instead of each player optimizing individually, they can agree on strategies that maximize joint payoff and redistribute the gains.

Cooperative approach: The players might agree: "If prize increases, Cindy still waits (even though it's no longer individually optimal for her) because Ann and Beth will compensate her with side payments from their increased expected payoffs." This requires:

  • Ability to make binding commitments (no one deviates)
  • Ability to transfer utility (side payments)
  • Trust or enforcement mechanisms

With these, the group can achieve a payoff distribution where everyone is at least as well off as in non-cooperative play, and some are strictly better off.

Game theory lesson: The paradox is fundamentally about externalities. Cindy's strategy choice imposes negative externalities on Ann and Beth. Cooperation internalizes these externalities through coordination.

Q4: When one player has extra knowledge, why don't the other players benefit from observing their behavior? ⌄

This gets at the subtlety of perfect versus imperfect information. In the model, the quiz is perfect information from a spectator's perspective: everyone sees every choice and the quiz master's random eliminations. But the informed player's knowledge isn't publicly observable—only their actions are.

Two effects of the informed player's knowledge:

  • Direct advantage: The informed player has 4/5 odds instead of 1/5 when they know one answer is wrong and decide to answer.
  • Strategic signaling: If the informed player's behavior differs visibly from what an uninformed player would do, the others might infer something. But in equilibrium, the informed player's strategy is optimized given their advantage, and naive inference can be misleading.

The uninformed players don't directly benefit because they can't reverse-engineer the informed player's knowledge from observed actions alone. They see a sequence of waits/answers, but they don't know if these reflect genuine advantage or just strategy.

Information asymmetry persists: The informed player's advantage doesn't evaporate. Instead, the question is whether the advantage is used immediately or reserved. When preserved, the informed player's odds stay better throughout, a structural advantage that other players cannot fully overcome.

Q5: How does the number of answer choices affect the optimal strategy? ⌄

The number of answer choices k is critical to the decision calculus. The more choices, the less attractive answering becomes; the fewer choices, the more attractive.

Decision rule: A player answers now (with k choices) if the expected payoff from answering exceeds the expected payoff from waiting:

(1/k)·n - ((k-1)/k)·m > E[wait | k choices]

As k decreases (from 5 to 4 to 3 to 2):

  • The expected payoff from answering increases (1/k grows)
  • The cost of being wrong decreases (fewer wrong options)
  • Waiting becomes less valuable (fewer answers to eliminate)

This creates a monotone strategy threshold: there's a critical number k* such that a player waits if k > k* and answers if k ≤ k*. The exact threshold depends on the payoff parameters (n, m) and the future game tree.

Endgame behavior: With k=2 (two choices left), most players answer because winning 50% is attractive and waiting doesn't help much. With k=5 (many choices), waiting is more attractive because answers are frequently wrong.
