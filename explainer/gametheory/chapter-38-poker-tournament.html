Chapter 38: End-of-Semester Poker Tournament

Feynman Bridge: The Robot Arena

Imagine a robot fighting arena, except instead of physical combat, each robot is a poker player defined entirely by 12 numbers — its DNA.

These 12 numbers control everything about how your robot plays:

  • As the first player (Ann): How often does it bluff? How cautiously does it fold?
  • As the second player (Beth): Does it call risky bets? Does it re-raise?

A classroom of students designed robots with different strategies. Some robots are aggressive bluffers. Others play it safe. One robot was designed to play mathematically perfect poker — the Nash equilibrium strategy. It should win against everyone, right?

Surprisingly, not necessarily.

Here's why: tournaments aren't about being theoretically optimal. They're about beating the specific opponents you face. A "weaker" robot might exploit your weaknesses perfectly. The perfect robot might be unbeatable in the long run but lose to a lucky opponent in the short term. The bracket matters. The matchups matter.

This chapter explores what happens when 16 robots enter a knockout tournament. We'll see the expected matchups, calculate winning probabilities, and discover why theory doesn't always predict practice.

Big Question: If you designed a robot to win this tournament, would you make it play perfect Nash poker, or would you make it exploit the specific weaknesses of the other robots in the room?

Mathematical Ascent: The Theory

Robot DNA: A 12-Dimensional Strategy Space

Each robot's behavior is defined by exactly 12 probabilities, arranged as a vector p = (p_1, p_2, ..., p_{12}) \in [0,1]^{12}.

Ann's Strategy (first player):
$p_1$ = Raise with Jack, $p_2$ = Raise with Queen, $p_3$ = Raise with King
$p_4$ = Call check-raise with Jack, $p_5$ = Call check-raise with Queen, $p_6$ = Call check-raise with King

Beth's Strategy (second player):
$p_7$ = Call raise with Jack, $p_8$ = Call raise with Queen, $p_9$ = Call raise with King
$p_{10}$ = Raise check with Jack, $p_{11}$ = Raise check with Queen, $p_{12}$ = Raise check with King

Expected Payoff from Two Strategies

When two robots play 200 rounds of Kuhn poker, their expected payoff depends on:

Card probabilities in Kuhn(3,4,2,3):
$p_{XX} = \frac{3}{12} \cdot \frac{1}{11} \approx 0.0227$ (both players same rank)
$p_{XY} = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{12} \cdot \frac{4}{11} \approx 0.0606$ (distinct ranks)

The expected payoff is a function of both robots' DNA vectors. Robots with higher expected payoff have a mathematical advantage, but in finite rounds, luck plays a huge role.

From Expected Value to Win Probability

In any single match, the outcome is random. We convert expected monetary value to probabilities:

Given expected payoff $\mu$ and empirical variance $\sigma^2$ over $n$ rounds:
$P(\text{win} \mid n \text{ rounds}) = \Phi\left(\frac{\mu\sqrt{n}}{\sigma}\right)$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.

This explains why "more rounds → skill dominates luck." As $n \to \infty$, even a small positive expected advantage becomes overwhelming.

DNA Similarity: Euclidean Distance

Two robots with similar DNA should play similarly. We measure similarity via Euclidean distance:

$d(p, q) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{12} (p_i - q_i)^2}$

Range: $d \in [0, 12]$ (0 = identical robots, 12 = maximally different)

Socratic Sandbox: Questions & Exploration

These questions invite deeper thinking about tournament design, probability, and strategy. Click each question to reveal a thoughtful answer.

Why doesn't the Nash equilibrium robot (Amadeus) always win tournaments? ▼

The Nash equilibrium is optimal against all opponents simultaneously, but a tournament is about beating specific opponents in sequence. Amadeus guarantees a non-negative expected payoff against anyone, but in 200 rounds, luck plays a huge role. Another robot might:

  • Exploit a specific opponent's weaknesses perfectly
  • Get lucky and win a high-variance match
  • Face opponents in an order that favors it

Tournament victory requires both skill (positive expected value) and luck (getting favorable variance at the right time). The Nash robot minimizes downside but doesn't maximize the specific tournament format.

How does the number of rounds affect whether skill or luck determines the winner? ▼

Skill is a like a tiny advantage that only matters in volume. In a single poker hand, skill might be worth just a few cents, but luck dominates. Chris Ferguson once said: "One hand is 99% luck. After 8 hours of poker, it's still 88% luck."

Mathematically, over $n$ rounds with expected advantage $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$:

$P(\text{skill determines outcome}) \propto \frac{\mu\sqrt{n}}{\sigma}$

With 200 rounds, skill is meaningful but not dominant. With 2000 rounds, the better robot almost always wins. With 20 rounds, almost anyone can win. Tournament format matters. Shorter matches favor upsets; longer matches favor skill.

Why might a "weaker" robot (lower expected value) win more often than a "stronger" one? ▼

This happened in the 16-robot tournament! Rupert, who doesn't have the highest expected payoff, actually won the tournament most often. Why?

  • Relative advantage matters more than absolute strength: Rupert might have a small advantage over Max, but a huge advantage over other robots. In the tournament bracket, Rupert avoids the strongest robots for longer.
  • Population composition: Rupert's strategy happens to exploit the other 15 robots in the room exceptionally well, even if it's not "objectively" optimal.
  • Matchup luck: A weaker robot can still get lucky runs of favorable pairings.

This is why tournament design is an art: who you play matters as much as how well you play.

What does robot DNA similarity tell us about performance similarity? ▼

DNA similarity (measured via Euclidean distance in 12-dimensional space) tells us how different two strategies are, but it doesn't directly predict performance differences.

  • Similar DNA ≠ similar results: Two robots might have DNA distance of 1.0, but face completely different opponents and get completely different results.
  • DNA distance is local: It measures behavioral differences, not performance differences. A small change in one probability might flip a robot from weak to dominant against a specific opponent.
  • Useful for exploration: If two robots with similar DNA have very different records, you know something interesting is happening in their matchups.

Think of DNA as the genotype (what the robot is) and tournament record as the phenotype (what the robot does). The link isn't deterministic—environment (other robots) matters hugely.

If you designed a robot to win this tournament, would you play Nash or exploit opponents? ▼

This is the deepest strategic question. You have two philosophies:

  • Play Nash (theoretically sound): You'll have positive expected value against everyone, guaranteeing long-term victory. But in a single tournament, you might lose to a specialized exploiter.
  • Exploit your opponents (pragmatically strong): Design your robot specifically to beat the 15 other robots in the room. You might lose to new opponents, but you'll dominate this tournament.

The real answer: It depends on what you optimize for. If you want to win this tournament, exploit. If you want to win any tournament against any opponents, play Nash.

In professional poker, the best players do both: they play a fundamentally sound strategy (close to Nash) but adjust at the margins to exploit specific opponents. Amadeus is pure Nash; a tournament winner might tweak 2-3 probabilities based on what it sees.
