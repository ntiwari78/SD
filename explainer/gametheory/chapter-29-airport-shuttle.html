🏖️ The Feynman Bridge: Ice Cream on a Beach

Imagine a mile-long beach on a hot summer day. Two ice cream vendors, Ann and Beth, must decide where to park their trucks. Customers are scattered uniformly along the beach and will walk to the nearest vendor.

Ann's logic: "If I park in the middle, I capture everyone who's closer to me than Beth."

Beth's response: "But if you park in the middle, I'll park right next to you—slightly to the right—so I steal all your customers who are to my right and are now closer to me!"

The result? Both vendors end up right in the center, back-to-back. This is the Nash equilibrium of spatial competition. Neither can move without losing customers to the other. The vendors compete away from the edges where customers would have to walk farther, concentrating instead where the most valuable real estate is: the center.

This is Hotelling's Law: the principle of minimum differentiation—competing firms converge to the same location (or similar products) to maximize their share of a uniform market.

Now imagine the beach is circular—a one-hour loop of passenger arrivals at an airport shuttle station. Two shuttle companies must choose their departure times. Passengers arrive uniformly and take whichever shuttle departs next. The economic logic is identical to the beach vendors, but now played out in time instead of space.

Impatient Customers: The Wait Threshold

Suppose a customer will wait at most w (measured as a fraction of the hour, so w ∈ [0,1]) for a shuttle. If the next shuttle is more than w away, they take a taxi instead (exit the game).

This dramatically changes the payoff structure. Now:

When w is very small, customers are highly impatient. Firms may now have incentive to spread out their departure times to avoid direct competition and ensure some customers don't defect to taxis.

Hotelling's Law and Minimum Differentiation

Hotelling's Law states: In competition for market share with uniformly distributed consumers, profit-maximizing firms will choose the same or very similar product characteristics.

This is inefficient from society's perspective—passengers would prefer shuttles spread throughout the hour (short wait times). But firms prefer convergence because it maximizes their individual market share by capturing customers near the boundary of the market.

❓ Socratic Sandbox: Five Guiding Questions

Use these questions to deepen your intuition about airport shuttle competition and spatial economics.

Why do both shuttles end up departing at the same time in equilibrium?

▼

Because each shuttle captures passengers based on departure time. If Ann departs at time 0:00 and Beth at 0:30, Ann gets all passengers arriving between 0:15 (the midpoint) and 1:00. But if Beth sees this, she'd want to move slightly before Ann—say to 0:29—to steal almost all of Ann's passengers.

Ann anticipates this and moves to 0:28. Beth then moves to 0:27. This race continues until they both converge to the same time. At that point, neither can move without handing all their customers to the rival. They're stuck—that's equilibrium.

The intuition: in spatial competition, firms have incentive to cluster because the "boundary" (where passengers are indifferent) is where the most valuable market share is. Moving to the center captures this boundary.

How does this relate to why competing stores often locate next to each other?

▼

This is a direct application of Hotelling's Law. In real cities, you often see competing coffee shops, gas stations, or fast-food restaurants clustered together rather than spread throughout town. This seems wasteful—wouldn't it be better to spread them out?

But from each firm's perspective, clustering is rational. If Starbucks locates in the busy downtown center, a nearby Dunkin' will want to open right across the street. Why? Because the downtown center has the most customer traffic. Each firm wants to capture some of that high-traffic location.

The equilibrium is socially inefficient (passengers walk farther if stores are clustered), but individually rational for firms. This is a key insight: Nash equilibrium doesn't guarantee social welfare!

What happens to the equilibrium when customers become impatient?

▼

When the impatience threshold w is small (customers won't wait long), the incentives change dramatically. Now, if both shuttles depart at the same time, passengers who arrive just before that departure time must wait a full hour for the next shuttle. They defect to taxis.

This creates an incentive to spread out departure times. If Ann departs at 0:00 and Beth at 0:30 (with w = 0.25), a passenger arriving at 0:10 waits 20 minutes for the next shuttle instead of 50 minutes. More passengers choose shuttles instead of taxis.

The equilibrium shifts: firms prefer to differentiate (choose different departure times) to reduce customer defection. Interestingly, this makes passengers better off! Impatient customers force firms to spread out and provide better coverage.

Is the Nash equilibrium socially optimal (best for passengers)?

▼

No. From a passenger's perspective, the best outcome is shuttles spread evenly throughout the hour—say Ann at 0:00, Beth at 0:30. Then no passenger waits more than 30 minutes.

But the Nash equilibrium has both at the same time. A passenger arriving just before that time waits a full hour. This is a classic example of market failure: individual incentives (for firms) don't align with social welfare (passenger convenience).

This is why cities often regulate taxi and transit services: to mandate coverage and prevent wasteful clustering. Without regulation, competition alone won't produce socially optimal outcomes.

How would adding a third shuttle company change the dynamics?

▼

With three or more firms, the analysis becomes more complex. The Nash equilibrium could involve either clustering (all three at the same time) or partial differentiation.

With three firms at the same departure time, each gets 1/3. But there's less incentive for a single firm to deviate unilaterally—moving slightly earlier only captures the other two firms' customers. With more firms, moving slightly can be less profitable.

Empirically, with more competitors, there's often more differentiation in equilibrium. This is called the median voter theorem effect in some models. More competition can push firms to spread out and serve more diverse customer preferences.

The exact equilibrium depends on the cost structure, market size, and other factors—but the key lesson is: competition alone may not prevent inefficient clustering.
