🗳️ Chapter 7: Election I

Electoral Resource Allocation in Zero-Sum Games

🎯 Section 1: The Campaign War Room

Allocating Scarce Resources Across Districts

The Analogy

Imagine you're a campaign manager with exactly 3 volunteers to deploy across 3 swing states. Your opponent has the same resources. Each state has different numbers of electoral votes:

  • District C: 7 electoral votes
  • District D: 8 electoral votes
  • District E: 13 electoral votes

You're currently behind by 1 vote in districts C and D, but ahead by 1 vote in district E. Where do you send your people? Can you defend your advantages while flipping opponent territory?

Key Insight: This is a Colonel Blotto-style game — simultaneous resource allocation across multiple battlefields with the constraint that x + y + z = r (total resources). You cannot create resources from nothing.

Core Game Mechanics

Why This Matters:

  • Pre-existing Advantages Matter: Being behind by 1 in a state means you need 2+ extra resources to flip it.
  • District Sizes Create Asymmetry: Winning the big district (13 votes) can outweigh losing two small ones (7+8=15, but not always defensible).
  • Zero-Sum Nature: One candidate's win is the other's loss. No deals, no cooperation.
  • Simultaneous Moves: You don't know opponent's allocation until after you commit your resources.

📐 Section 2: Formalizing Electoral Resource Allocation

Game Definition

ELECTION(c, d, e | c₁, d₁, e₁ | a, b) is a zero-sum game where:

  • c, d, e = electoral votes in districts C, D, E
  • c_1, d_1, e_1 = Ann's current lead in each district (negative = behind)
  • a, b = total resources available to Ann and Beth respectively

Move Space: A strategy (x, y, z) with x + y + z = a (for Ann), all non-negative integers.

Number of Possible Moves: C(r+2, 2) = \frac{(r+1)(r+2)}{2}

  • With 3 resources: 10 moves
  • With 4 resources: 15 moves
  • With 5 resources: 21 moves

Payoff Computation

For each district, Ann's net strength is:

\text{Net}_i = c_i + a_i - b_i

  • If \text{Net}_i > 0 → Ann wins district i (gets electoral votes)
  • If \text{Net}_i = 0 → District abstains (neutral)
  • If \text{Net}_i < 0 → Beth wins district i

Final Payoff:

  • Ann's total votes > Beth's total → Payoff = +1 (Ann wins)
  • Ann's total votes = Beth's total → Payoff = 0 (Draw)
  • Ann's total votes < Beth's total → Payoff = -1 (Beth wins)

Canonical Examples

ELECTION 1: \text{ELECTION}(7, 8, 13 | -1, -1, 1 | 3, 3)

  • 10 possible moves each
  • Beth's move (0,0,3) is dominant: She matches Ann in E and already leads in C, D → always wins
  • No pure Nash equilibrium exists

ELECTION 2: \text{ELECTION}(7, 8, 13 | -1, -1, 1 | 4, 4)

  • 15 possible moves each
  • No pure NE; requires iterated elimination of weakly dominated strategies (IEWD)
  • After IEWD: 4×4 symmetric matrix where each remaining move beats exactly one other (like extended Rock-Paper-Scissors)

🤔 Section 4: Socratic Reflection Questions

Expand each question to reveal the answer and deepen your understanding:

▼ Question 1: In ELECTION 1, why does Beth's move (0,0,3) always win?

Beth's strategy (0,0,3) allocates all resources to district E. Remember the initial state: Ann leads by 1 in E, while Beth leads by 1 in both C and D. By allocating all 3 resources to E, Beth matches Ann's investment there (1 initial lead + 3 resources vs. Ann's 1 initial lead + whatever Ann allocates to E). Meanwhile, in districts C and D, Beth already has a 1-vote advantage with zero additional investment.

This dominates because:

  • Even if Ann puts all 3 resources in E, she ties (1+3 vs. 0+3 before Beth's moves). But she does this while abandoning C and D, which Beth wins easily.
  • If Ann splits resources, she can't overcome Beth's advantages anywhere.
▼ Question 2: Why does IEWD leave a symmetric 4×4 matrix in ELECTION 2 where each move beats exactly one other?

After iterated elimination, the game becomes perfectly cyclical—like an extended Rock-Paper-Scissors. This structure emerges because:

  • The original game is symmetric (both players have 4 resources, identical initial state before leads).
  • Dominated strategies eliminate all "safe bets," leaving only strategies in a cycle.
  • No move is strictly better than all others—every remaining move has exactly one counter-move that beats it.

This cyclicity guarantees no pure Nash equilibrium exists. The game requires mixed strategies—each player randomizing over their remaining moves with specific probabilities (introduced in Chapter 14).

▼ Question 3: The Eight Cases classification shows that changing D from 8 to 9 votes doesn't fundamentally change the game. Why?

The critical property is the relationship between c+d and e:

  • Case 1 (7+8=15 > 13): Winning districts C and D beats E.
  • Case 2 (7+9=16 > 13): Winning districts C and D still beats E.

Since both satisfy c+d > e, the strategic incentives remain identical. A player winning C and D wins the election either way. The exact electoral vote counts (8 vs. 9) don't matter—only whether they're large enough to matter strategically. This illustrates a key principle: absolute vote counts matter less than relative relationships between districts.

▼ Question 4: In voting systems, why doesn't a 1-vote player always have voting power?

Consider a system with vote counts [4, 3, 2, 1] and quota 6 to pass.

  • Winning coalitions: {4,3}, {4,2,1}, {3,2,1}, {4,2}, {3,2}, {4,3,2,1}
  • The 1-vote player appears in {4,2,1} and {3,2,1}.
  • But in {4,2,1}, removing the 1-vote voter leaves {4,2}=6, still winning. Not pivotal!
  • In {3,2,1}, removing the 1-vote voter leaves {3,2}=5, losing. Now pivotal!

The 1-vote player has minimal power because they're rarely a swing voter. The 4-vote player is swing in every coalition they're in. Voting power ≠ vote count. Power depends on how often you're pivotal across all possible coalitions.

▼ Question 5: How would the Election game change if districts split votes proportionally instead of winner-take-all?

The game would fundamentally change:

  • No longer zero-sum: With proportional allocation, a 3-2 lead in a district gives 60% of votes to Ann, 40% to Beth. Both gain something, and the extreme +1/-1 payoffs disappear.
  • Continuous payoffs: Each district contributes a fraction proportional to relative resource investment, smoothing the landscape.
  • Pure NE likely emerge: The smooth payoff surface makes it easier for both players to find mutual best-response strategies without randomization.
  • Strategic focus shifts: Rather than all-or-nothing "capturing" districts, players fine-tune marginal resource allocation.

This mirrors real-world debates about electoral reform (moving away from winner-take-all toward proportional representation). The game structure—and thus optimal strategies—change dramatically.
