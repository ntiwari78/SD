The Feynman Bridge: Political Campaign Strategy

Imagine you're running for president with just 10 campaign days left before the election. Your opponent has 12 days. You both want to secure 270 electoral votes to win. In front of you is a map with 5 battleground states:

  • California: 54 electoral votes — massive, but your opponent is strong here
  • Texas: 40 electoral votes — your opponent's stronghold
  • Swing State A: 20 electoral votes — truly competitive
  • Swing State B: 15 electoral votes — truly competitive
  • Swing State C: 11 electoral votes — small but winnable

Here's the puzzle: Where do you spend your time?

Do you defend — spend more days where you're already strong, locking in sure victories? Or do you attack — concentrate your limited resources on the states your opponent currently leads, trying to flip them? The intuition pulls both ways. Defending secures what you have. Attacking tries to gain ground. But game theory reveals that the answer depends on the specific numbers, and sometimes counterintuitively, having more campaign days can actually hurt you.

This is the Election game, a beautiful example of how resources matter, but how you allocate them matters more. And when both candidates optimize their strategies, you often don't get a pure strategy equilibrium — you get mixing. Candidates randomize. They keep their opponent guessing about where they'll show up.

This chapter extends our analysis from Chapter 13 (Election I) with deeper tools: normal form payoff matrices, domination, and mixed strategy Nash equilibria. We'll also discover a surprising connection to military strategy (the Colonel Blotto problem) and why the Electoral College creates such strange incentives in real politics.

The Paradox of More Resources

A counterintuitive result, similar to "More May Be Less" from Chapter 17: Sometimes increasing your total resources makes you worse off, not better.

Why? When resources are scarce, both candidates focus on high-value targets. As one candidate's resource level increases, the balance tips. The game structure can shift so dramatically that the larger-resource player is forced into a worse equilibrium.

Connection to Colonel Blotto

The ELECTION game is a variant of the Colonel Blotto game (Chapter 21), a classical military strategy model. In Blotto, a colonel must deploy troops across n battlefields. The side that allocates more troops to a given battlefield wins it. The ELECTION game applies the same idea to politics: districts are battlefields, campaign days are troops, and electoral votes are the prize for winning.

Domination and IESD

In ELECTION games, we often apply Iterated Elimination of Strictly Dominated Strategies (IESD):

  • A pure strategy is strictly dominated if there exists another strategy that guarantees a higher payoff against every possible opponent strategy.
  • In ELECTION, strategies that waste resources (e.g., allocating far more than needed to one small district) are often dominated.
  • Repeatedly eliminating dominated strategies can dramatically reduce the game's complexity and sometimes reveal pure NE.

Socratic Sandbox: Five Guiding Questions

Expand each question below to explore deeper insights about election strategy and game theory:

1. When should a candidate focus on large districts vs. spread resources evenly? ▼

The optimal strategy depends on the resource balance. If you have significantly fewer resources than your opponent, spreading evenly can be a dominant strategy — you can't win big districts outright, but you can try to claim many small ones. This is called a "breadth" strategy.

Conversely, if you have a resource advantage or comparable resources, focusing on the largest districts makes sense: you can win the high-value prizes and achieve the electoral majority faster. This is a "focus" strategy.

In mixed strategy equilibrium, candidates often randomize between these extremes, keeping opponents uncertain about which regions they'll contest.

2. Why might having more campaign days sometimes hurt a candidate? ▼

This is the "More May Be Less" paradox. When one candidate's resources increase dramatically, the game's strategic structure can shift unfavorably. Here's why:

  • Forced Diversification: With more resources, you can't just exploit your opponent's resource-scarce choices. You must spread your extra resources somehow, possibly into wasteful allocations.
  • Equilibrium Shift: The Nash equilibrium of the game changes. A larger resource advantage might push you into a mixed strategy that yields a worse payoff than a more balanced game state.
  • Strategic Retaliation: Your opponent, knowing you have more, can adjust their strategy to block your attempts to dominate. The game becomes more competitive and unpredictable.

This mirrors the "military inefficiency" observed in some Colonel Blotto variants: more troops doesn't always win because the opponent can adapt.

3. How does the Electoral College system create different incentives than a popular vote? ▼

The Electoral College is fundamentally an ELECTION game with unequal district values. A popular vote would be a game where each voter (or region) has equal weight. The Electoral College violates this:

  • Winner-Take-All: In most US states, winning by a single vote gives you all that state's electors. This creates huge strategic incentive to focus on competitive states (districts with unclear advantage).
  • Unequal Weights: California has 54 electors, Wyoming has 3. This value asymmetry means campaigns can't simply maximize votes—they must maximize electoral votes.
  • Undervalues Margin of Victory: Winning California by 10 million votes or 100,000 votes yields the same electoral payoff. This encourages campaigns to "spread thin" across many competitive states rather than dominate safe ones.

Result: Campaigns focus on swing states, ignore safe states (both red and blue), and oddly, lose votes in safe territories where their support is strongest (since winning by 5 million in New York is "wasted").

4. Why do real campaigns focus on "swing states" rather than safe states? ▼

Swing states are competitive—neither candidate has a dominant resource advantage there. This makes them the highest-value targets in game-theoretic terms.

  • Marginal Payoff: An extra day in a swing state can flip its outcome (and its electoral votes). An extra day in a safe state adds zero marginal value (you're winning anyway).
  • Strategic Uncertainty: Both campaigns optimize toward swing states because that's where the marginal vote matters. Once both candidates do this, swings states become even more fiercely contested.
  • IESD Logic: Allocating significant resources to a safe state is dominated: you could shift those resources to a swing state and improve your payoff against every opponent strategy.

The result is a feedback loop: swing states get all the attention; safe states are ignored. This can distort which issues get campaign focus and which voters feel their vote "matters."

5. How does the Election game connect to Colonel Blotto and military strategy? ▼

The Election game is a direct application of Colonel Blotto, a classical military game (Chapter 21). The mapping is straightforward:

  • Battlefields ↔ Electoral Districts
  • Troops ↔ Campaign Days
  • Winning a Battlefield ↔ Winning a District (majority of troops/resources wins)
  • Strategic Prize ↔ Electoral Votes (aggregate victory condition)

The game's insights transfer directly: in Blotto, a colonel must decide whether to concentrate forces (attack key battlefields) or disperse (defend a broad front). The optimal strategy is often mixed and depends on resource ratios, exactly like Election.

Military historians recognize this: campaigns in war, like campaigns in politics, hinge on resource allocation under uncertainty. Game theory formalizes the intuition and sometimes reveals counterintuitive optimal strategies.
