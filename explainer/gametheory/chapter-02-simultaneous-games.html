Theory 2: Simultaneous Games

The Payoff Matrix, Solution Concepts, and Nash Equilibrium

Based on Chapter 2 of Erich Prisner's Game Theory Through Examples

🌉 The Feynman Bridge — The Game Board That Governs Everything

Imagine two competing food trucks parked on the same street. Each morning, they must SIMULTANEOUSLY decide their price for the day — neither knows what the other will charge. If Truck A charges $5 and Truck B charges $3, most customers flock to B. If they both charge $3, they split the crowd but barely cover costs. This dilemma — choosing without seeing your opponent's choice — is the essence of a simultaneous game.

Think of the payoff matrix as a chessboard — except instead of pieces, every square contains two envelopes. One envelope has Player A's payoff, the other has Player B's. Player A picks a ROW, Player B picks a COLUMN, and the square where they intersect is opened. That's the outcome. Neither player can see the other's move before the envelopes are opened, so both must choose blindly.

But how should they choose? Game theory gives us four powerful answers:

Maximin Strategy
The Paranoid's Strategy — Always assume the worst. If I pick this row, what's the LEAST I could get? Now pick the row where that least is highest.
Dominated Moves
The No-Brainer Filter — If option A gives you more money than option B NO MATTER WHAT your opponent does, why would you ever pick B? Throw it away.
Best Response
The Spy's Advantage — If you could peek at your opponent's sealed envelope before they submit it, what would you choose? That's your best response.
Nash Equilibrium
The Point of No Regret — You're both at a Nash equilibrium when, after seeing each other's choices, NEITHER of you wishes you had done something different.

⚠️ The Dark Truth: The most disturbing thing about simultaneous games? Sometimes the 'rational' choice makes EVERYONE worse off. Two perfectly logical people can rationally walk themselves into a terrible outcome — and both KNOW it's terrible, yet neither can escape. That's the Prisoner's Dilemma, and it will haunt you.

📐 The Mathematical Ascent — The Algebra of Strategy

2a. Normal Form Definition

A simultaneous game in normal form is formally defined as:

Normal Form Game (Bimatrix Form)

$$\Gamma = \langle N, (S_i)_{i \in N}, (u_i)_{i \in N} \rangle$$

where:

  • $N = \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ is the set of players
  • $S_i$ is the strategy set (available moves) for player $i$
  • $u_i: S_1 \times S_2 \times \cdots \times S_n \to \mathbb{R}$ is player $i$'s payoff function

For a two-player game with $|S_1| = m$ rows and $|S_2| = n$ columns, we represent it using two $m \times n$ matrices:

Bimatrix Representation

$$A = [a_{ij}] \quad \text{(Player 1's payoffs)}, \quad B = [b_{ij}] \quad \text{(Player 2's payoffs)}$$

Each cell $(i,j)$ shows the outcome pair $(a_{ij}, b_{ij})$:
Player 1 chooses row $i$, Player 2 chooses column $j$, and they receive payoffs $(a_{ij}, b_{ij})$ respectively.

2b. Solution Concepts — The Four Pillars

Maximin Value (Security Level) — Section 2.2.1

$$\overline{v}_i = \max_{s_i \in S_i} \min_{s_{-i} \in S_{-i}} u_i(s_i, s_{-i})$$

Interpretation: Player $i$ assumes the worst-case response from opponents. For each of their own strategies, they find the minimum payoff they could receive (opponent's best counter-move). Then they choose the strategy that maximizes this minimum — the "least bad" guaranteed payoff.

Philosophy: Pessimistic, risk-averse, conservative. "I assume my opponent is playing optimally against me."

Strict Domination — Section 2.2.2

Strategy $s_i$ strictly dominates strategy $s_i'$ if:

$$u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) > u_i(s_i', s_{-i}) \quad \forall s_{-i} \in S_{-i}$$

Meaning: $s_i$ yields a strictly higher payoff than $s_i'$ regardless of what opponents do. A dominated strategy is never optimal — it can be eliminated.

Iterated Elimination of Strictly Dominated Strategies (IESD):

Repeatedly remove dominated strategies for all players until none remain. The surviving strategies are those that are never "obviously stupid."

Weak Domination (Optional)

Strategy $s_i$ weakly dominates strategy $s_i'$ if:

$$u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) \geq u_i(s_i', s_{-i}) \quad \forall s_{-i}, \quad \text{with strict inequality for at least one } s_{-i}$$

Note: Weak domination is used less frequently because weakly dominated strategies might be optimal in some cases.

Best Response — Section 2.2.3

The best response of player $i$ to opponent's strategy $s_{-i}$ is:

$$BR_i(s_{-i}) = \arg\max_{s_i \in S_i} u_i(s_i, s_{-i})$$

Meaning: If player $i$ knew what opponents would do, this is the strategy they would choose. The set of all best responses is found by underlining the highest payoff in each row (for Player 1) and each column (for Player 2).

Pure Nash Equilibrium — Section 2.2.4

A strategy profile $s^* = (s_1^*, s_2^*, \ldots, s_n^*)$ is a Nash Equilibrium if:

$$\forall i \in N, \quad \forall s_i \in S_i: \quad u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^*)$$

Interpretation: No player can improve their payoff by unilaterally deviating from $s^*$. Every player's strategy is a best response to the others' strategies. No one has an incentive to cheat.

Finding NE in a matrix: Underline best responses for both players. A cell is a Nash Equilibrium if ALL payoffs are underlined.

Pareto Optimality

Outcome $s$ Pareto-dominates outcome $s'$ if:

$$u_i(s) \geq u_i(s') \quad \forall i, \quad \text{with strict inequality for at least one } i$$

An outcome is Pareto-optimal if no other outcome Pareto-dominates it.

Critical insight: A Nash Equilibrium need not be Pareto-optimal. Sometimes all players would prefer a different outcome, but the equilibrium structure prevents them from achieving it without risking exploitation.

2c. Worked Examples

Example 1: The ADVERTISING Dilemma

Two companies simultaneously decide whether to advertise. Advertising costs resources but can capture market share. The payoff matrix:

$$\begin{array}{c|cc} & \text{Advertise} & \text{Don't Advertise} \\ \hline \text{Advertise} & (3, 3) & (6, 2) \\ \text{Don't Advertise} & (2, 6) & (5, 5) \end{array}$$

Analysis:

  • Best responses:
    • Company 1 to "Advertise": Compare 3 vs 6 → Advertise is best
    • Company 1 to "Don't Advertise": Compare 2 vs 5 → Don't Advertise is best
    • Company 2: By symmetry, Advertise is always best
  • Nash Equilibrium: (Advertise, Advertise) with payoffs $(3, 3)$
  • The Paradox: Both firms would earn $(5, 5)$ if neither advertised, but this is NOT a Nash Equilibrium. If B plays "Don't Advertise," A's best response is "Advertise" (earning 6 instead of 5). So the equilibrium $(3,3)$ is Pareto-dominated by $(5,5)$.
  • Lesson: Nash Equilibrium predicts stable outcomes, not best outcomes.

Example 2: The PRISONER'S DILEMMA

Two suspects are arrested. Each can either Stay Silent (S) or Confess (C). If both stay silent, both serve 1 year (payoff 2). If one confesses, they go free (payoff 3) and the silent one serves 3 years (payoff 0). If both confess, both serve 2 years (payoff 1).

$$\begin{array}{c|cc} & \text{Silent} & \text{Confess} \\ \hline \text{Silent} & (2, 2) & (0, 3) \\ \text{Confess} & (3, 0) & (1, 1) \end{array}$$

Analysis:

  • Dominated strategy: Silent is strictly dominated by Confess.
    (Compare: Confess gives 3 vs 0, and 1 vs 2, depending on opponent)
  • Nash Equilibrium: (Confess, Confess) with payoffs $(1, 1)$
  • The Dilemma: Both would prefer $(2, 2)$ from staying silent, but each has an incentive to break the agreement. If the other stays silent, confessing yields 3 instead of 2. This makes Confess optimal regardless of opponent's move.
  • Why it's famous: It shows that individual rationality (playing best response) can lead to collective irrationality (everyone worse off at equilibrium). This structure appears in arms races, environmental protection, negotiations, and countless real-world conflicts.

Example 3: ROCK-PAPER-SCISSORS (Zero-Sum Game)

This is a zero-sum game: one player's gain is the other's loss. The payoff matrix (from Row player's perspective):

$$\begin{array}{c|ccc} & \text{Rock} & \text{Scissors} & \text{Paper} \\ \hline \text{Rock} & (0, 0) & (1, -1) & (-1, 1) \\ \text{Scissors} & (-1, 1) & (0, 0) & (1, -1) \\ \text{Paper} & (1, -1) & (-1, 1) & (0, 0) \end{array}$$

Analysis:

  • No dominated strategies: Each move beats one, loses to one, ties with one. No move strictly dominates.
  • No pure Nash Equilibrium: In any pure strategy cell, at least one player wants to deviate (e.g., Rock vs Rock is $(0,0)$, but Column wants to play Paper to win).
  • Maximin value: Every pure strategy has security level $-1$ (you lose if opponent plays the counter). So $\overline{v} = -1$.
  • Why it matters: This shows that not all games have pure Nash Equilibria. To find equilibrium here, you need mixed strategies — randomizing your moves (Chapter 27).

Example 4: The TWO BARS Pricing Game (IESD Example)

Two bars simultaneously choose a price: $2, $4, or $5. The payoff matrix shows weekly revenue in thousands:

$$\begin{array}{c|ccc} & \text{\$2} & \text{\$4} & \text{\$5} \\ \hline \text{\$2} & (10, 10) & (14, 12) & (15, 14) \\ \text{\$4} & (12, 14) & (20, 20) & (28, 15) \\ \text{\$5} & (14, 15) & (15, 28) & (25, 25) \end{array}$$

IESD Analysis (Step-by-Step):

  1. Step 1: Does $4 dominate $2 for Player 1?
    Row $4: (12, 14, 28)$ vs Row $2: (10, 14, 15)$
    Compare: $12 > 10$ ✓, $14 = 14$ ✗, $28 > 15$ ✓
    No, $4 does NOT strictly dominate $2 (tied on column 2).
    But $5 dominates $2: $(14, 15, 25)$ vs $(10, 14, 15)$ → $14 > 10$, $15 > 14$, $25 > 15$ ✓
    Eliminate $2.
  2. Step 2: In the reduced $2 \times 3$ game (drop $2), does $4 dominate $5 for Player 1?
    Row $4: (14, 28) vs Row $5: (15, 25)$
    Compare: $14 < 15$ ✗, $28 > 25$ ✓
    No. Try the other way: $4 vs $5...
    Actually, let's check column player. In reduced game (columns $4, $5):
    Column $4: (14, 20, 15)$ vs Column $5: (12, 28, 25)$
    $14 > 12$ ✓, $20 < 28$ ✗ → Not dominated.
  3. Step 3 (revised): Looking more carefully at dominated rows in original:
    For Player 1: Does any row dominate another?
    $4 vs $2: $(12, 20, 28)$ vs $(10, 14, 15)$ → $12 > 10$ ✓, $20 > 14$ ✓, $28 > 15$ ✓
    YES! $4 strictly dominates $2. Eliminate row $2.
  4. Step 4: In reduced game with rows {$4, $5}, look at columns:
    Column $4: (20, 15)$ vs Column $5: (28, 25)$
    $20 < 28$, $15 < 25$ → Column $5 dominates Column $4.
    Eliminate column $4.
  5. Result after IESD: Both players charge $5, earning $(25, 25)$ each. (Though charging $4 seems better individually, the IESD logic filters down to $5 as the only undominated strategy.)

Lesson: IESD helps us predict outcomes by eliminating obviously bad strategies, step by step. But it requires both players to be rational and to know the game structure.

Example 5: BATTLE OF THE SEXES (Multiple NE)

A couple wants to spend the evening together but disagree on the activity. He prefers soccer, she prefers ballet. If they go to their preferred activity together, great! (High payoff). If they go together to the other's choice, less good. If they go separate, terrible.

$$\begin{array}{c|cc} & \text{Soccer} & \text{Ballet} \\ \hline \text{Soccer} & (3, 2) & (1, 0) \\ \text{Ballet} & (0, 1) & (2, 3) \end{array}$$

Analysis:

  • Best responses (underline):
    • Row to Soccer: $3 > 0$ → Soccer is best
    • Row to Ballet: $2 > 1$ → Ballet is best
    • Column to Soccer: $2 > 1$ → Soccer is best
    • Column to Ballet: $3 > 0$ → Ballet is best
  • Nash Equilibria: (Soccer, Soccer) with $(3, 2)$ and (Ballet, Ballet) with $(2, 3)$
  • Coordination problem: Both equilibria are Pareto-optimal (better than the off-diagonal outcomes), but they don't know which one to coordinate on. Both players prefer to be at an equilibrium, but not necessarily the same one.
  • Why interesting: Multiple pure NE → need to understand how players coordinate. In real life, conventions, communication, or repeated play help resolve these.

2d. Additional Topics

Best Response Digraph (Section 2.3.1)

A bipartite directed graph showing players' strategies as circles, with arrows indicating best responses. Player 1's strategies are on the left, Player 2's on the right. An arrow from strategy $s$ to strategy $t$ means "$t$ is a best response to $s$."

Finding Nash Equilibria in digraphs: An equilibrium is a pair of strategies where arrows point to each other (mutual best response).

Zero-Sum Symmetric Games Theorem (Section 2.3.2)

For a 2-player zero-sum symmetric game:

  1. Every pure Nash Equilibrium has payoff $(0, 0)$
  2. Security level equals $0$ if and only if a pure Nash Equilibrium exists
  3. If no pure NE, both players have security level $< 0$, and they must play mixed strategies

Example: Rock-Paper-Scissors has no pure NE and security level $-1$. Chess, on the other hand, is believed to have a pure NE (though we don't know what it is).

🐍 Reference Implementation — Python

Below is a complete, well-documented Python implementation of the SimultaneousGame class and all solution concepts from this chapter. Use it as a reference or run it locally. 

"""
Game Theory Through Examples — Chapter 2
The Simultaneous Game Analyzer

Implements all four solution concepts for 2-player normal form games:
1. Maximin strategies and security levels (2.2.1)
2. Strict domination + IESD (2.2.2)
3. Best response computation (2.2.3)
4. Pure Nash equilibrium finder (2.2.4)
"""

import numpy as np
from itertools import product

class SimultaneousGame:
    """A 2-player simultaneous game in normal (bimatrix) form."""

    def __init__(self, name, row_strategies, col_strategies, A, B):
        self.name = name
        self.row_strats = row_strategies
        self.col_strats = col_strategies
        self.A = np.array(A, dtype=float)
        self.B = np.array(B, dtype=float)
        self.m, self.n = self.A.shape

    @property
    def is_zero_sum(self):
        return np.allclose(self.A + self.B, 0)

    @property
    def is_symmetric(self):
        if self.m != self.n:
            return False
        return np.allclose(self.B, self.A.T)

    # ─── 2.2.1 MAXIMIN ───
    def maximin(self):
        """Maximin strategy: guarantee the best of worst-case payoffs."""
        row_mins = np.min(self.A, axis=1)
        p1_idx = np.argmax(row_mins)
        p1_security = row_mins[p1_idx]

        col_mins = np.min(self.B, axis=0)
        p2_idx = np.argmax(col_mins)
        p2_security = col_mins[p2_idx]

        return (self.row_strats[p1_idx], p1_security,
                self.col_strats[p2_idx], p2_security)

    # ─── 2.2.2 DOMINATION ───
    def find_dominated(self):
        """Find strictly dominated strategies."""
        dominated = {"Player 1": [], "Player 2": []}

        for i in range(self.m):
            for j in range(self.m):
                if i != j and np.all(self.A[j] > self.A[i]):
                    dominated["Player 1"].append(
                        f"'{self.row_strats[i]}' dominated by '{self.row_strats[j]}'")

        for i in range(self.n):
            for j in range(self.n):
                if i != j and np.all(self.B[:, j] > self.B[:, i]):
                    dominated["Player 2"].append(
                        f"'{self.col_strats[i]}' dominated by '{self.col_strats[j]}'")

        return dominated

    def iesd(self):
        """Iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies."""
        A, B = self.A.copy(), self.B.copy()
        rows = list(range(self.m))
        cols = list(range(self.n))

        changed = True
        while changed:
            changed = False
            to_remove_rows = []
            for i in rows:
                for j in rows:
                    if i != j and i not in to_remove_rows:
                        i_idx = rows.index(i)
                        j_idx = rows.index(j)
                        if np.all(A[j_idx] > A[i_idx]):
                            to_remove_rows.append(i)
                            changed = True
            for r in to_remove_rows:
                idx = rows.index(r)
                A = np.delete(A, idx, axis=0)
                B = np.delete(B, idx, axis=0)
                rows.remove(r)

            to_remove_cols = []
            for i in cols:
                for j in cols:
                    if i != j and i not in to_remove_cols:
                        i_idx = cols.index(i)
                        j_idx = cols.index(j)
                        if np.all(B[:, j_idx] > B[:, i_idx]):
                            to_remove_cols.append(i)
                            changed = True
            for c in to_remove_cols:
                idx = cols.index(c)
                A = np.delete(A, idx, axis=1)
                B = np.delete(B, idx, axis=1)
                cols.remove(c)

        return [self.row_strats[i] for i in rows], \
               [self.col_strats[i] for i in cols]

    # ─── 2.2.3 BEST RESPONSE ───
    def best_responses(self):
        """Find best responses for both players."""
        br = {"Player 1": {}, "Player 2": {}}

        for j in range(self.n):
            max_val = np.max(self.A[:, j])
            best = [self.row_strats[i] for i in range(self.m)
                    if self.A[i, j] == max_val]
            br["Player 1"][self.col_strats[j]] = best

        for i in range(self.m):
            max_val = np.max(self.B[i, :])
            best = [self.col_strats[j] for j in range(self.n)
                    if self.B[i, j] == max_val]
            br["Player 2"][self.row_strats[i]] = best

        return br

    # ─── 2.2.4 NASH EQUILIBRIA ───
    def nash_equilibria(self):
        """Find all pure strategy Nash equilibria."""
        equilibria = []

        for i in range(self.m):
            for j in range(self.n):
                if (self.A[i, j] >= np.max(self.A[:, j]) and
                    self.B[i, j] >= np.max(self.B[i, :])):
                    equilibria.append({
                        "strategies": (self.row_strats[i], self.col_strats[j]),
                        "payoffs": (self.A[i, j], self.B[i, j])
                    })

        return equilibria

# EXAMPLE GAMES
advertising = SimultaneousGame(
    "ADVERTISING",
    ["Advertise", "Don't Advertise"],
    ["Advertise", "Don't Advertise"],
    A=[[3, 6], [2, 5]],
    B=[[3, 2], [6, 5]]
)

prisoners = SimultaneousGame(
    "PRISONER'S DILEMMA",
    ["Silent", "Confess"],
    ["Silent", "Confess"],
    A=[[2, 0], [3, 1]],
    B=[[2, 3], [0, 1]]
)

battle = SimultaneousGame(
    "BATTLE OF THE SEXES",
    ["Soccer", "Ballet"],
    ["Soccer", "Ballet"],
    A=[[3, 1], [0, 2]],
    B=[[2, 0], [1, 3]]
)

if __name__ == "__main__":
    for game in [advertising, prisoners, battle]:
        print(f"\n{'='*50}")
        print(f"  {game.name}")
        print(f"{'='*50}")

        # Maximin
        p1s, p1v, p2s, p2v = game.maximin()
        print(f"Maximin: P1 plays '{p1s}' (security {p1v}), "
              f"P2 plays '{p2s}' (security {p2v})")

        # Nash
        ne = game.nash_equilibria()
        print(f"Nash Equilibria: {[e['strategies'] for e in ne]}")

🏛️ The Socratic Sandbox — Can You Find the Equilibrium?

Five challenge questions to deepen your understanding. Click to expand and think before reading the answer.

1
Matrix Reading: Why don't the companies just agree not to advertise?
▼

In the ADVERTISING game, Company A's payoff when A advertises and B doesn't is 6 (million). But both companies end up advertising, earning only 3 each. So why don't they just agree not to advertise and each earn 5?

Answer: Because the agreement isn't enforceable. Even if both companies promise not to advertise, each has an incentive to break the agreement. If B sticks to "Don't Advertise," then A's best response is to advertise (earning 6 instead of 5). So B can't trust A to keep the promise, and vice versa.

This is exactly the Prisoner's Dilemma structure — non-cooperative games don't allow binding contracts. The Nash Equilibrium at (Advertise, Advertise) is self-enforcing even though (Don't, Don't) is Pareto-superior. No one wants to cheat from (Advertise, Advertise), but both would want to cheat from (Don't, Don't).

The lesson: Nash Equilibrium predicts stable outcomes — ones where no player wants to deviate — not necessarily the best outcomes.

2
Best Response: Find all NE in this custom game
▼

Consider this 2×2 game:

$$\begin{array}{c|cc} & L & R \\ \hline U & (3, 1) & (0, 2) \\ D & (1, 3) & (2, 0) \end{array}$$

Find all best responses and all Nash equilibria.

Answer:

Player 1's best responses:
• To opponent playing L: Compare 3 vs 1 → U is best
• To opponent playing R: Compare 0 vs 2 → D is best

Player 2's best responses:
• To opponent playing U: Compare 1 vs 2 → R is best
• To opponent playing D: Compare 3 vs 0 → L is best

Checking for Nash Equilibria:
We need cells where BOTH payoffs are best responses:
• (U, L): U is Player 1's BR to L ✓, but L is not Player 2's BR to U ✗
• (U, R): U is not Player 1's BR to R ✗
• (D, L): D is not Player 1's BR to L ✗
• (D, R): D is Player 1's BR to R ✓, but R is Player 2's BR to U, not D ✗

Conclusion: NO PURE NASH EQUILIBRIUM

This game has no stable outcome in pure strategies. Both players want to "escape" from any outcome once it's revealed. This motivates mixed strategies (Chapter 27) where players randomize to keep opponents indifferent.

3
IESD Walkthrough: Why do the bars end up charging $4?
▼

In the TWO BARS game with prices {$2, $4, $5}, both bars eventually charge $4 via IESD. But they'd both earn more at $5. Explain why.

Answer (Step-by-Step IESD):

Step 1: Eliminate dominated strategies for Player 1
Compare rows:
• $4: (12, 20, 28) vs $2: (10, 14, 15)
• $12 > $10, $20 > $14, $28 > $15 ✓
→ $4 strictly dominates $2 for Player 1. Remove row $2.

Step 2: In the reduced game {$4, $5}, eliminate dominated columns for Player 2
Compare columns (Player 2's payoff matrix, reduced rows):
• $5: (15, 28) vs $4: (14, 20)
• $15 > $14 and $28 > $20 ✓
→ $5 strictly dominates $4 for Player 2. Remove column $4.

Wait, that means we're left with rows {$4, $5} and column {$5}. But we also need to check Player 1 again in the reduced game...

Step 3: Back to Player 1 in reduced game
In game with only column $5:
• $4: 28 vs $5: 25
• $28 > $25 → $4 is still best

Result: After IESD, the surviving strategies are both charging $4, with payoffs $(20, 20)$.

Why it feels counterintuitive: If both charged $5, they'd each earn $25K. But the problem is incentive structure: each bar's incentive to undercut the other is too strong. At $5, one bar can capture the market by dropping to $4 (earning $28K). At $4, dropping to $2 doesn't help (goes down to $10K). So $4 is the "bottom" that survives IESD.

Lesson: IESD solves by progressive elimination. What survives is not necessarily good, just not stupid.

4
Security Level: What's the maximin value in Rock-Paper-Scissors?
▼

If you always play Rock in Rock-Paper-Scissors, why will you lose? What does this tell you about the security level of each pure strategy?

Answer:

If you always play Rock, your opponent learns this and always plays Paper, giving you a payoff of -1 (loss) every round.

Security level of each pure strategy:
• Rock: min(-1, 0, 1) = -1 (lose to Paper)
• Scissors: min(-1, 0, 1) = -1 (lose to Rock)
• Paper: min(-1, 0, 1) = -1 (lose to Scissors)

Maximin value: $\max(-1, -1, -1) = \mathbf{-1}$

Critical insight: NO pure strategy can guarantee you better than $-1$ in RPS. Every pure strategy has a counter that beats it. This is why pure Nash Equilibrium does not exist in RPS — you must randomize (mixed strategy) to avoid being exploited.

Why it matters: This explains why children's Rock-Paper-Scissors often shows pattern-play rather than true randomization — perfect randomization is hard!

5
Deep Philosophy: Is Nash Equilibrium a flawed concept?
▼

The Prisoner's Dilemma shows that Nash Equilibrium can be Pareto-dominated — players end up worse off than they could be. Does this mean Nash Equilibrium is a flawed concept, or is it capturing something real about the world?

Answer: Nash Equilibrium is not flawed; it's answering a different question.

What NE is NOT saying: "This is the best outcome" or "This is what rational players should aim for."

What NE IS saying: "This is the stable outcome — one where no player has an incentive to unilaterally deviate. Once both are here, no one wants to leave."

Why Pareto-dominated NE occur:
A Pareto-superior outcome (like $(2, 2)$ in PD instead of $(1, 1)$) is unstable. If both players are at $(2, 2)$, at least one (actually both) can improve by deviating. So the "better" outcome doesn't survive rational individual choice.

Real-world evidence:
• Arms races: Nations spend trillions on weapons, all worse off, but each fearing the other
• Overfishing: Fish stocks collapse because each fishery has incentive to catch more
• Climate change: Countries pollute, knowing it harms everyone including themselves
• Doping in sports: Athletes take risky drugs to compete, all gaining no advantage

The power of changing the GAME:
The "flaw" isn't in Nash Equilibrium but in the game structure. If we can change the game (binding contracts, repeated play, punishment mechanisms, external enforcement), we can change the equilibrium.

• Repeated games: If the PD is played infinitely (or indefinitely), cooperation can emerge (Chapter 23)
• Contracts: If both players can sign enforceable agreements, they can escape $(1, 1)$
• External punishment: If authorities fine defectors, incentives change

Conclusion: Nash Equilibrium is a powerful prediction tool for one-shot non-cooperative games. Its predictive failures (like the PD paradox) are real features of the world — not bugs in the theory, but insights into why mechanism design (Chapter 15) and institutional design matter so much.
