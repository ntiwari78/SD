Chapter 24: Natural Language Processing

How machines understand and generate human language through probability, grammar, and meaning

Section 1: Feynman Mental Model

Core Insight: Think about how you finish someone's sentence. If they say "I'm going to the...", you probably predict "store" or "park," not "quantum." Your brain is a language model — it assigns probabilities to what word comes next based on context. A formal language model does exactly this mathematically. Grammar, meanwhile, is the structural blueprint — like the frame of a building. Sentences must follow rules (subject-verb-object), just like buildings need structural integrity.

Language as Probability

Natural language processing (NLP) is fundamentally about managing uncertainty. Every word you read has multiple possible predecessors, and every word you might say has multiple possible successors. A language model learns to assign probabilities to these sequences.

Imagine you're transcribing speech. You hear: "I saw the bank of the..." What comes next? Probably "river" or "road," not "transaction." The more context you have, the better your prediction. This is why your phone's autocomplete works — it's tracking patterns in how words typically follow other words.

Grammar as Structure

Grammar provides the scaffolding. While a language model might predict that "bank" can follow "the," grammar tells you how "bank" relates to other words in a sentence. Is it a noun (financial institution) or a noun (river edge)? Can it be the subject? The object?

A context-free grammar (CFG) is like the blueprint for valid sentences. Just as a building blueprint specifies where walls must go, a grammar specifies where noun phrases must go, where verb phrases must go, and so on.

Key Concepts

N-Gram Language Models

Predicting the next word from the last N words. A unigram model assumes every word is independent. A bigram looks at one previous word. A trigram looks at two. The tradeoff: more context helps, but we see fewer examples of longer sequences, so we need more training data.

Smoothing Techniques

The Problem: A word like "cryptocurrency" appears once in your training data. What's the probability of the next word being "regulation"? If we never saw this exact bigram, do we say it's impossible (probability 0)? That breaks perplexity calculations.

Laplace Smoothing (Add-One): Pretend every possible bigram appeared one extra time. This gives unseen bigrams a tiny but non-zero probability. Simple, but it can over-smooth — assigning too much probability mass to rare events.

Good-Turing Smoothing: Adjust the probability of events you saw k times based on how many events you saw k+1 times. If you saw 50 bigrams once and 40 bigrams twice, use that ratio to smooth.

Parsing and Dependency

Once you have grammar rules, you need to find which parse tree fits a sentence. The CKY algorithm builds valid parse trees bottom-up in cubic time. It finds all possible structures that satisfy the grammar.

Dependency parsing is simpler: for each word, find which other word it depends on. "The cat sat" → "cat" depends on "sat," "the" depends on "cat." This captures who does what to whom.

Part-of-Speech Tagging

Label each word as noun, verb, adjective, etc. Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or neural networks learn the probability that a word is a noun given the words around it. The word "bank" is a noun in "river bank" but could be a verb in some contexts.

Word Embeddings

Modern NLP represents words as vectors in high-dimensional space. Words with similar meanings are close together. "King" and "queen" are close. "Good" and "bad" are far apart. The famous example: king − man + woman ≈ queen. Vector arithmetic captures analogy.

Information Extraction and Named Entity Recognition

Extract structured data from unstructured text. Named Entity Recognition identifies people, places, organizations. Information extraction builds relations: "Apple Inc. was founded by Steve Jobs in Cupertino." Extract: (founder: Steve Jobs, company: Apple, location: Cupertino).

Machine Translation

Translate from one language to another. Simple approach: word-by-word substitution (fails). Better approach: translate phrases, align syntactic structures. Modern: neural machine translation with attention mechanisms learns end-to-end.

Evaluation Metrics

Perplexity: How surprised is your model by held-out test data? A lower perplexity is better. It measures: "On average, how many equally-likely words could come next?" Mathematically, it's the exponentiated negative cross-entropy.

BLEU Score: For translation, compare n-gram overlap between machine translation and human reference. If the human said "the quick brown fox" and the machine said "the fast brown dog," there's 50% bigram overlap. BLEU is a geometric mean of 1-gram, 2-gram, 3-gram, 4-gram precisions.

Section 2: Mathematical Ascent

Probability Chain Rule

The foundation: decompose the joint probability of a sequence into conditional probabilities.

$$P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})$$

This is exact but intractable — we'd need to count all possible histories. So we use the Markov assumption: the next word depends only on the previous N−1 words, not the entire history.

Bigram Model

$$P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}, w_i)}{C(w_{i-1})}$$

Where C(w_{i-1}, w_i) is the count of bigram occurrences and C(w_{i-1}) is the count of times w_{i-1} appears. Maximum likelihood estimation from a corpus.

Laplace Smoothing

$$P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}, w_i) + 1}{C(w_{i-1}) + V}$$

Where V is the vocabulary size. We add 1 to the numerator and V to the denominator. This reserves probability mass for unseen bigrams.

Perplexity (Information-Theoretic Measure)

$$\text{PP}(W) = P(w_1, \ldots, w_N)^{-1/N} = 2^{H(W)}$$

Where H(W) is the cross-entropy. Equivalently:

$$\text{PP}(W) = 2^{-\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \log_2 P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})}$$

Interpretation: If your model has perplexity 100, it's as surprised by a test sentence as if it had to guess among 100 equally-likely words at each position.

Context-Free Grammar (CFG)

A set of production rules. For example:

$$S \to NP \; VP$$ $$NP \to \text{Det} \; N \mid \text{Det} \; Adj \; N$$ $$VP \to V \; NP$$ $$\text{Det} \to \text{the} \mid \text{a}$$ $$N \to \text{cat} \mid \text{dog}$$ $$V \to \text{chased}$$

A sentence is valid if we can derive it by repeatedly applying these rules, starting from S (sentence).

CKY Parsing Complexity

$$\mathcal{O}(n^3 |G|)$$

Where n is sentence length and |G| is grammar size. We fill an n \times n table with O(n) work per cell and O(|G|) time to check rules.

Word Vectors and Cosine Similarity

$$\cos(\vec{w_1}, \vec{w_2}) = \frac{\vec{w_1} \cdot \vec{w_2}}{||\vec{w_1}|| \; ||\vec{w_2}||} \in [-1, 1]$$

Measures angle between word vectors. Closer to 1 means similar words. This is how Word2Vec embeddings capture semantic relationships.

BLEU Score (Simplified)

$$\text{BLEU} = BP \cdot \exp\left( \sum_{n=1}^{N} w_n \log p_n \right)$$

Where p_n is the precision of n-grams (how many of the machine's n-grams appear in the reference), w_n are weights (typically uniform), and BP is a brevity penalty (penalizing too-short translations).

Section 3: Clean-Code Implementation

Here's a working implementation of an N-gram language model and CKY parser in Python:

from collections import defaultdict, Counter
import math
import random

class NGramModel:
    """
    N-gram Language Model.
    The agent learns to predict the next word based on the previous N-1 words.
    This is the simplest form of 'understanding' language — pattern matching.
    """
    def __init__(self, n=2):
        self.n = n
        self.counts = defaultdict(Counter)  # counts[context][word] = frequency
        self.context_counts = Counter()      # context_counts[context] = total
        self.vocab = set()

    def train(self, corpus):
        """Learn n-gram statistics from a corpus of sentences."""
        for sentence in corpus:
            # Pad with start tokens
            tokens = ['<s>'] * (self.n - 1) + sentence.split() + ['</s>']
            self.vocab.update(tokens)

            # Collect all n-grams
            for i in range(self.n - 1, len(tokens)):
                context = tuple(tokens[i-self.n+1:i])
                word = tokens[i]
                self.counts[context][word] += 1
                self.context_counts[context] += 1

    def probability(self, word, context):
        """P(word | context) with Laplace smoothing."""
        context = tuple(context[-(self.n-1):])  # Ensure correct context length
        count = self.counts[context][word]
        total = self.context_counts[context]
        V = len(self.vocab)
        # Laplace: (count + 1) / (total + V)
        return (count + 1) / (total + V) if total > 0 else 1.0 / V

    def log_probability(self, sentence):
        """Log probability of entire sentence."""
        tokens = ['<s>'] * (self.n - 1) + sentence.split() + ['</s>']
        log_prob = 0.0
        for i in range(self.n - 1, len(tokens)):
            context = tuple(tokens[i-self.n+1:i])
            word = tokens[i]
            prob = self.probability(word, context)
            log_prob += math.log2(prob)
        return log_prob

    def perplexity(self, test_sentences):
        """Lower perplexity = better model. Measures surprise."""
        log_prob = 0.0
        N = 0
        for sentence in test_sentences:
            tokens = ['<s>'] * (self.n - 1) + sentence.split() + ['</s>']
            for i in range(self.n - 1, len(tokens)):
                context = tuple(tokens[i-self.n+1:i])
                word = tokens[i]
                log_prob += math.log2(self.probability(word, context))
                N += 1

        if N == 0:
            return float('inf')
        return 2 ** (-log_prob / N)

    def generate(self, max_length=20):
        """Generate text by sampling from the model."""
        context = list(['<s>'] * (self.n - 1))
        result = []

        for _ in range(max_length):
            ctx = tuple(context[-(self.n-1):])
            words = list(self.counts[ctx].keys())

            if not words:  # No continuation possible
                break

            # Calculate probabilities
            probs = [self.counts[ctx][w] for w in words]
            total = sum(probs)
            probs = [p / total for p in probs]

            # Sample next word
            word = random.choices(words, weights=probs, k=1)[0]

            if word == '</s>':
                break

            result.append(word)
            context.append(word)

        return ' '.join(result)


class CKYParser:
    """
    CKY (Cocke-Kasami-Younger) parser for Context-Free Grammars in Chomsky Normal Form.
    Bottom-up parsing in O(n³) — finds all valid parse trees.
    """
    def __init__(self, grammar):
        """
        grammar: list of (LHS, RHS) tuples
        RHS can be either (word,) for terminal rules or (B, C) for non-terminal rules.
        """
        self.grammar = grammar
        self._build_rule_index()

    def _build_rule_index(self):
        """Index rules by their RHS for faster lookup."""
        self.terminal_rules = defaultdict(set)  # word -> set of LHS
        self.non_terminal_rules = defaultdict(set)  # (B, C) -> set of LHS

        for lhs, rhs in self.grammar:
            if len(rhs) == 1 and isinstance(rhs[0], str) and rhs[0][0].islower():
                self.terminal_rules[rhs[0]].add(lhs)
            elif len(rhs) == 2:
                self.non_terminal_rules[rhs].add(lhs)

    def parse(self, sentence):
        """Check if sentence is grammatically valid."""
        words = sentence.split()
        n = len(words)

        # table[i][j] = set of non-terminals that can derive words[i:j+1]
        table = [[set() for _ in range(n)] for _ in range(n)]

        # Fill diagonal: terminal rules
        for i, word in enumerate(words):
            for lhs in self.terminal_rules[word]:
                table[i][i].add(lhs)

        # Fill upper triangle
        for length in range(2, n + 1):  # subsequence length
            for i in range(n - length + 1):  # start position
                j = i + length - 1  # end position

                # Try all split points
                for k in range(i, j):
                    # Look for rules (B, C) where B derives [i:k] and C derives [k+1:j]
                    left_set = table[i][k]
                    right_set = table[k+1][j]

                    for B in left_set:
                        for C in right_set:
                            for lhs in self.non_terminal_rules[(B, C)]:
                                table[i][j].add(lhs)

        # Accept if 'S' can derive the entire sentence
        return 'S' in table[0][n-1]

    def parse_with_tree(self, sentence):
        """Return parse tree(s) for sentence."""
        words = sentence.split()
        n = len(words)

        # table[i][j] = dict mapping non-terminal -> list of derivations
        table = [[defaultdict(list) for _ in range(n)] for _ in range(n)]

        # Fill diagonal
        for i, word in enumerate(words):
            for lhs in self.terminal_rules[word]:
                table[i][i][lhs].append((word,))

        # Fill upper triangle
        for length in range(2, n + 1):
            for i in range(n - length + 1):
                j = i + length - 1

                for k in range(i, j):
                    left_dict = table[i][k]
                    right_dict = table[k+1][j]

                    for B, B_trees in left_dict.items():
                        for C, C_trees in right_dict.items():
                            for lhs in self.non_terminal_rules[(B, C)]:
                                for B_tree in B_trees:
                                    for C_tree in C_trees:
                                        table[i][j][lhs].append((lhs, B_tree, C_tree))

        return table[0][n-1].get('S', [])


# Example usage
if __name__ == '__main__':
    # Train n-gram model
    corpus = [
        "the cat sat on the mat",
        "the dog ran in the park",
        "the cat played with the dog",
        "the brown cat sat"
    ]

    model = NGramModel(n=2)
    model.train(corpus)

    print("Generated text:", model.generate(max_length=10))
    print("Perplexity on corpus:", model.perplexity(corpus))

    # CKY Parser
    grammar = [
        ('S', ('NP', 'VP')),
        ('NP', ('Det', 'N')),
        ('NP', ('Det', 'Adj', 'N')),  # Note: requires CNF conversion
        ('VP', ('V', 'NP')),
        ('Det', ('the',)),
        ('N', ('cat',)),
        ('N', ('dog',)),
        ('V', ('chased',)),
        ('Adj', ('brown',)),
    ]

    parser = CKYParser(grammar)
    print("Parse 'the cat chased the dog':", parser.parse("the cat chased the dog"))

Key design decisions:

  • Laplace Smoothing: Every n-gram gets a +1 count, vocabulary gets +1 in denominator. Simple and effective.
  • Perplexity: Computed as 2^{-\text{average log prob}}, measuring average branching factor.
  • CKY Indexing: Pre-indexed grammar rules by RHS for O(1) lookup during parsing.
  • Sampling: Generate method uses categorical sampling weighted by observed frequencies.

Section 4: Socratic Sandbox

A scenario and deep questions to guide your thinking:

Scenario

A bigram language model trained on news articles assigns P(\text{'States'} | \text{'United'}) = 0.32 to "States" as the next word. But it assigns P(\text{'furiously'} | \text{'sleep'}) = 0.001 to Chomsky's famous grammatical nonsense sentence "colorless green ideas sleep furiously."

Question 1: Context Depth

Q: The model gives "States" a high probability after "United." But what about after "the United"? A bigram only sees one word of context. How does a trigram improve this, and what's the tradeoff?
Hint

Think about data sparsity. How many times will "the United States" appear in your corpus? How many times will "United States" appear?

Question 2: Syntax vs. Semantics

Q: Chomsky's sentence is grammatically correct but semantically nonsensical. Can an n-gram model tell the difference between syntax and semantics? Why or why not?
Hint

An n-gram model only knows sequences of words. What would it need to know to judge whether ideas can actually be colorless or green?

Question 3: The Problem of Zero Probability

Q: The model encounters the word "cryptocurrency" which never appeared in training. Without smoothing, P(\text{'cryptocurrency'} | \text{any context}) = 0. Why does this make perplexity infinite? How does Laplace smoothing fix it?
Hint

What happens when you compute \log_2(0)? What does the Laplace smoothing formula add to every count?

Question 4: Parsing Ambiguity

Q: A CFG can parse "Time flies like an arrow" in multiple ways. List at least two valid parse trees. Why is this ambiguity hard for computers but easy for humans?
Hint

Consider two interpretations: (1) "Time" as subject, "flies" as verb. (2) "Time" as a verb (imperative), "flies" as noun. Draw the NP and VP structures.

Question 5: Geometry of Meaning

Q: Word2Vec maps words to vectors where \text{king} - \text{man} + \text{woman} \approx \text{queen}. What does this tell us about the geometry of meaning? Can n-gram models capture this?
Hint

An n-gram model only cares about frequency of word sequences. A word embedding cares about what contexts each word appears in. Which captures more about word meaning?
