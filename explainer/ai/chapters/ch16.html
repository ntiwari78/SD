Chapter 16: Making Simple Decisions

Utility Theory, Maximum Expected Utility, and Decision Networks — How to combine multiple outcomes into a single rational choice.

The Feynman Mental Model: Utility and Expected Utility

Imagine you're at a food truck festival. You have time for one meal before your train leaves. You spot three trucks:

The Setup:

Truck A (Gourmet Burger): Amazing food (happiness = 90), but a 2-hour wait.
Truck B (Mediocre Taco): Okay food (happiness = 50), no wait.
Truck C (Great Ramen): Excellent food (happiness = 75), 15-minute wait — but the chef is unreliable. 80% chance it's perfect, 20% chance it's cold and ruined (happiness = 10).

Here's the trap that humans fall into: We pick based on a single dimension. "I'll choose the gourmet burger because it's the best!" But we forget about time. Or we pick the taco because it's quick, ignoring quality.

This is where Utility comes in. Utility is a single number that combines all the dimensions that matter to you: taste, wait time, reliability, cost, etc. It's your personal "happiness score" for each outcome.

A Rational Decision:

You can't just maximize taste. You can't just minimize wait time. You need to combine them into a single score — your utility function. Then you calculate the expected utility of each action, accounting for uncertainty. Finally, you pick the action with the highest expected utility. This is the Maximum Expected Utility (MEU) principle.

Why We Need Utility Theory

  • Combining Multiple Outcomes: Real decisions have multiple consequences. Utility is the lingua franca that translates them into a single number.
  • Handling Uncertainty: We rarely know the future. Expected utility weighs outcomes by their probability.
  • Rational Preferences: The axioms of utility theory (orderability, transitivity, continuity) define what it means to be rational. They're not rules; they're logical constraints.
  • Encoding Values: Your utility function reveals your actual values, not what you say you value.

The Axioms of Utility Theory

These aren't commandments; they're logical statements. If you violate them, you can be exploited (Dutch Book argument):

  • Orderability: For any two outcomes A and B, you can always rank them. Either A is better, B is better, or you're indifferent.
  • Transitivity: If A is better than B, and B is better than C, then A is better than C. Without this, you'd go in circles.
  • Continuity: Between any two outcomes, there exists a lottery you'd find equally attractive. This enables us to assign numbers to outcomes.
  • Substitutability: If A is equivalent to a lottery [p, B; 1-p, C], you're indifferent between A and the lottery.
  • Monotonicity: If A is better than B, you prefer a higher probability of A.
  • Decomposability: Complex lotteries reduce to simple ones. [p, [q, A; 1-q, B]; 1-p, C] is equivalent to [pq, A; p(1-q), B; 1-p, C].

Utility and Money: Why $1M Isn't Twice $500K

A naive person might think: "If $500K gives me happiness = 500, then $1M gives happiness = 1000." This is linear utility — risk-neutral thinking.

Reality is different. Your first $100K buys food, shelter, safety. Your second $100K buys comfort and travel. Your 10th $100K buys a slightly nicer car. Each additional dollar brings less happiness. This is diminishing marginal utility, and it's captured by a concave utility function.

  • Risk-Averse: Concave utility curve. You'd rather have $500K for sure than a 50-50 gamble for $1M or $0. Most people are risk-averse about money.
  • Risk-Neutral: Linear utility. You're indifferent between $500K and a 50-50 gamble. Rare in reality.
  • Risk-Seeking: Convex utility curve. You'd rather gamble on $1M than take $500K. Explains why people buy lottery tickets (though mathematically terrible).

Decision Networks (Influence Diagrams)

Bayesian networks are great for representing probability. But decisions involve actions we choose (decision nodes) and utility we receive (utility nodes). A decision network extends Bayesian networks with these.

Structure:

  • Chance nodes: Random variables. The world's state.
  • Decision nodes: Actions we choose. The agent's choices.
  • Utility node: A function of outcomes. What we care about.
  • Edges: Show dependencies. A decision might affect a chance node. A chance node affects utility.

Value of Information (VOI)

Before deciding, should you gather more information? The Value of Information answers this question quantitatively.

Suppose you're deciding whether to take an umbrella. You could:

  • Decide now: 60% chance of rain. Best guess: take umbrella, EU = 75.
  • Check the weather first: If you learn it's definitely raining, take umbrella (EU = 90). If definitely not raining, leave it (EU = 100). Expected value of knowing = 0.6 × 90 + 0.4 × 100 = 94.

The Value of Perfect Information is 94 - 75 = 19. If checking the weather is free, do it! If it costs 30 units of happiness, don't bother.

A key insight: Information is never negative for a rational agent. You can always ignore it. But using it optimally always helps (or leaves you indifferent).

Mathematical Ascent: The Formalism of Utility

Expected Utility Formula

$$EU(a) = \sum_{s} P(\text{Result}(a) = s) \cdot U(s)$$

For action a, we sum over all possible outcome states s. Each state has a probability (likelihood given action a) and a utility (how much we like that outcome). Expected utility is the probability-weighted average happiness.

Maximum Expected Utility (MEU) Principle

$$a^* = \arg\max_a EU(a) = \arg\max_a \sum_s P(s|a) \cdot U(s)$$

A rational agent always picks the action a* that maximizes expected utility. This is the cornerstone of decision theory.

Lotteries and Preference Notation

A lottery is a probability distribution over outcomes. Notation:

$$[p, A; (1-p), B]$$

This means: "With probability p, get outcome A. With probability 1-p, get outcome B."

We write:

  • A ≻ B: Outcome A is strictly preferred to B.
  • A ≺ B: Outcome B is strictly preferred to A.
  • A ~ B: A and B are equally preferred (indifferent).

Utility Axioms (Formal)

Orderability (Completeness):

$$(A \succ B) \lor (B \succ A) \lor (A \sim B)$$

Transitivity:

$$(A \succ B) \land (B \succ C) \Rightarrow (A \succ C)$$

Continuity: For any A, B, C with A ≻ B ≻ C, there exists a probability p such that:

$$[p, A; (1-p), C] \sim B$$

This axiom is powerful. It says that between any two outcomes, there's a lottery equally good as an intermediate outcome. This justifies assigning numbers to preferences.

Risk Attitudes and Utility Curves

Risk-Averse (Concave): For a lottery L and its expected value E[L],

$$U(L) < U(E[L])$$

You prefer the certain expected value to the gamble. Utility function is concave (curves downward), like U(x) = √x.

Risk-Neutral (Linear):

$$U(L) = U(E[L])$$

Indifferent between the gamble and its expected value. Utility is linear, like U(x) = x.

Risk-Seeking (Convex):

$$U(L) > U(E[L])$$

You prefer the gamble to the sure thing. Utility is convex (curves upward), like U(x) = x².

Value of Perfect Information (VPI)

If we could observe a chance variable E_j before acting, how much would our expected utility improve?

$$VPI(E_j) = \left(\sum_{e_j} P(e_j) \max_a EU(a|e_j)\right) - \max_a EU(a)$$

Left term: Expected utility if we could observe E_j, then pick the best action for each observation.

Right term: Expected utility of the best action right now, without information.

The VPI is always non-negative:

$$VPI(E_j) \geq 0$$

Why? Because if the information is useless, we'd just ignore it and pick the same action. If it's useful, we pick a better action.

Decision Network Evaluation

To evaluate a decision network:

  1. Set all chance nodes to their observed values (evidence).
  2. For each possible choice at the decision node, compute the expected utility by summing over all unobserved chance nodes.
  3. Pick the decision with highest EU.

Formula: For decision node D with parents (decisions, chance variables) and children leading to utility U,

$$EU(d_i | \text{evidence}) = \sum_{\text{unobserved states}} P(\text{states} | d_i, \text{evidence}) \cdot U(d_i, \text{states})$$

Clean-Code Implementation: Decision Networks in Python

DecisionNetwork Class

class DecisionNetwork:
    """
    Decision Network (Influence Diagram).
    Extends Bayesian Networks with action and utility nodes.
    The rational agent selects the action maximizing expected utility.
    """
    def __init__(self):
        self.chance_nodes = {}    # random variables with CPTs
        self.decision_nodes = {}  # actions the agent can choose
        self.utility_node = None  # single utility function

    def add_chance(self, name, parents, cpt):
        """
        Add a chance (random) node.

        Args:
            name: Variable name
            parents: List of parent variable names
            cpt: Conditional probability table as dict
                 Keys are tuples of parent values
                 Values are dicts: outcome -> probability
        """
        self.chance_nodes[name] = {'parents': parents, 'cpt': cpt}

    def add_decision(self, name, options):
        """
        Add a decision node.

        Args:
            name: Decision variable name
            options: List of possible actions
        """
        self.decision_nodes[name] = options

    def set_utility(self, parents, utility_table):
        """
        Set the utility function.

        Args:
            parents: List of variables that affect utility
            utility_table: Dict mapping tuples (parent values) -> utility value
        """
        self.utility_node = {'parents': parents, 'table': utility_table}

    def expected_utility(self, action_name, action_value, evidence={}):
        """
        Compute EU for a specific action choice.

        Args:
            action_name: Name of decision node
            action_value: Which action to evaluate
            evidence: Dict of observed chance nodes

        Returns:
            Expected utility of this action
        """
        total_eu = 0

        # Find unobserved chance nodes that affect utility
        chance_parents = [p for p in self.utility_node['parents']
                         if p in self.chance_nodes and p not in evidence]

        # Enumerate all possible combinations of unobserved states
        for combo in self._enumerate_combos(chance_parents):
            # Build full state: observed + action + unobserved
            state = {**evidence, action_name: action_value, **combo}

            # Probability of this state given action and evidence
            prob = self._compute_probability(combo, evidence, action_name, action_value)

            # Utility of this state
            key = tuple(state[p] for p in self.utility_node['parents'])
            utility = self.utility_node['table'].get(key, 0)

            total_eu += prob * utility

        return total_eu

    def best_action(self, action_name, evidence={}):
        """
        Find action that maximizes expected utility — the MEU principle.

        Args:
            action_name: Name of decision node
            evidence: Dict of observed chance nodes

        Returns:
            Tuple (best_action, best_eu)
        """
        best = None
        best_eu = float('-inf')

        for option in self.decision_nodes[action_name]:
            eu = self.expected_utility(action_name, option, evidence)
            if eu > best_eu:
                best_eu = eu
                best = option

        return best, best_eu

    def value_of_information(self, info_var, action_name, evidence={}):
        """
        Compute VPI: How much would observing info_var help?

        The answer is always >= 0 for a rational agent.
        If VPI < cost_of_observation, don't observe.

        Args:
            info_var: Chance node we might observe
            action_name: Decision node we'll make
            evidence: Current observations

        Returns:
            VPI = EU(with info) - EU(without info)
        """
        # Current best EU without the information
        _, current_best_eu = self.best_action(action_name, evidence)

        # Expected EU if we could observe info_var first
        expected_eu_with_info = 0

        # For each possible value of info_var
        for val in self._get_values(info_var):
            new_evidence = {**evidence, info_var: val}
            p_val = self._marginal_prob(info_var, val, evidence)
            _, best_eu_given = self.best_action(action_name, new_evidence)
            expected_eu_with_info += p_val * best_eu_given

        vpi = expected_eu_with_info - current_best_eu
        return max(0, vpi)  # Never negative

    # Helper methods (simplified; real implementation would be more robust)

    def _enumerate_combos(self, variables):
        """Generate all combinations of variable values."""
        if not variables:
            yield {}
            return

        var = variables[0]
        rest = variables[1:]

        # Assume binary variables (True/False) for simplicity
        for val in [True, False]:
            for combo in self._enumerate_combos(rest):
                yield {var: val, **combo}

    def _compute_probability(self, unobserved, evidence, action_name, action_value):
        """
        Compute P(unobserved | action_value, evidence).
        Uses chain rule on chance nodes.
        """
        # Simplified: assume conditional independence given parents
        # In reality, would compute the full joint probability
        prob = 1.0

        for var, val in unobserved.items():
            parents = self.chance_nodes[var]['parents']
            parent_vals = tuple(evidence.get(p) or unobserved.get(p)
                               for p in parents)
            cpt_entry = self.chance_nodes[var]['cpt'].get(parent_vals, {})
            prob *= cpt_entry.get(val, 0.5)  # Default: uniform if missing

        return prob

    def _marginal_prob(self, var, val, evidence):
        """Compute P(var = val | evidence)."""
        # Simplified: assume independence
        return 0.5  # Replace with actual marginalization

    def _get_values(self, var):
        """Get possible values of a chance node."""
        return [True, False]  # Binary for simplicity

Utility Functions for Money

import math

def risk_averse_utility(wealth, alpha=0.5):
    """
    Concave utility function: U(x) = x^alpha, where alpha < 1.

    Example: risk_averse_utility(100, 0.5) = sqrt(100) = 10

    Property: U(a + a) = U(2a) < 2*U(a)
    The marginal utility of money decreases.
    """
    return max(0, wealth) ** alpha

def risk_neutral_utility(wealth):
    """
    Linear utility function: U(x) = x.

    Indifferent between $500K for sure and 50-50 gamble for $1M or $0.
    Typical for casinos and insurance companies (they're big enough
    to not care about individual outcomes).
    """
    return max(0, wealth)

def risk_seeking_utility(wealth, alpha=2.0):
    """
    Convex utility function: U(x) = x^alpha, where alpha > 1.

    Example: risk_seeking_utility(10, 2) = 100

    Property: U(a + a) = U(2a) > 2*U(a)
    You prefer risky gambles. Explains lotteries (irrational!).
    """
    return max(0, wealth) ** alpha

def expected_utility(action_outcomes, utility_func=risk_averse_utility):
    """
    Compute expected utility given a list of (probability, outcome) pairs.

    EU(a) = sum_s P(s) * U(s)

    Args:
        action_outcomes: List of (probability, outcome_value) tuples
        utility_func: Function to map outcome_value to utility

    Returns:
        Expected utility (a number)

    Example:
        outcomes = [(0.99, 100000), (0.01, 10000)]
        eu = expected_utility(outcomes)
    """
    return sum(p * utility_func(outcome) for p, outcome in action_outcomes)

# Example: Should you buy insurance?
# Without insurance: 99% chance of $100K wealth, 1% chance of $10K (house fire)
# With insurance: 100% chance of $97K wealth (pay $3K premium)

if __name__ == "__main__":
    # Define outcomes
    no_insurance = [(0.99, 100000), (0.01, 10000)]
    with_insurance = [(1.0, 97000)]

    # Compute EU for a risk-averse agent (typical person)
    eu_no_insurance = expected_utility(no_insurance, risk_averse_utility)
    eu_insurance = expected_utility(with_insurance, risk_averse_utility)

    print("RISK-AVERSE AGENT (you):")
    print(f"  EU(no insurance) = {eu_no_insurance:.2f}")
    print(f"  EU(insurance)    = {eu_insurance:.2f}")
    print(f"  → Choose: {'insurance' if eu_insurance > eu_no_insurance else 'no insurance'}")
    print()

    # For comparison: risk-neutral agent
    eu_no_insurance_neutral = expected_utility(no_insurance, risk_neutral_utility)
    eu_insurance_neutral = expected_utility(with_insurance, risk_neutral_utility)

    print("RISK-NEUTRAL AGENT (casino):")
    print(f"  EU(no insurance) = {eu_no_insurance_neutral:.2f}")
    print(f"  EU(insurance)    = {eu_insurance_neutral:.2f}")
    print(f"  → Choose: {'insurance' if eu_insurance_neutral > eu_no_insurance_neutral else 'no insurance'}")
    # The casino chooses no insurance because EV is higher (they benefit from the premium)

The key insight: Your choice reveals your risk attitude. If you buy insurance even though the expected value is worse, you're risk-averse. Your utility function is concave.

Socratic Sandbox: The Intersection Scenario

Scenario:

A self-driving car approaches an intersection. It can choose one of three actions:

(A) Go Straight: Fast, but 5% chance a red-light runner causes a collision.
(B) Slow and Check: Slower, but only 0.1% accident risk (the car checks for red-light runners).
(C) Detour: Takes 10 extra minutes, but zero risk of an accident.

The car's utility function assigns values to outcomes:
• Arrival on time: U = 100
• Slight delay (5 min): U = 80
• Major delay (10 min): U = 50
• Accident: U = -1000

Additional facts: Given traffic, there's a 20% chance you're already late. If you go straight, you arrive on time. If you slow/check, 30% chance of slight delay. If you detour, major delay.

Socratic Questions

  1. Calculate the expected utility for each action. Use the MEU principle to determine which action the car should choose. Once you have the answer, reflect: Is this the same choice a human driver might make? Why or why not? What does this tell you about how humans and AIs differ in risk assessment?
  2. Utility values encode real values. Right now, an accident has utility -1000. Suppose we change it to -10,000. Recalculate the MEU. Does the optimal choice change? What if we change it to -100? What does this reveal about how our numerical utility assignments reflect our deepest priorities? Try to think of a scenario where the accident value should be even more negative (like -1,000,000).
  3. Value of Information. Before acting, the car could activate expensive sensors (cost = 5 utils) to learn with certainty whether a red-light runner is coming. Should it? Calculate the VPI for knowing whether there's a red-light runner. Is VPI greater than the sensor cost? (Hint: This is how real self-driving cars work — they gather information because VPI can justify the cost.)
  4. Risk attitudes change the analysis. So far, we've assumed a linear utility (risk-neutral) for time delay. But humans are risk-averse about safety and risk-seeking about time (we'll gamble on speeding). How would the car's decision change if we use a concave utility function for accidents U_accident(p) = -1000 * p^2, where p is the accident probability? (This means small risks are even worse than linear.)
  5. Rational tradeoffs and the continuity axiom. Some might argue: "The car should NEVER accept any accident risk. Safety is paramount." But this violates the continuity axiom. Explain what the axiom says. According to it, there should be some probability p where you're indifferent between "definitely arrive 30 minutes late" and "probability p of accident, probability 1-p of on-time arrival." What does this tell you about rational tradeoffs? Is it rational to sometimes accept small safety risks to save time?
Reflection Prompt:

Think about a recent decision you made (choosing a route, accepting a risk, etc.). Did you explicitly calculate expected utility? Probably not. But implicitly, you did. You weighed outcomes by likelihood and assigned rough utility values. The MEU principle isn't just for AIs — it's how rational minds should work. The chapter teaches you to make this implicit calculation explicit.
