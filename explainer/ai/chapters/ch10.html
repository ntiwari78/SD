Chapter 10

Knowledge Representation

Part III: Knowledge, Reasoning, and Planning

The Encyclopedia of Everything

In the previous chapters, we learned how to reason—the inference engines, the rules, the resolution. Now we ask the deeper question: what do we put inside the knowledge base? How do we represent the messy, complex, contradictory real world in logic?

Ontological Engineering

Imagine designing a universal encyclopedia. Before you write a single entry, you need to answer: What are the major categories? How do they relate? This is ontological engineering—building the organizational skeleton that everything else hangs on.

  • Upper ontology: Starting from Anything, branch into AbstractObjects (numbers, sets, concepts) and GeneralizedEvents (actions, processes, states).
  • Categories and inheritance: Like a library. Basketballs is a subcategory of Balls. All balls are spherical, so basketballs inherit sphericity without needing to state it for each one.
  • The IsA puzzle: SubsetOf relates categories to categories (Dogs ⊂ DomesticAnimals). MemberOf relates objects to categories (Fido ∈ Dogs). Properties live at different levels—"dogs have four legs" is about members; "dogs are endangered" is about the category itself.

Physical Composition & Stuff vs. Things

Consider the PartOf relation: Romania is part of Europe, which is part of Earth. It's transitive—so Romania is part of Earth. But there's a crucial distinction:

  • Things (count nouns): If you cut an aardvark in half, you don't have two aardvarks. Things have identity.
  • Stuff (mass nouns): If you cut butter in half, you get two pieces of butter. Stuff has intrinsic properties (density, flavor) that survive subdivision.

Events and Time

The world isn't static. Event calculus represents what happens: events occur between times $t_1$ and $t_2$, and they cause fluents (facts) to become true or false. An event might initiate one fluent and terminate another. The key axiom is the persistence assumption: a fluent stays true until something explicitly makes it false.

Allen's interval algebra goes deeper, describing temporal relationships: Meet (intervals are adjacent), Before, During, Overlap, Starts, Finishes, Equals. These relations are the atoms of temporal reasoning.

Knowledge, Belief, and Modal Logic

Now add agents. What does Agent A know vs. believe? Modal logic captures this with the $\mathbf{K}$ operator: $\mathbf{K}_A P$ means "A knows P." Semantically, A knows P in world $w$ if P is true in every world that is "accessible" from A's epistemic position. If A doesn't know whether it's raining, there are accessible worlds where it rains and worlds where it doesn't.

Semantic Networks & Default Reasoning

Semantic networks visualize knowledge as graphs: nodes for objects/categories, labeled edges for relations. Inheritance follows edges upward. But the real world is messier—birds fly, except penguins. This is default reasoning: "typically true, but with exceptions." Nonmonotonic logics (circumscription, default logic) formalize this: minimize abnormality, or use default rules with justifications.

Truth Maintenance

When you learn something new that contradicts an old belief, you can't just delete the old fact—other inferences might depend on it. A truth maintenance system (TMS) tracks justifications, so you can efficiently retract a belief and cascade the consequences throughout the KB.

Mathematical Foundations

Upper Ontology Structure

The foundational category hierarchy:

$$\text{Anything} \rightarrow \begin{cases} \text{AbstractObjects} \rightarrow \{\text{Sets}, \text{Numbers}, \text{Categories}\} \\ \text{GeneralizedEvents} \rightarrow \{\text{Intervals}, \text{Places}, \text{PhysicalObjects}, \text{Processes}\} \end{cases}$$

Category Relations

$$BB_9 \in \text{Basketballs} \quad \text{(membership)}$$ $$\text{Basketballs} \subset \text{Balls} \quad \text{(subcategory)}$$ $$x \in \text{Basketballs} \Rightarrow \text{Spherical}(x) \quad \text{(inherited property)}$$

Disjoint, Exhaustive, and Partition

$$\text{Disjoint}(s) \Leftrightarrow \forall c_1, c_2 \in s. \; c_1 \neq c_2 \Rightarrow c_1 \cap c_2 = \emptyset$$ $$\text{ExhaustiveDecomposition}(s, c) \Leftrightarrow \forall x. \; x \in c \Leftrightarrow \exists c_2 \in s. \; x \in c_2$$ $$\text{Partition}(s, c) \Leftrightarrow \text{Disjoint}(s) \land \text{ExhaustiveDecomposition}(s, c)$$

Physical Composition

$$\text{PartOf}(x, y) \land \text{PartOf}(y, z) \Rightarrow \text{PartOf}(x, z) \quad \text{(transitive)}$$ $$\text{PartOf}(x, x) \quad \text{(reflexive)}$$

Measurements and Unit Conversion

$$\text{Length}(L_1) = \text{Inches}(1.5) = \text{Centimeters}(3.81)$$ $$\text{Centimeters}(2.54 \times d) = \text{Inches}(d)$$

Stuff: Mass Nouns and Intrinsic Properties

$$b \in \text{Butter} \land \text{PartOf}(p, b) \Rightarrow p \in \text{Butter}$$ $$b \in \text{Butter} \Rightarrow \text{MeltingPoint}(b) = \text{Centigrade}(30)$$

Event Calculus Predicates

Core predicates of event calculus:

$$T(f, t_1, t_2) \quad \text{fluent } f \text{ is true between } t_1 \text{ and } t_2$$ $$\text{Happens}(e, t_1, t_2) \quad \text{event } e \text{ occurs from } t_1 \text{ to } t_2$$ $$\text{Initiates}(e, f, t) \quad \text{event } e \text{ causes fluent } f \text{ to become true}$$ $$\text{Terminates}(e, f, t) \quad \text{event } e \text{ causes fluent } f \text{ to become false}$$

Persistence Axiom (Core of Event Calculus)

$$\text{Happens}(e, t_1, t_3) \land \text{Initiates}(e, f, t_2) \land \neg \text{Terminated}(f, t_2, t_4) \land t_1 \leq t_2 \leq t_3 \leq t_4$$ $$\Rightarrow T(f, t_2, t_4)$$

Allen's Interval Relations

$$\text{Meet}(i, j) \Leftrightarrow \text{End}(i) = \text{Begin}(j)$$ $$\text{Before}(i, j) \Leftrightarrow \text{End}(i) < \text{Begin}(j)$$ $$\text{During}(i, j) \Leftrightarrow \text{Begin}(j) < \text{Begin}(i) < \text{End}(i) < \text{End}(j)$$ $$\text{Overlap}(i, j) \Leftrightarrow \text{Begin}(i) < \text{Begin}(j) < \text{End}(i) < \text{End}(j)$$

Modal Logic: Knowledge (K Operator)

$$\mathbf{K}_A P \text{ is true in world } w \text{ iff } P \text{ is true in every world accessible from } w$$ $$(\mathbf{K}_a P \land \mathbf{K}_a(P \Rightarrow Q)) \Rightarrow \mathbf{K}_a Q$$ $$\mathbf{K}_a P \Rightarrow P \quad \text{(knowledge is true)}$$ $$\mathbf{K}_a P \Rightarrow \mathbf{K}_a(\mathbf{K}_a P) \quad \text{(positive introspection)}$$

Description Logic: CLASSIC Syntax

$$\text{Bachelor} = \text{And}(\text{Unmarried}, \text{Adult}, \text{Male})$$ $$\text{Bachelor}(x) \Leftrightarrow \text{Unmarried}(x) \land \text{Adult}(x) \land \text{Male}(x)$$

Circumscription: Minimizing Abnormality

$$\text{Bird}(x) \land \neg \text{Abnormal}_1(x) \Rightarrow \text{Flies}(x)$$ \text{Minimize } \text{Abnormal}_1 \quad \text{(assume } \neg \text{Abnormal}_1(x) \text{ unless proven)}$$

Default Logic Rules

$$\frac{\text{Bird}(x) : \text{Flies}(x)}{\text{Flies}(x)} \quad \text{"If Bird(x), then by default Flies(x)")}$$

Clean-Code Implementation

A complete ontology system with inheritance, semantic networks, and event calculus in Python:

# ── Knowledge Representation: Ontology & Semantic Network ──

from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import Dict, List, Optional, Set, Tuple, Any
from collections import defaultdict

# ── Category System with Inheritance ──

class Ontology:
    """An ontology of categories with inheritance reasoning.

    Supports:
    - Category membership (x ∈ C)
    - Subcategory relations (C₁ ⊂ C₂)
    - Property inheritance along subcategory links
    - Disjoint and partition constraints
    - Default values with overriding
    """
    def __init__(self):
        self.members: Dict[str, Set[str]] = defaultdict(set)
        self.subcategories: Dict[str, Set[str]] = defaultdict(set)
        self.parent_of: Dict[str, Set[str]] = defaultdict(set)
        self.properties: Dict[str, Dict[str, Any]] = defaultdict(dict)
        self.object_props: Dict[str, Dict[str, Any]] = defaultdict(dict)

    def add_category(self, child: str, parent: str = None):
        """Add a category, optionally as subcategory of parent."""
        if parent:
            self.subcategories[parent].add(child)
            self.parent_of[child].add(parent)

    def add_member(self, obj: str, category: str):
        """Assert that obj ∈ category."""
        self.members[category].add(obj)

    def set_property(self, category: str, prop: str, value: Any):
        """Set a default property for all members of a category."""
        self.properties[category][prop] = value

    def set_object_property(self, obj: str, prop: str, value: Any):
        """Set a property directly on an object (overrides defaults)."""
        self.object_props[obj][prop] = value

    def is_member(self, obj: str, category: str) -> bool:
        """Check if obj ∈ category (considering inheritance)."""
        if obj in self.members.get(category, set()):
            return True
        for sub in self.subcategories.get(category, set()):
            if self.is_member(obj, sub):
                return True
        return False

    def is_subcategory(self, child: str, parent: str) -> bool:
        """Check if child ⊂ parent (transitive)."""
        if child == parent:
            return True
        for p in self.parent_of.get(child, set()):
            if self.is_subcategory(p, parent):
                return True
        return False

    def get_property(self, obj: str, prop: str) -> Optional[Any]:
        """Get property using inheritance: most-specific-first."""
        if prop in self.object_props.get(obj, {}):
            return self.object_props[obj][prop]

        obj_categories = self._get_categories(obj)
        for cat in self._sort_by_specificity(obj_categories):
            if prop in self.properties.get(cat, {}):
                return self.properties[cat][prop]
        return None

    def _get_categories(self, obj: str) -> Set[str]:
        """Get all categories the object belongs to."""
        result = set()
        for cat, members in self.members.items():
            if obj in members:
                result.add(cat)
                result.update(self._get_ancestors(cat))
        return result

    def _get_ancestors(self, category: str) -> Set[str]:
        """Get all ancestor categories (transitive parents)."""
        ancestors = set()
        for parent in self.parent_of.get(category, set()):
            ancestors.add(parent)
            ancestors.update(self._get_ancestors(parent))
        return ancestors

    def _sort_by_specificity(self, categories: Set[str]) -> List[str]:
        """Sort categories most-specific-first for inheritance."""
        cat_list = list(categories)
        cat_list.sort(key=lambda c: -len(self._get_ancestors(c)))
        return cat_list


# ── Event Calculus ──

@dataclass
class Event:
    """An event with initiates/terminates effects on fluents."""
    name: str
    start: float
    end: float
    initiates: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)
    terminates: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)

class EventCalculus:
    """Simple event calculus implementation with persistence axiom."""
    def __init__(self):
        self.events: List[Event] = []
        self.initial_fluents: Set[str] = set()

    def add_event(self, event: Event):
        """Record an event."""
        self.events.append(event)
        self.events.sort(key=lambda e: e.start)

    def set_initial(self, fluent: str, value: bool = True):
        """Set initial state of a fluent."""
        if value:
            self.initial_fluents.add(fluent)
        else:
            self.initial_fluents.discard(fluent)

    def holds(self, fluent: str, time: float) -> bool:
        """Check if fluent holds at given time (persistence axiom)."""
        current = fluent in self.initial_fluents
        for event in self.events:
            if event.end > time:
                break
            if fluent in event.terminates:
                current = False
            if fluent in event.initiates:
                current = True
        return current

    def timeline(self, fluent: str, start: float, end: float,
                 steps: int = 20) -> List[Tuple[float, bool]]:
        """Generate a timeline of fluent values."""
        step_size = (end - start) / steps
        return [(start + i * step_size,
                 self.holds(fluent, start + i * step_size))
                for i in range(steps + 1)]


# ── Semantic Network ──

class SemanticNetwork:
    """A semantic network with nodes and labeled edges."""
    def __init__(self):
        self.nodes: Set[str] = set()
        self.edges: List[Tuple[str, str, str]] = []
        self.node_properties: Dict[str, Dict[str, Any]] = defaultdict(dict)

    def add_node(self, name: str, **properties):
        self.nodes.add(name)
        self.node_properties[name].update(properties)

    def add_edge(self, from_node: str, relation: str, to_node: str):
        self.nodes.add(from_node)
        self.nodes.add(to_node)
        self.edges.append((from_node, relation, to_node))

    def get_related(self, node: str, relation: str) -> List[str]:
        return [to for fr, rel, to in self.edges
                if fr == node and rel == relation]

    def inherit_property(self, node: str, prop: str) -> Optional[Any]:
        """Follow MemberOf/SubsetOf links upward."""
        if prop in self.node_properties.get(node, {}):
            return self.node_properties[node][prop]
        for rel in ["MemberOf", "SubsetOf"]:
            parents = self.get_related(node, rel)
            for parent in parents:
                val = self.inherit_property(parent, prop)
                if val is not None:
                    return val
        return None


# ── Example Usage ──

if __name__ == "__main__":
    ont = Ontology()

    # Build hierarchy
    ont.add_category("Balls", "PhysicalObjects")
    ont.add_category("Basketballs", "Balls")
    ont.add_category("Persons", "Mammals")
    ont.add_category("OneLeggedPersons", "Persons")

    # Set properties
    ont.set_property("Balls", "shape", "spherical")
    ont.set_property("Persons", "legs", 2)
    ont.set_property("OneLeggedPersons", "legs", 1)

    # Add members
    ont.add_member("BB9", "Basketballs")
    ont.add_member("John", "OneLeggedPersons")
    ont.add_member("Mary", "Persons")

    # Query
    print(f"BB9 shape: {ont.get_property('BB9', 'shape')}")  # spherical
    print(f"Mary legs: {ont.get_property('Mary', 'legs')}")   # 2
    print(f"John legs: {ont.get_property('John', 'legs')}")   # 1 (overridden)

    # Event calculus demo
    ec = EventCalculus()
    ec.set_initial("At(Shankar,SF)", True)
    ec.add_event(Event("Fly1", 10, 15,
                       initiates=["At(Shankar,DC)"],
                       terminates=["At(Shankar,SF)"]))

    print(f"\nAt(Shankar,SF) at t=5: {ec.holds('At(Shankar,SF)', 5)}")   # True
    print(f"At(Shankar,SF) at t=20: {ec.holds('At(Shankar,SF)', 20)}")   # False
    print(f"At(Shankar,DC) at t=20: {ec.holds('At(Shankar,DC)', 20)}")   # True

Socratic Questions & Answers

Q1: SubsetOf vs. MemberOf confusion in inheritance ▼

The Problem: You've built an ontology with both SubsetOf and MemberOf links, but inheritance keeps returning properties from the wrong level.

The Answer: This is the classic "IsA" ambiguity. SubsetOf relates categories to categories: Basketballs ⊂ Balls. MemberOf relates objects to categories: BB₉ ∈ Basketballs. The critical insight: if Persons is a SubsetOf Mammals, then Mammals is not just a category, it's a category of categories.

Properties work at different levels. "Persons have two legs" describes properties of the members of Persons. But "Dogs is an endangered category" is a property of the category itself. Always ask: is this a property of the members or of the category as a whole?

Q2: Default values and exceptions in inheritance ▼

The Problem: Your system says all persons have 2 legs, but John has 1 leg. The algorithm returns 2 for John because it finds the Persons category first.

The Answer: The inheritance search must be most-specific-first. If John is a member of OneLeggedPersons (a subcategory of Persons), the algorithm should check OneLeggedPersons before checking Persons. The implementation sorts categories by depth in the hierarchy and searches from deepest to shallowest. This way, more specific defaults take precedence.

Q3: The Nixon Diamond and nonmonotonic reasoning ▼

The Problem: Nixon is both a Quaker (pacifists by default) and a Republican (hawks by default). Your system either derives both Pacifist(Nixon) and ¬Pacifist(Nixon), or neither.

The Answer: This is the famous "Nixon Diamond" in nonmonotonic reasoning. The correct answer is neither. A properly implemented circumscriptive reasoner produces two preferred models: one where Nixon is a pacifist, one where he isn't. Default logic produces two valid "extensions." The reasoning system should reflect the ambiguity rather than forcing a choice. If you need a definitive answer, you need prioritized circumscription: e.g., "religious beliefs take precedence over political ones."

Q4: Fluent persistence and the frame problem ▼

The Problem: Using event calculus, your fluents aren't persisting correctly. They seem to randomly become false between events.

The Answer: The persistence axiom requires that a fluent remains true from when it was initiated until something explicitly terminates it. The key condition is ¬Terminated(f, t₂, t₄)—meaning NO event terminated the fluent in the interval. A common bug: checking only the most recent event rather than scanning for ANY terminating event in the interval. Also verify your time ordering: the axiom requires $t_1 \leq t_2 \leq t_3 \leq t_4$.

Q5: Description Logic tractability vs. expressiveness ▼

The Problem: Your description logic implementation is slow. Subsumption checking takes exponential time even for simple problems.

The Answer: Description logics are designed for decidable subsumption, but complexity varies by dialect. CLASSIC (And, All, AtLeast, AtMost, Fills) allows polynomial-time subsumption. Adding negation, disjunction, or full number restrictions pushes complexity to NP-hard or EXPTIME. This is the fundamental tradeoff: restricted languages are efficient but less expressive; expressive languages are slow. Choose the DL dialect matching your needs.
