Chapter 15: Probabilistic Programming

Inference by Sampling and MCMC — When Exact Probabilities Become Intractable

Inference by Sampling: A Rational Agent's Monte Carlo Strategy

The Intuition: Random Walks and Convergence

Imagine you're trying to find the average height of people in a city, but you can't measure everyone. Instead, you walk around randomly and measure people you bump into. If you walk long enough and don't stick to one neighborhood, your sample average will converge to the true average. This is Monte Carlo sampling.

Similarly, in probabilistic inference, we face an exponential explosion: computing exact probabilities requires summing over all possible variable assignments, which is exponentially expensive. Instead of computing, we simulate. We generate many possible scenarios ("worlds") by sampling, and use statistics to approximate the true probability distribution.

Why Exact Inference Fails

For a Bayesian Network with n binary variables, there are 2^n possible complete assignments. Computing P(Query | Evidence) via exact inference (as in Chapter 13) requires:

  • Enumerating or summing over all hidden variable assignments
  • For each assignment, looking up conditional probabilities and multiplying them
  • Normalizing by the total probability of the evidence

With 30 variables, this is 2^30 ≈ 1 billion calculations. With 100 variables, it's infeasible.

Four Sampling Approaches: A Spectrum

Direct Sampling

  • Sample all variables in topological order
  • No evidence constraints
  • Fast but often inapplicable

Rejection Sampling

  • Generate full samples, reject if inconsistent with evidence
  • Simple to implement
  • Wasteful when evidence is rare

Likelihood Weighting

  • Fix evidence variables, weight by likelihood
  • Every sample counts
  • More efficient than rejection

MCMC / Gibbs

  • Random walk through state space
  • Converges to true distribution
  • Scales best to many variables

Direct Sampling from a Bayesian Network

Generate one sample by visiting each variable in topological order (parents before children) and sampling:

$$x_i \sim P(X_i | \text{parents}(X_i))$$

Example: In the Alarm network, sample Burglary first, then Earthquake, then Alarm (conditioned on Burglary and Earthquake), then JohnCalls and MaryCalls.

Rejection Sampling: The Wasteful Approach

Generate many samples from the prior distribution. Keep only those consistent with the evidence. Estimate the query probability from the accepted samples.

$$\hat{P}(X|e) = \frac{N(X, e)}{N(e)}$$

where N(X, e) is the count of samples where X is true and all evidence variables match e.

Problem: If the evidence is rare (e.g., P(e) = 0.001), then 99.9% of samples are rejected. With 100,000 samples, only ~100 are accepted, making the estimate extremely noisy.

Likelihood Weighting: Every Sample Counts

Instead of rejecting, we weight each sample by how likely it is to produce the observed evidence:

w(x, e) = \prod_{i: X_i \in e} P(e_i | \text{parents}(E_i))

This weights samples that explain the evidence more highly. The algorithm:

  1. For each evidence variable, set it to the observed value (don't sample)
  2. For non-evidence variables, sample normally from their conditional distribution
  3. Weight the sample by the likelihood of observing the evidence
  4. Accumulate weighted counts
  5. Normalize to estimate probabilities

Likelihood weighting uses all samples, so it's much more efficient than rejection sampling.

MCMC: The Random Walk Solution

Markov Chain Monte Carlo avoids generating independent samples. Instead, we perform a random walk through the state space where each step depends only on the previous state (the Markov property). Eventually, the walk settles into the true distribution.

The key idea is detailed balance: the probability of moving from state x to x' times the stationary probability of x equals the reverse:

\pi(x) T(x \to x') = \pi(x') T(x' \to x)

Gibbs Sampling: The Practical MCMC Algorithm

The simplest MCMC variant for Bayesian Networks: resample one variable at a time, conditioned on all others.

P(X_i | x_1, ..., x_{i-1}, x_{i+1}, ..., x_n) = P(X_i | \text{MarkovBlanket}(X_i))

The Markov blanket of a variable is its parents, children, and children's other parents. Because the network is acyclic, this conditional is often efficient to compute.

Burn-In and Mixing Time

MCMC doesn't generate independent samples. The chain has a burn-in period: early samples depend strongly on the initial state, so we discard them (typically 10–50% of total samples). After burn-in, samples approximately follow the true distribution, though they are still correlated.

The mixing time is how long it takes for the chain to converge. Fast mixing means high correlation between successive samples (good for speed); slow mixing means you need many more samples to get independent estimates (bad for efficiency).

Mathematical Foundations

1. Direct Sampling

\text{For each variable } X_i \text{ in topological order:}\\ x_i \sim P(X_i | \text{parents}(X_i))\\ \text{Return } x = (x_1, ..., x_n)

2. Rejection Sampling Estimator

After generating N samples:

\hat{P}(X|e) = \frac{\#\text{ samples where } X \text{ is true and evidence matches}}{{\#\text{ samples where evidence matches}}}\\ = \frac{N(X, e)}{N(e)}

The estimator is unbiased, but variance is high when P(e) is small.

3. Likelihood Weighting

Sample non-evidence variables; fix evidence variables. Each sample x has weight:

w(x, e) = \prod_{X_i \in \mathbf{E}} P(e_i | \text{parents}(E_i))

The weighted estimate is:

\hat{P}(X|e) = \frac{\sum_{x: X \text{ true}} w(x, e)}{\sum_{x} w(x, e)}

4. MCMC Stationary Distribution

A transition kernel T(x → x') has stationary distribution π if:

\pi(x) = \sum_{x'} \pi(x') T(x' \to x)

For reversible chains (detailed balance):

\pi(x) T(x \to x') = \pi(x') T(x' \to x)

5. Gibbs Sampling Update

At each step, uniformly choose a variable X_i (or cycle through them). Sample its new value from:

X_i^{(t+1)} \sim P(X_i | x_1^{(t)}, ..., x_{i-1}^{(t)}, x_{i+1}^{(t)}, ..., x_n^{(t)})

For Bayesian Networks, this simplifies to the Markov blanket:

P(X_i | \text{MB}(X_i)) \propto P(X_i | \text{parents}(X_i)) \prod_{Y_j \in \text{children}(X_i)} P(Y_j | \text{parents}(Y_j))

6. Convergence Guarantees

Under mild conditions (irreducibility and aperiodicity):

  • The chain's distribution converges to the stationary distribution π
  • The law of large numbers applies: sample averages converge to expectations
  • Convergence is geometric, with rate determined by the spectral gap

7. Variance and Mixing Time

For correlated samples from a slow-mixing chain, the effective sample size (ESS) is:

\text{ESS} = \frac{N}{1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \rho(k)}

where ρ(k) is the autocorrelation at lag k. High autocorrelation means you need many more samples to achieve the same variance reduction as independent sampling.

Clean-Code Implementation

A self-contained Python implementation of sampling-based inference:

import random
from typing import Dict, Tuple, List

class ProbabilisticSampler:
    """
    Sampling-based inference for Bayesian Networks.
    When exact inference is too expensive, the rational agent
    approximates probabilities by simulating many possible worlds.
    """

    def __init__(self, network):
        """
        network: A BayesNet object with .nodes (dict) and .order (topological sort)
        Each node has .cpt (conditional probability table), .parents, .children
        """
        self.net = network

    def prior_sample(self) -> Dict[str, bool]:
        """Generate one sample from the prior distribution (no evidence)."""
        sample = {}
        for var in self.net.order:
            node = self.net.nodes[var]
            # Get the probability that this variable is True
            parent_vals = tuple(sample[p] for p in node.parents) if node.parents else ()
            p_true = node.cpt.get(parent_vals, 0.5)
            sample[var] = random.random() < p_true
        return sample

    def rejection_sampling(
        self,
        query: str,
        evidence: Dict[str, bool],
        n_samples: int = 10000
    ) -> Dict[bool, float]:
        """
        Simple but wasteful: generate samples, keep only consistent ones.
        Like fishing with a net — you catch many, keep few.

        Returns: {True: P(query=True|evidence), False: P(query=False|evidence)}
        """
        counts = {True: 0, False: 0}
        accepted = 0

        for _ in range(n_samples):
            sample = self.prior_sample()
            # Check if sample is consistent with evidence
            if all(sample.get(k) == v for k, v in evidence.items()):
                counts[sample[query]] += 1
                accepted += 1

        if accepted == 0:
            return None  # No samples matched evidence!

        # Normalize to probabilities
        total = sum(counts.values())
        return {k: v / total for k, v in counts.items()}

    def likelihood_weighting(
        self,
        query: str,
        evidence: Dict[str, bool],
        n_samples: int = 10000
    ) -> Dict[bool, float]:
        """
        Smarter sampling: fix evidence variables, weight by likelihood.
        Every sample counts — no wasteful rejection.

        Returns: weighted probability estimate
        """
        weights = {True: 0.0, False: 0.0}

        for _ in range(n_samples):
            sample = {}
            w = 1.0  # Weight starts at 1

            for var in self.net.order:
                node = self.net.nodes[var]
                parent_vals = tuple(sample[p] for p in node.parents) if node.parents else ()

                if var in evidence:
                    # Evidence variable: fix the value
                    sample[var] = evidence[var]
                    # Weight by likelihood of observing this value
                    p = node.cpt.get(parent_vals, 0.5)
                    w *= p if evidence[var] else (1 - p)
                else:
                    # Non-evidence variable: sample normally
                    p_true = node.cpt.get(parent_vals, 0.5)
                    sample[var] = random.random() < p_true

            # Accumulate weighted counts
            weights[sample[query]] += w

        # Normalize
        total = sum(weights.values())
        if total == 0:
            return None
        return {k: v / total for k, v in weights.items()}

    def gibbs_sampling(
        self,
        query: str,
        evidence: Dict[str, bool],
        n_samples: int = 10000,
        burn_in: int = 1000
    ) -> Dict[bool, float]:
        """
        MCMC via Gibbs: random walk through state space.
        Each step changes one variable, conditioned on all others.
        Like exploring a city by randomly taking one turn at a time.

        Returns: probability estimate after burn-in period
        """
        # Initialize non-evidence variables randomly
        state = {var: random.choice([True, False]) for var in self.net.order}
        state.update(evidence)  # Fix evidence variables

        non_evidence = [v for v in self.net.order if v not in evidence]
        counts = {True: 0, False: 0}

        total_iterations = n_samples + burn_in

        for iteration in range(total_iterations):
            # Gibbs step: resample each non-evidence variable
            for var in non_evidence:
                # Resample var conditioned on all other variables
                node = self.net.nodes[var]
                parent_vals = tuple(state[p] for p in node.parents) if node.parents else ()

                # P(var=True | all others)
                # This is proportional to: P(var) * product of P(child | parents)
                p_true = node.cpt.get(parent_vals, 0.5)

                # Incorporate influence from children
                for child_name in node.children:
                    child = self.net.nodes[child_name]
                    child_parent_vals_true = tuple(
                        state[p] if p != var else True for p in child.parents
                    )
                    child_parent_vals_false = tuple(
                        state[p] if p != var else False for p in child.parents
                    )
                    p_child_true = child.cpt.get(child_parent_vals_true, 0.5)
                    p_child_false = child.cpt.get(child_parent_vals_false, 0.5)
                    child_val = state[child_name]

                    p_true *= (p_child_true if child_val else (1 - p_child_true))

                # Compute P(var=False | all others)
                p_false_base = 1 - node.cpt.get(parent_vals, 0.5)
                p_false = p_false_base
                for child_name in node.children:
                    child = self.net.nodes[child_name]
                    child_parent_vals_true = tuple(
                        state[p] if p != var else True for p in child.parents
                    )
                    child_parent_vals_false = tuple(
                        state[p] if p != var else False for p in child.parents
                    )
                    p_child_true = child.cpt.get(child_parent_vals_true, 0.5)
                    p_child_false = child.cpt.get(child_parent_vals_false, 0.5)
                    child_val = state[child_name]

                    p_false *= (p_child_false if child_val else (1 - p_child_false))

                # Normalize and sample
                total_prob = p_true + p_false
                if total_prob > 0:
                    prob_true_normalized = p_true / total_prob
                else:
                    prob_true_normalized = 0.5

                state[var] = random.random() < prob_true_normalized

            # After burn-in, accumulate counts
            if iteration >= burn_in:
                counts[state[query]] += 1

        # Estimate probability
        total = sum(counts.values())
        if total == 0:
            return None
        return {k: v / total for k, v in counts.items()}

    def compare_methods(
        self,
        query: str,
        evidence: Dict[str, bool],
        n_samples: int = 5000
    ) -> Dict[str, Dict[bool, float]]:
        """Compare all three methods on the same problem."""
        return {
            'rejection': self.rejection_sampling(query, evidence, n_samples),
            'likelihood': self.likelihood_weighting(query, evidence, n_samples),
            'gibbs': self.gibbs_sampling(query, evidence, n_samples)
        }

Usage Example

# Assume we have a BayesNet 'alarm_net' from Chapter 13
sampler = ProbabilisticSampler(alarm_net)

# Query: P(Burglary | JohnCalls=True, MaryCalls=True)
query = 'Burglary'
evidence = {'JohnCalls': True, 'MaryCalls': True}

# Use rejection sampling
result_rej = sampler.rejection_sampling(query, evidence, n_samples=10000)
print(f"Rejection: P({query}|evidence) = {result_rej[True]:.4f}")

# Use likelihood weighting (more efficient)
result_lw = sampler.likelihood_weighting(query, evidence, n_samples=10000)
print(f"Likelihood: P({query}|evidence) = {result_lw[True]:.4f}")

# Use Gibbs sampling (scales better)
result_gibbs = sampler.gibbs_sampling(query, evidence, n_samples=10000)
print(f"Gibbs: P({query}|evidence) = {result_gibbs[True]:.4f}")

Key Implementation Notes

  • Markov Blanket: In Gibbs sampling, P(X_i | all others) depends only on X_i's parents, children, and co-parents (children's other parents)
  • Efficiency: Pre-compute topological order and Markov blankets to speed up sampling
  • Variance Reduction: Use antithetic sampling or importance reweighting for lower variance estimates
  • Diagnostics: Monitor autocorrelation, effective sample size (ESS), and potential scale reduction factor (PSRF) to check convergence

Socratic Sandbox: Deep Dive Questions

Scenario: An agent uses rejection sampling to compute P(Burglary | JohnCalls=True, MaryCalls=True) from the Alarm network. After 100,000 samples, only 37 match the evidence (both calls are true). The estimate is noisy and unreliable.

Q1: Why were only 37 out of 100,000 samples accepted? Calculate the approximate probability that both John and Mary call in a random sample. ▼

Answer: Only 37 out of 100,000 samples were accepted means P(JohnCalls ∧ MaryCalls) ≈ 37/100,000 = 0.00037, or about 0.037%. This is a rare event.

In the Alarm network, JohnCalls and MaryCalls are influenced by Alarm, which is influenced by Burglary and Earthquake. Both of these base events are rare:

  • P(Burglary) = 0.001
  • P(Earthquake) = 0.002
  • P(Alarm | Burglary, Earthquake) chains these probabilities
  • P(JohnCalls | Alarm) ≈ 0.9 (highly correlated)

The joint probability is the product of these small probabilities, making the combined event rare. This is why rejection sampling fails for unlikely evidence.

Q2: Likelihood weighting would use all 100,000 samples. How does it achieve this? What are the weights, and why do they correct the bias? ▼

Answer: Likelihood weighting fixes the evidence variables and weights each sample by its likelihood of producing the evidence:

w(x) = P(\text{JohnCalls}=\text{true} | \text{Alarm}=a) \cdot P(\text{MaryCalls}=\text{true} | \text{Alarm}=a)

Samples that lead to Alarm=true get high weight; samples with Alarm=false get nearly zero weight.

Why it corrects bias: Rejection sampling rejects 99.963% of samples, leading to high variance. Likelihood weighting keeps all samples but down-weights the ones that don't align well with evidence. The weighted average:

\hat{P}(\text{Burglary}|\text{evidence}) = \frac{\sum_{x: \text{Burglary}=\text{true}} w(x)}{\sum_x w(x)}

approximates the true posterior without throwing away information. The weights naturally handle rare events by emphasizing paths that lead to the observed evidence.

Q3: Gibbs sampling doesn't generate independent samples — each sample depends on the previous one. Why is this both a strength and a weakness? ▼

Strength: The chain naturally stays in high-probability regions. Once it reaches a plausible state (matching the evidence), it stays nearby and explores similar, likely states. This is more efficient than rejection sampling, which wastes samples on unlikely regions of the state space.

Weakness: Successive samples are correlated. If the chain moves slowly (slow mixing), you need many more samples to get the same variance reduction as independent samples. The effective sample size (ESS) is much smaller than the nominal sample size. Example: If autocorrelation is 0.9, you need ~10 times more samples to achieve the same precision as independent samples.

Solution: Use thinning (keep every k-th sample) or diagnostic tools (trace plots, autocorrelation plots, potential scale reduction factor) to assess mixing and plan sample sizes accordingly.

Q4: What is the 'burn-in' period in MCMC, and why is it necessary? What happens if you start counting too early? ▼

Burn-in: The initial phase of MCMC where the chain "forgets" its starting state and converges to the stationary distribution. Typically, we discard the first 10–50% of samples.

Why necessary: The first sample depends heavily on the initial state (which is arbitrary). If you start at an unlikely state, early samples will be unrepresentative. The chain needs time to explore and settle into the true distribution.

If you start counting too early: Your estimates will be biased toward the starting state. Example: If you start with Burglary=true and only burn 100 samples, the early samples over-represent Burglary=true, biasing your estimate of P(Burglary | evidence) upward. With large datasets, this bias becomes visible in diagnostics (e.g., PSRF > 1.1 indicates non-convergence).

Diagnostic: Plot the trace (sample values over time). If the early samples clearly drift toward typical values, you haven't burned long enough.

Q5: If you increase the network to 100 variables, rejection sampling becomes virtually useless. Why? How do likelihood weighting and Gibbs sampling scale differently? ▼

Rejection sampling: The acceptance rate decays exponentially with network size and evidence strength. With 100 variables and rare evidence, you might need 10^30 samples to accept even one! This is the curse of dimensionality.

Likelihood weighting: Scales polynomially, not exponentially. The weight computation is O(n) (one multiplication per evidence variable), so even with 100 variables, you can use all 10,000 samples. However, if evidence is very rare, weights become extremely imbalanced (some near 1, others near 0), causing high variance and poor effective sample size.

Gibbs sampling: Each update is O(n) (or O(c) if you only update the Markov blanket, which is typically small). The chain explores the state space efficiently, and mixing time scales much better than the acceptance rate of rejection sampling. With good mixing, you get reasonable estimates with 10,000 samples even in high dimensions.

Practical rule:

  • Small networks (5–10 vars): Exact inference or rejection sampling
  • Medium networks (10–50 vars): Likelihood weighting or Gibbs
  • Large networks (50+ vars): Gibbs sampling or modern variants (Hamiltonian MCMC, variational inference)
