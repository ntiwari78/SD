Chapter 27: Robotics

Perception, Planning, and Control—The Dance of Autonomous Systems

Section 1: Feynman Mental Model

The Sense-Plan-Act Cycle

Analogy: "Imagine blindfolding yourself and navigating your kitchen to make coffee. You'd need to:

(1) PERCEIVE — feel the countertop, listen for the coffee machine's gurgle, smell when it's ready;

(2) PLAN — figure out the sequence: find mug → find machine → pour water → etc.;

(3) CONTROL — actually move your arm smoothly to grab the mug without knocking things over.

A robot faces all three challenges simultaneously, in real-time, with imperfect sensors and wobbly actuators. The magic is in the tight loop: perceive → plan → act → perceive → plan → act. Each cycle happens in milliseconds. One mistake cascades; one success builds confidence."

Robot Hardware

Sensors (The Robot's Eyes, Ears, and Touch)

  • Cameras: 2D images (RGB) or stereo depth. Good for shape, color, landmarks.
  • LIDAR: Sweeping laser that measures distance to obstacles. Perfect for mapping and collision detection.
  • IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit): Accelerometer + gyroscope. Measures robot's motion, tilt, spin.
  • Encoders: Count wheel rotations. Estimate position but drift over time (odometry error).
  • Ultrasonic/Infrared: Simple distance sensors, cheaper, noisier.
  • Touch Sensors: Detect contact—essential for safe grasping.

Actuators (The Robot's Hands and Feet)

  • DC/Stepper Motors: Spin wheels, joints. Discrete power, simple control.
  • Servo Motors: Position-controlled, precise angles. Used for arms and grippers.
  • Linear Actuators: Extend and retract. Good for sliding, lifting, pushing.
  • Grippers: Parallel-jaw (two fingers) or under-actuated (mimic hand). Tricky: how hard to squeeze without crushing?

Motion Planning

A robot at point A needs to reach point B while avoiding obstacles. This is the motion planning problem.

Configuration Space (C-space)

Instead of thinking about positions in physical space, think about configurations—the values of all the robot's joints. For a 2D mobile robot: $q = (x, y, \theta)$ (position and orientation). For a 7-joint arm: $q = (q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_7)$.

An obstacle in physical space carves out a region in C-space—the "C-obstacle." The robot must find a path in C-space that avoids all C-obstacles.

Probabilistic Roadmap (PRM)

PRM is the slow-and-steady approach:

  1. Sample: Randomly generate thousands of collision-free configurations $q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n$.
  2. Connect: For each pair of nearby configurations, try to connect them with a straight-line path. If the path is collision-free, add an edge.
  3. Query: Add start and goal to the graph, then run Dijkstra or A*.

Pros: Works well for high-dimensional spaces (robotic arms). Cons: Slow to build; doesn't handle narrow passages well.

Rapidly-Exploring Random Trees (RRT)

RRT is the eager, impatient approach:

  1. Sample: Pick a random point $q_{\text{rand}}$ in C-space (or bias toward goal 10% of the time).
  2. Nearest: Find the node $q_{\text{near}}$ in the tree closest to $q_{\text{rand}}$.
  3. Extend: Take a small step of size $\delta$ from $q_{\text{near}}$ toward $q_{\text{rand}}$, creating $q_{\text{new}}$.
  4. Add: If the path from $q_{\text{near}}$ to $q_{\text{new}}$ is collision-free, add $q_{\text{new}}$ to the tree and recurse.
  5. Goal check: If $q_{\text{new}}$ is close enough to the goal, declare success.

Why it works: By always extending toward random points, RRT naturally explores space. Large empty regions grow fast; narrow passages take longer but are eventually found.

Localization & Mapping

Where Am I? (Localization)

Wheel encoders drift. A laser range-finder is noisy. The robot's initial position is uncertain. Localization means inferring where you are, despite noisy sensors.

Particle Filter: Maintain a cloud of $n$ possible positions (particles), each with a weight. At each time step:

  1. Predict: Move all particles according to motion commands + noise (odometry model).
  2. Update: Compare sensor readings to predicted readings. Increase weight of particles with small prediction error.
  3. Resample: Discard unlikely particles; duplicate likely ones. The cloud converges.

What Does the World Look Like? (Mapping)

Build a map of obstacles, landmarks, and free space. Methods include occupancy grid (cells are free or occupied) or feature-based (extract corners, edges, doors).

SLAM: The Chicken-and-Egg Problem

SLAM = Simultaneous Localization and Mapping. The robot doesn't know the map, so it can't localize. It doesn't know where it is, so it can't build a map. Yet it must do both at once.

Modern SLAM (e.g., FastSLAM, ORB-SLAM) uses particle filters + visual landmarks. Each particle maintains its own map hypothesis. Particles that predict sensor readings accurately are kept; others discarded.

Control Theory: Bringing Plans to Life

A plan says "move to (5, 3)." But the wheels are noisy. The terrain is uneven. Control is the feedback loop that continuously corrects.

PID Controller

The most famous control law. Given a target (setpoint) and a current measurement, output a command:

$$u(t) = K_p e(t) + K_i \int_0^t e(\tau) \, d\tau + K_d \frac{de(t)}{dt}$$

Where $e(t) = \text{setpoint} - \text{measurement}$ is the error.

  • Proportional (P): $K_p e(t)$. React to current error. Too high → oscillates. Too low → sluggish.
  • Integral (I): $K_i \int e(\tau) d\tau$. Eliminate steady-state error. Accumulates past mistakes.
  • Derivative (D): $K_d \frac{de}{dt}$. Dampen oscillations. Reduce overshoot.

For a robot steering toward a target angle, the error is the angle difference. Apply PID and the heading converges smoothly.

Manipulation: Grasping and Handling

Once a robot reaches an object, how does it pick it up? Grasping is a complex research area.

  • Grasp quality: Is the grasp stable? Can the gripper apply enough force without damaging the object?
  • Grasp planning: Given a 3D model of the object, find contact points that won't slip.
  • In-hand manipulation: Reorient the object using gravity and finger contact—no lifting.

Multi-Robot Systems

Multiple robots must coordinate: avoid each other, share tasks, communicate under bandwidth constraints. Decentralized control (no central planner) is more robust.

  • Swarm behavior: Simple local rules (follow neighbors, avoid obstacles) lead to emergent group motion—like a flock of birds.
  • Task allocation: Assign jobs to robots efficiently (e.g., via auction or consensus algorithms).

Section 2: Mathematical Ascent

Configuration Space & Kinematics

$$q = (q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n)$$

Configuration vector for an n-DOF (degree-of-freedom) robot.

Forward Kinematics

$$\mathbf{x} = f(q)$$

Given joint angles $q$, compute end-effector position and orientation $\mathbf{x}$. This is a composition of rotations and translations.

Inverse Kinematics

$$q = f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})$$

Given desired end-effector pose $\mathbf{x}$, find joint angles. Often has multiple solutions (or none). Harder to compute than forward kinematics.

PID Control (Discrete Form)

Robots run on discrete time steps $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$

$$u_k = K_p e_k + K_i \sum_{j=0}^k e_j \Delta t + K_d \frac{e_k - e_{k-1}}{\Delta t}$$

RRT Extension Step

Sample a random configuration $q_{\text{rand}}$. Find nearest node $q_{\text{near}}$ (using Euclidean distance in C-space). Extend by step size $\delta$:

$$q_{\text{new}} = q_{\text{near}} + \delta \cdot \frac{q_{\text{rand}} - q_{\text{near}}}{\|q_{\text{rand}} - q_{\text{near}}\|}$$

Particle Filter Update (Bayes Rule)

Each particle $i$ has a weight $w_i^{(t)}$. After observing sensor reading $z_t$:

$$w_i^{(t)} = P(z_t \mid x_i^{(t)}) \cdot w_i^{(t-1)}$$

Then normalize: $w_i^{(t)} \leftarrow w_i^{(t)} / \sum_j w_j^{(t)}$

Odometry Model (2D Mobile Robot)

Given linear velocity $v$ and angular velocity $\omega$, update pose over time step $\Delta t$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} x' \\ y' \\ \theta' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} x + v\Delta t \cos\theta \\ y + v\Delta t \sin\theta \\ \theta + \omega\Delta t \end{pmatrix}$$

This model is exact for differential-drive robots (two independently controlled wheels).

Collision Checking: Line-Segment vs. Circle

To check if a path from $q_1$ to $q_2$ collides with a circular obstacle centered at $(c_x, c_y)$ with radius $r$:

Find the closest point on line segment $q_1 q_2$ to $(c_x, c_y)$. If this distance is $< r$, there's a collision.

Algebraic approach: parameterize the segment as $\mathbf{p}(t) = q_1 + t(q_2 - q_1)$ for $t \in [0, 1]$. The closest point has parameter:

$$t^* = \max\left(0, \min\left(1, \frac{(c - q_1) \cdot (q_2 - q_1)}{\|q_2 - q_1\|^2}\right)\right)$$

Section 3: Clean-Code Implementation

Below are three core robotics algorithms in readable Python, suitable for understanding and teaching.

PID Controller

import math
import random

class PIDController:
    """
    PID Controller: the workhorse of robot control.
    Combines three terms to smoothly drive error to zero:
    - Proportional: react to current error
    - Integral: eliminate steady-state error
    - Derivative: dampen oscillations
    """
    def __init__(self, kp=1.0, ki=0.0, kd=0.0, dt=0.01):
        self.kp = kp
        self.ki = ki
        self.kd = kd
        self.dt = dt
        self.integral = 0
        self.prev_error = 0

    def update(self, setpoint, measurement):
        """
        Compute control output given a setpoint and measurement.

        Args:
            setpoint: desired value
            measurement: current value (from sensor)

        Returns:
            control command (effort)
        """
        error = setpoint - measurement
        self.integral += error * self.dt
        derivative = (error - self.prev_error) / self.dt
        self.prev_error = error

        output = (self.kp * error +
                  self.ki * self.integral +
                  self.kd * derivative)
        return output

    def reset(self):
        """Clear accumulated error (use when switching setpoints)."""
        self.integral = 0
        self.prev_error = 0

Rapidly-Exploring Random Tree

class RRT:
    """
    Rapidly-exploring Random Tree: motion planning algorithm.
    Builds a tree by randomly sampling the space and extending
    toward new samples, efficiently exploring the configuration space.
    """
    def __init__(self, start, goal, bounds, obstacles, step_size=0.5, max_iter=1000):
        """
        Initialize RRT planner.

        Args:
            start, goal: (x, y) tuples
            bounds: [(min_x, max_x), (min_y, max_y)]
            obstacles: list of (cx, cy, radius)
            step_size: distance to extend per iteration
            max_iter: max iterations before giving up
        """
        self.start = start
        self.goal = goal
        self.bounds = bounds
        self.obstacles = obstacles
        self.step = step_size
        self.max_iter = max_iter
        self.tree = {start: None}  # node -> parent

    def plan(self):
        """
        Plan a collision-free path from start to goal.

        Returns:
            path (list of configs) or None if no path found
        """
        for _ in range(self.max_iter):
            # Sample: random point, biased toward goal
            if random.random() < 0.1:
                q_rand = self.goal
            else:
                q_rand = (random.uniform(*self.bounds[0]),
                         random.uniform(*self.bounds[1]))

            # Nearest: find closest node in tree
            q_near = min(self.tree.keys(),
                        key=lambda q: self._dist(q, q_rand))

            # Extend: step toward q_rand
            q_new = self._steer(q_near, q_rand)

            # Check collision
            if not self._collides(q_near, q_new):
                self.tree[q_new] = q_near

                # Goal check
                if self._dist(q_new, self.goal) < self.step:
                    self.tree[self.goal] = q_new
                    return self._extract_path()

        return None  # No path found

    def _dist(self, a, b):
        """Euclidean distance."""
        return math.sqrt((a[0]-b[0])**2 + (a[1]-b[1])**2)

    def _steer(self, from_q, to_q):
        """Steer from_q toward to_q by step_size."""
        d = self._dist(from_q, to_q)
        if d < self.step:
            return to_q
        ratio = self.step / d
        return (from_q[0] + ratio*(to_q[0]-from_q[0]),
                from_q[1] + ratio*(to_q[1]-from_q[1]))

    def _collides(self, q1, q2):
        """Check if segment q1→q2 hits any obstacle."""
        for cx, cy, r in self.obstacles:
            if self._segment_circle_collision(q1, q2, cx, cy, r):
                return True
        return False

    def _segment_circle_collision(self, p1, p2, cx, cy, r):
        """Detect collision between line segment p1-p2 and circle."""
        dx, dy = p2[0]-p1[0], p2[1]-p1[1]
        fx, fy = p1[0]-cx, p1[1]-cy

        a = dx*dx + dy*dy
        b = 2*(fx*dx + fy*dy)
        c = fx*fx + fy*fy - r*r

        if a == 0:
            return c <= 0

        disc = b*b - 4*a*c
        if disc < 0:
            return False

        sqrt_disc = math.sqrt(disc)
        t1 = (-b - sqrt_disc) / (2*a)
        t2 = (-b + sqrt_disc) / (2*a)

        # Collision if intersection within segment
        return (0 <= t1 <= 1) or (0 <= t2 <= 1) or (t1 < 0 and t2 > 1)

    def _extract_path(self):
        """Backtrack from goal to start."""
        path = [self.goal]
        node = self.goal
        while self.tree[node] is not None:
            node = self.tree[node]
            path.append(node)
        return list(reversed(path))

Particle Filter

class ParticleFilter:
    """
    Particle Filter for robot localization.
    Maintains a cloud of possible positions (particles),
    updating weights based on sensor readings.
    """
    def __init__(self, n_particles, bounds):
        """
        Initialize particle cloud.

        Args:
            n_particles: number of particles
            bounds: [(min_x, max_x), (min_y, max_y)]
        """
        self.particles = [
            (random.uniform(*bounds[0]),
             random.uniform(*bounds[1]),
             random.uniform(0, 2*math.pi))
            for _ in range(n_particles)
        ]
        self.weights = [1.0/n_particles] * n_particles

    def predict(self, v, omega, dt, noise=0.1):
        """
        Move particles according to motion model + noise.

        Args:
            v: linear velocity
            omega: angular velocity
            dt: time step
            noise: motion noise std dev
        """
        new_particles = []
        for x, y, theta in self.particles:
            x_new = x + (v*math.cos(theta) +
                        random.gauss(0, noise))*dt
            y_new = y + (v*math.sin(theta) +
                        random.gauss(0, noise))*dt
            theta_new = theta + (omega +
                                random.gauss(0, noise*0.5))*dt
            new_particles.append((x_new, y_new, theta_new))
        self.particles = new_particles

    def update(self, measurement, landmarks):
        """
        Update particle weights based on sensor data.

        Args:
            measurement: list of measured distances to landmarks
            landmarks: list of (x, y) landmark positions
        """
        for i, (x, y, theta) in enumerate(self.particles):
            # Expected distances
            expected = [math.sqrt((x-lx)**2 + (y-ly)**2)
                       for lx, ly in landmarks]

            # Error
            error = sum((m - e)**2
                       for m, e in zip(measurement, expected))

            # Weight: likelihood of this measurement
            self.weights[i] = math.exp(-error / 2)

        # Normalize
        total = sum(self.weights)
        self.weights = [w/total for w in self.weights]

    def resample(self):
        """
        Importance resampling: keep likely particles,
        discard unlikely ones.
        """
        n = len(self.particles)
        cumulative = [sum(self.weights[:i+1]) for i in range(n)]

        new_particles = []
        for _ in range(n):
            r = random.random()
            for i, c in enumerate(cumulative):
                if r <= c:
                    new_particles.append(self.particles[i])
                    break

        self.particles = new_particles
        self.weights = [1.0/n] * n

    def get_estimate(self):
        """Get weighted mean position."""
        n = len(self.particles)
        x_mean = sum(p[0]*w for p, w in zip(self.particles, self.weights))
        y_mean = sum(p[1]*w for p, w in zip(self.particles, self.weights))
        return (x_mean, y_mean)

Section 4: Socratic Sandbox

Scenario: The Warehouse Robot

A warehouse robot must navigate from shelf A to shelf B while avoiding other robots and humans. Its LIDAR sensor gives noisy distance readings, its wheel encoders drift over time, and the map was made 6 months ago (some shelves have been moved).

Question 1: The robot's wheel encoders say it has moved 10 meters straight. But due to wheel slip, it has actually moved 9.5 meters and drifted 0.3 meters to the left. How does the particle filter handle this accumulated error (odometry drift)?

Hint: Think about the predict step. How do particles spread?

Question 2: RRT found a path to shelf B, but the path goes through a narrow gap between two shelves. The robot is 60cm wide, the gap is 65cm. Should the planner account for the robot's size? How?

Hint: Configuration space. What changes if we enlarge obstacles?

Question 3: A PID controller steers the robot along the planned path. With $K_p$ too high, the robot oscillates around the path. With $K_p$ too low, it turns too slowly. Explain the role of $K_d$ in solving this.

Hint: What does the derivative term do to the rate of change of error?

Question 4: SLAM is described as a "chicken-and-egg" problem. Why? What are the two things the robot needs to estimate simultaneously, and why can't it solve one before the other?

Hint: The very names: Simultaneous Localization AND Mapping.

Question 5: Two robots need to navigate the same corridor in opposite directions without a centralized controller. They can communicate locally (short-range radio). What simple protocol could prevent deadlocks?

Hint: Think about yielding. Can they agree who has priority?
