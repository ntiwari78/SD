PART IV: Uncertain Knowledge and Reasoning

Chapter 12: Quantifying Uncertainty

Why logic isn't enough for a toothache. Navigating the fuzzy real world with probability, Bayes' Rule, and rational belief updates.

The Toothache Problem: Why Logic Breaks Down

You wake up with a toothache. Does that definitely mean you have a cavity?

Classical logic says: "If toothache, then cavity" is either True or False. But the real world is messier.

The Qualification Problem

A cavity is ONE possible cause. But a toothache could also result from:

  • Gum disease
  • A cracked tooth
  • Sinus pressure
  • Jaw tension
  • Recent dental work
  • Referred pain from an infected tooth elsewhere

To write a complete logical rule, you'd need to enumerate EVERY exception and every combination of conditions. This leads to:

Three Sources of Incompleteness in Logic

  • Laziness: Writing all the rules is tedious. We'd need thousands of clauses to capture every caveat and edge case.
  • Theoretical ignorance: We don't fully understand all the causes of a toothache. Medical science is incomplete.
  • Practical ignorance: We might not have measured all relevant variables. Is the tooth sensitive to hot? Cold? Pressure?

The Solution: Degrees of Belief

Instead of True or False, assign a number between 0 and 1 representing your confidence that the toothache is caused by a cavity:

  • 0 = "Definitely not a cavity"
  • 0.5 = "Equally likely cavity or something else"
  • 1 = "Definitely a cavity"

This is the insight at the heart of Bayesian reasoning: probability is a measure of your belief given incomplete information.

Mathematical Ascent: Probability Theory

Before we can update beliefs, we need to formalize what "probability" means. Here are the foundations:

Core Axioms

Axiom 1: Probabilities are non-negative
$$0 \leq P(A) \leq 1$$

Axiom 2: Certain event has probability 1
$$P(\Omega) = 1$$ where $\Omega$ is the sample space (all possible outcomes).

Axiom 3: Probability of disjoint events
$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$

Conditional Probability

The probability of A given that we know B is true:

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$

Read as: "P of A given B equals the probability of both A and B occurring, divided by the probability of B."

The Product Rule

A handy rearrangement:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B)$$

Independence

Two events are independent if knowing one tells you nothing about the other:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$
if and only if $P(A|B) = P(A)$.

Conditional Independence

Sometimes, two events are independent given a third:

$$P(A \cap B | C) = P(A|C) \cdot P(B|C)$$

For example: Given that we know the weather (C), knowing whether it rained yesterday (A) tells us nothing about whether we're eating ice cream today (B). They're conditionally independent.

Bayes' Rule: The Star of the Show

This is the formula that powers Bayesian inference, spam filters, medical diagnosis AI, and much of modern machine learning:

$$P(H|E) = \frac{P(E|H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)}$$

Read as: "The probability of hypothesis H given evidence E equals the probability of evidence E given H, times the prior probability of H, divided by the total probability of seeing evidence E."

Breaking Down Each Term

$P(H|E)$ — Posterior probability
Your updated belief in H after observing evidence E. This is what we want to compute.

$P(E|H)$ — Likelihood
How probable is evidence E if hypothesis H is true? "If the patient has the disease, how likely is a positive test?"

$P(H)$ — Prior probability
Your belief in H before seeing the evidence. "What fraction of people have this disease in the general population?"

$P(E)$ — Marginal probability (evidence)
The total probability of observing evidence E under all hypotheses. This is a normalizing constant.

Computing the Denominator

The denominator $P(E)$ is computed by the law of total probability:

$$P(E) = P(E|H) \cdot P(H) + P(E|\neg H) \cdot P(\neg H)$$

So the full expansion of Bayes' Rule is:

$$P(H|E) = \frac{P(E|H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E|H) \cdot P(H) + P(E|\neg H) \cdot P(\neg H)}$$

Example: The Toothache Problem Revisited

Let's quantify the toothache scenario with numbers:

  • Prior: $P(\text{cavity}) = 0.05$ (5% of people have cavities)
  • Likelihood: $P(\text{toothache|cavity}) = 0.90$ (90% of cavity patients feel pain)
  • False positive rate: $P(\text{toothache|\neg cavity}) = 0.15$ (15% of non-cavity people still have toothaches)

Now compute the probability of a cavity given we observe a toothache:

$$P(\text{cavity|toothache}) = \frac{0.90 \times 0.05}{0.90 \times 0.05 + 0.15 \times 0.95}$$ $$= \frac{0.045}{0.045 + 0.1425} = \frac{0.045}{0.1875} \approx 0.24$$

Result: Even with a toothache, there's only about a 24% chance you have a cavity! This surprises many people—the prior is very important. But intuitively, if cavities are rare (5%) but many other things cause toothaches (15%), then most toothaches aren't cavities.

Clean-Code Implementation

Now let's translate theory into working Python. A rational agent updates its beliefs when new evidence arrives—this is exactly what we implement:

Bayes' Rule Calculator

def bayes_update(prior, likelihood, evidence_prob):
    """
    Apply Bayes' Rule to update belief.

    Args:
        prior: P(H) — initial belief in hypothesis H
        likelihood: P(E|H) — probability of seeing evidence E if H is true
        evidence_prob: P(E) — total probability of evidence E

    Returns:
        P(H|E) — updated (posterior) belief after seeing evidence
    """
    if evidence_prob == 0:
        return 0
    posterior = (likelihood * prior) / evidence_prob
    return min(1, max(0, posterior))  # Clamp to [0, 1]


# Example: The Toothache Problem
# Prior belief: 5% of people have cavities
prior_cavity = 0.05

# Likelihood: 90% of cavity patients experience toothaches
likelihood = 0.90

# Total probability of observing a toothache:
# = P(toothache|cavity) * P(cavity) + P(toothache|no cavity) * P(no cavity)
p_toothache_given_cavity = 0.90
p_toothache_given_no_cavity = 0.15
p_toothache = (p_toothache_given_cavity * prior_cavity +
               p_toothache_given_no_cavity * (1 - prior_cavity))

# Update belief
posterior = bayes_update(prior_cavity, likelihood, p_toothache)
print(f"P(cavity|toothache) = {posterior:.4f}")
# Output: P(cavity|toothache) = 0.2400

# Second observation: dentist looks and doesn't see obvious cavity
# P(no visible cavity | cavity) = 0.1 (small chance cavity isn't visible)
# P(no visible cavity | no cavity) = 0.95
second_likelihood = 0.1
second_posterior_prior = posterior  # Use previous posterior as new prior
p_no_visible = (0.1 * posterior + 0.95 * (1 - posterior))
posterior_after_exam = bayes_update(second_posterior_prior, second_likelihood, p_no_visible)
print(f"P(cavity|toothache AND no visible cavity) = {posterior_after_exam:.4f}")
# Output: Much lower!

Full Joint Probability Distribution

For complex problems with multiple variables, we can represent the complete probability model as a table:

class JointDistribution:
    """
    Full joint probability distribution over discrete variables.

    The complete model: contains the probability of every possible
    combination of variable values. A rational agent uses this as a
    complete model of uncertainty.

    Drawback: grows exponentially (2^n for n binary variables).
    This motivates Bayesian Networks (Chapter 13).
    """

    def __init__(self, variables, probabilities):
        """
        Args:
            variables: List of variable names, e.g., ['Toothache', 'Cavity', 'Catch']
            probabilities: Dict mapping tuples of values to probabilities
                          e.g., {(True, True, True): 0.05, ...}
        """
        self.variables = variables
        self.table = probabilities
        self._validate()

    def _validate(self):
        """Ensure probabilities sum to 1."""
        total = sum(self.table.values())
        if abs(total - 1.0) > 0.0001:
            raise ValueError(f"Probabilities must sum to 1, got {total}")

    def probability(self, assignment):
        """Get probability of a specific assignment."""
        return self.table.get(tuple(assignment), 0)

    def query(self, query_var, evidence=None):
        """
        Compute P(query_var | evidence) by enumeration.

        Args:
            query_var: Name of variable to query
            evidence: Dict of variable assignments, e.g., {'Toothache': True}

        Returns:
            Dict with probabilities for each value of query_var
        """
        if evidence is None:
            evidence = {}

        query_var_idx = self.variables.index(query_var)
        results = {}

        for query_value in [True, False]:
            total = 0

            # Sum probabilities of all assignments matching query and evidence
            for assignment, prob in self.table.items():
                row = dict(zip(self.variables, assignment))

                # Check if this assignment matches evidence
                matches_evidence = all(
                    row[var] == value
                    for var, value in evidence.items()
                )

                # Check if this assignment matches query value
                matches_query = row[query_var] == query_value

                if matches_evidence and matches_query:
                    total += prob

            results[query_value] = total

        # Normalize to make probabilities sum to 1
        norm = sum(results.values())
        if norm > 0:
            return {k: v / norm for k, v in results.items()}
        return results

    def marginalize(self, *drop_vars):
        """
        Remove variables by summing them out.
        Returns a new distribution over remaining variables.
        """
        remaining_vars = [v for v in self.variables if v not in drop_vars]
        new_table = {}

        for assignment, prob in self.table.items():
            row = dict(zip(self.variables, assignment))
            remaining_assignment = tuple(
                row[v] for v in remaining_vars
            )
            new_table[remaining_assignment] = new_table.get(remaining_assignment, 0) + prob

        return JointDistribution(remaining_vars, new_table)


# Example: Toothache × Cavity × Catch (dentist catches cavity)
# Joint distribution over three binary variables = 8 possible assignments
joint = JointDistribution(
    ['Toothache', 'Cavity', 'Catch'],
    {
        (True, True, True):   0.108,
        (True, True, False):  0.012,
        (False, True, True):  0.072,
        (False, True, False): 0.008,
        (True, False, True):  0.016,
        (True, False, False): 0.064,
        (False, False, True): 0.144,
        (False, False, False): 0.576,
    }
)

# Query: P(Cavity | Toothache)
result = joint.query('Cavity', evidence={'Toothache': True})
print(f"P(Cavity | Toothache=True) = {result[True]:.4f}")
# Output: 0.6000

# Marginalize out 'Catch' to get 2D distribution
marginal = joint.marginalize('Catch')
print(f"Variables after marginalizing Catch: {marginal.variables}")
# Output: Variables after marginalizing Catch: ['Toothache', 'Cavity']

Socratic Sandbox: Learning Through Failure

Let's examine a real failure case: medical diagnosis using Bayes' Rule.

The Scenario

A medical diagnosis AI uses Bayes' Rule to interpret test results. The hospital runs a screening test for a rare disease:

  • Prior: 0.1% of the population has the disease (very rare)
  • Test sensitivity (true positive rate): 99% — if you have the disease, the test is positive 99% of the time
  • Test specificity (true negative rate): 95% — if you DON'T have the disease, the test is negative 95% of the time

A patient tests positive. The AI naively reports: "99% chance you have the disease."

But this is WRONG. Here's why:

Question 1: What is the ACTUAL posterior probability? Show answer →

Solution: Use Bayes' Rule with the full formula:

P(\text{disease|positive}) = \frac{P(\text{positive|disease}) \cdot P(\text{disease})}{P(\text{positive})}

Where:

  • $P(\text{positive|disease}) = 0.99$ (sensitivity)
  • $P(\text{disease}) = 0.001$ (prior)
  • $P(\text{positive|no disease}) = 0.05$ (false positive rate = 1 - specificity)
  • $P(\text{no disease}) = 0.999$

Denominator (total probability of positive test):

P(\text{positive}) = 0.99 \times 0.001 + 0.05 \times 0.999 = 0.00099 + 0.04995 = 0.05094

Posterior:

P(\text{disease|positive}) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.001}{0.05094} = \frac{0.00099}{0.05094} \approx 0.0194 = 1.94\%

Wow! A positive test means only a ~2% chance of the disease, NOT 99%. Why? Because the disease is so rare that false positives vastly outnumber true positives.

Question 2: What is the "base rate fallacy" and why is the prior so critical? Show answer →

Base Rate Fallacy: Ignoring the prior probability (base rate) and relying only on the test accuracy.

The AI saw "99% sensitivity" and concluded "99% chance you have the disease." But this confuses $P(\text{positive|disease})$ with $P(\text{disease|positive})$—they're very different!

Why the prior matters: Even a highly accurate test can be misleading if the condition is rare. When disease prevalence is 0.1% but false positives occur in 5% of healthy people, there are roughly 50 false positives for every 1 true positive in a population screening.

The prior probability $P(\text{disease}) = 0.001$ is CRUCIAL. Without it, we can't interpret what a positive test means. It's not just a detail—it's half the story.

Question 3: How does conditional independence simplify a complex model? Show answer →

The Problem: Suppose we have 10 symptoms and 5 diseases. The full joint distribution has $2^{10} \times 2^5 = 32,768$ possible states. Storing and updating this table is computationally expensive.

The Insight: If we assume symptoms are conditionally independent given the disease, we can factor the distribution. The "naive Bayes" model assumes:

P(\text{symptoms|disease}) = P(s_1|d) \cdot P(s_2|d) \cdot \ldots \cdot P(s_{10}|d)

This is only ~15 probabilities to learn instead of 32,768! And it works surprisingly well in practice—it's the foundation of spam filters, recommendation systems, and more.

Trade-off: We lose some representational power (we're making an assumption), but gain massive computational efficiency. This is a core theme in AI: making smart independence assumptions to escape exponential complexity.

Question 4: How does the Bayesian approach compare to pure logic? Show answer →

Logical approach: "IF positive_test THEN disease" is either true or false.

Problem: This rule is incorrect under uncertainty! A positive test doesn't guarantee disease (as we showed: only 2% posterior).

Bayesian approach: Model the relationship probabilistically. When you see a positive test, update your degree of belief from 0.1% to 2%.

What's lost in logic?

  • The magnitude of belief: "How confident are you?"
  • The ability to combine evidence: sequential updates
  • Quantifying trade-offs: "Is a 2% risk acceptable?"

Logic gives binary answers (True/False). Bayes gives graded answers (0.02 out of 1.0). In a messy world, graded answers are more honest.

Question 5: Sequential Bayesian updates — running two independent tests Show answer →

Scenario: First test is positive (posterior = 1.94%). Then the patient runs a second independent test, which is also positive. How do we update?

Key insight: The posterior from the first test becomes the prior for the second test:

P(\text{disease|both positive}) = \frac{P(\text{pos}_2|\text{disease}) \cdot P(\text{disease|pos}_1)}{P(\text{pos}_2)}

With $P(\text{disease|pos}_1) = 0.0194$ as the new prior:

P(\text{pos}_2) = 0.99 \times 0.0194 + 0.05 \times (1 - 0.0194) = 0.0519

New posterior:

P(\text{disease|both}) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.0194}{0.0519} \approx 0.369 = 36.9\%

Two independent positive tests raise belief from 1.9% to 37%! This is rational belief updating in action. Each piece of evidence shifts our confidence, but the base rate still matters—we're not at 99%.
