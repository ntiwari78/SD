The Detective in the Dark Cave

Imagine you're a detective exploring a mysterious dark cave. You can only see the room you're standing in, but the world beyond remains invisible. However, you have tools: a notebook to record facts, and logic to draw conclusions.

As you explore, you notice patterns. A strong smell signals danger nearby — there's likely something harmful in an adjacent room. A breeze means an open pit is close. You find a shiny object (gold!). After every observation, you write it down in your notebook. This notebook is your knowledge base — it contains all the facts you trust to be true.

But your notebook is more powerful than mere memory. You use logical deduction. You might reason: "I'm in room [1,1]. I don't sense any danger. Therefore, the adjacent rooms [1,2] and [2,1] must be safe." You combine clues from different observations to narrow down where dangers lurk. You don't need to explore every single square — logic does the work for you.

The Wumpus World: This is the setting for Chapter 7's canonical example. A 4×4 grid cave contains a deadly Wumpus (one location), multiple bottomless pits, and a gold nugget. Your agent starts at [1,1] facing east. It must navigate safely to the gold and escape, using only the percepts: stench (adjacent to Wumpus), breeze (adjacent to pit), glitter (gold here), bump (walked into wall), scream (Wumpus died). The agent has limited arrows and must reason under uncertainty — it never knows the full map.

This is exactly how a knowledge-based agent works. The agent performs three key operations:

  1. TELL: When you perceive something, you record it. "I sense a breeze" → add to KB
  2. ASK: When you need to act, you ask the KB what you should do. "Is [2,1] safe?" → query KB
  3. Infer: The KB doesn't just memorize; it derives new facts from old ones. From "I'm at [1,1] with no breeze" and "Breeze ↔ Adjacent to pit", you infer "No pit at [1,2] or [2,1]"

The power of this approach is knowledge representation: you describe the world in a formal language (propositional logic, first-order logic, etc.), encode facts as sentences, and let inference engines prove new theorems. This separates knowledge level (what facts are true?) from implementation level (how do you store and query them efficiently?).

Logical agents are declarative (you tell the agent facts and it reasons) rather than procedural (you tell the agent exactly what to do). They scale to complex worlds because you don't hardcode every action — you encode the rules and let logic do the heavy lifting.

Mathematical Foundations

Entailment: The Core of Logical Inference

In logic, we often ask: "Does knowledge base KB imply that sentence α is true?" This relationship is called entailment.

$$\alpha \models \beta$$

Read as: "α entails β". Formally, α entails β if and only if, in every world where α is true, β is also true. In set notation: if $M(\alpha)$ is the set of all models (possible worlds) where α is true, then:

$$\alpha \models \beta \text{ iff } M(\alpha) \subseteq M(\beta)$$

Example: The sentence "It is raining and cold" entails "It is raining" because every world where both conditions hold also has rain.

Propositional Logic: Language and Semantics

Propositional logic is built from atomic propositions (like P, Q, R) combined with five logical connectives: negation (¬), conjunction (∧), disjunction (∨), implication (→), and biconditional (↔).

Truth Tables for Logical Connectives:

P Q ¬P P ∧ Q P ∨ Q P → Q P ↔ Q
T T F T T T T
T F F F T F F
F T T F T T F
F F T F F T T

Key observation: The implication $P \Rightarrow Q$ is false only when P is true and Q is false. This is crucial for representing rules like "If stench, then Wumpus nearby."

Inference Rules: How We Derive New Knowledge

Modus Ponens is the most fundamental inference rule:

$$\frac{\alpha \Rightarrow \beta, \quad \alpha}{\beta}$$

If we know "$P \Rightarrow Q$" (if P then Q) and "$P$" is true, we can conclude "$Q$" must be true.

The Resolution Rule is more powerful and forms the basis of automated theorem proving:

$$\frac{\ell_1 \vee \cdots \vee \ell_k, \quad m_1 \vee \cdots \vee m_n}{\ell_1 \vee \cdots \vee \ell_{i-1} \vee \ell_{i+1} \vee \cdots \vee \ell_k \vee m_1 \vee \cdots \vee m_{j-1} \vee m_{j+1} \vee \cdots \vee m_n}$$

Where $\ell_i$ and $m_j$ are complementary literals (one is the negation of the other). Resolution says: if two disjunctions contain opposite literals, we can combine them by removing those literals.

Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)

To use resolution, formulas must be in Conjunctive Normal Form — a conjunction of disjunctions, like $(A \vee B) \wedge (\neg C \vee D)$.

4-Step CNF Conversion:

  1. Eliminate $\Leftrightarrow$: Replace $\alpha \Leftrightarrow \beta$ with $(\alpha \Rightarrow \beta) \wedge (\beta \Rightarrow \alpha)$
  2. Eliminate $\Rightarrow$: Replace $\alpha \Rightarrow \beta$ with $\neg\alpha \vee \beta$
  3. Move $\neg$ inward: Use De Morgan's laws to push negations down to atoms
  4. Distribute $\vee$ over $\wedge$: Convert to clauses joined by $\wedge$

Key Logical Equivalences

De Morgan's Laws:

$$\neg(\alpha \wedge \beta) \equiv (\neg\alpha \vee \neg\beta)$$ $$\neg(\alpha \vee \beta) \equiv (\neg\alpha \wedge \neg\beta)$$

Implication Elimination:

$$(\alpha \Rightarrow \beta) \equiv (\neg\alpha \vee \beta)$$

Contraposition:

$$(\alpha \Rightarrow \beta) \equiv (\neg\beta \Rightarrow \neg\alpha)$$

Double Negation:

$$\neg\neg\alpha \equiv \alpha$$

Refutation and Proof by Contradiction

A powerful technique: to prove $KB \models \alpha$ (KB entails α), show that $KB \wedge \neg\alpha$ is unsatisfiable (has no models). This is called proof by refutation.

$$KB \models \alpha \text{ iff } (KB \wedge \neg\alpha) \text{ is unsatisfiable}$$

Complexity of Inference

Truth-Table Enumeration (TT-ENTAILS): Tests all $2^n$ possible truth assignments for n propositions. Time complexity $O(2^n)$, space complexity $O(n)$. Infeasible for large KB.

Forward Chaining on Horn Clauses: Restricted to Horn clauses (at most one positive literal per clause). Runs in linear time $O(n)$ where n is the size of the KB. Much more efficient.

DPLL Algorithm: Uses backtracking with early termination, pure symbol heuristic, and unit propagation. Solves many practical SAT problems efficiently.

Completeness: A sound and complete inference procedure is one that derives all and only the entailments of the KB. Resolution with refutation is both sound and complete for propositional logic.

Clean-Code Implementation

1. Truth-Table Enumeration (TT-ENTAILS?)

Brute-force algorithm that enumerates all possible truth assignments and checks whether KB entails α.

Python: Truth-Table Enumeration
def tt_entails(kb, alpha):
    """
    Check if KB entails alpha using truth-table enumeration.

    Args:
        kb: Knowledge base (list of sentences)
        alpha: Query sentence

    Returns:
        True if KB ⊨ alpha, False otherwise

    Complexity: O(2^n) time, O(n) space where n = number of propositions
    """
    # Extract all symbols from KB and alpha
    symbols = extract_symbols(kb + [alpha])

    # Try all 2^n truth assignments
    return tt_check_all(kb, alpha, symbols, {})

def tt_check_all(kb, alpha, symbols, assignment):
    """
    Recursively enumerate truth assignments and check entailment.
    """
    if not symbols:
        # Base case: all symbols assigned
        # Check if KB being true implies alpha is true
        kb_value = all(evaluate(sentence, assignment) for sentence in kb)
        alpha_value = evaluate(alpha, assignment)

        if kb_value and not alpha_value:
            # Found a model where KB is true but alpha is false
            return False
        return True

    # Pick first symbol and try both values
    symbol = symbols[0]
    rest = symbols[1:]

    # Try symbol = True
    assignment[symbol] = True
    if not tt_check_all(kb, alpha, rest, assignment):
        return False

    # Try symbol = False
    assignment[symbol] = False
    return tt_check_all(kb, alpha, rest, assignment)

def evaluate(sentence, assignment):
    """Evaluate a sentence given truth assignments."""
    if isinstance(sentence, bool):
        return sentence
    if isinstance(sentence, str):
        return assignment.get(sentence, False)
    if sentence[0] == 'NOT':
        return not evaluate(sentence[1], assignment)
    if sentence[0] == 'AND':
        return all(evaluate(s, assignment) for s in sentence[1:])
    if sentence[0] == 'OR':
        return any(evaluate(s, assignment) for s in sentence[1:])
    return False

2. Resolution Algorithm (PL-RESOLUTION)

Inference by refutation. Converts KB ∧ ¬α to CNF, then resolves clauses until empty clause is found (unsatisfiable) or no new clauses can be derived (satisfiable).

Python: Resolution Theorem Proving
def pl_resolution(kb, alpha):
    """
    Prove KB ⊨ alpha using resolution (proof by refutation).

    Args:
        kb: Knowledge base (list of CNF clauses)
        alpha: Query sentence

    Returns:
        True if KB ⊨ alpha (alpha is entailed)
        False otherwise

    Complexity: Exponential worst-case, but often fast in practice
    """
    # Convert KB ∧ ¬alpha to CNF clauses
    clauses = [convert_to_cnf(s) for s in kb]
    clauses.append(negate(alpha))
    clauses = flatten_cnf(clauses)  # Collect into single list

    new = set()
    while True:
        # Try to resolve every pair of clauses
        clause_list = list(set(clauses) | new)

        for i in range(len(clause_list)):
            for j in range(i + 1, len(clause_list)):
                # Attempt resolution
                resolved = resolve(clause_list[i], clause_list[j])

                if resolved is not None:
                    # Check if we derived the empty clause (contradiction)
                    if len(resolved) == 0:
                        return True  # KB entails alpha
                    new.add(frozenset(resolved))

        # If no new clauses were derived, we're done
        if new.issubset(set(clauses)):
            return False  # Cannot prove alpha

        clauses = list(set(clauses) | new)

def resolve(clause1, clause2):
    """
    Resolve two clauses by finding complementary literals.

    Returns resolved clause or None if no resolution possible.
    """
    # Find complementary literals
    for lit1 in clause1:
        for lit2 in clause2:
            if negate_literal(lit1) == lit2:
                # Found complementary pair, resolve
                resolved = (clause1 - {lit1}) | (clause2 - {lit2})
                return resolved

    return None  # No resolution possible

def negate_literal(lit):
    """Negate a single literal."""
    if isinstance(lit, tuple) and lit[0] == 'NOT':
        return lit[1]
    return ('NOT', lit)

3. Forward Chaining (PL-FC-ENTAILS?)

Efficient inference on Horn clauses (definite clauses). Data-driven: start with known facts and derive new ones until query is proven or no more facts can be derived.

Python: Forward Chaining on Horn Clauses
def pl_fc_entails(kb, q):
    """
    Forward chaining to prove q from KB (Horn clauses only).

    Args:
        kb: List of (premises, conclusion) tuples for Horn clauses
        q: Query atom

    Returns:
        True if q can be derived from KB, False otherwise

    Complexity: O(n) where n = size of KB (linear!)
    """
    # count[c] = number of premises in clause c
    count = {}
    for premises, conclusion in kb:
        count[(premises, conclusion)] = len(premises)

    # inferred[p] = True if p has been derived
    inferred = {atom: False for premises, conclusion in kb
                for atom in premises + [conclusion]}

    # agenda = known facts (atoms with no premises)
    agenda = [atom for premises, conclusion in kb
              if len(premises) == 0 for _ in [conclusion]]

    while agenda:
        p = agenda.pop(0)

        if p == q:
            return True  # Query proved!

        if not inferred[p]:
            inferred[p] = True

            # Check all clauses with p as a premise
            for premises, conclusion in kb:
                if p in premises:
                    count[(premises, conclusion)] -= 1

                    # If all premises satisfied, conclusion can be inferred
                    if count[(premises, conclusion)] == 0:
                        agenda.append(conclusion)

    return False  # Query cannot be derived

def convert_to_horn_clauses(kb):
    """
    Convert KB to Horn clause format: (premises, conclusion)
    premises = list of atoms
    conclusion = single atom
    """
    horn_clauses = []
    for clause in kb:
        # Parse clause and extract premises and conclusion
        # Horn clause: P1 ∧ P2 ∧ ... → C
        if isinstance(clause, tuple) and clause[0] == 'IMPLIES':
            premises = clause[1]
            conclusion = clause[2]
            horn_clauses.append((premises, conclusion))

    return horn_clauses

4. DPLL Algorithm with Heuristics

Backtracking SAT solver with early termination, pure symbol detection, and unit propagation. Much more efficient than truth-table enumeration for satisfiability.

Python: DPLL SAT Solver
def dpll_satisfiable(clauses):
    """
    Determine if a set of clauses is satisfiable using DPLL.

    Args:
        clauses: List of clauses (each clause is a set of literals)

    Returns:
        Assignment dict if satisfiable, False if unsatisfiable

    Heuristics:
    - Early termination: return True if all clauses satisfied
    - Pure symbol: literal appearing only positive or only negative
    - Unit propagation: clause with single literal must be true
    """
    return dpll(clauses, {})

def dpll(clauses, assignment):
    """
    Recursive DPLL procedure.
    """
    # Early termination: all clauses satisfied
    if all(clause_is_true(c, assignment) for c in clauses):
        return assignment

    # Contradiction: empty clause or unsatisfied clause
    if any(len(c) == 0 for c in clauses):
        return False

    # Unit propagation: find unit clauses (single literal)
    for clause in clauses:
        if len(clause) == 1:
            literal = list(clause)[0]
            value = not is_negated(literal)
            symbol = extract_symbol(literal)

            new_assignment = assignment.copy()
            new_assignment[symbol] = value
            new_clauses = simplify_clauses(clauses, symbol, value)

            result = dpll(new_clauses, new_assignment)
            if result is not False:
                return result
            return False

    # Pure symbol heuristic
    pure_symbol = find_pure_symbol(clauses, assignment)
    if pure_symbol:
        symbol, value = pure_symbol
        new_assignment = assignment.copy()
        new_assignment[symbol] = value
        new_clauses = simplify_clauses(clauses, symbol, value)

        result = dpll(new_clauses, new_assignment)
        if result is not False:
            return result
        return False

    # Choose a symbol and try both values
    symbol = choose_symbol(clauses, assignment)

    for value in [True, False]:
        new_assignment = assignment.copy()
        new_assignment[symbol] = value
        new_clauses = simplify_clauses(clauses, symbol, value)

        result = dpll(new_clauses, new_assignment)
        if result is not False:
            return result

    return False

def find_pure_symbol(clauses, assignment):
    """
    Find a symbol appearing only positive or only negative.
    Returns (symbol, value) or None.
    """
    symbols = {extract_symbol(lit) for clause in clauses
               for lit in clause if extract_symbol(lit) not in assignment}

    for symbol in symbols:
        positive = any(lit == symbol for clause in clauses for lit in clause)
        negative = any(is_negated(lit) and extract_symbol(lit) == symbol
                      for clause in clauses for lit in clause)

        if positive and not negative:
            return (symbol, True)
        if negative and not positive:
            return (symbol, False)

    return None
Implementation Notes: These implementations show the core ideas. Production systems use optimizations like clause indexing, learned clauses (in SAT solvers), and caching to handle large KB. The key insight: resolution and forward chaining trade off expressiveness for efficiency.

Socratic Sandbox: Common Questions

Q1: "Your TT-ENTAILS algorithm works but takes forever on a KB with 30 proposition symbols. Why?"
▼
Answer: TT-ENTAILS enumerates all $2^n$ models where n = number of propositions. With 30 symbols, that's over 1 billion models. It's a brute-force approach guaranteed to be correct but infeasible for large domains.

Solutions:
  • Use resolution: Resolution can ignore irrelevant symbols and often finds proofs much faster
  • Use forward/backward chaining: On Horn clauses, these run in linear time $O(n)$ where n = KB size
  • Use DPLL: Modern SAT solvers use backtracking with unit propagation, pure symbol heuristics, and learned clauses. They solve many practical instances with thousands of variables
  • Accept incompleteness: Use local search (WalkSAT) to find models quickly, though you can't prove unsatisfiability
Q2: "Your wumpus agent deduces [2,2] is safe, but actually there's a pit there. How is this possible?"
▼
Answer: Logic guarantees: IF the KB is true, then any entailed conclusion is true. But logic can't fix garbage-in-garbage-out. If you TELLed the KB false information, it derives false conclusions.

Common causes:
  • Incorrect percepts: You recorded a percept wrong (e.g., you said "no breeze" when there was one)
  • KB rules don't match reality: The rules you encoded don't capture the actual world (e.g., you said "breeze ↔ adjacent to pit" but breeze can come from other sources)
  • Grounding problem: The symbols in the KB don't correctly represent physical reality. Logic works perfectly in its formal domain, but translating the messy real world into logic is hard

Lesson: A logical agent is only as good as its KB. You must carefully design the sensors, the rules, and the initial facts to match reality.
Q3: "You converted a formula to CNF and it exploded from 10 symbols to 1000 clauses. What happened?"
▼
Answer: Distributing $\vee$ over $\wedge$ in step 4 of CNF conversion can cause exponential blowup.

Example: Consider $(A_1 \wedge B_1) \vee (A_2 \wedge B_2) \vee \cdots \vee (A_n \wedge B_n)$. To convert to CNF, you distribute the outer OR over inner ANDs, creating all combinations of choosing one element from each pair. With n pairs, you get $2^n$ clauses!

Solution: Use the Tseytin transformation. Instead of distributing, introduce auxiliary variables to represent sub-formulas:
Let $y_i = A_i \wedge B_i$
Original: $(y_1 \vee y_2 \vee \cdots \vee y_n)$
Add clauses: $(y_i \Rightarrow (A_i \wedge B_i))$ and $(A_i \Rightarrow y_i), (B_i \Rightarrow y_i)$

This keeps CNF size linear in the original formula size, at the cost of extra variables.
Q4: "Your forward chaining keeps running and never stops. What went wrong?"
▼
Answer: Forward chaining on Horn clauses always terminates because there are finitely many atomic sentences that can be derived. But if you're making a mistake, it could hang.

Check these:
  • Are your clauses really Horn clauses? Horn clauses have at most one positive literal. If you have $(A \vee B) \Rightarrow C$ with multiple disjuncts in the antecedent, it's not a proper Horn clause. Convert it first
  • Infinite loops: If you have rules like $P \Rightarrow Q$ and $Q \Rightarrow P$, forward chaining will derive both, mark both as inferred, and stop (because you track inferred atoms). Make sure your tracking works
  • Bug in implementation: Double-check that you're only processing each atom once, and that you're properly decrementing the count for premises

Debugging: Add print statements to show which atoms are being derived and when. Stop after a fixed number of iterations if you suspect an infinite loop.
Q5: "WalkSAT says it can't find a model for your formula. Does that mean the formula is unsatisfiable?"
▼
Answer: Not necessarily! WalkSAT is a local search algorithm and is incomplete. It can find models but cannot prove unsatisfiability.

What WalkSAT does:
  • Starts with a random truth assignment
  • Repeatedly picks an unsatisfied clause and flips a random variable in it
  • Occasionally (with low probability) makes a random move to escape local minima
  • Returns a model if found, or "couldn't find one" after N iterations

Interpretation of failure: Either (a) the formula is unsatisfiable, or (b) WalkSAT didn't search long enough or got stuck in a bad region.

To prove unsatisfiability: Use DPLL or resolution (complete algorithms). WalkSAT is only reliable when it finds a model.

When to use WalkSAT: For satisfiability testing in applications where you just need a model (not a proof), or for very large instances where DPLL is too slow. Trade completeness for speed.
