Mountain Climbing in the Fog

Imagine you're trying to find the highest peak in a mountain range, but you're lost in thick fog and can only feel the ground right beneath your feet. You have no map. You can't see the overall landscape. All you know is: which direction is uphill right now?

⛰️

Hill Climbing

Always step uphill. Simple! But you might get stuck at a small peak and think you've reached Everest.

🎲

Random Restart

"If at first you don't succeed, teleport to a random spot and try again." Surprisingly effective — 94% of 8-queens problems solved.

🔥❄️

Simulated Annealing

Start by shaking vigorously (accept bad moves), then gradually calm down. Like cooling hot metal into a low-energy crystal.

🧬

Genetic Algorithms

Nature's optimizer! A population breeds — fittest survive, crossover combines parents, mutations introduce novelty.

↓📉

Gradient Descent

Continuous spaces. Follow the slope using calculus. The gradient shows you the steepest direction down.

The Three Problems of Hill Climbing

1. Local Maxima

You reach a small hill (local maximum) and every direction goes down. You're stuck. On the 8-queens problem without sideways moves: 86% failure rate.

2. Ridges & Shoulders

The landscape is like a knife-edge ridge. Moving in any cardinal direction goes down, but the ridge itself leads somewhere good. Hill climbing can't walk the ridge.

3. Plateaus

A flat area where the value doesn't change. Hill climbing refuses to move because no neighbor is strictly better. You're paralyzed on flatland.

Solution: Sideways Moves

Add one line of code: allow moves that don't improve the value, up to a limit (e.g., 100 sideways steps). Why does this help so much? Many "stuck" cases are actually plateaus. Sideways moves let you traverse them and find an edge where you can ascend again.

Result: With sideways moves, 8-queens success rate jumps from 14% to 94%. A tiny change, dramatic improvement.

The Cooling Schedule in Simulated Annealing

Acceptance probability for a worse move: P = e^(ΔE/T)

  • High T: Probability ≈ 1 (accept almost anything, explore wildly)
  • Low T: Probability ≈ 0 (accept almost nothing, greedy like hill climbing)
  • T → 0: Becomes pure hill climbing (exploitation)

The cooling schedule determines everything. Too fast: freezes in local minimum. Too slow: wastes time. Theoretical schedules like T = C / log(t) guarantee convergence but are impractically slow. Practical schedules use geometric cooling: T(t) = T₀ · α^t with α ≈ 0.95–0.99.

Genetic Algorithms: Evolution in Action

A population of candidate solutions "breeds." Each generation:

  1. Selection: Pick parents proportional to fitness (fittest reproduce more)
  2. Crossover: Combine parent genes at a random point
  3. Mutation: Flip bits randomly (introduce novelty)
  4. Repeat: Build new generation

For the 8-queens problem, this is like beam search with sexual reproduction. Crossover can combine good ideas from two parents. Mutation prevents stagnation when the population converges.

Gradient Descent: Calculus for Optimization

In continuous spaces, forget neighbors. Instead, use calculus. The gradient ∇f points in the steepest direction. Follow it downhill (or uphill if maximizing).

Update rule: x ← x + α∇f(x)

The step size α is critical:

  • Too small: convergence is glacially slow
  • Too big: you overshoot and oscillate
  • Adaptive step sizes: Start big, shrink as you approach optimum

Newton-Raphson uses second derivatives (the Hessian matrix) to choose the step size automatically. Faster convergence, but expensive to compute.

Mathematical Ascent

State-Space Landscape

Goal: Find $\text{argmax}_s f(s)$ where $f: \text{States} \to \mathbb{R}$

The "landscape" is high-dimensional: $f(s)$ plotted against all possible states $s$.

Simulated Annealing (Core Equations)

Temperature schedule: $T(t) = \text{schedule}(t)$

Acceptance probability for worse move: $$P(\text{accept}) = e^{\Delta E / T}$$ where $\Delta E = \text{Value}(\text{next}) - \text{Value}(\text{current}) < 0$

As $T \to 0$, probability $\to 0$ (pure exploitation).

Boltzmann Distribution: If cooling schedule is slow enough, the probability of being in state $s$ approaches: $$P(s) = \frac{e^{f(s)/T}}{Z}$$ where $Z = \sum_s e^{f(s)/T}$ (partition function).

Genetic Algorithms: Selection & Crossover

Population of $k$ individuals.

Fitness-Proportionate Selection: $$P(\text{select } i) = \frac{f(i)}{\sum_j f(j)}$$

Crossover at point $c$: $$\text{child} = \text{parent1}[1:c] + \text{parent2}[c+1:n]$$

Mutation: Each position flipped with probability $\mu$ (typically 0.01).

Gradient-Based Optimization

Gradient (direction of steepest ascent): $$\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right)$$

Gradient Ascent Update: $$\mathbf{x} \leftarrow \mathbf{x} + \alpha \nabla f(\mathbf{x})$$ where $\alpha$ is the step size (learning rate).

Newton-Raphson (2nd-order): $$\mathbf{x} \leftarrow \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{H}_f^{-1}(\mathbf{x}) \nabla f(\mathbf{x})$$ where $\mathbf{H}_f$ is the Hessian matrix of second derivatives.

Belief-State Search (Partial Observability)

Physical state space has $N$ states → Belief-state space has $2^N$ possible belief states.

Nondeterministic Transition: $$b' = \bigcup_{s \in b} \text{RESULTS}_P(s, a)$$

Percept Update (filtering): $$b_o = \{s : o = \text{PERCEPT}(s) \text{ and } s \in \hat{b}\}$$

Search is in belief-space, not physical state space. Solutions are conditional plans (AND-OR trees).

LRTA* (Learning Real-Time A*)

Update heuristic estimate after visiting states: $$H[s] \leftarrow \min_{a \in \text{Actions}(s)} \text{LRTA*-COST}(s, a, \text{result}[s,a], H)$$

where $$\text{LRTA*-COST}(s, a, s', H) = c(s, a, s') + H[s']$$ if $s'$ is known, otherwise $h(s)$ (optimistic: assume direct path to goal).

Key insight: Optimism under uncertainty. Every time you get stuck, raise the estimate for that area, making it less attractive next time.

Clean-Code Implementation

python | hill_climbing.py
import random
import math

def hill_climbing(problem):
    """Steepest-ascent hill climbing.
    Returns a state that is a local maximum.
    Gets stuck 86% of the time on 8-queens without sideways moves."""
    current = problem.initial
    while True:
        neighbor = max(problem.successors(current),
                      key=problem.value, default=None)
        if neighbor is None or problem.value(neighbor) <= problem.value(current):
            return current  # Stuck at local max or plateau
        current = neighbor

def hill_climbing_with_sideways(problem, max_sideways=100):
    """Hill climbing that allows sideways moves on plateaus.
    Raises 8-queens success rate from 14% to 94%."""
    current = problem.initial
    sideways_count = 0
    while True:
        neighbor = max(problem.successors(current),
                      key=problem.value, default=None)
        if neighbor is None:
            return current
        delta = problem.value(neighbor) - problem.value(current)
        if delta > 0:
            current = neighbor
            sideways_count = 0
        elif delta == 0 and sideways_count < max_sideways:
            current = neighbor
            sideways_count += 1
        else:
            return current

def random_restart_hill_climbing(problem, max_restarts=100):
    """Random-restart wrapper. Expected restarts = 1/p where p = success rate.
    For 8-queens: ~7 restarts without sideways, ~1.06 with sideways."""
    best = None
    for _ in range(max_restarts):
        result = hill_climbing_with_sideways(problem)
        if best is None or problem.value(result) > problem.value(best):
            best = result
        if problem.is_goal(result):
            return result
    return best

def simulated_annealing(problem, schedule):
    """Simulated annealing: accepts bad moves with decreasing probability.
    schedule(t) returns temperature T at time t.
    If T=0, return current. If schedule is slow enough, finds global optimum."""
    current = problem.initial
    for t in range(1, 1000000):
        T = schedule(t)
        if T == 0:
            return current
        next_state = random.choice(problem.successors(current))
        delta_e = problem.value(next_state) - problem.value(current)
        if delta_e > 0:
            current = next_state  # Always accept improvements
        elif random.random() < math.exp(delta_e / T):
            current = next_state  # Accept bad moves with probability e^(ΔE/T)
    return current

# Example cooling schedules
def exp_schedule(k=20, lam=0.005):
    """Exponential cooling: T(t) = k * exp(-λt)"""
    return lambda t: k * math.exp(-lam * t)

def geometric_schedule(T0=100, alpha=0.95):
    """Geometric cooling: T(t) = T₀ * α^t (practical, fast)"""
    return lambda t: T0 * (alpha ** t)

def genetic_algorithm(population, fitness_fn, mutate_fn, crossover_fn,
                      generations=1000, mutation_rate=0.01):
    """Genetic algorithm: evolve a population using selection, crossover, mutation.
    Mimics natural selection — fittest individuals reproduce more often."""
    for gen in range(generations):
        # Calculate fitness-proportionate selection weights
        weights = [fitness_fn(individual) for individual in population]
        total = sum(weights)
        if total == 0:
            probs = [1/len(population)] * len(population)
        else:
            probs = [w/total for w in weights]

        new_population = []
        for _ in range(len(population)):
            # Select two parents (fitness-proportionate)
            parent1 = random.choices(population, weights=probs)[0]
            parent2 = random.choices(population, weights=probs)[0]
            # Crossover to produce child
            child = crossover_fn(parent1, parent2)
            # Mutate with small probability
            if random.random() < mutation_rate:
                child = mutate_fn(child)
            new_population.append(child)
        population = new_population

        # Check for solution
        best = max(population, key=fitness_fn)
        if fitness_fn(best) == 28:  # Perfect for 8-queens
            return best
    return max(population, key=fitness_fn)
python | gradient_descent.py
def gradient_descent(f, grad_f, x0, alpha=0.01, iterations=1000):
    """Gradient descent for continuous optimization.
    f: objective function, grad_f: gradient function.
    alpha (step size) is critical: too small = slow, too big = overshoot."""
    x = x0
    for _ in range(iterations):
        gradient = grad_f(x)
        x = [xi + alpha * gi for xi, gi in zip(x, gradient)]  # Ascent
    return x

def newton_raphson(f, grad_f, hessian_f, x0, iterations=100):
    """Newton-Raphson for continuous optimization.
    Uses 2nd derivatives (Hessian) to compute optimal step size.
    Faster convergence, but more expensive."""
    import numpy as np
    x = np.array(x0)
    for _ in range(iterations):
        gradient = np.array(grad_f(x))
        hess = np.array(hessian_f(x))
        # x ← x - H^(-1) * ∇f
        x = x - np.linalg.solve(hess, gradient)
    return x.tolist()

Socratic Sandbox

Click questions to reveal insights. Think before you peek!

▼
A hill-climbing agent solving 8-queens gets stuck 86% of the time. You add sideways moves (limit 100) and suddenly success jumps to 94%. Why?

Many "local maxima" in 8-queens are actually plateaus — flat areas where the objective function (number of non-attacking queens) stays constant across many states.

Pure steepest-ascent refuses to move on a plateau because no neighbor is strictly better. The agent freezes, thinking it's stuck, when actually a few steps sideways would lead to an edge where it can ascend again.

Sideways moves let the agent traverse the plateau. The limit of 100 prevents infinite wandering on truly flat local maxima. This single modification transforms most "stuck" cases into solvable ones.

Lesson: Small implementation changes can have dramatic effects when the problem structure matches the fix.

▼
A simulated annealing agent optimizing circuit layouts accepts terrible designs early but gets trapped in bad local minima later. The engineer says it's no better than random search. What went wrong?

The cooling schedule is wrong. This is the most common mistake in simulated annealing.

If temperature drops too fast, the algorithm doesn't have enough time to escape local minima before it "freezes" into pure hill climbing. If temperature stays too high too long, it's just random walking without improvement.

The key insight: the schedule must be "slow enough" for the Boltzmann distribution $P(s) \propto e^{f(s)/T}$ to sample increasingly good states as T decreases.

  • Theoretical schedule: $T(t) = C / \log(t)$ (slow, guaranteed convergence)
  • Practical schedule: $T(t) = T_0 \cdot \alpha^t$ with $\alpha \approx 0.95–0.99$ (geometric cooling)

Lesson: Cooling schedule is the control knob. Tune it as carefully as learning rate in neural networks.

▼
A genetic algorithm for antenna design keeps converging to mediocre solutions. After 50 generations, all individuals look nearly identical. What's happening?

Loss of diversity — the population has converged prematurely to a local optimum. Everyone has the same genes, so crossover can't create novelty.

Likely causes:

  1. Selection pressure too high (only top 10% breed)
  2. Mutation rate too low (no new genetic material)
  3. Population too small (not enough variation to begin with)

Fixes:

  • Increase mutation rate from 0.01 to 0.05–0.1
  • Use tournament selection (weaker than fitness-proportionate)
  • Introduce elitism (keep top 2 individuals) while allowing more random ones
  • Double population size

Lesson: Exploration vs. exploitation trade-off is fundamental. You need diversity for innovation.

▼
An agent in the erratic vacuum world executes [Suck, Right, Suck]. Sometimes it works; sometimes dirt reappears. Why can't we use a standard search algorithm?

In a nondeterministic environment, the same action can produce different outcomes. Suck might clean the square AND the adjacent square, or deposit dirt on a clean square, depending on environment whims.

A simple action sequence assumes deterministic outcomes: "do A, then B, then C, and I'll be done." Not true here.

Solution: Conditional Plans (AND-OR Trees)

  • OR nodes = agent's choices (which action to execute)
  • AND nodes = environment's possible responses (which outcome occurred)
  • The plan branches on observations

Example: [Suck, if Status=5 then [Right, Suck] else []] — handle both cases.

Lesson: Contingency planning is required when nature can surprise you.

▼
A robot in an unknown maze uses heuristic search (Manhattan distance) but after 500 steps is still circling the start. What should it use?

The robot needs LRTA* (Learning Real-Time A*). Standard A* assumes a complete map; the robot doesn't have one.

The key insight: Optimism under uncertainty. Assume untried actions lead directly to the goal (cost = $h(s)$, the heuristic estimate). After actually trying actions, update your cost estimate:

H[s] ← mina [c(s,a,s') + H[s']]

This "flattens out" local minima over time. Each time you get stuck in the same area, you raise its cost estimate, making it less attractive. Eventually, all estimates converge to true costs, and the robot finds the exit.

The circling problem happens because a naive online DFS has no memory of past cost estimates. LRTA* learns from failure.

Lesson: Real-time agents learn by interacting with their environment. Past mistakes inform future plans.
