Chapter 20: Knowledge in Learning

Explanation-Based Learning: How Prior Knowledge Dramatically Reduces the Examples Needed to Learn

← Previous: Learning from Examples Next: Robotics →

Section 1: Feynman Mental Model — Learning from Explanation

The Hot Stove Parable: A child touches a hot stove and learns "don't touch hot things." But a smart child doesn't need to touch EVERY hot thing — they EXPLAIN why it hurt: "heat causes burns" and generalize: avoid all hot surfaces. This is explanation-based learning. Instead of needing hundreds of examples like a decision tree, EBL uses ONE example plus BACKGROUND KNOWLEDGE to learn a general rule. The key insight: prior knowledge dramatically reduces the examples needed.

Why Pure Inductive Learning is Slow

In Chapter 19, we learned that inductive learning algorithms (decision trees, neural networks) extract patterns from DATA. But they ignore something crucial: what you already know. A decision tree learning "what's a good coffee" must see hundreds of positive and negative examples before it infers that "dark roast + hot water = good coffee." But YOU don't need 100 examples — you understand PHYSICS (hot liquid extracts flavors), CHEMISTRY (caffeine dissolves), and BIOLOGY (caffeine stimulates). With this knowledge, ONE example teaches you the rule.

Explanation-Based Learning: The Core Idea

Explanation-Based Learning (EBL) is the process of learning a GENERAL RULE from a SINGLE EXAMPLE by using a DOMAIN THEORY to explain why that example works. The algorithm has three steps:

  1. Explain: Use your background knowledge to prove why the example satisfies the target concept.
  2. Generalize: Abstract the proof by replacing specific constants with variables.
  3. Learn: The generalized proof becomes a new rule in your knowledge base.

Example: Learning to Stack Blocks

Suppose your domain theory includes:

  • SafeToStack(X, Y) ← Lighter(X, Y) ∧ Stable(Y)
  • Lighter(X, Y) ← Weight(X) < Weight(Y)
  • Stable(block_B) [fact]
  • Weight(block_A) = 1 kg, Weight(block_B) = 5 kg [facts]

Your teacher shows you ONE example: "block_A is safe to stack on block_B." Your algorithm:

  1. Explains: Proves SafeToStack(block_A, block_B) is true by proving Lighter(block_A, block_B) [1 < 5 ✓] and Stable(block_B) ✓.
  2. Generalizes: Replaces specific weights with variables: SafeToStack(X, Y) ← Lighter(X, Y) ∧ Stable(Y).
  3. Learns: Now you can safely stack any X on any Y where X is lighter and Y is stable.

This ONE example generalizes to INFINITE cases. A decision tree would need to see thousands of examples (block_A on block_B, block_C on block_D, etc.) to learn the same rule.

Current-Best-Hypothesis Learning

In EBL, we maintain a CURRENT-BEST-HYPOTHESIS: the most general rule consistent with all observed examples. When a new example arrives, we check if our current hypothesis explains it. If not, we generalize further. This is more efficient than version spaces (which maintain ALL consistent hypotheses).

Version Spaces: The Set of Consistent Hypotheses

A version space is the set of all hypotheses in your hypothesis space that are consistent with your examples. As you gather more data, the version space shrinks. For example, if you're learning a conjunctive concept (a rule like "AND" of features), the version space has two boundaries:

  • S (most Specific): The smallest rule consistent with all examples.
  • G (most General): The largest rule consistent with all examples.

All hypotheses between S and G are equally valid. When you gather enough examples, S and G converge to a single hypothesis.

Relevance-Based Learning

Not all features in your domain theory are relevant to learning the target. Relevance-based learning uses your domain knowledge to identify which features MATTER. For instance, when learning "safe to stack," the COLOR of the block is irrelevant, but WEIGHT and STABILITY are crucial. By pruning irrelevant features from your explanation, you learn more efficient rules and generalize better.

Knowledge Compilation: From General Rules to Special Cases

Knowledge compilation is the process of transforming general, slow-to-apply rules into specialized, fast-to-apply rules. For instance, instead of proving "SafeToStack(X, Y) ← Lighter(X, Y) ∧ Stable(Y)" every time, you "compile" common cases into lookup tables: "SafeToStack(blockA, blockB): true". This trades memory for speed — a common pattern in intelligent systems.

Inductive Logic Programming (ILP)

ILP extends EBL to learn first-order logic rules. Instead of learning rules about specific objects (block_A), ILP learns rules with variables and quantifiers: "Grandparent(X, Z) ← Parent(X, Y) ∧ Parent(Y, Z)". This is powerful because it captures relational structure that decision trees cannot represent.

Section 2: Mathematical Ascent

Hypothesis Spaces and Version Spaces

Formally, a version space is defined as:

$$VS = \{h \in H : \text{Consistent}(h, \text{Examples})\}$$

where $H$ is your hypothesis space, and $\text{Consistent}(h, \text{Examples})$ is true if hypothesis $h$ correctly predicts all examples. The smaller your version space, the closer you are to the true hypothesis.

EBL Generalization Process

Given:

  • A domain theory $B$ (your background knowledge)
  • An example $(x, f(x))$ where $f(x)$ is the target label

The EBL process:

  1. Explain: Construct a proof $\pi$ such that $B \vdash f(x)$ (the domain theory proves the example is correct).
  2. Generalize: Replace constants in the proof with variables. The generalized proof becomes a rule: $\text{Target}(X) \leftarrow L_1(X) \wedge L_2(X) \wedge \ldots$ where $X$ is the variable replacing the constant $x$, and $L_i$ are literals (conditions) from the proof.
  3. Learn: Add this rule to your domain theory.

Bayesian View: How Prior Knowledge Reduces Examples

By Bayes' theorem:

$$P(h|d) \propto P(d|h) P(h)$$

Here, $P(h)$ is your prior belief in hypothesis $h$ (your domain knowledge), and $P(d|h)$ is the likelihood of observing data $d$ under hypothesis $h$.

  • With a WEAK prior ($P(h)$ uniform), you need many examples $d$ to update your posterior belief significantly.
  • With a STRONG prior (domain knowledge concentrating probability on plausible hypotheses), a few examples suffice because $P(h)$ is already high for the correct hypothesis.

PAC Learning with Prior Knowledge

The PAC (Probably Approximately Correct) learning bound is:

$$N \geq \frac{1}{\epsilon}\left(\ln\frac{|H_{\text{effective}}|}{\delta}\right)$$

where:

  • $N$ = number of examples needed
  • $\epsilon$ = error tolerance
  • $\delta$ = failure probability
  • $|H_{\text{effective}}|$ = effective size of hypothesis space after applying domain knowledge

The key insight: when prior knowledge constrains the hypothesis space, $|H_{\text{effective}}| \ll |H|$ (effective space is much smaller than the full space). Thus, $N$ decreases dramatically. Pure inductive learning uses the full space $|H|$; EBL uses only the plausible subset $|H_{\text{effective}}|$.

Inductive Logic Programming: Learning Clauses

ILP learns horn clauses of the form:

$$\text{Target}(x) \leftarrow L_1(x) \wedge L_2(x) \wedge \ldots \wedge L_n(x)$$

where each $L_i(x)$ is a literal (a predicate applied to variables). For instance:

  • $\text{Grandparent}(X, Z) \leftarrow \text{Parent}(X, Y) \wedge \text{Parent}(Y, Z)$
  • $\text{Ancestor}(X, Y) \leftarrow \text{Parent}(X, Y)$
  • $\text{Ancestor}(X, Z) \leftarrow \text{Parent}(X, Y) \wedge \text{Ancestor}(Y, Z)$

ILP algorithms search through the space of possible clauses, using examples to guide the search toward clauses that are true for positive examples and false for negative examples.

Section 3: Clean-Code Implementation

Here is a clean, self-contained Python implementation of Explanation-Based Learning: 

class ExplanationBasedLearner:
    """
    EBL: Learn general rules from a single example + domain theory.
    The rational agent uses its knowledge of HOW things work to
    generalize from one experience, rather than needing thousands.
    """
    def __init__(self, domain_theory):
        """
        Initialize with a domain theory.

        domain_theory: dict mapping consequence -> list of rules.
        Example:
            {
                'SafeToStack': [
                    {'conditions': ['Lighter', 'Stable'], 'name': 'rule_1'}
                ]
            }
        """
        self.theory = domain_theory
        self.learned_rules = []

    def explain(self, example, goal):
        """
        Build an explanation: prove why the example satisfies the goal.

        Args:
            example: dict of facts, e.g., {'Weight_A': 1, 'Weight_B': 5, 'Stable_B': True}
            goal: string, the predicate to prove, e.g., 'SafeToStack_A_B'

        Returns:
            proof: dict representing the proof tree, or None if unprovable.
        """
        proof = self._prove(goal, example, [])
        return proof

    def _prove(self, goal, facts, path):
        """Recursively prove a goal using the domain theory."""
        # Base case: if goal is in facts, we have a proof
        if goal in facts:
            return {
                'goal': goal,
                'type': 'fact',
                'path': path,
                'value': facts[goal]
            }

        # Recursive case: try to prove goal using rules
        if goal.split('_')[0] in self.theory:
            predicate = goal.split('_')[0]
            for rule in self.theory[predicate]:
                subproofs = []
                success = True

                # Try to prove all conditions in the rule
                for condition in rule['conditions']:
                    # Replace placeholders with actual arguments from goal
                    bound_condition = self._bind_condition(condition, goal, facts)
                    subproof = self._prove(bound_condition, facts, path + [goal])

                    if subproof is None:
                        success = False
                        break
                    subproofs.append(subproof)

                if success:
                    return {
                        'goal': goal,
                        'type': 'rule',
                        'rule_name': rule['name'],
                        'conditions': rule['conditions'],
                        'subproofs': subproofs
                    }

        return None

    def _bind_condition(self, condition, goal, facts):
        """Bind a condition to facts from the goal."""
        # Simple binding: match predicate arguments
        goal_args = goal.split('_')[1:]
        condition_args = condition.split('_')[1:]

        # For simplicity, assume order matches
        bound = condition.split('_')[0] + '_' + '_'.join(goal_args[:len(condition_args)])
        return bound

    def generalize(self, proof):
        """
        Generalize a proof by replacing constants with variables.

        Args:
            proof: dict, the proof tree from explain()

        Returns:
            generalized_rule: dict with conclusion and conditions (as variables)
        """
        if proof is None:
            return None

        # Extract the leaf conditions (facts) from the proof
        leaves = self._extract_leaves(proof)

        # Replace specific constants with variables
        # For simplicity, we'll assume variables are introduced based on predicate structure
        general_conditions = []
        for leaf in leaves:
            # Extract predicate name and generalize arguments to variables
            parts = leaf.split('_')
            predicate = parts[0]
            # Represent as: Predicate(X, Y) where X, Y are generic variables
            general_conditions.append(f"{predicate}(*args)")

        # Create generalized rule
        goal_predicate = proof['goal'].split('_')[0]
        generalized_rule = {
            'conclusion': f"{goal_predicate}(X, Y)",
            'conditions': general_conditions,
            'original_proof': proof
        }

        self.learned_rules.append(generalized_rule)
        return generalized_rule

    def _extract_leaves(self, proof):
        """Extract leaf nodes (facts) from the proof tree."""
        if proof['type'] == 'fact':
            return [proof['goal']]

        leaves = []
        if 'subproofs' in proof:
            for subproof in proof['subproofs']:
                leaves.extend(self._extract_leaves(subproof))
        return leaves

    def print_proof(self, proof, indent=0):
        """Pretty-print a proof tree."""
        if proof is None:
            print("  " * indent + "No proof found.")
            return

        if proof['type'] == 'fact':
            print("  " * indent + f"✓ {proof['goal']} (fact)")
        else:
            print("  " * indent + f"✓ {proof['goal']} (via {proof['rule_name']})")
            if 'subproofs' in proof:
                for sp in proof['subproofs']:
                    self.print_proof(sp, indent + 1)


# ========== EXAMPLE USAGE ==========

# Define domain theory
domain_theory = {
    'SafeToStack': [
        {
            'name': 'rule_lighter_stable',
            'conditions': ['Lighter', 'Stable']
        }
    ],
    'Lighter': [
        {
            'name': 'rule_weight_comparison',
            'conditions': []  # Handled by facts
        }
    ]
}

# Create learner
learner = ExplanationBasedLearner(domain_theory)

# Define example: block_A is safe to stack on block_B
example = {
    'Lighter_A_B': True,      # A is lighter than B (Weight_A=1 < Weight_B=5)
    'Stable_B': True,         # B is stable
    'SafeToStack_A_B': True   # A is safe to stack on B
}

# Explain the example
goal = 'SafeToStack_A_B'
proof = learner.explain(example, goal)

# Print and generalize
print("Proof Tree:")
learner.print_proof(proof)

generalized = learner.generalize(proof)
print("\nGeneralized Rule:")
print(f"  Conclusion: {generalized['conclusion']}")
print(f"  Conditions: {generalized['conditions']}")

print("\nLearned Rules:")
for rule in learner.learned_rules:
    print(f"  {rule}")

Key Design Patterns

  • Modular Proof System: Separation of concern between proving (explain) and generalizing (generalize).
  • Recursive Proof Search: _prove() uses depth-first search with backtracking to explore the proof space.
  • Generalization Strategy: Replaces constants with variables based on the proof structure.
  • Extensible Domain Theory: Easy to add new rules and facts without changing the core algorithm.

Section 4: Socratic Sandbox — Dialogue & Discovery

Scenario: Robot Learning to Make Beverages

A robot learns to make coffee from ONE demonstration. Its domain theory includes knowledge about liquids, containers, heating, and pouring. It successfully generalizes to making tea. But when asked to make a smoothie (which requires blending, not heating), it fails.

1.Explanation and Generalization

The robot successfully generalized from coffee to tea. What parts of its explanation were general enough to transfer? What specific constants (like "coffee beans" or "hot water") were replaced with variables (like "flavor compound" or "hot liquid")? How did this abstraction enable transfer?

2.Knowledge Gaps and Domain Theory

The robot failed on smoothies because its domain theory lacked the concept of BLENDING. What was missing from its knowledge? What rule would need to be added: "MakeBeverage(X) ← CombineIngredients(X) ∧ ApplyProcess(X)" where BLENDING is a type of ApplyProcess? How does this reveal EBL's fundamental limitation: it can only generalize within the scope of its domain theory?

3.EBL vs. Decision Trees: Sample Complexity

Compare this to a decision tree approach: how many coffee/tea/smoothie examples would a decision tree need to learn the same task? What's the tradeoff? The decision tree might eventually learn "heated liquid makes coffee/tea, but blended mixture makes smoothie" — but it needs 50+ examples. EBL generalizes with 1 example + domain knowledge. When would you prefer a decision tree to EBL?

4.Version Spaces and Hypothesis Collapse

Version spaces maintain ALL consistent hypotheses. If after 3 examples, the version space contains 50 hypotheses, what does that tell us? When might the version space NEVER collapse to a single hypothesis, even with infinite examples? (Hint: think about irrelevant features.)

5.Relational Learning with ILP

ILP can learn rules like "Grandparent(X,Z) ← Parent(X,Y) ∧ Parent(Y,Z)" from examples. Why can't a standard decision tree learn such relational rules? Decision trees operate on ATTRIBUTE-VALUE pairs (e.g., Age=5, Color=blue), not on RELATIONS between objects (e.g., Parent(Alice, Bob)). What representational power does ILP gain by working with first-order logic?
