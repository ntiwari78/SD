Beyond Text: Vision, Speech & Multimodal Transformers

From Vision Transformers to wav2vec 2.0: Teaching machines to see, hear, and understand multiple modalities simultaneously

⚡ Level 1: The Feynman Bridge (Zero Jargon)

Let's start by understanding how transformers process vision, speech, and video—modalities beyond pure text.

Analogy: A Museum Visit

Imagine you're at a museum viewing an impressionist painting. Your experience involves three components:

  • Vision: Your eyes scan the painting, piece by piece. You notice colors, brushstrokes, composition.
  • Speech: An audio guide explains the artist's technique, historical context, and meaning. Information flows sequentially.
  • Multimodal Understanding: Your brain connects what you see with what you hear—the guide explains a technique, and you look back at the painting to observe it. Both modalities reinforce each other.

Transformers can do all three: Transformers can now process images directly (ViT), understand speech (wav2vec 2.0), and combine multiple modalities (ViLBERT). They've moved beyond being text-only models.

🧠 Key Intuitions

  • Vision Transformer (ViT): "Cut photos into puzzle pieces and read them like sentences." Break an image into 16×16 patches, flatten each to a vector, embed it—now you have a sequence.
  • wav2vec 2.0: "Learn speech patterns by filling in missing audio, like a hearing version of Mad Libs." Mask random audio chunks, predict the masked signal.
  • HuBERT: "Cluster audio into pseudo-phonemes, then teach the model to predict them." Self-supervised learning without manual labels.
  • TimeSformer: "Video is just stacked frames. Pay attention to what changes over time." Use divided space-time attention to process efficiently.
  • ViLBERT: "Give transformers two eyes and ears: one stream for images, one for text. Make them talk to each other." Two-stream architecture with co-attention.
  • Decision Transformer: "Reframe reinforcement learning as sequence modeling. Condition on 'return-to-go' to control behavior quality."

Vision Transformer (ViT): "Pictures as Sequences"

Patch Embedding and Tokenization

Problem: Images are 2D grids (H, W, C). Transformers expect 1D sequences of tokens.

Solution: Split image into non-overlapping P×P patches and embed each patch as a token.

Patch Extraction:

$$x_p \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times (P^2 \cdot C)}$$

where N = (H/P)(W/P) is the number of patches, P is patch size, C is channels (3 for RGB).

Linear Projection + Class Token:

$$z_0 = [x_{\text{cls}} ; x_p^1 E ; x_p^2 E ; \ldots ; x_p^N E] + E_{\text{pos}}$$

where E ∈ ℝ^{P²C × D} projects patches to embedding dimension D, E_pos ∈ ℝ^{(N+1) × D} are learnable positional embeddings, x_cls is a special class token (like BERT's [CLS]).

Example (224×224 image, P=16, D=768):

  • Number of patches: (224/16)² = 196
  • Patch dimension: 16² × 3 = 768 (perfect for linear projection!)
  • Sequence length: 196 + 1 (CLS) = 197 tokens
  • Processing: Standard Transformer encoder on these 197 tokens
  • Classification: Use the CLS token's final representation as image embedding

ViT Architecture Details

Encoder: Standard transformer encoder (no decoder), L layers of multi-head attention + feedforward.

Pre-norm variant (more stable training):

$$z_\ell' = z_{\ell-1} + \text{MultiHeadAttn}(\text{LayerNorm}(z_{\ell-1}))$$
$$z_\ell = z_\ell' + \text{MLP}(\text{LayerNorm}(z_\ell'))$$

Classification head:

$$y = \text{Linear}(\text{LayerNorm}(z_\ell^0))$$

Takes only the class token (position 0) at the final layer.

ViT-Base specs: 12 layers, d=768, 12 heads, d_ff=3072, ~86M parameters.

ViT-Large specs: 24 layers, d=1024, 16 heads, d_ff=4096, ~304M parameters.

wav2vec 2.0: Self-Supervised Speech Representation Learning

Architecture: CNN Encoder → Transformer Context Network

Problem: Speech is a waveform (1D audio signal). Transformers need discrete tokens or meaningful frames.

Solution: CNN encodes raw audio into acoustic features, then Transformer learns context.

Feature Extraction (CNN Encoder):

$$f_t = \text{CNN}(x_{t})$$

7 convolutional blocks, stride = 2 per block → 16kHz audio downsampled to 49Hz (320× compression)

Quantization (Discrete Representation):

To enable contrastive learning, apply vector quantization with Gumbel-Softmax:

$$q_t = \arg\max_k \mathbf{e}_k \cdot f_t$$

where e_k are learned codebook entries. G codebooks, V entries each: G = 320, V = 320 → 102,400 possible discrete codes.

Context Network (Transformer):

Bidirectional transformer processes quantized feature frames:

$$c_t = \text{Transformer}(q_t)$$

Contrastive Loss:

For masked time steps, predict the quantized target from context:

$$L = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(c_t, q_t) / \kappa)}{\sum_{\tilde{q}} \exp(\text{sim}(c_t, \tilde{q}) / \kappa)}$$

where κ is temperature, and negative samples are other codebook entries. 100 negatives per positive.

Diversity Loss: Encourage all codebook entries to be used:

$$L_{\text{div}} = -\frac{1}{G \cdot V} \sum_g \sum_v \log p_g(v)$$

Each codebook should have uniform usage. Combined with α = 0.1 weight.

HuBERT: Masked Prediction with Pseudo-Labels

Key Innovation: Use k-means clustering to generate pseudo-labels instead of learning quantization end-to-end.

Offline Clustering: First iteration clusters MFCC features (traditional acoustic features) to get pseudo-labels.

$$c_t = k\text{-means}(\text{MFCC}_t)$$

Masked Prediction Loss:

$$L = \sum_{t \in \mathcal{M}} -\log p(c_t | x^{\text{masked}}, t)$$

where x^masked has 50% of frames randomly masked, and p is a softmax over cluster classes.

Iterative Refinement: In later iterations, re-cluster on intermediate layer representations. More abstract clusters emerge.

  • Iteration 0: Cluster on MFCC (acoustic)
  • Iteration 1: Cluster on layer 6 output (mid-level)
  • Iteration 2: Cluster on layer 12 output (semantic)

Result: 316M parameters, ~10 days on 80 V100 GPUs. Achieves 1.9% WER on LibriSpeech test-clean (competitive with supervised).

ViLBERT: Vision-and-Language BERT with Co-Attention

Two-Stream Architecture with Co-Attention

Problem: How do you process both images and text in a single model? Naive concatenation wastes computation.

Solution: Two separate transformer streams (visual, linguistic) that attend to each other.

Visual Stream (from regions):

$$\mathbf{h}_v = [h_v^{(0)}, h_v^{(1)}, \ldots, h_v^{(R)}]$$

R visual regions extracted by Faster R-CNN detector (101 regions typical), each region → vector via RoI pooling.

Linguistic Stream (from text):

$$\mathbf{h}_l = [h_l^{(0)}, h_l^{(1)}, \ldots, h_l^{(T)}]$$

T text tokens from standard BERT tokenizer.

Co-Transformer Attention (Co-TRM):

$$h_v^{\ell} = \text{Co-TRM}(q_v, K_l^{\ell-1}, V_l^{\ell-1})$$
$$h_l^{\ell} = \text{Co-TRM}(q_l, K_v^{\ell-1}, V_v^{\ell-1})$$

Visual queries attend to linguistic keys/values, and vice versa. Cross-attention between modalities.

Within-stream Self-Attention: Each stream also has self-attention on its own modality (intra-attention).

Pre-training Objectives (VILBERT uses both):

  • MLM (Masked Language Modeling): Mask 15% of text tokens, predict them
  • MRFR (Masked Region Feature Regression): Mask 15% of visual regions, predict region features from Faster R-CNN
  • Alignment: Align visual regions to text tokens (weak supervision)

TimeSformer: Video Understanding with Divided Space-Time Attention

Efficient Video Processing

Problem: Video = sequence of frames. Using standard ViT on all frames at once: O(N·S·T)² where S=spatial patches, T=temporal frames. For 30fps × 2 sec = 60 frames, 196 patches each = 11,760 positions. Attention: ~138M operations per layer!

Solution: Divided Space-Time Attention

Decompose into two separate attention operations, avoiding the full joint space-time matrix:

$$\text{Temporal:} \quad \text{O}(N \cdot T)$$
$$\text{Spatial:} \quad \text{O}(N \cdot S)$$
$$\text{Total:} \quad \text{O}(N \cdot S + N \cdot T) \text{ instead of } \text{O}((N \cdot S \cdot T)^2)$$

For the same 11,760 positions: 11,760 × 60 + 11,760 × 196 ≈ 2.4M operations vs 138M!

Temporal Attention: Each spatial position attends across time (how does this part move?)

$$\text{Input: }(S, T, D) \rightarrow \text{Reshape: }(S, T, D) \rightarrow \text{Attn: }(S, T, T) \rightarrow \text{Output: }(S, T, D)$$

Spatial Attention: Each frame's patches attend to each other (intra-frame relationships)

$$\text{Input: }(S, T, D) \rightarrow \text{Reshape: }(T, S, D) \rightarrow \text{Attn: }(S, S) \rightarrow \text{Output: }(T, S, D)$$

Comparison of Variants (from paper):

  • Space only (no temporal): 67.4% accuracy, fast
  • Joint space-time: 75.9% accuracy, very slow
  • Divided space-time (our method): 75.5% accuracy, 3× faster than joint
  • Sparse local + global: 75.1%, similar speed to divided

Decision Transformer: Reinforcement Learning as Sequence Modeling

Problem: Rethinking RL

Traditional RL: Train a policy π(a|s) via temporal difference learning, Bellman equations, value functions, etc. Requires exploration (trial and error).

Decision Transformer Insight: Frame RL as a supervised sequence modeling problem. No Bellman equations, no value functions.

Input Sequence (interleaved return, state, action):

$$R̂_1, s_1, a_1, R̂_2, s_2, a_2, \ldots, R̂_t, s_t, a_t$$

where R̂_t = Σ_{t'=t}^{T} γ^{t'-t} r_{t'} is the return-to-go (future discounted rewards from time t onward).

Prediction Objective: Given history up to a_t, predict the next return and action.

$$p(R̂_{t+1}, s_{t+1}, a_{t+1} | R̂_1, s_1, a_1, \ldots, a_t)$$

At Inference: Condition on desired return R̂_target to control behavior. High return → high-quality trajectory. Low return → exploratory/baseline.

Architecture: Standard GPT-2 backbone with context window of K timesteps (K=20 typical).

Key Advantage: Scale to offline RL without exploration. Learn from fixed replay buffer. No environmental interactions needed at test time.

💾 Code Block 1: Vision Transformer Patch Embedding
import torch import torch.nn as nn class PatchEmbedding(nn.Module): """ViT Patch Embedding - converts image to sequence of patch tokens""" def __init__(self, img_size=224, patch_size=16, in_channels=3, embed_dim=768): super().__init__() self.num_patches = (img_size // patch_size) ** 2 # Efficient: use Conv2d with kernel=patch_size, stride=patch_size self.proj = nn.Conv2d(in_channels, embed_dim, kernel_size=patch_size, stride=patch_size) # CLS token (like BERT) self.cls_token = nn.Parameter(torch.randn(1, 1, embed_dim)) # Positional embeddings self.pos_embed = nn.Parameter( torch.randn(1, self.num_patches + 1, embed_dim) ) def forward(self, x): B = x.shape[0] # x: (B, 3, 224, 224) x = self.proj(x) # (B, embed_dim, 14, 14) x = x.flatten(2).transpose(1, 2) # (B, 196, embed_dim) cls = self.cls_token.expand(B, -1, -1) x = torch.cat([cls, x], dim=1) # (B, 197, embed_dim) x = x + self.pos_embed # add positional embeddings return x # Usage patch_embed = PatchEmbedding() img = torch.randn(2, 3, 224, 224) tokens = patch_embed(img) print(tokens.shape) # (2, 197, 768)

💡 Key insight: Conv2d with kernel_size=patch_size, stride=patch_size efficiently extracts patches in one operation, rather than reshaping.

💾 Code Block 2: ViT Image Classification with HuggingFace
from transformers import ViTForImageClassification from transformers import ViTImageProcessor from PIL import Image import torch # Load pretrained ViT (trained on ImageNet-21K, fine-tuned on ImageNet) processor = ViTImageProcessor.from_pretrained( 'google/vit-base-patch16-224-in21k' ) model = ViTForImageClassification.from_pretrained( 'google/vit-base-patch16-224-in21k' ) model.eval() # Load and classify an image image = Image.open("cat.jpg") inputs = processor(images=image, return_tensors="pt") with torch.no_grad(): outputs = model(**inputs) predicted_class_idx = outputs.logits.argmax(-1).item() predicted_class = model.config.id2label[predicted_class_idx] print(f"Predicted: {predicted_class}") # Access attention weights with torch.no_grad(): outputs = model(**inputs, output_attentions=True) attentions = outputs.attentions # 12 layers # Each attention: (batch=1, heads=12, seq_len=197, seq_len=197) print(attentions[0].shape) # (1, 12, 197, 197)

  • Load ViT pre-trained on ImageNet-21K (14M images, 21k classes)
  • Fine-tune the classification head on your custom dataset (e.g., medical imaging)
  • Typically: freeze backbone for 5-10 epochs, then unfreeze and fine-tune with low learning rate
  • Data augmentation crucial: RandAugment, MixUp, CutMix improve robustness
💾 Code Block 3: wav2vec 2.0 Speech Recognition
from transformers import Wav2Vec2ForCTC from transformers import Wav2Vec2Processor import torch import librosa # Load pretrained wav2vec 2.0 fine-tuned on LibriSpeech processor = Wav2Vec2Processor.from_pretrained( "facebook/wav2vec2-base-960h" ) model = Wav2Vec2ForCTC.from_pretrained( "facebook/wav2vec2-base-960h" ) model.eval() # Load audio at 16kHz audio, sr = librosa.load("speech.wav", sr=16000) # Preprocess and encode inputs = processor(audio, sampling_rate=16000, return_tensors="pt", padding=True) # Forward pass: CNN encoder downsamples 16kHz → 50Hz (320× compression) with torch.no_grad(): logits = model(**inputs).logits # CTC decode: greedy (argmax per frame) predicted_ids = torch.argmax(logits, dim=-1) transcription = processor.batch_decode(predicted_ids)[0] print(f"Transcription: {transcription}") # For better accuracy: use beam search decoder (requires ctcdecode package) # Pair with a language model (e.g., KenLM) for re-scoring

wav2vec 2.0 Decoding Options:

  • Greedy (CTC argmax): Pick most likely token per frame. Fast, okay quality.
  • Beam search: Keep top-K hypotheses, more likely to find optimal path.
  • Language model re-scoring: Combine acoustic scores (logits) with language model scores. Reduces errors on OOV/rare words.
💾 Code Block 4: Divided Space-Time Attention for Video
import torch import torch.nn as nn class DividedSpaceTimeAttention(nn.Module): """TimeSformer's efficient video attention""" def __init__(self, dim=768, num_heads=12): super().__init__() self.dim = dim self.num_heads = num_heads self.temporal_attn = nn.MultiheadAttention( dim, num_heads, batch_first=True ) self.spatial_attn = nn.MultiheadAttention( dim, num_heads, batch_first=True ) self.norm1 = nn.LayerNorm(dim) self.norm2 = nn.LayerNorm(dim) def forward(self, x, num_frames, num_spatial_patches): """ x: (B, T*S, D) where T=frames, S=spatial patches per frame """ B, N, D = x.shape # 1. Temporal attention: each spatial position across time x_t = x.view(B, num_frames, num_spatial_patches, D) x_t = x_t.permute(0, 2, 1, 3) # (B, S, T, D) x_t = x_t.reshape(B * num_spatial_patches, num_frames, D) x_t = self.norm1(x_t) temporal_out, _ = self.temporal_attn(x_t, x_t, x_t) temporal_out = temporal_out.reshape(B, num_spatial_patches, num_frames, D) temporal_out = temporal_out.permute(0, 2, 1, 3) # (B, T, S, D) temporal_out = temporal_out.reshape(B, N, D) x = x + temporal_out # 2. Spatial attention: within each frame's patches x_s = x.view(B, num_frames, num_spatial_patches, D) x_s = x_s.reshape(B * num_frames, num_spatial_patches, D) x_s = self.norm2(x_s) spatial_out, _ = self.spatial_attn(x_s, x_s, x_s) spatial_out = spatial_out.reshape(B, num_frames, num_spatial_patches, D) spatial_out = spatial_out.reshape(B, N, D) x = x + spatial_out return x # Example: 8 frames × 196 patches (14×14 from 224×224, patch=16) attn = DividedSpaceTimeAttention() video_tokens = torch.randn(1, 8 * 196, 768) out = attn(video_tokens, num_frames=8, num_spatial_patches=196) print(out.shape) # (1, 1568, 768)

Complexity Analysis:

  • Temporal: (B·S, T, D) → O(B·S·T²) = 1·196·64 = 12K ops/head vs joint (1568)² = 2.5M
  • Spatial: (B·T, S, D) → O(B·T·S²) = 1·8·196² = 308K ops/head
  • Total: 320K ops/head vs 2.5M. ~8× speedup per layer!

🤔 Level 4: Socratic Sandbox (Model Failure Scenarios)

These diagnostic questions reveal common pitfalls in vision, speech, and multimodal transformers.

▼
Q1: You train ViT-Base from scratch on 10,000 medical X-rays. Accuracy is 58%, far below the CNN baseline (82%). Training loss decreases but validation accuracy plateaued at epoch 20. What's the problem?

Answer: Lack of Inductive Bias + Data Insufficiency

ViT lacks the inductive biases that CNNs have built-in: translation equivariance, locality, and hierarchical feature extraction. With only 10K images, ViT can't learn these priors from scratch. CNNs' design encodes them, so they learn faster with less data.

Why this happens:

  • CNNs: Weight sharing (convolution), local receptive fields, pooling → learn efficiently
  • ViT: No architectural priors, learns from raw pixels via attention. Needs 14M+ images to discover what CNNs get "for free"
  • Overfitting: With 10K images, ViT memorizes rather than generalizes

Solutions:

  • Use pretrained ViT: Start from ImageNet-21K (or medical domain pretraining like MIMIC-CXR). Fine-tune on your 10K. This provides inductive bias from pre-training.
  • Hybrid CNN-ViT: Use ResNet backbone + ViT head. CNN extracts local features, ViT does global reasoning.
  • Data augmentation: RandAugment, MixUp, Mosaic. Synthetic data diversity compensates for small dataset.
  • Regularization: DropPath, L2 regularization, early stopping. Prevent overfitting.
  • Increase training data: Collect more X-rays or use medical imaging datasets (CheXpert, MIMIC). ViT benefits from scale.

Key Insight: ViT's lack of inductive bias is a feature (flexibility for different domains) but a bug (needs more data). Pretrained ViT solves this by borrowing inductive bias from ImageNet.

▼
Q2: Your wav2vec 2.0 ASR system gets 3.2% WER on LibriSpeech test-clean but 28.4% WER on Indian English speakers. Management says "just fine-tune more." Why doesn't that work?

Answer: Data Distribution Mismatch

The pretrained wav2vec 2.0 encoder was trained on diverse unlabeled speech, but LibriSpeech is audiobook reading (clear American English). Indian English has different phonetics, prosody, accents. Fine-tuning the CTC head on 100 hours of Indian English won't fix the encoder mismatch.

Why this happens:

  • Self-supervised pretraining learns acoustic patterns from training distribution. Indian English is out-of-distribution.
  • Fine-tuning only updates the classifier (CTC head), not the learned encoder representations.
  • The quantizer and feature extractor are frozen, still optimized for LibriSpeech-like audio.

Solutions:

  • Continual pre-training: Further pre-train the encoder on unlabeled Indian English audio before fine-tuning. Adapts the quantizer/feature extractor.
  • Multilingual/diverse pretraining: Use models pretrained on Multilingual LibriSpeech (60+ languages). Cover Indian English variants.
  • Domain-specific pretraining: If you have 100+ hours of unlabeled Indian English, pre-train on it (w/ contrastive loss). Then fine-tune.
  • Data augmentation on audio: Pitch shift, time stretch, speed perturbation. Synthetic accents.
  • Ensemble: Train separate models on different English variants, ensemble predictions.

Best Practice: Self-supervised speech models need diversity in pretraining. If target accent is significantly different, either use a diverse pretrained model or do continual pretraining on target dialect.

Q3: Your ViLBERT model correctly answers "Is there a dog in the image?" (95% acc) but fails on "Is the dog to the LEFT of the cat?" (52% acc, random). Attention maps show it attends to both objects. Why is spatial reasoning broken?

Answer: Lack of Explicit Spatial Encoding

ViLBERT's co-attention captures object presence (via region features) but not spatial relationships. The regions are from Faster R-CNN (which outputs bounding boxes), but ViLBERT doesn't explicitly encode bbox coordinates into embeddings.

Why this happens:

  • Region features: RoI pooled features (2048-D) capture appearance, not geometry
  • No position encoding: Bounding box coordinates (x, y, w, h) aren't part of region embeddings
  • Spatial relations require explicit reasoning: "left" = x_dog < x_cat, but this is just arithmetic on bboxes

Solutions:

  • Add spatial positional encodings: Encode bbox coordinates as: sine/cosine positional embeddings, then concatenate with region features.
  • Relation embeddings: Learn embeddings for spatial relations (LEFT, ABOVE, OVERLAPS). Condition on relation for queries.
  • Explicit geometry module: Add a small MLP that takes (bbox_dog, bbox_cat) → spatial relation score. Use as auxiliary loss.
  • Scene graphs: Represent image as a graph where nodes=objects, edges=relations. Use graph attention (Graph Transformer) on scene graph.
  • Use models designed for VQA: LXMERT, ALBEF explicitly handle spatial reasoning via region coordinates + spatial embeddings.

Takeaway: Multimodal models struggle with reasoning that requires explicit geometry. Add spatial encodings or relation modules for spatial QA.

▼
Q4: You use TimeSformer to classify 30-second clips at 30fps (900 frames). Even with divided space-time attention, inference takes 45 seconds per clip on V100. You need <2 second latency. How do you solve this?

Answer: Quadratic Complexity + Over-sampling

900 frames × 196 spatial patches = 176,400 tokens. Even with divided attention O(S·T + T·S), each layer processes ~35M attention ops. With 12 layers, that's 420M ops per inference. Add batch processing, memory bandwidth, and you hit latency walls.

Why this is hard:

  • Attention is quadratic in sequence length (even divided, it's 176K per dimension)
  • 30fps sampling is high. Most video models use 8fps (24 frames for 3-sec clip)
  • Transformer memory: 176K tokens × 12 layers × 768-D = huge KV cache

Solutions (ordered by effectiveness):

  • Frame sampling: Reduce 900 frames → 30 frames (every 30th frame). Still covers 30 seconds. 24×196=4,704 tokens. 35× faster.
  • Temporal pooling: Instead of dividing attention, use max/avg pool frames 2×, reducing temporal dimension by 4×.
  • Hierarchical approach: Process 300-frame chunks independently, aggregate via lightweight pooling. Avoid full-video attention.
  • Sparse attention patterns: Local attention (attend to ±8 frames) instead of all frames. O(T) instead of O(T²).
  • Model distillation: Train a smaller TimeSformer (6 layers, d=384) to mimic large model. Trade accuracy for speed.
  • Different architecture: Use 3D CNN (like SlowFast) for videos instead. CNNs are faster than Transformers for video at scale.

Recommended approach: Frame sampling (8fps instead of 30fps) is the biggest win. Most action recognition only needs 8-16fps. Combine with temporal pooling (2×) for 64× total speedup.

Q5: You train a Decision Transformer on Atari with returns 0-2000. At test time, you condition on return-to-go = 5000 (above max training). Agent behaves chaotically, sometimes getting 0 points. Why can't it extrapolate?

Answer: Out-of-Distribution Conditioning + Supervised Learning Limitations

Decision Transformer is trained on pairs (R̂, trajectory). It learns P(trajectory | R̂ ≤ 2000). Conditioning on R̂=5000 is extrapolation beyond training distribution. The model has no experience with such high returns, so it produces unreliable outputs.

Why this fails (unlike traditional RL):

  • Decision Transformer is supervised (behavioral cloning + return conditioning). It learns to mimic high-return trajectories from the dataset.
  • No exploration: At test time, it can't try new actions. It only has the data distribution.
  • Extrapolation: R̂=5000 is outside the training support. The model essentially hallucinátes, producing invalid actions.
  • Traditional RL: Agents explore via epsilon-greedy or action noise. They discover new high-return trajectories at test time.

Solutions:

  • Use only in-distribution returns: Condition on R̂ ∈ [max(data), ∞). For Atari dataset with max=2000, condition on ≤2000.
  • Predict return ceiling: Train a separate regression model to predict max achievable return given initial state. Use that as conditioning cap.
  • Relative return conditioning: Instead of absolute R̂, use (R̂ - baseline) normalized by std(R̂). Keeps values in-distribution.
  • Conservative Q-learning: Instead of DT, use CQL (Conservative Q-Learning) which is more robust to OOD actions.
  • Ensemble with rejection: Train multiple DTs on subsets of data. Ensemble predictions; reject if confidence is low (hallucination indicator).
  • Hybrid approach: Use DT for in-distribution control, fallback to a learned value function for extrapolation.

Key Difference from Traditional RL: Traditional RL agents can explore beyond their training distribution. DT is pure behavior cloning, so it's bound by the data. This is both a feature (safer, no catastrophic OOD failures) and a limitation (no innovation).

Summary: Multimodal Transformers

Vision Transformers (ViT): Transformers don't require inductive biases like convolutions. By converting images to patches, ViT achieves SOTA on ImageNet with pure attention. Requires either large-scale pretraining (ImageNet-21K, 14M images) or domain-specific pretraining.

Speech Models (wav2vec 2.0, HuBERT): Self-supervised learning on unlabeled speech. wav2vec 2.0 uses contrastive learning with quantization. HuBERT uses pseudo-labels from clustering. Both learn universal speech representations that transfer to downstream tasks (ASR, speaker recognition, emotion detection).

Multimodal Learning (ViLBERT): Two-stream architectures with co-attention enable models to process vision and language jointly. Pre-training on image-text pairs teaches alignment.

Video Understanding (TimeSformer): Videos are sequences of spatial patches over time. Divided space-time attention decomposes into temporal (how patches move) and spatial (intra-frame relationships), reducing complexity from O((N·S·T)²) to O(N·S + N·T).

Decision Transformer: Frames RL as sequence modeling. No Bellman equations, no value functions. Condition on desired return-to-go to control behavior. Limited to in-distribution returns but enables offline RL at scale.

Key Technical Insights:

  • Transformers are modality-agnostic. Any data can be tokenized and processed (images via patches, audio via spectrograms, video via frames)
  • Self-supervised pretraining is crucial. Unlabeled data teaches universal representations that transfer to many tasks
  • Inductive biases matter for small data. ViT needs ImageNet-21K to match CNN performance on small datasets
  • Multimodal learning requires explicit alignment. Co-attention, shared embeddings, or contrastive losses bridge modalities
  • Efficiency is key for deployment. Divided attention, sparse patterns, and hierarchical processing reduce inference cost

Next Steps: Start with pretrained models (HuggingFace, timm library). Experiment with vision-language models (CLIP, ViLBERT) for zero-shot learning. Fine-tune on your domain. For production, profile latency and consider distillation or pruning.
