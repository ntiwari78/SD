The Mad Libs Machine

Understanding BERT through analogy and interaction

What is BERT? The Core Analogy

Imagine you're playing Mad Libs — someone hides a word in a sentence and asks you to guess it from all the context around it. That's BERT.

Unlike GPT (which reads left-to-right only, like reading a book), BERT reads the entire sentence in both directions simultaneously. It's like having eyes on both sides of your head.

The Technical Name: BERT = Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers. But you don't need to remember the name — just remember: "it understands context from both sides."

Special Tokens: The Punctuation of AI

[CLS] — The "Summary Badge"

BERT adds a special [CLS] token at the start of every sentence. Think of it as a badge that summarizes the entire sentence into a single representation. This is what you use for classification tasks (sentiment, NLI, etc.).

[SEP] — The "Chapter Divider"

When BERT sees two sentences, it uses [SEP] to mark where one sentence ends and another begins. This helps BERT understand sentence boundaries.

[MASK] — The Hidden Word

During pre-training, BERT replaces ~15% of words with [MASK]. Its job is to predict what the original word was. This teaches BERT to understand context deeply.

Next Sentence Prediction (NSP) Demo

Does Sentence B logically follow Sentence A? Think of it as a story coherence checker.

Sentence A: "I went to the grocery store yesterday afternoon."

Sentence B: I bought milk and eggs.

How BERT Represents Input

Each token becomes a vector (a list of 768 numbers for BERT-Base). These 768 numbers come from three components that are added together:

Math formula:

$$\text{Input}_i = E_{\text{token}_i} + E_{\text{segment}_i} + E_{\text{position}_i}$$

Fine-tuning: Specialization After General Learning

BERT is like a college student who learns general knowledge first. Then they apply to medical school and specialize in medicine.

  1. Pre-training Phase: BERT learns English on 3.3 billion words. It learns grammar, facts, relationships.
  2. Fine-tuning Phase: You take pre-trained BERT and train it on your specific task (sentiment analysis, NER, QA, etc.) with your labeled data.
  3. Why it works: The general knowledge transfers. BERT doesn't start from scratch on your task.

This is why you need surprisingly little data to fine-tune BERT (100-1000 examples vs millions for training from scratch).

The Mathematical Architecture

Deep dive into BERT's structure and variants

§3.1 BERT Architecture

BERT is an encoder-only transformer with multiple stacked layers. Each layer applies multi-head self-attention to understand relationships between tokens.

Model Sizes

Model Layers (L) Hidden Size (H) Attention Heads (A) Parameters
BERT-Base 12 768 12 110M
BERT-Large 24 1024 16 340M

Key insight: BERT-Base is typically "good enough" for most tasks. BERT-Large provides marginal improvements but needs more compute and memory.

§3.1.2 Pre-Training Objectives

Objective 1: Masked Language Modeling (MLM)

BERT's primary goal. During pre-training, 15% of tokens are masked. BERT tries to predict them from context.

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}} = -\sum_{i \in \mathcal{M}} \log P(x_i \mid \mathbf{x}_{\setminus\mathcal{M}})$$

Where $\mathcal{M}$ is the set of masked token positions and $\mathbf{x}_{\setminus\mathcal{M}}$ is the context.

The 80/10/10 Masking Heuristic:

Objective 2: Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)

Binary classification on the [CLS] token. BERT learns if Sentence B follows Sentence A.

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{NSP}} = -[y \log P(\text{IsNext}) + (1-y) \log P(\text{NotNext})]$$

Combined Loss

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{NSP}}$$

Note: Later research (RoBERTa) showed NSP doesn't help much. Many models dropped it entirely.

§3.1.3 Fine-tuning Architectures

Depending on your task, BERT's output is used differently:

Architecture 1: Token-Level Tasks (NER, POS)

Use the output of every token. Add a classification layer on top of each token representation.

Example: "John lives in New York" → Each word gets its own NER label (PERSON, location, location).

Architecture 2: Single Sentence Classification

Use only the [CLS] token output. This representation summarizes the entire sentence.

Example: Sentiment classification: "I loved this movie!" → [CLS] → Positive

Architecture 3: Sentence Pair Classification (NLI)

Two sentences separated by [SEP]. Use [CLS] for binary/multi-class classification.

Example: "A dog is sitting" + "The dog is resting" → Entailment

Architecture 4: Question Answering

Use span prediction. Two special tokens mark the start and end of the answer span.

Example: Question: "Where is John?" Context: "John lives in New York" → Span: [lives in] "New York"

§3.2 BERT Variants: RoBERTa

RoBERTa (Robustly Optimized BERT) improved upon BERT with 6 key training changes:

Improvement BERT RoBERTa
Masking Static (same for 40 epochs) Dynamic (new masks each epoch)
NSP Loss Included Removed (found to hurt performance)
Batch Size 256 8,192 (32× larger!)
Training Data 16 GB 160 GB (10× more)
Training Time 40 epochs 500K steps (~100 epochs)
Sequence Length Mixed (512 and shorter) Full 512 tokens

Result: RoBERTa typically outperforms BERT on downstream tasks by 1-2% because of these training improvements.

§3.2 BERT Variants: ALBERT

ALBERT (A Lite BERT) achieves similar performance with ~12× fewer parameters using two tricks:

Trick 1: Factorized Embeddings

Instead of embedding dimension H, use a smaller dimension E. Then project to H.

$$V \times H \rightarrow V \times E + E \times H \quad \text{(where } E \ll H \text{)}$$

For BERT-Base: Instead of vocab(30K) × 768, use 30K × 128 + 128 × 768. This saves millions of parameters.

Trick 2: Cross-Layer Parameter Sharing

All 12 layers share the same weights. This reduces parameters by ~12×.

Downside: Slightly slower convergence during fine-tuning. But once trained, it's much faster and uses less memory.

Bonus: Sentence Order Prediction (SOP)

ALBERT replaces NSP with SOP: Can BERT tell if two sentences are in the correct order? This is harder and more useful than NSP.

§3.3 Domain-Specific BERT Applications

TaBERT: BERT for Tabular Data

Pre-trains on both tables and text. Uses:

  • Horizontal self-attention: Across columns within a row
  • Vertical self-attention: Across rows within a column
  • Hybrid attention: Combinations for semantic understanding

Result: Excellent at table question-answering and semantic search over structured data.

BERTopic: Topic Modeling with BERT

Pipeline: Document embeddings (BERT) → Dimensionality reduction (UMAP) → Clustering (HDBSCAN) → Topic extraction (c-TF-IDF)

$$c\text{-}TF\text{-}IDF_i = \frac{t_i}{w_i} \times \log \frac{m}{\sum_j t_j}$$

This creates semantic topics from unlabeled documents with minimal hyperparameter tuning.

§3.4 BERTology: What Does BERT Learn?

Researchers have dissected BERT to understand what each layer learns. Use the slider below to explore:

Three Types of Knowledge

Syntactic Knowledge

Lower layers learn grammar rules, word order, parts of speech. Detected through attention probes on POS tags.

Semantic Knowledge

Middle layers learn relationships: "king - man + woman ≈ queen". Word embeddings are semantically meaningful.

Task-Specific

Top layers adapt to the task. Sentiment heads form. NLI entailment patterns emerge during fine-tuning.

BERT Limitations (From BERTology Research)

  • Negation Blindness: "not bad" often classified as negative. Negation scope is hard for attention.
  • Number Struggles: Can't reliably predict "2 + 2 = ?" or count objects in text.
  • Relational Inference: Weak at multi-hop reasoning ("John's mother is Mary. Mary's brother is Bob. Who is Bob to John?").

1import torch
2import torch.nn as nn
3
4class BERTEmbedding(nn.Module):
5 """BERT Input = Token + Segment + Position"""
6 def __init__(self, vocab_size, hidden_size,
7 max_seq_len, num_segments=2, dropout=0.1):
8 super().__init__()
9 self.token_embed = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, hidden_size)
10 self.segment_embed = nn.Embedding(num_segments, hidden_size)
11 self.position_embed = nn.Embedding(max_seq_len, hidden_size)
12 # OPT: BERT uses learned positional embeddings, not sinusoidal
13 self.norm = nn.LayerNorm(hidden_size)
14 self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
15
16 def forward(self, input_ids, segment_ids):
17 seq_len = input_ids.size(1)
18 positions = torch.arange(seq_len, device=input_ids.device)
19 positions = positions.unsqueeze(0)
20 # OPT: Positions created on-the-fly, no storage needed
21 x = (self.token_embed(input_ids) +
22 self.segment_embed(segment_ids) +
23 self.position_embed(positions))
24 return self.dropout(self.norm(x))

Code Block 2: MLM Head Implementation

The prediction head that turns hidden representations into vocabulary predictions:

mlm_head.py
1class MLMHead(nn.Module):
2 """Masked Language Model prediction head"""
3 def __init__(self, hidden_size, vocab_size):
4 super().__init__()
5 self.dense = nn.Linear(hidden_size, hidden_size)
6 self.activation = nn.GELU()
7 # OPT: BERT uses GELU activation, not ReLU
8 self.norm = nn.LayerNorm(hidden_size)
9 self.decoder = nn.Linear(hidden_size, vocab_size)
10 # OPT: Decoder shares weights with token embeddings
11
12 def forward(self, hidden_states, masked_positions):
13 # Only compute for masked positions (saves memory)
14 masked_hidden = hidden_states[masked_positions]
15 # OPT: Sparse computation improves efficiency
16 x = self.activation(self.dense(masked_hidden))
17 x = self.norm(x)
18 logits = self.decoder(x)
19 return logits

Socratic Sandbox

Model failure scenarios and debugging

Question 1: Negation Blindness ▼

Problem: Your BERT sentiment classifier labels "The movie was not bad" as negative sentiment. Why?

Answer:

Negation blindness is a documented BERT limitation (from BERTology research §3.4.2). Attention mechanisms don't fully capture negation scope. Here's what happens:

  • The token "bad" gets high attention weights due to strong semantic signal (negative word)
  • The "not" token fails to modulate these weights across the full phrase
  • BERT sees "[not] + [bad]" as ~80% negative, despite negation

Solutions:

  • Fine-tune on negation-aware data: Use datasets explicitly labeled with negations
  • Augment with synthetic data: Generate negation pairs automatically
  • Use specialized models: Models like BERT-for-negation or RoBERTa fine-tuned on adversarial examples
  • Post-processing: Add a negation rule layer: flip predictions for sentences containing negation markers
Question 2: Few-Shot Catastrophe ▼

Problem: You fine-tune BERT on only 50 labeled examples and get 52% accuracy (random is 50%). What went wrong?

Answer:

Few-shot catastrophe: BERT requires sufficient fine-tuning data (~1000+ examples). With 50 examples:

  • BERT overfits to noise in your tiny dataset
  • The model memorizes your 50 examples instead of learning generalizable patterns
  • Validation loss improves but test accuracy stays random

Solutions:

  • Data augmentation: Use EDA, backtranslation, or paraphrasing to expand 50 → 500 examples
  • Few-shot methods: Try SetFit (semantic textual similarity + logistic regression) or in-context learning with larger models
  • Larger pre-trained models: BERT-Large or domain-specific models (SciBERT, FinBERT) may transfer better
  • Prompt-based learning: Use prompt templates: "This review is [positive/negative]" instead of full fine-tuning
  • Collect more data: The gold standard solution
Question 3: RoBERTa Outperformance ▼

Problem: RoBERTa consistently outperforms BERT on your task despite having identical architecture. Explain all 6 reasons.

Answer:

RoBERTa made 6 training improvements. Each contributes independently:

  1. Dynamic masking: BERT masks statically (same 40 epochs). RoBERTa generates new masks each epoch → 40× more masking patterns.
  2. Removed NSP: The NSP task was noise. Removing it simplified the loss landscape.
  3. Larger batches (8K): Better gradient estimates and more stable training (BERT used 256).
  4. 10× more data (160GB): More diverse language patterns learned. Classic "more data" benefit.
  5. Longer training: BERT: 40 epochs. RoBERTa: ~100 epochs. More optimization time → better convergence.
  6. Full 512-token sequences: BERT trained on mixed lengths. RoBERTa always used full 512 tokens → better long-range understanding.

Key insight: These aren't architectural changes — they're training recipe improvements. The architecture is identical, but the training process was better.

Question 4: Language Transfer Failure ▼

Problem: Your BERT model handles English perfectly (95% F1) but fails on German text (52% F1). What's happening?

Answer:

Vocabulary mismatch and over-fragmentation: BERT's WordPiece vocabulary is 30K tokens, optimized for English.

  • English word "unhappy" → 1-2 tokens
  • German word "Unglücklichkeit" → 5-7 subword tokens (broken up)
  • Over-fragmented tokens lose semantic meaning. BERT sees noise instead of words.

Solutions:

  • Use mBERT (multilingual BERT): 104 languages, shared vocabulary. Works across languages.
  • Use language-specific BERT: German BERT, French BERT, etc. Pre-trained on in-language data.
  • Fine-tune on German: Take BERT and continue pre-training on German corpus before fine-tuning.
  • Use XLM-RoBERTa: Better multilingual model than mBERT, supports 100+ languages.
Question 5: ALBERT Parameter Reduction Miracle ▼

Problem: ALBERT has 12M parameters vs BERT's 110M (9× smaller) but achieves similar performance. How?

Answer:

ALBERT uses two parameter reduction tricks:

Trick 1: Factorized Embeddings

Instead of projecting vocabulary directly to hidden dimension H, use intermediate dimension E:

$$V \times H \quad \rightarrow \quad V \times E + E \times H \quad \text{(where } E \ll H \text{)}$$

Numbers: BERT-Base uses 30K vocab × 768 hidden = 23M parameters just for embeddings. ALBERT-Base uses 30K × 128 + 128 × 768 = 4.8M. That's 5× reduction in one layer.

Trick 2: Cross-Layer Parameter Sharing

All 12 transformer layers share the exact same weights. Normally:

  • BERT: 12 layers × different weights = 12× parameters
  • ALBERT: 12 layers × same weights = 1× parameters

This reduces transformer layers by ~12×.

Bonus: Sentence Order Prediction (SOP)

ALBERT replaces NSP with SOP: "Can you tell if these two sentences are in the correct order?" This is harder and more useful for downstream tasks.

Trade-off: ALBERT converges slower during fine-tuning because all layers must learn together. But inference is much faster and uses less memory.

Key Takeaway

BERT's weaknesses are systematic: negation, numerics, relational reasoning. Knowing these limitations helps you choose the right model and preprocessing strategy for your task. When in doubt, try RoBERTa (better training) or domain-specific variants.
