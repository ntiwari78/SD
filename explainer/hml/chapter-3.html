The Accuracy Paradox

Why 90% accuracy on a fraud detection model might be completely useless

The Smoke Detector Analogy
Imagine a smoke detector that never goes off. It has 99.99% "accuracy" at detecting non-fires because 99.99% of the time there's no fire. But when a real fire starts, it fails to warn you. Accuracy alone tells you nothing about whether you're solving the right problem.

The Core Challenge:

In the MNIST dataset, only ~10% of digits are the number 5. A "dummy" classifier that predicts "not a 5" for every image gets ~90% accuracy instantly—without learning anything. This is why we need:

  • Precision: When I predict "5", how often am I right?
  • Recall: Of all the actual 5s, how many did I catch?
  • F1 Score: A balanced harmonic mean of both
  • Confusion Matrix: Separate the 4 types of errors and correct predictions
  • ROC Curves: How the precision/recall trade-off works

Binary Classification & The Accuracy Trap

Why accuracy is a terrible metric for skewed datasets

The Setup: Is it a 5?

We train a binary classifier to answer: "Is this digit a 5?" (Yes/No)

Using SGDClassifier, we get ~95% accuracy in cross-validation. This sounds amazing!

But here's the trap: In MNIST, only ~10% of images are 5s. A dummy classifier that always predicts "not a 5" gets ~90% accuracy without learning anything.

Confusion Matrix Visualizer

Understanding all four types of predictions

What is a Confusion Matrix?

A confusion matrix shows the breakdown of all predictions:

  • TN (True Negatives): Correctly predicted "not a 5"
  • FP (False Positives): Wrongly predicted "is a 5"
  • FN (False Negatives): Missed a real 5 (worst case for detection)
  • TP (True Positives): Correctly detected a 5

Performance Metrics with KaTeX

The formulas that matter

Precision: Accuracy of Positive Predictions
$$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}$$

Meaning: Of all the times we predicted "5", how many were actually 5s?

Formula: Correct positives divided by all positive predictions.

Book Value: 3,530 / (3,530 + 687) = 83.7%

Interpretation: When our model says "this is a 5", it's right 83.7% of the time.

Recall: Catching All Positives
$$\text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}$$

Meaning: Of all the actual 5s in the dataset, how many did we catch?

Formula: Correct positives divided by all actual positives.

Book Value: 3,530 / (3,530 + 1,891) = 65.1%

Interpretation: We only catch 65.1% of actual 5s; we miss 34.9%.

F1 Score: Harmonic Mean
$$F_1 = \frac{2}{\frac{1}{\text{Precision}} + \frac{1}{\text{Recall}}} = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}$$

Meaning: A single score balancing precision and recall. Punishes extreme imbalance.

Book Value: 2 × (0.837 × 0.651) / (0.837 + 0.651) = 0.7325 or 73.25%

When to Use: When you care equally about precision and recall. For fraud: use Recall. For spam: use Precision.

Why These Three?
Precision answers: "How many of my positive predictions were correct?"
Recall answers: "How many actual positives did I find?"
F1 answers: "What's the balanced overall score?"

Precision/Recall Trade-off

Moving the decision threshold

The Decision Threshold

Classifiers don't output binary 0/1 decisions. They output decision scores or probabilities. We then pick a threshold: if score ≥ threshold, predict "5"; else predict "not 5".

Different thresholds give different precision/recall trade-offs:

  • Lower threshold: More "5" predictions → higher Recall, lower Precision
  • Higher threshold: Fewer "5" predictions → lower Recall, higher Precision
Interactive Threshold Trade-off
Drag the threshold slider (vertical line) to see precision/recall change

Key Trade-off: You cannot maximize both precision and recall simultaneously. Choosing one means sacrificing the other. The right choice depends on your use case:
• Fraud detection: Maximize Recall (catch all fraud, even if false alarms)
• Email spam: Maximize Precision (avoid filtering good emails, even if spam gets through)
• Medical diagnosis: Usually Recall (better to warn false alarm than miss disease)

ROC Curves & AUC

A complete picture of classifier performance

ROC Curve Explained

ROC (Receiver Operating Characteristic) shows the trade-off between True Positive Rate and False Positive Rate at different thresholds.

  • TPR (True Positive Rate): TP / (TP + FN) = Recall
  • FPR (False Positive Rate): FP / (FP + TN) = "false alarm" rate

The diagonal line represents a random classifier (50% accuracy). Curves above it are better. The area under the curve (AUC) ranges from 0.5 (random) to 1.0 (perfect).

ROC Curve Comparison
Toggle classifiers to see different ROC curves
Why AUC? AUC (Area Under Curve) is threshold-independent. It tells you: "If I pick a random positive and random negative example, what's the probability my classifier ranks the positive higher?" A score above 0.9 is excellent.

Multiclass Classification Strategies

Extending binary classifiers to 10 classes

One-vs-Rest (OvR)

Strategy: Train N separate binary classifiers (one per class).

For MNIST (10 digits), train 10 classifiers:

  • Classifier 1: "Is it a 0?" vs. "Is it not a 0?"
  • Classifier 2: "Is it a 1?" vs. "Is it not a 1?"
  • ... (8 more)

Prediction: Get decision scores from all 10, pick the highest.

Used by: SGDClassifier (fast, memory-efficient)

One-vs-One (OvO)

Strategy: Train N×(N-1)/2 binary classifiers, one for each pair of classes.

For MNIST, that's 10×9/2 = 45 classifiers:

  • Classifier 1: "Is it a 0 or 1?"
  • Classifier 2: "Is it a 0 or 2?"
  • ... (43 more pairwise comparisons)

Prediction: Each classifier votes; the class with the most votes wins.

Used by: SVM/SVC (slower but often more accurate for small N)

Decision Scores Example

Here are the decision scores from an SVC classifier for a single test image:

# SVC decision scores for a test digit
scores = [3.79, 0.73, 6.06, 8.3, -0.29, 9.3, 1.75, 2.77, 7.21, 4.82]
# Index 5 has the highest score (9.3), so predict digit 5
prediction = np.argmax(scores) # → 5

The highest score (9.3) corresponds to digit 5, so we predict 5.

Production Code: End-to-End Pipeline

From data to metrics, fully functional examples

Binary Classification Pipeline
from sklearn.datasets import fetch_openml
from sklearn.linear_model import SGDClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score, cross_val_predict
from sklearn.metrics import (
    confusion_matrix, precision_score, recall_score, f1_score,
    precision_recall_curve, roc_curve, roc_auc_score
)

# Fetch MNIST
mnist = fetch_openml('mnist_784', as_frame=False)
X, y = mnist.data, mnist.target
X_train, X_test = X[:60000], X[60000:]
y_train, y_test = y[:60000], y[60000:]

# Binary target: is-5 detector
y_train_5 = (y_train == '5')
y_test_5 = (y_test == '5')

# Train SGD classifier
sgd_clf = SGDClassifier(random_state=42)
sgd_clf.fit(X_train, y_train_5)

# Cross-validation accuracy
cv_scores = cross_val_score(sgd_clf, X_train, y_train_5, cv=3, scoring="accuracy")
# array([0.95035, 0.96035, 0.9604])

# Get predictions for confusion matrix
y_train_pred = cross_val_predict(sgd_clf, X_train, y_train_5, cv=3)
cm = confusion_matrix(y_train_5, y_train_pred)
# array([[53892, 687], [1891, 3530]])

# Compute metrics
precision = precision_score(y_train_5, y_train_pred) # 0.8370
recall = recall_score(y_train_5, y_train_pred) # 0.6511
f1 = f1_score(y_train_5, y_train_pred) # 0.7325

print(f"Precision: {precision:.4f}")
print(f"Recall: {recall:.4f}")
print(f"F1: {f1:.4f}")
ROC Curve & AUC
# Get decision scores for ROC curve
y_scores = cross_val_predict(sgd_clf, X_train, y_train_5, cv=3, method="decision_function")

# Compute ROC curve
fpr, tpr, thresholds = roc_curve(y_train_5, y_scores)

# Compute AUC
roc_auc = roc_auc_score(y_train_5, y_scores)
print(f"AUC: {roc_auc:.4f}") # 0.9605

# (In a real notebook, you'd plot fpr vs tpr)
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 6))
plt.plot(fpr, tpr, linewidth=2, label=f"AUC = {roc_auc:.4f}")

plt.xlabel("False Positive Rate")
plt.ylabel("True Positive Rate")
plt.title("ROC Curve - MNIST 5-Detector")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

The War Room: Socratic Sandbox

Diagnose a broken fraud detection system

The Scenario

You're a data scientist at a fintech company. The business team comes to you with a crisis:

"Our fraud detection model shows 99.5% accuracy in testing, but we're hemorrhaging money from fraudulent transactions. The model only catches 12% of actual fraud cases. What's going on? How do we fix it?"
1 What does 99.5% accuracy mean if only 0.5% of transactions are fraudulent?

Answer: A dummy classifier that predicts "not fraudulent" for every transaction would get 99.5% accuracy instantly. This is the accuracy trap: when classes are extremely imbalanced (fraud is rare), accuracy is a useless metric.

Example: If 99.5% of transactions are legitimate, even a broken model gets 99.5% right just by never flagging fraud.

2 What are precision and recall for the fraud class, and why do they matter here?

Answer:

  • Precision (of fraud): Of all transactions flagged as fraudulent, how many actually were? High precision = fewer false alarms.
  • Recall (of fraud): Of all actual frauds, how many did we catch? High recall = we catch more fraud.

Your model has 12% recall—it only catches 1 in 8 frauds. This is a business disaster, even if precision is high. You need higher recall, even if it means more false alarms (lower precision).

3 Should you optimize for precision or recall in fraud detection? Why?

Answer: Recall. The cost of missing fraud (false negatives) is catastrophic—actual money lost. The cost of a false alarm (false positive) is low—just human review of a flagged transaction.

In general:

  • Fraud, disease detection, security: Maximize Recall
  • Spam filtering, hiring: Maximize Precision
  • Balanced scenarios: Use F1 or domain-specific metrics
4 How would you adjust the decision threshold to catch more fraud?

Answer: Lower the decision threshold.

Instead of requiring decision_score ≥ 0.5 to flag fraud, require decision_score ≥ 0.3 or ≥ 0.1. This flags more transactions as potentially fraudulent, increasing recall but decreasing precision (more false alarms).

Steps:

  1. Get decision scores for all test transactions
  2. Plot precision/recall curve (what we saw earlier)
  3. Pick a threshold that gives you acceptable recall (say, 80%+) without making precision too low
  4. Deploy with new threshold
5 What would the ROC curve look like? What AUC would you target?

Answer: The ROC curve plots all (FPR, TPR) pairs as you vary the threshold from 0 to 1.

For a good fraud detector, you'd want:

  • High TPR at low FPR: Curve bows up to the left—catches fraud with few false alarms
  • AUC ≥ 0.95: Excellent discrimination between fraud and legitimate

If your AUC is only 0.60 (near the random diagonal), the model is barely better than a coin flip—time to retrain with better features or a different algorithm. If AUC is high (0.95+), the model is good; you just need to adjust the threshold.

Takeaway: The core issue is threshold, not model quality. If the ROC AUC is high but recall is low, simply lower the threshold. If the AUC is low, you need a better model (new features, different algorithm, more data).
