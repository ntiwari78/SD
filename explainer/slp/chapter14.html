Feynman Bridge

The Musical Instrument Analogy

Your voice is a musical instrument — arguably the most versatile one ever built. The lungs are the bellows, pushing air upward. The vocal folds in the larynx are like a reed instrument's vibrating blades: when they're close together, air makes them buzz (producing voiced sounds like vowels). When they're apart, air passes freely (producing voiceless sounds like [s] or [f]).

But what makes your voice truly extraordinary is the resonating chamber above — your mouth, tongue, and nasal cavity. By changing the shape of this chamber (moving your tongue, rounding your lips), you selectively amplify certain frequencies and dampen others. This is exactly how a trombone changes pitch by extending its slide: different tube lengths amplify different harmonics.

Each vowel you say corresponds to a unique filter shape — tongue high and forward for "ee", tongue low for "ah". The specific frequencies that get amplified are called formants, and they're like a fingerprint for each speech sound. A computer "listening" to speech needs to extract these formants from the raw waveform — and the tool for that is the mel spectrogram, which mimics how the human ear perceives frequency.

14.1 — Speech Sounds & Phonetic Transcription

We represent pronunciation as a string of phones — speech sounds written with a standard alphabet. The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) is the global standard, while ARPAbet uses ASCII symbols for American English. The mapping between English spelling and phones is opaque: the letter "c" maps to [k] in "cougar" but [s] in "cell".

Key resources: the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary (134,000 words), the TIMIT corpus (6,300 phonetically hand-labeled sentences from 630 speakers), and the Switchboard corpus for spontaneous speech.

14.2 — Articulatory Phonetics

Articulatory phonetics studies how speech sounds are physically produced by the vocal organs. Sound begins when air from the lungs passes through the trachea into the larynx, where the vocal folds may vibrate (voiced sounds) or remain open (voiceless). Above the larynx, the vocal tract — consisting of the oral and nasal tracts — shapes the airflow into distinct phones.

Syllable Structure

Consonants and vowels combine into syllables. A syllable has a vowel-like nucleus, optional onset consonants, and optional coda consonants. The nucleus + coda form the rime. For example, in "ham": onset=[h], nucleus=[ae], coda=[m]. English phonotactics constrains which consonant clusters can appear (e.g., [zdr] cannot start a syllable).

Technical Ascent

14.3 — Prosody

Prosody covers the intonational and rhythmic aspects of language — specifically F0 (fundamental frequency / pitch), energy (loudness), and duration. Prosody conveys discourse structure, emphasis, emotion, and turn-taking.

Prominence: Accent, Stress & Schwa

Pitch accent makes certain words perceptually salient. Lexical stress is a word-level property that determines which syllable bears the accent (e.g., surPRISED, not SURprised). Unstressed vowels often reduce to schwa [ax] — the most common vowel in English.

Prosodic Structure & Tune

Utterances group into intonation phrases with boundaries often at commas or clause edges. The tune — the F0 contour over time — distinguishes statements (final fall: ↘) from yes/no questions (question rise: ↗). The ToBI system transcribes intonation using 5 pitch accent types (H*, L*, L*+H, L+H*, H+!H*) and 4 boundary tones (L-L%, L-H%, H-H%, H-L%).

14.4 — Acoustic Phonetics & Signals

Waves, Frequency & Amplitude

A sound wave is described by its frequency (cycles per second, measured in Hz) and amplitude (maximum displacement). The period T = 1/f.

(14.3) $$y = A \cdot \sin(2\pi f t)$$
🌊 Interactive: Waveform Generator

Adjust frequency, amplitude, and add harmonics to see how complex waveforms are built from sine waves — the basis of Fourier analysis.

Sampling & the Nyquist Frequency

Digitizing a waveform requires sampling (measuring amplitude at discrete times) and quantization (storing as integers). The Nyquist frequency — the maximum frequency that can be captured — is half the sampling rate. Telephone speech (≤4kHz) uses 8kHz sampling; microphone speech uses 16kHz.

Spectra, Spectrograms & Formants

The spectrum decomposes a signal into its frequency components (via Fourier analysis). A spectrogram shows how those frequencies change over time (x=time, y=frequency, darkness=amplitude). Dark bands in the spectrogram are called formants — frequency regions amplified by the vocal tract's resonant shape. F1 and F2 are the most important for distinguishing vowels.

Source-Filter Model

The voice produces a raw source signal (glottal pulses with harmonics at multiples of F0), and the vocal tract acts as a filter that amplifies certain harmonics into formants. Different tongue/lip positions = different filter shapes = different phones.

Key Acoustic Equations

(14.6) $$\text{RMS amplitude} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}x_i^2}$$
(14.9) $$m = 1127\,\ln\!\left(1 + \frac{f}{700}\right) \qquad \text{(mel scale)}$$

The mel scale models human pitch perception: linear below 1000 Hz, logarithmic above. This is why mel spectrograms give finer resolution at low frequencies (where formants live) and coarser resolution at high frequencies.

14.5–14.6 — Feature Extraction Pipeline

The standard speech processing pipeline transforms raw waveforms into feature vectors that capture phonetically relevant information. The two main representations are:

StepOperationOutput
1Sample at 16kHz, quantize to 16-bit PCMDigital waveform x[n]
2Apply 25ms Hamming window, 10ms strideFrames y[n] = w[n]·s[n]
3Compute FFT (Discrete Fourier Transform)Spectrum X[k] per frame
4Apply mel-spaced triangular filter bankMel spectrum (80–128 channels)
5Take log of each channelLog mel spectrum vectors
6 (optional)Apply DCT to log mel → keep first 12+1MFCC (39-dim with Δ,ΔΔ)

Windowing (Eq 14.11–14.13)

$$y[n] = w[n] \cdot s[n] \qquad w[n]_{\text{Hamming}} = 0.54 - 0.46\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi n}{L}\right)$$

The Hamming window smoothly tapers the signal at frame edges, avoiding spectral artifacts from abrupt cutoffs.

DFT (Eq 14.15)

$$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n]\, e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{N}kn}$$

The FFT efficiently computes this for N = power of 2, giving the energy at each frequency band.

Mel Filter Bank & Log

A bank of triangular filters, spaced logarithmically along the mel scale, collects energy from each frequency band. Taking the log models human loudness perception (more sensitive to small differences at low amplitudes).

log_mel_spectrogram.py — Full pipeline with librosa + torchaudio 
import numpy as np
import librosa
import librosa.display
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# ── Step 1: Load & sample audio at 16kHz ──
# librosa automatically resamples to sr=16000
y, sr = librosa.load("speech.wav", sr=16000)
print(f"Loaded: {len(y)} samples at {sr} Hz")
print(f"Duration: {len(y)/sr:.2f}s")
print(f"Nyquist frequency: {sr//2} Hz")  # 8000 Hz

# ── Step 2-4: Compute mel spectrogram ──
# 25ms window = 400 samples at 16kHz
# 10ms hop    = 160 samples
n_fft = 400           # window size (25ms × 16000)
hop_length = 160      # stride (10ms × 16000)
n_mels = 80           # number of mel filter bank channels

# This internally applies:
#   1. Hamming window (Eq 14.13)
#   2. FFT (Eq 14.15)
#   3. Mel filter bank (Eq 14.16)
mel_spec = librosa.feature.melspectrogram(
    y=y, sr=sr,
    n_fft=n_fft,
    hop_length=hop_length,
    n_mels=n_mels,
    window='hamming',     # Eq 14.13: Hamming window
    fmin=0, fmax=8000     # frequency range
)

# ── Step 5: Take log (power → dB) ──
log_mel = librosa.power_to_db(mel_spec, ref=np.max)

print(f"Mel spectrogram shape: {log_mel.shape}")
# (80, T) — 80 mel channels × T time frames

# ── Visualize ──
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(12, 6))

# Waveform
librosa.display.waveshow(y, sr=sr, ax=axes[0])
axes[0].set_title('Waveform')

# Log mel spectrogram
img = librosa.display.specshow(
    log_mel, sr=sr, hop_length=hop_length,

    ax=axes[1], cmap='magma'

axes[1].set_title('Log Mel Spectrogram (80 channels)')
fig.colorbar(img, ax=axes[1], format='%+2.0f dB')

mfcc_from_scratch.py — Manual MFCC pipeline (NumPy) 
import numpy as np
from scipy.io import wavfile
from scipy.fftpack import dct

# ── Manual MFCC Implementation ──
# Following the pipeline: Sample → Window → FFT → Mel → Log → DCT

def hz_to_mel(f):
    """Eq 14.16: convert Hz to mel scale."""
    return 1127 * np.log(1 + f / 700)

def mel_to_hz(m):
    """Inverse mel → Hz."""
    return 700 * (np.exp(m / 1127) - 1)

def compute_mfcc(signal, sr, n_mfcc=13, n_mels=26,
                  frame_ms=25, stride_ms=10):
    """
    Full MFCC pipeline from raw waveform.
    Returns: (n_frames, n_mfcc) array
    """
    # Step 1: Pre-emphasis (boost high freqs)
    emphasized = np.append(signal[0],
                           signal[1:] - 0.97 * signal[:-1])

    # Step 2: Framing
    frame_len = int(sr * frame_ms / 1000)    # 400 samples
    frame_step = int(sr * stride_ms / 1000)   # 160 samples
    n_frames = (len(emphasized) - frame_len) // frame_step + 1

    frames = np.stack([
        emphasized[i*frame_step : i*frame_step + frame_len]
        for i in range(n_frames)
    ])

    # Step 3: Hamming window (Eq 14.13)
    hamming = 0.54 - 0.46 * np.cos(
        2 * np.pi * np.arange(frame_len) / frame_len
    )
    frames *= hamming

    # Step 4: FFT (Eq 14.15)
    NFFT = 512  # next power of 2 ≥ frame_len
    mag_spec = np.abs(np.fft.rfft(frames, NFFT))
    pow_spec = mag_spec ** 2 / NFFT

    # Step 5: Mel filter bank
    low_mel = hz_to_mel(0)
    high_mel = hz_to_mel(sr / 2)
    mel_points = np.linspace(low_mel, high_mel, n_mels + 2)
    hz_points = mel_to_hz(mel_points)
    bins = np.floor((NFFT + 1) * hz_points / sr).astype(int)

    fbank = np.zeros((n_mels, NFFT // 2 + 1))
    for m in range(n_mels):
        # Rising slope
        for k in range(bins[m], bins[m+1]):
            fbank[m, k] = (k - bins[m]) / (bins[m+1] - bins[m])
        # Falling slope
        for k in range(bins[m+1], bins[m+2]):
            fbank[m, k] = (bins[m+2] - k) / (bins[m+2] - bins[m+1])

    # Step 6: Apply filter bank + log
    mel_energies = pow_spec @ fbank.T
    mel_energies = np.where(mel_energies == 0, np.finfo(float).eps,
                            mel_energies)
    log_mel = np.log(mel_energies)

    # Step 7: DCT → keep first n_mfcc coefficients
    mfcc = dct(log_mel, type=2, axis=1, norm='ortho')[:, :n_mfcc]

    return mfcc

# ── Demo ──
sr_demo = 16000
t = np.linspace(0, 1, sr_demo)
# Simulate vowel [iy]: F0=150Hz, F1=270Hz, F2=2300Hz
signal = (np.sin(2*np.pi*150*t) +
          0.8*np.sin(2*np.pi*270*t) +
          0.5*np.sin(2*np.pi*2300*t))
signal = (signal / np.max(np.abs(signal)) * 0.9).astype(np.float32)

mfcc = compute_mfcc(signal, sr_demo)
print(f"MFCC shape: {mfcc.shape}")  # (n_frames, 13)
print(f"First frame MFCCs: {mfcc[0].round(2)}")
Modern Bridge · 2026

From Spectrograms to End-to-End Speech

The Decline of MFCCs

While MFCCs dominated speech recognition for decades, modern end-to-end systems (Whisper, wav2vec 2.0, HuBERT) increasingly work directly from raw waveforms or log mel spectrograms, letting neural networks learn their own optimal features. The 80-channel log mel spectrogram has become the de facto standard input for Transformer-based ASR.

Self-Supervised Speech Models

wav2vec 2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020) and HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) learn speech representations from unlabeled audio via masked prediction — analogous to BERT for text. These models discover phonetic categories, speaker identity, and even prosodic features without any transcription labels.

Whisper & Universal ASR

OpenAI's Whisper (Radford et al., 2023) takes 80-channel log mel spectrograms as input to a Transformer encoder-decoder, trained on 680,000 hours of web audio. It handles 99 languages, translation, and timestamp prediction — making the traditional phonetics pipeline implicit rather than explicit.

Neural Codec Models & Voice Cloning

Models like EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2022) and SoundStream compress audio into discrete neural tokens — essentially learning a new "phonetic alphabet" directly from data. These tokens power voice synthesis systems (VALL-E, Bark) that can clone a voice from a 3-second sample.

📜 Historical Context: From Writing Systems to Digital Speech

5,000 Years of Phonetic Representation

The earliest writing systems (Sumerian, Chinese, Mayan) were logographic. Sound-based writing emerged when symbols began representing syllables or consonants — a principle that spread from Egyptian hieroglyphs to Phoenician, Greek, Roman, and Arabic scripts. The IPA was developed in 1888 to provide a universal transcription. The digital era began with PCM sampling (1930s–40s at Bell Labs) and the mel scale (Stevens et al., 1937). MFCCs were introduced by Davis & Mermelstein (1980) and dominated ASR for 30+ years before neural approaches superseded them around 2015.

Socratic Challenge

The Telephone Bandwidth Mystery

Scenario: Why Does Phone Speech Sound "Tinny"?

Your team is building an ASR system trained on high-quality microphone recordings (16kHz sampling). When you deploy it on telephone audio (8kHz sampling), accuracy drops by 15%. A colleague suggests "just upsample the phone audio to 16kHz." You suspect this won't work.

Question 1
What is the Nyquist frequency for telephone audio at 8kHz sampling? What frequencies are permanently lost, and which speech sounds are most affected?
The Nyquist frequency is half the sampling rate: 8000/2 = 4000 Hz. All frequencies above 4kHz are lost. This primarily affects fricatives like [s], [sh], [f] — which have significant energy above 4kHz — and the higher formants (F3, F4) that help distinguish similar vowels and identify speaker characteristics. The critical F1 and F2 formants (roughly 200-2500 Hz) are preserved, which is why telephone speech remains intelligible.
Question 2
Why won't upsampling the 8kHz audio to 16kHz restore the lost information? What does upsampling actually do?
Upsampling just adds interpolated samples between existing ones — it creates new sample points but cannot recover frequency information that was never captured. The frequencies above 4kHz were filtered out by the telephone network before digitization. You can't reconstruct information that was never recorded. The upsampled signal will have the same spectral content as the original, just with more samples representing it. (Some modern "bandwidth extension" neural networks can hallucinate plausible high-frequency content, but this is prediction, not recovery.)
Question 3
Your mel filter bank has 80 channels spanning 0-8000 Hz. If you apply it to telephone audio, what happens to the upper channels? How should you handle training/testing mismatch?
The upper mel filter channels (roughly above channel 50-55, corresponding to >4kHz) will have zero or near-zero energy for telephone audio. If the model was trained on wideband audio where these channels carried real information, this mismatch causes the accuracy drop. The correct solution is to downsample all training data to 8kHz to match the test conditions, or to train on mixed-bandwidth data. This is why corpus sampling rate consistency is crucial in ASR.
Question 4
The mel scale is "linear below 1000 Hz and logarithmic above." How does this specifically benefit speech recognition compared to a uniform linear frequency scale?
The mel scale allocates more resolution (more, narrower filters) to low frequencies where F1 and F2 live — the formant region that carries the most phonetic information. Above 1000 Hz, it compresses resolution because fine frequency distinctions matter less perceptually. On a linear scale, half the filters would cover 0-4000 Hz and half would cover 4000-8000 Hz — wasting resolution on high frequencies while under-representing the crucial formant region. The mel scale mirrors the human cochlea, which has ~3500 hair cells for 20-1000 Hz but only ~500 for 1000-20000 Hz.
Question 5
Modern systems like Whisper bypass MFCCs entirely, using log mel spectrograms directly. What information do MFCCs discard (via DCT) that the log mel spectrum retains, and why might keeping it help modern neural networks?
The DCT step in MFCC computation is essentially a dimensionality reduction — it compresses 80+ mel channels into 13 cepstral coefficients by keeping only the smoothest spectral shape (discarding fine spectral detail and speaker-specific harmonics). Log mel spectrograms retain the full 80-128 channel resolution, preserving fine-grained spectral detail, harmonic structure, and subtle inter-formant patterns. Modern neural networks (Transformers, ConvNets) are powerful enough to learn what to keep and discard from this richer representation — doing internally what the DCT did externally, but in a task-optimized way.
