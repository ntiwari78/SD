Chapter 22: Lexicons for Sentiment, Affect, and Connotation

Measuring the emotional temperature of words

The Feynman Bridge: Think of a movie review — "This film is absolutely brilliant" vs "This film is utterly terrible." Even without understanding the movie itself, certain words carry emotional charge. Sentiment lexicons are like emotional dictionaries that catalog this charge, allowing us to computationally decode the affective content of text. Just as a thermometer measures physical temperature, these lexicons measure emotional temperature.
From Chapter 21: In Semantic Role Labeling, we learned WHO did WHAT to determine the semantic structure of sentences. Now we add another critical dimension: HOW DO THEY FEEL about it? By combining semantic roles with sentiment lexicons, we can not only parse what happened, but understand the emotional valence — whether the event was viewed positively or negatively.

Technical Foundations

22.1: Defining Emotion and Affect

Scherer's Typology of Affective States

Emotions are complex affective experiences that differ from related states. Scherer identifies five types of affective phenomena:

  • Emotions: Intense, brief reactions to specific events (e.g., joy at victory, anger at insult)
  • Mood: Lower intensity, longer duration, less tied to specific causes (e.g., melancholy, cheerfulness)
  • Interpersonal Stance: Emotional orientation toward other people (e.g., trust, distrust, resentment)
  • Attitude: Stable evaluation of objects or ideas (e.g., liking jazz, disliking pop)
  • Personality Traits: Stable affective dispositions (e.g., cheerfulness, anxiety-proneness)

Two Families of Emotion Theories:

1. Basic Emotions Theory

Ekman identified 6 universal basic emotions:

  • Surprise
  • Happiness
  • Anger
  • Fear
  • Disgust
  • Sadness

Plutchik extended this to 8 basic emotions in 4 opposing pairs:

  • Joy ↔ Sadness
  • Anger ↔ Fear
  • Trust ↔ Disgust
  • Anticipation ↔ Surprise
2. Dimensional Theory (PAD)

Emotions distributed along three continuous dimensions:

  • Valence: Pleasantness (negative ← → positive)
  • Arousal: Activation/Energy (low ← → high)
  • Dominance: Control (submissive ← → dominant)

Sentiment is the special case where we focus on the valence dimension alone.

Key Distinction: Concrete vs Abstract Words

Concrete words (e.g., "cancer", "bomb") tend to have consistent affective associations across individuals and domains. Abstract words (e.g., "energy", "freedom") show more variation depending on context and user beliefs. Lexicons perform better on concrete vocabulary.

22.3: Creating Lexicons by Human Labeling

Crowdsourcing methodology

The NRC EmoLex was created using a scalable crowdsourcing approach:

Synonym Priming Method
  1. For each target word, retrieve synonyms from a dictionary
  2. Show workers: target word + synonym pair
  3. Ask: "Is the target word associated with [emotion]?"
  4. Collect ratings: not, weakly, moderately, strongly
  5. Aggregate across workers using a consensus threshold

Best-Worst Scaling (BWS)

An alternative to direct rating. Given 4 items, workers select:

  • The BEST item (most associated with the emotion)
  • The WORST item (least associated with the emotion)

BWS reduces cognitive burden and produces more consistent labels than rating scales.

Quality Control: Split-Half Reliability

Divide workers into two random groups, have each label the same words independently, then measure agreement. High correlation indicates stable labels.

22.4: Semi-Supervised Lexicon Induction

Expanding beyond seed words

Approach 1: The Semantic Axis Method (Turney & Littman 2003)

Given small seed sets of positive and negative words for a domain:

Step 1: Compute Pole Centroids
$$\mathbf{V}^+ = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} E(w_i^+), \quad \mathbf{V}^- = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m} E(w_i^-)$$

where $E(w)$ is the word embedding and $n, m$ are seed set sizes.

Step 2: Define Sentiment Axis
$$\mathbf{V}_{\text{axis}} = \mathbf{V}^+ - \mathbf{V}^-$$

This vector points from negative to positive sentiment in embedding space.

Step 3: Score New Words
$$\text{score}(w) = \cos(E(w), \mathbf{V}_{\text{axis}}) = \frac{E(w) \cdot \mathbf{V}_{\text{axis}}}{||E(w)|| \, ||\mathbf{V}_{\text{axis}}||}$$

Ranges from -1 (strongly negative) to +1 (strongly positive).

Approach 2: Label Propagation (Hamilton et al. 2016a)

Use a k-nearest neighbor graph to spread labels from seeds to similar words:

Step 1: Build k-NN Graph with Weights
$$\mathbf{E}_{i,j} = \arccos\left(\frac{\mathbf{w}_i^T\mathbf{w}_j}{||\mathbf{w}_i|| \, ||\mathbf{w}_j||}\right)$$

Edge weights are angular distances (cosine distance).

Step 2: Random Walks

Start random walks from positive and negative seed sets, accumulating how often each word is visited. Higher visit count = stronger association.

Step 3: Normalize Scores
$$\text{score}^+(w_i) = \frac{\text{rawscore}^+(w_i)}{\text{rawscore}^+(w_i) + \text{rawscore}^-(w_i)}$$

Final polarity is in $[0,1]$.

Bootstrap Confidence: Run the algorithm $B=50$ times with resampled k-NN graphs to estimate confidence intervals on scores.

Related Resource: SentiWordNet

Extends WordNet synsets with positive/negative/objective scores. Enables phrase-level sentiment (e.g., "extremely good" is scored differently than "good").

22.5: Supervised Sentiment Learning

Leveraging review ratings as labels

Use reviews with star ratings (IMDb 1-10, Yelp 1-5) as labeled data:

Potts Score (2011)

Normalized likelihood of word given class:

$$\text{PottsScore}(w) = \frac{P(w|c)}{\sum_{c'} P(w|c')}$$

Visualized as "Potts diagrams":

  • J-shape: Strongly positive words appear almost exclusively in high ratings
  • Reverse-J: Strongly negative words appear almost exclusively in low ratings
  • Hump-shape: Weak indicators, spread across ratings

Log Odds Ratio with Informative Dirichlet Prior (Monroe et al. 2008)

For comparing word distributions in two classes:

Raw log-likelihood ratio:

$$\text{llr}(w) = \log\frac{P(w|c)}{\sum_c P(w|c)} - \log\frac{P(w|c')}{\sum_c P(w|c')}$$

Prior-adjusted log odds ratio:

$$\hat{\delta}_w^{(i-j)} = \log\left(\frac{f_w^i + \alpha_w}{n^i + \alpha_0 - (f_w^i + \alpha_w)}\right) - \log\left(\frac{f_w^j + \alpha_w}{n^j + \alpha_0 - (f_w^j + \alpha_w)}\right)$$

Variance:

$$\sigma^2\left(\hat{\delta}_w^{(i-j)}\right) \approx \frac{1}{f_w^i + \alpha_w} + \frac{1}{f_w^j + \alpha_w}$$

Final Z-score:

$$z_w = \frac{\hat{\delta}_w^{(i-j)}}{\sigma\left(\hat{\delta}_w^{(i-j)}\right)}$$

where $f_w^i$ is word frequency in class $i$, $n^i$ is total words in class $i$, $\alpha_w$ is a Dirichlet prior proportional to background frequency, and $\alpha_0$ is the total prior mass.

Why the Prior? Without the Dirichlet prior, rare words get extreme scores. The prior shrinks toward background frequency, producing more stable estimates.

22.6: Lexicon-Based Sentiment Classification

Simple rule-based approach

Once you have a lexicon, classify documents by summing word scores:

Positive Score
$$f^+ = \sum_{w \in \text{pos}} \theta_w^+ \cdot \text{count}(w)$$

where $\theta_w^+$ is the positive weight for word $w$.

Classification with Threshold
$$\text{sentiment} = \begin{cases} + & \text{if } \frac{f^+}{f^-} \gt \lambda \\ - & \text{if } \frac{f^-}{f^+} \gt \lambda \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The threshold $\lambda$ controls decision boundary (often $\lambda=1$).

Challenges:

  • Negation: "not good" should be treated differently than "good"
  • Intensifiers: "very good" vs "somewhat good" have different intensities
  • Sarcasm: "Yeah, great job!" may be ironic
  • Domain Adaptation: "small" is negative for hotels but positive for file sizes

22.8: Entity-Centric Affect

Sentiment toward specific entities

Field & Tsvetkov (2019) extended sentiment to be entity-specific using neural approaches:

Three Dimensions of Entity Affect
  • Sentiment: Overall valence toward the entity
  • Agency: Degree of control/responsibility ascribed
  • Power: Relative power/status relative to other entities

Method: Train BERT models with multi-task regression to predict S/A/P scores for character pairs in fiction. Example from "The Dark Knight":

  • Batman: High agency, high power, mixed sentiment (admired but feared)
  • Joker: High agency, moderate power, negative sentiment
  • Rachel: Lower agency, low power, positive sentiment

This extends lexicon-based sentiment beyond simple polarity to richer semantic characterization.

22.9: Connotation Frames

Verbs and sentiments toward arguments

Verbs encode implicit sentiment toward their semantic role arguments. Compare:

  • "to survive": Implies the subject is a sympathetic victim + positive sentiment toward Role1
  • "to violate": Implies the subject is an aggressor + negative sentiment toward Role2
  • "to triumph": Positive sentiment toward Role1 + high agency
  • "to suffer": Negative sentiment toward Role1 + low agency

Connotation Frame Dimensions:

  • Writer Sentiment toward Role1/2: Does the verb express positive/negative view?
  • Reader Sentiment toward Role1/2: How should the reader feel?
  • Power Differential: Role1 vs Role2 relative power
  • Agency: Which role is acting deliberately?
  • Mental State: States of mind imputed to agents
  • Effect: Consequences for affected parties
  • Value: Moral or practical worth

Example: "The government subjected the protesters to interrogation."

  • Verb: "subjected" (conveys power differential, hardship)
  • Role1 (Government): High power, negative sentiment (coercive)
  • Role2 (Protesters): Low power, sympathetic (victims)
  • Effect: Physical/psychological harm
  • Agency: Role1 is deliberately acting

Lab 1: Semantic Axis Sentiment Scorer

Computing sentiment via embedding geometry

import numpy as np

# Simulated word embeddings (in practice, use word2vec/GloVe)
np.random.seed(42)
embeddings = {
    'good': np.array([0.8, 0.6, 0.3]),
    'excellent': np.array([0.9, 0.7, 0.4]),
    'wonderful': np.array([0.85, 0.65, 0.5]),
    'bad': np.array([-0.7, -0.5, 0.2]),
    'terrible': np.array([-0.8, -0.6, 0.1]),
    'awful': np.array([-0.75, -0.55, 0.15]),
    'movie': np.array([0.1, 0.3, 0.8]),
    'brilliant': np.array([0.7, 0.8, 0.5]),
    'boring': np.array([-0.4, -0.3, 0.6]),
}

positive_seeds = ['good', 'excellent', 'wonderful']
negative_seeds = ['bad', 'terrible', 'awful']

# Compute pole centroids
V_pos = np.mean([embeddings[w] for w in positive_seeds], axis=0)
V_neg = np.mean([embeddings[w] for w in negative_seeds], axis=0)
V_axis = V_pos - V_neg

def sentiment_score(word):
    e = embeddings[word]
    return np.dot(e, V_axis) / (np.linalg.norm(e) * np.linalg.norm(V_axis))

print("Word".ljust(15), "Sentiment Score")
print("-" * 32)
for word in ['movie', 'brilliant', 'boring']:
    print(f"{word.ljust(15)} {sentiment_score(word):.4f}")

Output: Words scoring near +1 are positive, near -1 are negative, near 0 are neutral or domain-specific.

Lab 2: Log Odds Ratio with Dirichlet Prior

Domain-sensitive word polarity extraction

import math

# Simulated word counts from 1-star and 5-star reviews
one_star = {'terrible': 50, 'awful': 30, 'bad': 80, 'worst': 25,
            'great': 5, 'excellent': 2, 'love': 8, 'the': 500}
five_star = {'terrible': 3, 'awful': 2, 'bad': 5, 'worst': 1,
             'great': 60, 'excellent': 45, 'love': 55, 'the': 480}

# Background corpus counts (sum of both)
n_i = sum(one_star.values())
n_j = sum(five_star.values())
alpha_0 = n_i + n_j  # scaled background size

all_words = set(one_star) | set(five_star)
background = {w: one_star.get(w, 0) + five_star.get(w, 0) for w in all_words}
total_bg = sum(background.values())

def log_odds_ratio_dirichlet(word):
    f_i = one_star.get(word, 0)
    f_j = five_star.get(word, 0)
    alpha_w = background[word] / total_bg * alpha_0

    delta = (math.log(f_i + alpha_w) - math.log(n_i + alpha_0 - f_i - alpha_w)
           - math.log(f_j + alpha_w) + math.log(n_j + alpha_0 - f_j - alpha_w))
    variance = 1/(f_i + alpha_w) + 1/(f_j + alpha_w)
    z_score = delta / math.sqrt(variance)
    return z_score

print(f"{'Word':<12} {'Z-score':>10}  Direction")
print("-" * 40)
for word in sorted(all_words, key=lambda w: log_odds_ratio_dirichlet(w)):
    z = log_odds_ratio_dirichlet(word)
    direction = "← 1-star" if z > 0 else "→ 5-star"
    print(f"{word:<12} {z:>10.3f}  {direction}")

Key Insight: The Dirichlet prior prevents rare words like "excellent" from being overweighted just because it appears only in 5-star reviews. It shrinks toward background frequency.

Lab 3: Lexicon-Based Sentiment Classifier

Rule-based classification using polarity lexicons

# Simple rule-based sentiment using a lexicon
positive_lexicon = {'great', 'excellent', 'amazing', 'wonderful',
                    'fantastic', 'love', 'brilliant', 'perfect',
                    'delicious', 'awesome', 'best', 'beautiful'}
negative_lexicon = {'terrible', 'awful', 'horrible', 'bad', 'worst',
                    'hate', 'disgusting', 'boring', 'poor', 'ugly',
                    'disappointing', 'mediocre'}

def lexicon_sentiment(text, threshold=1.0):
    words = text.lower().split()
    f_pos = sum(1 for w in words if w in positive_lexicon)
    f_neg = sum(1 for w in words if w in negative_lexicon)

    if f_neg > 0 and f_pos / max(f_neg, 1) > threshold:
        return '+', f_pos, f_neg
    elif f_pos > 0 and f_neg / max(f_pos, 1) > threshold:
        return '-', f_pos, f_neg
    elif f_pos > 0 and f_neg == 0:
        return '+', f_pos, f_neg
    elif f_neg > 0 and f_pos == 0:
        return '-', f_pos, f_neg
    return '0', f_pos, f_neg

reviews = [
    "This movie is absolutely brilliant and amazing",
    "Terrible food and horrible service worst experience",
    "The hotel was great but the restaurant was mediocre",
    "I love this beautiful wonderful place",
]

for review in reviews:
    sent, pos, neg = lexicon_sentiment(review)
    print(f"[{sent}] (pos={pos}, neg={neg}) {review}")

Expected Output:

[+] (pos=3, neg=0) This movie is absolutely brilliant and amazing
[-] (pos=0, neg=3) Terrible food and horrible service worst experience
[0] (pos=1, neg=1) The hotel was great but the restaurant was mediocre
[+] (pos=3, neg=0) I love this beautiful wonderful place

Socratic Challenge: Test Your Understanding

Q1: In Plutchik's emotion wheel, which of the following pairs are opposites?
Correct! (Joy and Sadness)

Plutchik's model defines 4 opposing pairs:
  • Joy ↔ Sadness
  • Anger ↔ Fear
  • Trust ↔ Disgust
  • Anticipation ↔ Surprise
Q2: What is a key advantage of the log odds ratio with Dirichlet prior compared to simple raw frequency ratios?
Correct! (It better handles rare words by shrinking toward background frequency)

The Dirichlet prior $\alpha_w$ is proportional to background word frequency. This prevents rare words from getting extreme scores just because they appear only in one class. Without the prior, a word appearing 5 times in class A and 0 times in class B would get an infinite score, which is unstable.
Q3: In the semantic axis method, what does the vector $\mathbf{V}_{\text{axis}} = \mathbf{V}^+ - \mathbf{V}^-$ represent?
Correct! (The direction from negative to positive pole)

$\mathbf{V}^+$ is the centroid of positive seed words, $\mathbf{V}^-$ is the centroid of negative seed words. Subtracting gives a vector pointing from negative toward positive. Any word's projection onto this axis gives its sentiment score via cosine similarity.
Q4: In connotation frames, what sentiment does the verb "survive" express toward its subject (Role1)?
Correct! (Positive/sympathetic sentiment)

"Survive" frames the subject as a victim of circumstances who managed to endure. This frames the subject sympathetically — readers are meant to empathize with the subject's struggle. By contrast, verbs like "violate" express negative sentiment toward the object, and "perpetrate" suggests blame toward the subject.

Historical Context & Modern Bridges

Foundations (1950s-1990s)

From psychology to computational linguistics

  • Osgood et al. (1957): "The Measurement of Meaning" — proposed the Semantic Differential, establishing that word meaning can be captured on evaluative, potency, and activity dimensions. This foundational work showed that meaning is not purely referential but includes affective components.
  • Hatzivassiloglou & McKeown (1997): Discovered that adjectives connected by conjunctions (and/or) tend to have the same polarity. "Strong and powerful" (both positive), not "strong and weak." This became the basis for bootstrapping polarity classifiers.

Emergence of Sentiment Analysis (1990s-2000s)

Computational methods take center stage

  • Wiebe (1994): Formalized "subjectivity detection" — distinguishing subjective (opinion-bearing) from objective (factual) sentences. This became crucial for understanding when to apply sentiment analysis.
  • Das & Chen (2001): Coined the term "sentiment" for NLP and applied it to mining stock prediction from news. This was one of the first applications showing that computational sentiment could have real-world financial impact.
  • Hu & Liu (2004): Created one of the first comprehensive polarity lexicons, becoming a standard resource. Manual compilation of 2,006 positive and 4,783 negative words with high precision.

The Lexicon Era (2000s-2010s)

Building and refining resources

  • Wilson et al. (2005): MPQA Subjectivity Lexicon — expanded to include clues for recognizing subjectivity and polarity together, with distinctions for direct vs. indirect subjective expressions.
  • Turney & Littman (2003): Semantic axis method using PMI (Pointwise Mutual Information) — showed that polarity could be induced semi-automatically from unlabeled data via seed words and similarity metrics.
  • Mohammad et al. (2013): NRC EmoLex — crowdsourced emotion annotations for 14,000 words using the "synonym priming" methodology, providing association strengths for Plutchik's 8 basic emotions.

Neural & Contextual Era (2015+)

Beyond static lexicons

  • Socher et al. (2013): Recursive Neural Networks (RvNN) for composing sentiment at the phrase level — realized that "good movie" is not just a lookup, but compositional (good + movie affects the sentiment).
  • Mohammad & Turney (2013): NRC Lexicons generalized to valence-arousal-dominance (VAD) — added emotional intensity and control dimensions beyond binary positive/negative.
  • Devlin et al. (2019) / BERT: Transformer-based contextual embeddings enabled "contextual sentiment" — the same word has different sentiment in different contexts (e.g., "sick" is positive slang but negative when literal).
  • Field & Tsvetkov (2019): Entity-centric affect with BERT regression — linked sentiment to specific semantic role arguments, enabling fine-grained analysis of who feels what toward whom.

Modern Challenges & Directions (2020+)

Beyond English, beyond polarity

  • Cross-Lingual Sentiment: Extending lexicons to 100+ languages without manual translation. Methods like multilingual BERT and zero-shot transfer enable sentiment analysis in low-resource languages.
  • Fine-Grained Sentiment: Moving beyond document/sentence level to aspect-based sentiment (e.g., "great food, terrible service"). Target-dependent models use relation extraction to connect sentiments to what they're about.
  • Figurative Language: Sarcasm, irony, and metaphor violate lexicon assumptions. "Dying laughing" is positive, not negative. Recent work uses pragmatic reasoning and context to resolve these.
  • Social Context: Recognizing that sentiment is subjective and community-dependent. What counts as positive in one community may be neutral or negative in another. Stance detection and perspective mining address this.
  • Emotion & Reasoning: Combining sentiment with causal reasoning and event extraction to understand "why" emotions occur, not just "what" emotions are expressed.

Key Takeaway

From static to dynamic sentiment

The trajectory of sentiment analysis mirrors NLP itself: from hand-crafted lexicons and rules, to semi-supervised bootstrapping, to supervised learning on annotated corpora, to representation learning with embeddings and neural networks, and finally to contextual understanding with transformers. Yet lexicons remain valuable — they're interpretable, don't require massive training data, and work in low-resource settings. Modern systems often blend lexicon-based heuristics with neural approaches for robustness.
