CHAPTER 3

N-gram Language Models

"The future is determined entirely by the immediate past." — Claude Shannon's Markov Assumption

N-gram language models capture the probability of word sequences using a simple but powerful idea: predict the next word based only on the previous n-1 words. Despite the rise of neural networks and transformers, n-gram models remain foundational—they're still used in speech recognition, machine translation, and mobile keyboards. In this chapter, we'll master the mathematics of probability estimation, smoothing techniques, and perplexity measurement, then see how these ideas extend to modern neural architectures.

Feynman Bridge

The Autocomplete Game

Let's start with what you already understand: your smartphone's autocomplete feature. When you type a few words, your phone guesses what comes next. That's an n-gram model in action. Here are three analogies that capture the essence:

Analogy 1: Phone Keyboard Prediction Your phone predicts the next word based on what words typically follow the current one. If you type "I love", it might suggest "you" or "pizza" because those words frequently follow "love" in the texting patterns it has learned. An n-gram model works exactly this way—it counts how often word sequences appear in a training corpus and uses those frequencies to predict what comes next. Type "I love" a million times followed by "pizza", and "pizza" becomes the top suggestion.
Analogy 2: Weather Forecasting by Pattern Imagine predicting tomorrow's weather by only looking at the last 1-2 days. If it was sunny yesterday and sunny today, you guess sunny tomorrow. If it was rainy yesterday and today is rainy, you guess rain tomorrow. You ignore the entire history from last year, last month, or last week. That's the Markov assumption—the future depends only on the recent past, not the entire history. Bigrams look at 1 word back, trigrams at 2 words back. This is why n-gram models are fast: they only look at a tiny window of context.
Analogy 3: Restaurant Menu Pricing Imagine a restaurant that's never served "dragon fruit sorbet." Instead of pricing it at $0 (impossible), they estimate a small price based on similar dishes—tropical fruit smoothies, sorbets made from common fruits, etc. That's smoothing in language models. We give every unseen event (every word sequence that didn't appear in training) a small probability so nothing is "impossible." Without smoothing, any unseen bigram breaks the model. With smoothing, rare events get a fair chance.
Technical Ascent

N-grams and the Mathematics of Probability

5.1 N-grams and the Chain Rule

The foundation of all language modeling is the chain rule of probability. To compute the probability of an entire sequence of words, we can decompose it into conditional probabilities:

$$P(w_1:n) = P(w_1) \cdot P(w_2 | w_1) \cdot P(w_3 | w_1:2) \cdots P(w_n | w_1:n-1)$$

This is exact but impractical—to estimate P(wₙ|w₁:ₙ₋₁), we'd need to count every possible sequence of n-1 words, which is exponential in the history length. The Markov assumption saves us:

$$P(w_n | w_1:n-1) \approx P(w_n | w_{n-1})$$

For a bigram model (n=2), we only look at the immediately preceding word. For a trigram (n=3), we look at two preceding words. The general n-gram Markov assumption is:

$$P(w_n | w_1:n-1) \approx P(w_n | w_{n-N+1:n-1})$$

This reduces the problem from exponential to polynomial in n.

5.2 Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Given a training corpus, the most intuitive way to estimate bigram probabilities is to count:

$$P(w_n | w_{n-1}) = \frac{C(w_{n-1} \, w_n)}{C(w_{n-1})}$$

where C(wₙ₋₁ wₙ) is the count of the bigram wₙ₋₁ wₙ in the corpus, and C(wₙ₋₁) is the count of wₙ₋₁ appearing as a context word. This is Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)—it chooses the probabilities that maximize the likelihood of the observed training data.

Worked Example: Suppose our tiny corpus is: "I am Sam. Sam I am. I do not like."

Bigram counts (treating sentence boundaries carefully):

  • C(<s> I) = 2
  • C(I am) = 2
  • C(am Sam) = 1
  • C(Sam I) = 1
  • C(I do) = 1
  • C(do not) = 1
  • C(not like) = 1
  • C(like </s>) = 1

P(Sam | I) = C(I Sam) / C(I) = 0 / 3 = 0. This is the zero probability problem — a real issue we'll fix with smoothing!

5.3 Perplexity

How do we evaluate whether our language model is good? The standard metric is perplexity:

$$\text{PP}(W) = P(w_1 w_2 \ldots w_N)^{-1/N} = \sqrt[N]{\frac{1}{P(w_1 w_2 \ldots w_N)}}$$

Equivalently, using log probabilities:

$$\text{PP}(W) = 2^{-\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \log_2 P(w_i | w_1:i-1)}$$

Intuition: Perplexity is the geometric mean of 1/P for each word. A lower perplexity means the model assigns higher probabilities to the test set—better predictions. You can think of it as the weighted average branching factor: if perplexity is 100, the model is as confused as if there were 100 equally likely words at each position (even though the actual probabilities vary).

Context: A bigram model trained on English news has perplexity ~200 on test news. GPT-4 achieves perplexity under 10 on many benchmarks. Shannon's estimate of English entropy is about 1.3 bits per character, corresponding to perplexity ≈ 2.4 characters—a lower bound on any model.

5.4 Smoothing Techniques

The zero probability problem is severe: if a bigram never appears in training, MLE gives it P = 0. But unseen sequences absolutely will occur in test data. We need to assign non-zero probability to unseen events. Here are the main techniques:

Laplace Smoothing (Add-One):

$$P_{\text{Laplace}}(w_n | w_{n-1}) = \frac{C(w_{n-1} \, w_n) + 1}{C(w_{n-1}) + V}$$

where V is the vocabulary size. We pretend we saw every possible bigram exactly once extra. This is simple but tends to oversmooth (assign too much probability to unseen events).

Add-k Smoothing:

$$P_{\text{Add-k}}(w_n | w_{n-1}) = \frac{C(w_{n-1} \, w_n) + k}{C(w_{n-1}) + kV}$$

Same idea, but we add k (a tunable constant, usually 0 < k < 1) instead of 1. Smaller k is less aggressive smoothing; larger k is more aggressive. You'd tune k on a development set.

Stupid Backoff (Brants et al., 2007):

$$S(w_n | w_{n-N+1:n-1}) = \begin{cases} \frac{C(w_{n-N+1:n})}{C(w_{n-N+1:n-1})} & \text{if count} > 0 \\ 0.4 \times S(w_n | w_{n-N+2:n-1}) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Stupid Backoff doesn't compute true probabilities (doesn't sum to 1) but computes a "score" that's good for ranking. If a trigram is unseen, fall back to bigram with a discount factor (0.4). If the bigram is unseen, fall back to unigram with another discount. This is used in Google's production systems because it scales to web-sized n-gram tables.

Kneser-Ney Smoothing (1995):

$$P_{\text{KN}}(w_n | w_{n-1}) = \frac{\max(C(w_{n-1} \, w_n) - d, 0)}{C(w_{n-1})} + \lambda(w_{n-1}) \cdot P_{\text{continuation}}(w_n)$$

where the continuation probability is:

$$P_{\text{continuation}}(w_n) = \frac{|\{ w : C(w, w_n) > 0 \}|}{|\{ (w', w'') : C(w', w'') > 0 \}|}$$

Kneser-Ney is considered the best smoothing method for n-gram models. Instead of using raw frequency, it uses continuation probability: how many different contexts does wₙ appear in? A word that appears in many diverse contexts is likely useful even if the specific bigram is unseen. The parameter d (usually 0.75) is the discount—we subtract it from seen counts to reserve probability mass for unseen events. λ is a normalization factor.

5.5 Entropy and Cross-Entropy

For deeper theoretical understanding, we need entropy. Shannon entropy measures the average surprise:

$$H(X) = -\sum_x P(x) \log_2 P(x)$$

Cross-entropy measures the average bits needed to encode using a wrong distribution q instead of true distribution p:

$$H(p, q) = -\sum_x p(x) \log_2 q(x)$$

When we compute perplexity on a test set, we're computing cross-entropy between the true test distribution and our model's predicted distribution. The relationship is:

$$\text{Perplexity} = 2^{H(p, q)}$$

Lower cross-entropy (lower perplexity) means our model q is closer to the true distribution p.

Code Implementation

Building an N-gram Language Model from Scratch

7.1 Complete Python Implementation

Here's a full, annotated implementation of an n-gram language model with multiple smoothing options: 

import collections
import random
import math

class NgramLM:
    """N-gram Language Model with multiple smoothing options."""

    def __init__(self, n=2):
        # Order of the n-gram (2=bigram, 3=trigram, etc.)
        self.n = n
        # Store raw n-gram counts: {(context_tuple, word): count}
        self.ngram_counts = collections.Counter()
        # Store (n-1)-gram context counts for denominator: {context_tuple: count}
        self.context_counts = collections.Counter()
        # Vocabulary set for smoothing calculations
        self.vocab = set()

    def train(self, corpus):
        """Train on tokenized corpus (list of sentences, each a list of tokens)."""
        for sentence in corpus:
            # Add start tokens () and end tokens () for proper probability modeling
            padded = ['<s>'] * (self.n - 1) + sentence + ['</s>']
            self.vocab.update(sentence + ['</s>'])

            # Iterate through the padded sentence to extract n-grams
            for i in range(self.n - 1, len(padded)):
                # Extract the (n-1) context words before position i
                context = tuple(padded[i-self.n+1:i])
                # Extract the target word at position i
                word = padded[i]
                # Increment the count for this (context, word) pair
                self.ngram_counts[(context, word)] += 1
                # Increment the count for seeing this context
                self.context_counts[context] += 1

    def prob(self, word, context, smoothing='none', k=1):
        """Compute P(word | context) with optional smoothing."""
        # Ensure context is a tuple for dictionary lookup
        context = tuple(context)
        # Get the count of this (context, word) pair in training data
        count = self.ngram_counts[(context, word)]
        # Get the total count of this context in training data
        total = self.context_counts[context]
        # Vocabulary size for smoothing
        V = len(self.vocab)

        # Apply the selected smoothing method
        if smoothing == 'none':
            # Raw MLE: P = count / total (may be zero)
            return count / total if total > 0 else 0
        elif smoothing == 'laplace':
            # Laplace (Add-1): add 1 to count and V to total
            return (count + 1) / (total + V)
        elif smoothing == 'add_k':
            # Add-k: add k to count and kV to total (k < 1 is gentler)
            return (count + k) / (total + k * V)

    def perplexity(self, test_corpus, smoothing='laplace'):
        """Compute perplexity on test corpus: PP = 2^(-1/N * sum log P)."""
        # Accumulate log probabilities across all test words
        log_prob_sum = 0
        # Count total words in test set
        N = 0

        for sentence in test_corpus:
            # Pad the test sentence the same way as training
            padded = ['<s>'] * (self.n - 1) + sentence + ['</s>']

            # Compute probability for each word given its context
            for i in range(self.n - 1, len(padded)):
                context = tuple(padded[i-self.n+1:i])
                word = padded[i]
                # Get probability with smoothing to avoid log(0)
                p = self.prob(word, context, smoothing=smoothing)
                # Add log probability (log_2 for bits)
                log_prob_sum += math.log2(p) if p > 0 else -float('inf')
                N += 1

        # Perplexity = 2^(-1/N * sum log_2 P)
        return 2 ** (-log_prob_sum / N)

    def generate(self, max_len=20, temperature=1.0):
        """Sample a sentence from the model using temperature-scaled softmax."""
        # Initialize context with start symbols
        context = ['<s>'] * (self.n - 1)
        result = []

        for _ in range(max_len):
            # Build a distribution over candidate next words
            candidates = {}
            ctx = tuple(context[-((self.n-1)):])

            # Find all words that have appeared after this context
            for (c, w), count in self.ngram_counts.items():
                if c == ctx:
                    candidates[w] = count

            # If no candidates, generation ends
            if not candidates:
                break

            # Apply temperature scaling: higher T = more uniform, lower T = more peaked
            words, counts = zip(*candidates.items())
            # Divide log counts by temperature (log-space softmax)
            logits = [math.log(c) / temperature for c in counts]
            # Subtract max for numerical stability
            max_l = max(logits)
            exps = [math.exp(l - max_l) for l in logits]
            # Normalize to get probabilities
            total = sum(exps)
            probs = [e / total for e in exps]

            # Sample a word according to the probability distribution
            word = random.choices(words, weights=probs, k=1)[0]

            # Stop if we generate the end-of-sentence token
            if word == '</s>':
                break

            result.append(word)
            context.append(word)

        # Return the generated sequence as a string
        return ' '.join(result)

7.2 Usage Example: Train, Evaluate, and Generate

# Training corpus: list of sentences, each sentence is list of tokens
train_corpus = [
    ['the', 'cat', 'sat', 'on', 'the', 'mat'],
    ['the', 'dog', 'ran', 'in', 'the', 'park'],
    ['the', 'cat', 'and', 'dog', 'played'],
]

# Create and train a bigram model
model = NgramLM(n=2)
model.train(train_corpus)

# Evaluate on test data with different smoothing methods
test_corpus = [['the', 'cat', 'ran']]
pp_unsmoothed = model.perplexity(test_corpus, smoothing='none')
pp_laplace = model.perplexity(test_corpus, smoothing='laplace')
pp_add_k = model.perplexity(test_corpus, smoothing='add_k')

print(f"Unsmoothed: {pp_unsmoothed:.2f}")
print(f"Laplace: {pp_laplace:.2f}")
print(f"Add-k: {pp_add_k:.2f}")

# Generate text with different temperatures
print(model.generate(max_len=10, temperature=0.5))  # More deterministic
print(model.generate(max_len=10, temperature=1.5))  # More random
Modern Bridge

From Counting to Attention: N-grams to Transformers

N-gram models seem ancient compared to GPT-4 and Claude, but the core ideas are still relevant:

The Markov Assumption in Neural Models

N-gram models enforce a strong Markov assumption: predict the next word using only the previous n-1 words. Transformer models don't enforce this—the attention mechanism can look at any token in the context. Yet even with unlimited context, transformers learn something n-gram-like: attention often concentrates on recent tokens, and the model learns shortcuts that depend mostly on local context. The key difference is that transformers learn what to attend to (via attention weights), while n-grams force fixed-window dependencies.

Perplexity is Still King

When researchers report performance of GPT-4, Llama 2, or Claude, the headline metric is perplexity on standard benchmarks like WikiText, LAMBADA, or Penn Treebank. The formula hasn't changed:

$$\text{PP} = 2^{-\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \log_2 P_{\text{model}}(w_i | w_1:i-1)}$$

Lower perplexity still means the model assigns higher probability to natural text. Scaling laws in deep learning are often expressed as how perplexity decreases with more parameters and data—a direct continuation of the insight from n-gram models.

Smoothing's Echo in Neural Handling of Rare Words

Kneser-Ney smoothing uses continuation probability—a word's usefulness is tied to how many diverse contexts it appears in, not its raw frequency. Neural language models achieve something similar through subword tokenization: instead of word-level tokens, we use BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) or SentencePiece, which breaks rare words into frequent subunits. An unseen word can still be represented and predicted because its subwords are common. The mechanism differs, but the goal is identical: assign reasonable probability to unseen sequences.

Stupid Backoff at Google Scale

Stupid Backoff isn't just theoretical—it's used in production at Google for web search ranking, speech recognition rescoring, and other applications. Why? It's fast, doesn't require true probability distributions, and scales to terabyte-scale n-gram tables on billions of web documents. Even with modern neural systems, large-scale systems often use hybrid approaches: neural models for quality + n-gram backoff for coverage.

N-grams in Modern Applications

Mobile keyboard prediction: Still uses n-grams (often bigram or trigram) with smoothing. It's fast, doesn't require a GPU, and updates quickly with user data.

Speech recognition lattice rescoring: The acoustic model produces a lattice of possible word sequences. Language models score these sequences. State-of-the-art systems use neural LMs, but n-gram models still appear as fallback components.

Machine translation: Neural models dominate, but n-gram language model scores are often included as features in reranking systems.

Spell checking and autocompletion: Both often rely on n-gram models, sometimes trained on domain-specific corpora.

Attention as Learned N-gram Patterns

Here's a surprising connection: you can view attention weights in transformers as soft, learned n-gram patterns. When a transformer attends to the previous few tokens with high weight, it's learning something like the dependency structure an n-gram model would capture. The difference is that the model learns these weights from data and can make them task-dependent, whereas n-grams force fixed patterns. Probing studies show that transformers do learn n-gram-like patterns in lower layers and more complex syntactic/semantic patterns in higher layers.

The takeaway: N-gram models are not obsolete. They're a foundational technique whose core ideas (conditional probability, smoothing, perplexity measurement, backoff) appear throughout modern NLP, from subword tokenization to attention mechanisms to evaluation metrics.

Historical Context

A Brief History of Language Modeling

Claude Shannon (1948): Information Theory

Claude Shannon's foundational paper, "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," introduced entropy as a measure of information. Shannon estimated the entropy of English at roughly 1.3 bits per character based on human experiments (people guessing the next character given context). This means English text is highly predictable—the true entropy is a lower bound on the perplexity of any language model. Modern neural models are nowhere close to Shannon's theoretical limit, but it gives us a target.

Andrei Markov (1913): Markov Chains

Long before computers, Russian mathematician Andrei Markov studied sequences of states where the next state depends only on the current state. He famously analyzed the first-order transitions in Eugene Onegin, Pushkin's poem, counting how often consonants followed vowels. This memoryless property—the future depends only on the present, not the full history—is the Markov assumption underlying n-gram models.

Good-Turing (1953): Frequency Smoothing

During World War II, Alan Turing and I.J. Good developed frequency smoothing techniques to decrypt Enigma messages. They faced the same problem we discussed: unseen n-grams. Good-Turing smoothing estimates the probability of unseen events by looking at frequencies of rarely seen events. If you see 1000 bigrams once, 50 bigrams twice, etc., you can estimate how many bigrams you probably missed. This elegant idea is still used in statistical NLP.

Kneser & Ney (1995): Absolute Discounting with Continuation

Reinhard Kneser and Hermann Ney published "Improved Backing-off for M-gram Language Models," which became the de facto standard smoothing method for n-gram models. Their insight: don't just count how often a word appears; count how many different predecessors it has (continuation probability). A rare word that appears in many contexts is more predictive than a common word that always appears in the same context. Kneser-Ney dominated for decades until neural models took over.

Stupid Backoff (2007): Scaling to the Web

Thorsten Brants, Ashok C. Popat, Peng Xu, Franz J. Och, and Jeffrey Dean published "Large Language Models in Machine Translation" at ACL 2007. They showed that with access to massive web-scale n-gram counts (billions of n-grams from terabytes of text), you could build effective language models using a deliberately "stupid" backoff method (not computing true probabilities) that was orders of magnitude faster than Kneser-Ney smoothing. This paper showed that, in the era of big data, simple methods with more data beat complex methods with less data.

Neural Language Models (2003–Present): Deep Learning Takes Over

In 2003, Yoshua Bengio et al. proposed "A Neural Probabilistic Language Model," using a feedforward neural network to predict words. Since then, the field has evolved: RNNs (2013), LSTMs (1997, but popularized in 2013), Transformers (2017), and GPT-scale models (2018+). Yet even now, papers on scaling laws and architecture still measure progress using perplexity—the exact same metric developed for n-gram models.

Socratic Knowledge Check

Guiding Questions: The Pizza Recognition Failure

Scenario: A restaurant's speech recognition system uses a trigram model trained on voice orders collected over the past year. When a new customer says "I want to order a pepperoni pizza," the system transcribes it as "I want to order a pepperoni piece of" because "pepperoni pizza" never appeared in the training data (people usually just say "pepperoni" or "pizza", not both). The trigram model assigns zero probability to "pepperoni pizza", making it an impossible phrase.

Q1: Why does the trigram model assign zero probability to "pepperoni pizza" even though both words are common individually?

The trigram model only learns sequences of exactly 3 words that appeared in the training corpus. Even if "pepperoni" and "pizza" are individually frequent, the specific trigram (..., "pepperoni", "pizza") was never observed. MLE gives it probability 0. The model can't generalize from "I saw 'pepperoni' and 'pizza' separately" to "the combination must be possible." N-gram models are brittle this way—they can only reproduce patterns they've seen.

Q2: Which smoothing technique would help most here, and why? Compare Laplace vs. Kneser-Ney.

Laplace smoothing would help by assigning a small probability (1/(count+V)) to the unseen "pepperoni pizza" trigram. But it would also smooth away distinctions between very common and rare trigrams, potentially degrading overall performance.

Kneser-Ney smoothing is better because it's more targeted. It checks: how many different words can follow "pepperoni"? If many words follow it (high continuation probability for the second word), Kneser-Ney assigns higher probability to "pepperoni pizza" even if that exact trigram is unseen. Conversely, if "pizza" rarely follows other words, it gets lower probability. This smoothing respects the contours of the data rather than applying blanket add-one smoothing.

Q3: How does the Markov assumption limit the model here? Would a 5-gram model fix the problem?

The Markov assumption says "the next word depends only on the previous n-1 words." A trigram looks at 2 words back. This is limiting because it can't capture longer-range dependencies. Maybe "pepperoni pizza" is more common in certain contexts ("I would like to order a pepperoni pizza") than others ("is that pepperoni pizza?"), but the trigram model doesn't see that difference.

A 5-gram model (looking 4 words back) might see "order pepperoni pizza" more often and thus assign higher probability. But it won't fundamentally solve the problem. If "order a pepperoni pizza" never appeared, the 5-gram still fails. You'd need a 4-gram, then a 3-gram, then eventually backoff to a bigram or unigram. And if even the bigram "pepperoni pizza" is unseen, you still need smoothing. The Markov assumption trades flexibility for tractability. Neural models (like Transformers) don't have this limitation—they can see the entire context window.

Q4: Calculate: If C("pepperoni pizza")=0, C("pepperoni")=50, and V=10,000, what probability does Laplace smoothing assign to P("pizza" | "pepperoni")?

Using Laplace smoothing:

$$P_{\text{Laplace}}(\text{pizza} | \text{pepperoni}) = \frac{C(\text{pepperoni pizza}) + 1}{C(\text{pepperoni}) + V} = \frac{0 + 1}{50 + 10000} = \frac{1}{10050} \approx 0.0000995$$

That's about 0.01%, or 1 in 10,000 chance. It's very small, but non-zero. In contrast, the unsmoothed probability would be 0/50 = 0. Laplace gives the unseen bigram a fighting chance, but maybe too small of a chance—it's the same probability assigned to any other unseen word following "pepperoni", even if some are more plausible (like "slice") than others (like "motorcycle").

Q5: Why might a neural language model handle this better than any n-gram model, regardless of smoothing?

A neural language model learns dense representations (embeddings) for words. "Pepperoni" and "pizza" are represented as vectors in a high-dimensional space where similar words are close together. The neural model learns that these words often co-occur and assigns high probability to their combination even if the exact sequence is rare or unseen in training.

More fundamentally, neural models can look at unbounded context (via attention in Transformers). They might learn: "In the context of 'I want to order', almost any food word is possible. Pepperoni and pizza have similar semantic roles (both food), and combining them is natural even if unseen." Smoothing is a band-aid for the brittleness of n-grams; neural models address the brittleness itself by learning generalizable representations and patterns.
