Chapter 5: Embeddings

"Words are for meaning; once you get the meaning, you can forget the words."

— Zhuangzi (translated)

This chapter introduces embeddings — vector representations of word meaning learned from distributional patterns in text. From sparse co-occurrence matrices to dense word2vec, we explore how machines capture semantic similarity. Embeddings are the foundation of modern NLP: they encode meaning as geometry, allowing us to compute similarity, perform analogy reasoning, and transfer semantic knowledge across tasks.

Feynman Words as Addresses in Meaning-Space

Analogy 1: City Neighborhoods

Imagine every word lives at a specific address in a vast city. Words with similar meanings live in the same neighborhood: "cat," "dog," and "puppy" are on the same block; "laptop," "keyboard," and "mouse" (the device) live across town. An embedding is just the GPS coordinates of a word in this meaning-city. Words in similar neighborhoods (close coordinates) have similar meanings. The farther apart two words live, the less semantically related they are.

Analogy 2: You Are Known by the Company You Keep

The distributional hypothesis says we can figure out what a word means by looking at which words appear near it. If you see "ongchoi" always near "sauteed," "garlic," "rice," and "delicious," you'd guess it's a food — even if you've never heard of it. This is exactly how computers learn word meaning: by counting neighbors. Context reveals character. A word surrounded by cooking terms belongs to the kitchen; a word surrounded by mathematical symbols belongs to mathematics.

Analogy 3: The Perfume Wheel (Cosine Similarity)

Imagine describing perfumes by their ingredients: floral, citrus, woody, musky. Each perfume is a point in "ingredient space." To compare two perfumes, you don't care about the total amount of ingredients (one might be a sample, the other a full bottle), only the direction of their profiles. Cosine similarity measures this directional similarity — ignoring magnitude, focusing on the mix. A concentrated extract and a diluted spray can have identical "flavor profiles" if their ingredient ratios match.

Analogy 4: Word Arithmetic

The most magical property of embeddings: king - man + woman ≈ queen. It's as if the vector from "man" to "king" encodes the concept of "royalty," and you can apply that same transformation to "woman" to get "queen." The embedding space has learned abstract relationships as geometric directions. These directions are like hidden features of meaning that the network discovered through exposure to text.

Technical The Mathematics of Meaning

4.1 Lexical Semantics Foundations

Before diving into embeddings, we establish key concepts:

  • Lemma vs wordform: "walk," "walks," "walking" are wordforms of the lemma "walk"
  • Polysemy: One word, multiple meanings. "Bank" can mean financial institution or river bank
  • Word senses: The individual meanings of a polysemous word
  • Synonymy: Different words, same meaning ("couch" ≈ "sofa")
  • Similarity: Graded semantic closeness (not binary)
  • Relatedness: Words can be related without being synonymous ("doctor" and "hospital")
  • Connotation: Emotional associations. Osgood (1957) proposed three dimensions: valence (pleasant–unpleasant), arousal (calm–excited), dominance (weak–strong)

4.2 The Distributional Hypothesis

"Words that occur in similar contexts have similar meanings." (Firth 1957, Harris 1954)

This foundational principle drives all distributional semantics. The core mechanism:

  • Count word co-occurrences in a large corpus
  • Build a co-occurrence matrix M of shape |V| × |V|, where V is vocabulary size
  • M[i,j] = count of times word i appears within ±k words of word j
  • Row i is the context vector for word i
  • Words with similar row patterns have similar meaning

Context window: The range of neighboring words considered. A window of ±4 means we look 4 words left and 4 words right. Smaller windows capture syntactic information; larger windows capture topical similarity.

4.3 Cosine Similarity

The standard metric for comparing vectors in high-dimensional spaces.

$$\text{cosine}(v, w) = \frac{v \cdot w}{|v| |w|} = \frac{\sum_i v_i w_i}{\sqrt{\sum_i v_i^2} \cdot \sqrt{\sum_i w_i^2}}$$

Intuition: Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors. It equals 1 when vectors point in the same direction, 0 when perpendicular, and -1 when opposite. It's scale-invariant: [1, 1] and [10, 10] have cosine similarity 1.

Worked Example: From the text, cosine(cherry, information) = 0.018 (nearly orthogonal, low semantic similarity), while cosine(digital, information) = 0.996 (nearly parallel, high similarity).

4.4 Word2vec (Skip-gram with Negative Sampling)

Mikolov et al. (2013) revolutionized embeddings with a clever training approach. Instead of computing dense SVD, word2vec uses self-supervised learning: the training signal comes free from running text.

Core Idea: Train a classifier to answer "Is word c likely to appear near word w?" Use the learned classifier weights as embeddings.

Model Structure:

  • Two embedding matrices: W (target words, shape |V| × d) and C (context words, |V| × d)
  • d is embedding dimension (typically 50–300)
  • For word w and context word c, predict: P(+|w,c) = σ(c·w)
  • σ is the sigmoid function: σ(x) = 1/(1+e^(-x))
$$P(+|w,c) = \sigma(c \cdot w) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-c \cdot w}}$$

Negative Sampling: For computational efficiency, instead of updating all |V| words, sample k noise words from a noise distribution.

$$L = -[\log \sigma(c_{\text{pos}} \cdot w) + \sum_{i=1}^k \log \sigma(-c_{\text{neg}_i} \cdot w)]$$

This loss has two parts: (1) make the true context word look similar to w, (2) make noise words look dissimilar.

Noise Distribution: Sample negative words with probability proportional to count(word)^0.75. This exponent prevents common words from dominating negative samples.

Gradient Updates: Backprop through the loss updates both w and c vectors. After training:

  • Some systems use w (target embeddings)
  • Some use c (context embeddings)
  • Some concatenate w + c

4.5 Other Static Embeddings

FastText (Bojanowski et al. 2017): Instead of one embedding per word, embed subword n-grams. The embedding of "jumping" is the sum of bigram embeddings: . This handles unknown words gracefully by composing from subword pieces.

GloVe (Pennington et al. 2014): Global Vectors. Explicitly incorporate global co-occurrence statistics. Objective combines corpus statistics (how often words co-occur) with semantic properties (similar words should have similar embeddings). GloVe embeddings often achieve comparable or better performance than word2vec.

4.6 Semantic Properties: The Geometry of Meaning

Parallelogram Model for Analogies: Given "a is to b as c is to ?" solve for the closest word d*:

$$d^* = \arg\min_d \text{distance}(d, b - a + c)$$

Classic Examples:

  • king - man + woman ≈ queen (gender analogy)
  • Paris - France + Italy ≈ Rome (capital analogy)
  • good - bad + dark ≈ light (semantic opposition)

Co-occurrence Types:

  • Syntagmatic (first-order): Words that appear together. "coffee" co-occurs with "cup," "hot," "drink." Larger context windows capture syntagmatic relations.
  • Paradigmatic (second-order): Words that appear in similar contexts. "coffee," "tea," and "juice" appear in similar contexts. Smaller context windows capture paradigmatic relations.

4.7 Bias in Embeddings

Embeddings trained on text corpora absorb and amplify societal biases present in that text.

Allocational Harm: The system denies resources or opportunities to protected groups. Example: A hiring algorithm using biased embeddings might downrank female candidates.

Representational Harm: The system perpetuates negative stereotypes. Example: Man:Computer_Programmer :: Woman:Homemaker, embedding and reinforcing occupational stereotypes.

Debiasing Attempts: Researchers have tried post-hoc correction (hard debiasing, soft debiasing), regularization during training, and different training data. However, debiasing embeddings is incomplete—it's better to address bias at the data collection stage.

4.8 Evaluation

Intrinsic Evaluation: Evaluate embeddings directly.

  • Word similarity tasks: WordSim-353, SimLex-999. Correlate model cosine similarity with human judgments.
  • Analogy tasks: Evaluate how well the parallelogram method works on structured analogy datasets.

Extrinsic Evaluation: Evaluate embeddings by their utility in downstream tasks (POS tagging, sentiment analysis, semantic role labeling). Extrinsic evaluation is more meaningful but slower.

Code Implementation Walkthrough

6.1 Building Co-occurrence Vectors in Python

This function builds a sparse word-context co-occurrence matrix from a corpus:

import numpy as np
from collections import Counter, defaultdict

def build_cooccurrence_matrix(corpus, vocab, window=4):
    """Build a word-context co-occurrence matrix from corpus.

    Args:
        corpus: List of sentences (strings)
        vocab: List of vocabulary words
        window: Context window size (±window words)

    Returns:
        M: Co-occurrence matrix of shape (len(vocab), len(vocab))
    """
    # Create word-to-index mapping
    word2idx = {w: i for i, w in enumerate(vocab)}
    V = len(vocab)

    # Initialize sparse matrix
    matrix = np.zeros((V, V))

    # Process each sentence
    for sentence in corpus:
        tokens = sentence.lower().split()

        # For each target word
        for i, target in enumerate(tokens):
            if target not in word2idx:
                continue

            # Count words within ±window
            for j in range(max(0, i-window), min(len(tokens), i+window+1)):
                if i == j:  # Skip the target itself
                    continue

                context = tokens[j]
                if context in word2idx:
                    # Increment co-occurrence count
                    matrix[word2idx[target]][word2idx[context]] += 1

    return matrix

def cosine_similarity(v, w):
    """Compute cosine similarity between two vectors.

    Args:
        v, w: numpy arrays or lists

    Returns:
        similarity: float in [-1, 1]
    """
    v = np.array(v, dtype=float)
    w = np.array(w, dtype=float)

    # Compute dot product
    dot = np.dot(v, w)

    # Compute magnitudes
    norm_v = np.linalg.norm(v)
    norm_w = np.linalg.norm(w)

    # Handle zero vectors
    if norm_v == 0 or norm_w == 0:
        return 0.0

    # Return cosine similarity
    return dot / (norm_v * norm_w)

# Example usage
corpus = [
    "the cat sat on the mat",
    "the dog ran in the park",
    "a cat and dog are friends"
]
vocab = ["the", "cat", "dog", "sat", "ran", "on", "in"]
M = build_cooccurrence_matrix(corpus, vocab, window=2)
print(cosine_similarity(M[vocab.index("cat")], M[vocab.index("dog")]))

6.2 Simplified Word2vec (Skip-gram) from Scratch

A minimal implementation of skip-gram with negative sampling:

import numpy as np

class Word2Vec:
    """Simplified Word2Vec (Skip-gram) implementation."""

    def __init__(self, vocab_size, embedding_dim=100):
        """Initialize embeddings.

        Args:
            vocab_size: Size of vocabulary
            embedding_dim: Dimension of embeddings (d)
        """
        self.embedding_dim = embedding_dim

        # Two embedding matrices
        self.W = np.random.randn(vocab_size, embedding_dim) * 0.01  # Target word embeddings
        self.C = np.random.randn(vocab_size, embedding_dim) * 0.01  # Context word embeddings

    def sigmoid(self, x):
        """Sigmoid activation function."""
        return 1 / (1 + np.exp(-np.clip(x, -500, 500)))

    def train_pair(self, target_idx, context_idx, negative_idxs, learning_rate=0.1):
        """Train on one (target, context) pair with negative sampling.

        Args:
            target_idx: Index of target word w
            context_idx: Index of positive context word c_pos
            negative_idxs: Indices of k negative context words
            learning_rate: Learning rate for gradient descent
        """
        # Positive example: maximize log σ(c·w)
        c_pos = self.C[context_idx]
        pred = self.sigmoid(np.dot(self.W[target_idx], c_pos))

        # Gradient for positive
        grad = (pred - 1) * learning_rate
        self.W[target_idx] -= grad * c_pos
        self.C[context_idx] -= grad * self.W[target_idx]

        # Negative examples: maximize log σ(-c_neg·w)
        for neg_idx in negative_idxs:
            c_neg = self.C[neg_idx]
            pred = self.sigmoid(np.dot(self.W[target_idx], c_neg))

            # Gradient for negative
            grad = pred * learning_rate  # Note: no -1 since we want to minimize
            self.W[target_idx] -= grad * c_neg
            self.C[neg_idx] -= grad * self.W[target_idx]

    def get_embedding(self, word_idx):
        """Get the embedding for a word."""
        return self.W[word_idx]

# Example usage
model = Word2Vec(vocab_size=10000, embedding_dim=100)

# Simulate training on a sequence
# (In practice, you'd iterate over a corpus and sample context windows)
target, context, negatives = 42, 137, [8, 9, 13]
model.train_pair(target, context, negatives, learning_rate=0.01)

6.3 Using Gensim Word2vec in Practice

In production, use the gensim library for efficiency and robustness:

from gensim.models import Word2Vec

# Prepare corpus: list of tokenized sentences
corpus = [
    "the quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog".split(),
    "the dog sat on the mat".split(),
    "a quick brown fox and a lazy dog met in the forest".split(),
]

# Train Word2Vec model
# - size: embedding dimension (typically 100-300)
# - window: context window size
# - min_count: ignore words appearing < min_count times
# - workers: number of CPU threads
# - sg: 1 = skip-gram, 0 = CBOW
# - negative: number of negative samples
model = Word2Vec(
    sentences=corpus,
    vector_size=100,
    window=5,
    min_count=1,
    workers=4,
    sg=1,  # Skip-gram
    negative=5
)

# Use embeddings
embedding_fox = model.wv["fox"]  # Get embedding vector
print(embedding_fox.shape)  # (100,)

# Find similar words
similar = model.wv.most_similar("fox", topn=5)
print(similar)  # [('dog', 0.87), ('brown', 0.76), ...]

# Analogy: "king - man + woman = ?"
# most_similar with positive and negative parameters
king_analogy = model.wv.most_similar(
    positive=["king", "woman"],
    negative=["man"],
    topn=1
)
print(king_analogy)  # Should output something like [('queen', 0.92)]

# Compute similarity
sim = model.wv.similarity("dog", "cat")
print(sim)  # e.g., 0.78

# Save and load
model.save("word2vec.model")
model = Word2Vec.load("word2vec.model")

Modern Bridge From Word2vec to GPT: The Embedding Revolution

Static vs. Contextualized Embeddings: Word2vec and GloVe assign one embedding vector per word type. This is a fundamental limitation: the word "bank" has the same embedding whether it appears in "bank account" or "river bank," even though these are different senses.

Contextualized Embeddings: Modern models (BERT, GPT, etc.) generate embeddings dynamically based on context. The embedding of "bank" in "river bank" differs from "bank account." This allows the model to disambiguate word senses and capture nuanced meaning.

The Embedding Layer in Transformers: Every large language model starts with an embedding layer. Input tokens are mapped to dense vectors. For GPT-3, this is a learned 12288-dimensional vector for each token. The embedding layer is often parameter-heavy in smaller models but becomes a tiny fraction of a multi-billion parameter model.

Sentence and Document Embeddings: We can average word embeddings to create sentence representations. These are used in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG): encode documents as embeddings, store them in a vector database, and at query time, find similar documents using cosine similarity. This retrieval step augments the LLM's context window.

Scaling Embedding Dimensions: Different models use different embedding dimensions:

  • word2vec: 100–300 (lightweight)
  • BERT-base: 768 (standard)
  • GPT-2: 1600
  • GPT-3 (175B): 12288 (very large)

Practical Applications:

  • Semantic search: Embed queries and documents, rank by similarity
  • Clustering: Group documents by embedding similarity
  • Recommendation systems: Find similar users/items in embedding space
  • Zero-shot learning: Classify into unseen categories by embedding similarity
  • Few-shot learning: Learn from a small number of examples via embedding-space adaptation

6.5 Embedding-Based Similarity Search (RAG)

Using embeddings for retrieval-augmented generation:

import numpy as np
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity

# Simulate a document collection
documents = [
    "The cat sat on the mat",
    "Dogs are loyal pets",
    "Cats and dogs can be friends",
    "Machine learning uses embeddings",
    "Natural language processing is fascinating",
]

# Encode documents (in practice, use BERT or sentence-transformers)
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer
model = SentenceTransformer('all-MiniLM-L6-v2')
document_embeddings = model.encode(documents)

# Query embedding
query = "Are cats and dogs similar?"
query_embedding = model.encode([query])[0]

# Find most similar documents
similarities = cosine_similarity([query_embedding], document_embeddings)[0]
top_indices = np.argsort(similarities)[::-1][:3]

print("Top 3 relevant documents:")
for idx in top_indices:
    print(f"  {documents[idx]} (similarity: {similarities[idx]:.3f})")

6.6 Debiasing Embeddings

One approach to reduce gender bias in embeddings (Bolukbasi et al. 2016):

import numpy as np

def hard_debias(W, gender_subspace):
    """Remove gender bias from embedding matrix W.

    Args:
        W: Embedding matrix (vocab_size, dim)
        gender_subspace: Principal gender direction (1D array)

    Returns:
        W_debiased: Embedding matrix with gender bias removed
    """
    # Normalize gender direction
    g = gender_subspace / np.linalg.norm(gender_subspace)

    # Project each embedding onto gender subspace
    projections = np.dot(W, g[:, np.newaxis]) * g

    # Remove projection
    W_debiased = W - projections

    return W_debiased

# Identify gender direction from word pairs
# e.g., man - woman, he - she, etc.
gender_words = [("man", "woman"), ("he", "she"), ("king", "queen")]

# Compute average direction (simplified)
gender_vecs = []
for w1, w2 in gender_words:
    if w1 in model.wv and w2 in model.wv:
        v = model.wv[w1] - model.wv[w2]
        gender_vecs.append(v)

gender_direction = np.mean(gender_vecs, axis=0)

# Debias the embedding matrix
W_debiased = hard_debias(model.wv.vectors, gender_direction)

# Note: This is a simplification. Full debiasing is more nuanced
# and researchers debate its effectiveness and fairness implications.

Historical The Evolution of Embeddings

1954
Zellig Harris — Distributional Hypothesis

Harris formalized the principle that words occurring in similar contexts have similar meanings, laying the theoretical foundation for all distributional semantics.

1957
J.R. Firth — "You shall know a word by the company it keeps"

Firth's famous phrase crystallized the distributional hypothesis in linguistics. This became the mantra of modern NLP.

1957
Charles Osgood — Semantic Differential

Osgood proposed that meaning could be quantified using multiple dimensions (valence, arousal, dominance), placing words as points in multidimensional semantic space — an early intuition for embeddings.

1971
Gerard Salton — Vector Space Model

Salton formalized the vector space model for information retrieval, representing documents as bag-of-words vectors. This influenced later work on distributional semantics.

1990
Deerwester et al. — Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA)

LSA applied singular value decomposition (SVD) to term-document matrices, reducing dimensionality and creating dense low-rank approximations. It was the first successful dense embedding method but computationally expensive.

2003
Bengio et al. — Neural Language Models

Bengio's neural language model learned embeddings as a byproduct of predicting the next word, showing that neural networks could learn meaningful dense vectors.

2013
Mikolov et al. — Word2Vec

Word2vec made embeddings practical and efficient with skip-gram and negative sampling. Suddenly, training embeddings on billions of words was feasible. Word2vec's efficiency and effectiveness triggered the embedding era in NLP.

2014
Pennington et al. — GloVe

Global Vectors combined global matrix factorization with local context windows, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to word2vec while incorporating corpus-level statistics.

2016
Bolukbasi et al. — Gender Bias in Embeddings

Bolukbasi revealed that embeddings amplify gender stereotypes from training data. This sparked research into bias in embeddings and fairness in NLP more broadly.

2018
Devlin et al. — BERT

BERT introduced contextualized embeddings using bidirectional transformers. Unlike static embeddings, BERT embeddings vary per context, capturing polysemy and nuanced meaning. BERT catalyzed a shift toward pre-trained transformer models.

Socratic Knowledge Check: A Case Study in Bias

Failure Scenario

A job recommendation system uses word2vec embeddings to match resumes to job postings by computing cosine similarity between the average word vectors of each document.

A qualified female software engineer's resume contains words like "collaborated," "communicated," and "organized." The system consistently ranks her lower than less qualified male candidates whose resumes use words like "executed," "engineered," and "dominated."

Root cause: The embedding space associates the first set of words more closely with administrative and support roles; the second set with technical leadership. These associations reflect biases in the training corpus.

Guiding Questions

1. How does the distributional hypothesis explain why these word vectors carry gender bias? What kind of training data would produce these associations?

Explanation: The distributional hypothesis says words are defined by their contexts. If the training corpus contains more instances of women in administrative roles and men in engineering roles, the contexts of "collaborated" will more frequently include administrative keywords (schedule, organize, support, facilitate), while "engineered" will co-occur more with technical keywords (architecture, algorithm, optimize, debug).

The embedding captures this statistical reality from the corpus. The embeddings are a faithful, compressed representation of bias present in the training data. The problem is not the embeddings themselves but the biased patterns in language that embeddings faithfully reflect.

2. If we compute cos("collaborated", "engineering") and cos("executed", "engineering"), which would likely be higher in a biased embedding space, and why?

Answer: cos("executed", "engineering") would be higher.

Why: If the corpus associates "executed" with engineering leadership (e.g., "She executed the algorithm efficiently"), its embedding will be closer to the "engineering" embedding. Conversely, "collaborated" might be more associated with support and coordination, making it more distant from "engineering" in the embedding space.

This geometric difference in the embedding space is invisible to the resume-screening algorithm but has real consequences: it biases the system against candidates using collaborative language.

3. Would averaging word vectors be a good document representation for this task? What information is lost?

Information Loss: Averaging word embeddings destroys word order and contextual information. It treats "The engineer managed the team" and "The team managed the engineer" identically, even though these have opposite meanings.

Additionally, averaging obscures the relative importance of words. In a resume, "software engineer" (the job title) should probably matter more than common words like "the" or "and," but averaging treats all words equally.

Better approaches: Weighted averaging (emphasize important words), pooling operations that preserve more information, or using sentence-level embeddings from models like BERT or sentence-transformers that are trained to produce meaningful document-level vectors.

4. The parallelogram method shows man:computer_programmer :: woman:homemaker. What does this tell us about the geometry of bias in embedding spaces?

Geometric Insight: The analogy reveals that the embedding space has learned an "occupational gender vector" — the direction from man to woman encodes associations with different occupational roles.

Formally, the vector (woman - man) is similar to the vector (homemaker - computer_programmer). This means:

computer_programmer + (woman - man) ≈ homemaker

This is not a bug in the embedding algorithm — it reflects the actual statistical patterns in text. Women are mentioned less frequently in programming contexts and more frequently in homemaking contexts. The embeddings faithfully encode this bias.

The alarming part is that simple vector arithmetic exposes these biases. The geometry of the embedding space encodes hidden stereotypes that become actionable through these operations.

5. Compare three approaches to fix this: (a) debiasing the embeddings, (b) using contextualized embeddings like BERT, (c) not using embeddings at all. What are the tradeoffs?

(a) Debiasing Embeddings:

  • Pro: Preserves the efficiency and utility of embeddings; fixes the bias directly
  • Con: Post-hoc debiasing is incomplete; hard debiasing can reduce downstream task performance; it doesn't address the root cause (biased training data)

(b) Contextualized Embeddings (BERT):

  • Pro: Embeddings adapt to context; "engineer" has different embeddings in different sentences; can be more semantically robust
  • Con: BERT is also trained on biased text (Wikipedia, BookCorpus) so it inherits similar biases; requires significantly more computation; doesn't fully solve the bias problem

(c) Not Using Embeddings:

  • Pro: Explainability; you can inspect exactly what features the system uses; avoids embedding-specific biases
  • Con: Requires hand-engineering features; often achieves worse performance; still vulnerable to bias if features are biased; impractical for large-scale systems

Best Practice: Combine multiple approaches: (1) audit and diversify training data, (2) use contextualized models, (3) apply algorithmic debiasing as a safeguard, (4) continuously monitor for bias in deployment, and (5) use explainable methods alongside embeddings for high-stakes applications.
