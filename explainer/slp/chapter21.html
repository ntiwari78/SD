Chapter 21: Semantic Role Labeling

Understanding WHO did WHAT to WHOM and WHERE, WHEN, and HOW

🧠 Feynman Bridge: The Newspaper Headline
Imagine reading a newspaper headline: "Chef serves customer pasta." Your brain instantly assigns roles:
  • The chef = the DOER (Agent)
  • The customer = the RECEIVER (Beneficiary/Recipient)
  • The pasta = the THING (Theme/Patient)
  • Serves = the EVENT (Predicate)

Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) teaches machines to automatically identify and extract these semantic relationships from natural language. It's the bridge between shallow syntax ("who did what grammatically") and deep meaning ("who did what semantically").

Connection from Chapter 20: We learned to extract relations (e.g., "Company X acquired Company Y"). Now we dive deeper—not just extracting facts, but understanding the semantic structure of events at a fine-grained level.

Technical Ascent: Core Concepts

21.1 Semantic & Thematic Roles

Semantic roles (also called thematic roles) represent the way participants in an event relate to the predicate. Unlike syntactic roles (subject, object), semantic roles describe the meaning of the participant's involvement.

Core Thematic Roles:

Role Definition Example
AGENT Volitional causer of an event; acts intentionally The waiter brought the check
EXPERIENCER Conscious entity that experiences an event or state John has a headache; Mary fears spiders
FORCE Non-volitional causer; an external force The wind blows down the tree
THEME / PATIENT Entity affected or changed by the action The waiter brought the check
RESULT Object created or produced by an action She built a house
CONTENT Propositional content of cognitive verbs He believes the earth is flat
INSTRUMENT Tool or means used to accomplish an action He cut the bread with a knife
BENEFICIARY Entity that benefits from an action She baked him a cake
SOURCE Origin or starting point of movement/transfer He flew from LA
GOAL Destination or endpoint of movement/transfer He flew to NYC
LOCATION Place where an event occurs The meeting happens in Boston
TEMPORAL Time at which an event occurs We met last Tuesday

Key Insight: The same syntactic position can hold different semantic roles. For example, "I gave John a book" (John = BENEFICIARY) vs. "I gave the problem thought" (thought = THEME). Conversely, the same role can appear in different syntactic positions across verb classes.

21.2 Diathesis Alternations

A single verb can appear in multiple argument structures with different surface realizations. These diathesis alternations preserve the semantic relationships but change the grammatical form.

Common Alternations:

Dative Alternation:

  • "She gave the dog a bone" (double object)
  • "She gave a bone to the dog" (prepositional)
  • Same semantic roles: AGENT (she), THEME (bone), GOAL (dog)

Locative Alternation:

  • "She poured water into the bucket" (LOCATION as oblique)
  • "She poured the bucket with water" (LOCATION as object)

Causative-Inchoative:

  • "The chef melted the chocolate" (transitive)
  • "The chocolate melted" (intransitive)

Beth Levin's Classification: Levin's seminal work organized 3,100 English verbs into classes based on shared alternation patterns. For example, "break" verbs (break, shatter, crack) all undergo the causative-inchoative alternation.

Problems with Thematic Roles (21.3):

  • Fragmentation Problem: How many roles do we need? (10? 30? 100?). Different theories propose different inventories, making comparison difficult.
  • Role Ambiguity: "The key opened the door"—is the key an INSTRUMENT or an AGENT?
  • Dowty's Solution (1991): Rather than discrete roles, use proto-agent and proto-patient properties. An entity is a stronger AGENT if it has more agent-like properties (volition, sentience, change of state causation, etc.)
Dowty's Proto-Roles Framework:

An entity is a proto-agent if it has properties like:

  • Volitional involvement in the event
  • Sentience (perception, consciousness)
  • Causation of an event or change of state
  • Movement relative to other participants
  • Exists independently of the event

21.4 PropBank: A Resource

PropBank (Palmer et al. 2005) is a large annotated corpus of English with semantic role labels aligned to verb predicates. It uses a numbering scheme rather than abstract role names.

Role Numbering Scheme:

Argument Meaning (Proto-Role) Example
Arg0 Proto-Agent (typically subject) The chef served the customer pasta
Arg1 Proto-Patient (typically object) The chef served the customer pasta
Arg2 Instrument, start point, attribute, or beneficiary The chef served the customer pasta
Arg3 End point, beneficiary, attribute, or attribute value —
Arg4 Secondary predicate —

Adjunct/Modifier Arguments (ArgM):

ArgM Type Meaning Example
ArgM-TMP Temporal modifier The chef served pasta yesterday
ArgM-LOC Locative modifier The chef served pasta in the kitchen
ArgM-DIR Directional modifier He moved to the left
ArgM-MNR Manner modifier He spoke loudly
ArgM-PRP Purpose/reason He ran to catch the bus
ArgM-DIS Discourse connective However, she continued

Frame Files & Rolesets:

Each verb in PropBank has one or more rolesets, each with its own frame. Different verb senses can have different argument structures.

Example: PropBank Annotation
Sentence: The San Francisco Examiner issued a special edition.

Annotations:
  Predicate: issued
  Arg0 (Agent): The San Francisco Examiner
  Arg1 (Theme/Patient): a special edition

  Roleset: issue.01 - emit, put out, supply
    Arg0: Publisher/Issuer
    Arg1: Thing issued
    Arg2: Issued-to

NomBank: A parallel resource for nominal predicates (nouns). For example, "the destruction of the city" annotates "destruction" with Arg0 (destroyer) and Arg1 (thing destroyed).

21.5 FrameNet: Semantic Frames

FrameNet (Fillmore et al., ongoing since 1997) is based on frame semantics—the idea that word meanings are best understood in the context of structured conceptual schemas called frames.

A frame is a script-like structure describing a scenario. For example, the COMMERCIAL_TRANSACTION frame includes roles like BUYER, SELLER, GOODS, MONEY, PLACE.

Frame Elements (Core vs. Non-Core):

  • Core Frame Elements: Essential to the frame; if missing, the sentence is incomplete or unusual. (e.g., BUYER and SELLER for COMMERCIAL_TRANSACTION)
  • Non-Core Frame Elements: Peripheral modifiers; present in some sentences but not necessary. (e.g., TIME, PLACE, MANNER)

Example: CHANGE_POSITION_ON_A_SCALE Frame

Frame Element Definition Example
ITEM (core) Entity whose value changes Interest rates rose by 5%
ATTRIBUTE (core) Property being measured Interest rates rose by 5%
INITIAL_VALUE Starting value (often implicit) —
FINAL_VALUE Ending value Interest rates rose to 5%
DIFFERENCE Amount of change Interest rates rose by 5%
RATE_OF_CHANGE Speed of change Interest rates rose rapidly

Lexical Units: A frame is evoked by lexical units—pairings of a word and a sense. For CHANGE_POSITION_ON_A_SCALE: "rise" (climb), "fall" (descend), "increase", "decrease", etc.

PropBank Approach
Annotation: Arg0, Arg1, Arg2, ...
Coverage: Verb-centric; mainly predicates
Frame: Simple numbered arguments
Advantage: Fast annotation; clear semantics
FrameNet Approach
Annotation: Named semantic roles (e.g., ITEM, SELLER)
Coverage: Broader semantic phenomena including nouns, adjectives
Frame: Complex conceptual scenarios
Advantage: Richer semantic interpretation; cross-lexical consistency

21.6 Automatic SRL: Algorithms

SRL is typically framed as a sequence labeling task: given a sentence and a predicate, assign semantic role labels to each token (or span of tokens).

Traditional Feature-Based Approach (Gildea & Jurafsky 2002):

Early systems used syntactic and lexical features extracted from parse trees:

  • Path Features: The path from predicate to argument in the parse tree. Example: NP↑S↓VP↓VBD means "go up to NP, up to S, down to VP, down to VBD"
  • Phrase Type: NP, VP, PP, ADVP, etc.
  • Headword: The syntactic head of the argument
  • Position: Before or after the predicate?
  • Voice: Active or passive?
  • Subcategorization: The argument frame of the verb
Example Path Feature
Sentence: The waiter brought the check.

Predicate: brought
Argument: check (Arg1)
Path: NP↑VP↑S → NP↑VP

This path encodes that "check" is an NP within the same VP as the predicate.

Neural Sequence Labeling (Modern Approach):

Modern SRL systems use BERT or other pre-trained transformers with BIO (Begin-Inside-Outside) tagging:

Token Tag Example Meaning
The B-ARG0 Begin Argument 0
waiter I-ARG0 Inside Argument 0
brought B-V Begin Predicate (Verb)
the B-ARG1 Begin Argument 1
check I-ARG1 Inside Argument 1

Architecture: BERT encoder → predicate indicator → feed-forward network → softmax over tags

Probability Model for Tagging:
$$\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \text{argmax}_{\mathbf{y} \in \mathcal{T}} P(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{w})$$

Where $\mathbf{w}$ is the word sequence and $\mathcal{T}$ is the set of valid tag sequences.

Global Optimization with ILP:

Some systems use Integer Linear Programming (ILP) to enforce global constraints:

  • Each role (Arg0, Arg1, ...) appears at most once per predicate
  • Arg0 cannot appear after Arg1 in some cases (structural constraints)
  • Overlapping roles are disallowed

Evaluation Metrics:

  • Precision: Of predicted arguments, how many are correct?
  • Recall: Of gold-standard arguments, how many did we find?
  • F-measure: Harmonic mean of precision and recall
  • Benchmarks: CoNLL-2005, CoNLL-2012 shared tasks

21.7 Selectional Restrictions & Preferences

Verbs typically impose constraints on the semantic types of their arguments. For example, the THEME of "eat" must be something edible.

Selectional Restrictions: Hard constraints that arguments must satisfy. Example: "I want to eat someplace nearby"—the verb "eat" has an implicit THEME constraint (must be food-like).

Neo-Davidsonian Event Semantics:

Represent selectional restrictions formally using event variables:

Event Structure with Selectional Constraints:
$$\exists e, x, y \; \text{Eating}(e) \wedge \text{Agent}(e, x) \wedge \text{Theme}(e, y) \wedge \text{EdibleThing}(y)$$

The constraint $\text{EdibleThing}(y)$ restricts what can be the THEME.

WordNet Approach:

Represent semantic types as WordNet synsets. The verb "bark" selects for Arg0 = DOG_ANIMAL (or subordinates).

Selectional Preferences (Resnik 1993):

Rather than hard constraints, model soft preferences. How much information does a verb tell us about its argument's semantic class?

Selectional Preference Strength (KL Divergence):
$$S_R(v) = D(\mathcal{P}(c|v) \| \mathcal{P}(c)) = \sum_{c} \mathcal{P}(c|v) \log \frac{\mathcal{P}(c|v)}{\mathcal{P}(c)}$$

$\mathcal{P}(c|v)$ = probability of semantic class $c$ given verb $v$
$\mathcal{P}(c)$ = prior probability of class $c$
High $S_R(v)$ = verb is selective (tells us a lot about argument types)

Selectional Association for Role-Class Pair:
$$A_R(v, c) = \frac{1}{S_R(v)} \mathcal{P}(c|v) \log \frac{\mathcal{P}(c|v)}{\mathcal{P}(c)}$$

Normalized measure of how strongly a verb-role prefers a semantic class.

Conditional Probability Model:

Estimating P(noun | verb, role):
$$\mathcal{P}(n|v, r) = \frac{C(n, v, r)}{C(v, r)}$$

Where $C(n, v, r)$ = count of noun $n$ in role $r$ of verb $v$
Can use smoothing and back-off to WordNet hypernyms for unseen nouns.

Pseudowords Evaluation: Evaluate selectional preferences by artificially creating ambiguous "pseudoword" pairs (e.g., combining "bank" and "stream") and seeing if the model can disambiguate based on selectional preferences.

21.8 Primitive Decomposition: Conceptual Dependency

Some theories propose that verb meanings can be decomposed into primitive semantic components. This approach, called Conceptual Dependency (CD), was influential in early AI and NLP.

Example Decomposition:

KILL Decomposition (Schank):
$$\text{KILL}(x, y) \iff \text{CAUSE}(x, \text{BECOME}(\text{NOT}(\text{ALIVE}(y))))$$

"To kill" = to cause the state of being not-alive to come into being for $y$

Schank's Primitive Acts:

All events decompose into a small set of primitives:

  • ATRANS: Abstract transfer (e.g., "give", "take", "receive")
  • PTRANS: Physical transfer of location (e.g., "go", "move", "drive")
  • MTRANS: Mental transfer (e.g., "tell", "say", "communicate")
  • MBUILD: Mental construction (e.g., "think", "imagine", "plan")
  • PROPEL: Application of force (e.g., "push", "throw", "hit")
  • MOVE: Movement of a body part (e.g., "kick", "punch", "raise")
  • INGEST: Taking in substances (e.g., "eat", "drink", "breathe")
  • EXPEL: Expelling substances (e.g., "spit", "vomit", "exhale")
  • SPEAK: Vocalization (e.g., "say", "shout", "whisper")
  • ATTEND: Focusing sensory receptors (e.g., "look", "listen", "smell")

Limitations: While theoretically elegant, primitive decomposition has proven difficult to scale and is less used in modern NLP. However, it provides valuable insights into the structure of verb meanings.

Lab 1: Simple Rule-Based SRL with spaCy

Extract semantic roles using dependency parsing. This approach is fast and interpretable, though less accurate than neural models.

Python
# Simple rule-based SRL using dependency parsing
import spacy
nlp = spacy.load("en_core_web_sm")

def simple_srl(sentence):
    """Extract semantic roles using syntactic dependencies."""
    doc = nlp(sentence)
    roles = {}
    predicate = None

    for token in doc:
        # Find the predicate (main verb)
        if token.dep_ == "ROOT" and token.pos_ == "VERB":
            predicate = token.text
            roles["PREDICATE"] = predicate

        # Extract core arguments by dependency relation
        if token.dep_ == "nsubj":  # Nominal subject
            roles["ARG0 (Agent)"] = token.text
        elif token.dep_ == "nsubjpass":  # Passive subject
            roles["ARG0 (Affected)"] = token.text
        elif token.dep_ == "dobj":  # Direct object
            roles["ARG1 (Theme)"] = token.text
        elif token.dep_ == "iobj":  # Indirect object
            roles["ARG2 (Recipient)"] = token.text

        # Handle modifiers
        if token.dep_.startswith("prep"):
            roles[f"ArgM-{token.text}"] = [child.text for child in token.children]
        elif token.dep_ == "advmod":
            roles["ArgM-MNR/TMP"] = token.text

    return roles

# Test the function
test_sentences = [
    "The chef served the customer pasta",
    "She gave him a beautiful painting for his birthday",
    "The wind knocked down the old tree"
]

for sent in test_sentences:
    print(f"\nSentence: {sent}")
    print("Roles:")
    for role, word in simple_srl(sent).items():
        print(f"  {role}: {word}")

Note: This rule-based approach relies on syntactic dependencies, which are imperfect proxies for semantic roles. For production systems, use pre-trained neural models like allennlp or transformers-based SRL.

Lab 2: Selectional Preference Strength Calculation

Compute how selective a verb is about its argument types using KL divergence. High selectional preference strength means the verb strongly constrains its arguments' semantic types.

Python
import math
from collections import Counter

# Simulated verb-class co-occurrence statistics
# In practice, extract from large corpora (e.g., SRL-annotated data)
verb_object_classes = {
    "eat": Counter({
        "FOOD": 45,
        "SUBSTANCE": 5,
        "PHYSICAL_OBJECT": 2,
        "ENTITY": 1
    }),
    "read": Counter({
        "WRITING": 40,
        "TEXT": 8,
        "ARTIFACT": 4,
        "ENTITY": 2
    }),
    "be": Counter({
        "ENTITY": 20,
        "ABSTRACT": 18,
        "PHYSICAL": 15,
        "STATE": 10
    }),
}

# Compute prior P(c) across all verbs
total_count = sum(sum(v.values()) for v in verb_object_classes.values())
class_totals = Counter()
for counts in verb_object_classes.values():
    class_totals += counts

# Prior probability
P_c = {c: n / total_count for c, n in class_totals.items()}

def selectional_preference_strength(verb):
    """
    Calculate S_R(v) = D(P(c|v) || P(c))
    High values indicate the verb is selective about its arguments.
    """
    counts = verb_object_classes.get(verb, Counter())
    verb_total = sum(counts.values())

    if verb_total == 0:
        return 0.0

    # P(c|v) = count(c,v) / count(v)
    P_c_given_v = {c: n / verb_total for c, n in counts.items()}

    # KL divergence: sum P(c|v) * log(P(c|v) / P(c))
    sps = 0.0
    for c, p_c_v in P_c_given_v.items():
        if p_c_v > 0 and P_c.get(c, 0) > 0:
            sps += p_c_v * math.log2(p_c_v / P_c.get(c, 1e-10))

    return sps

def selectional_association(verb, class_name):
    """
    A_R(v, c) = (1/S_R(v)) * P(c|v) * log(P(c|v) / P(c))
    Association strength for a specific (verb, class) pair.
    """
    counts = verb_object_classes.get(verb, Counter())
    verb_total = sum(counts.values())
    sps = selectional_preference_strength(verb)

    if verb_total == 0 or sps == 0:
        return 0.0

    p_c_v = counts.get(class_name, 0) / verb_total
    if p_c_v == 0:
        return 0.0

    p_c = P_c.get(class_name, 1e-10)
    assoc = (1.0 / sps) * p_c_v * math.log2(p_c_v / p_c)
    return assoc

# Compute and display results
print("Selectional Preference Strength by Verb:")
print("-" * 50)
results = []
for verb in verb_object_classes:
    sps = selectional_preference_strength(verb)
    results.append((verb, sps))

results.sort(key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
for verb, sps in results:
    print(f"S_R({verb:8s}) = {sps:.3f} bits")

print("\nSelectional Association Examples:")
print("-" * 50)
print(f"eat + FOOD:    {selectional_association('eat', 'FOOD'):.3f}")
print(f"eat + ENTITY:  {selectional_association('eat', 'ENTITY'):.3f}")
print(f"read + WRITING:{selectional_association('read', 'WRITING'):.3f}")
print(f"be + ABSTRACT: {selectional_association('be', 'ABSTRACT'):.3f}")

Interpretation: "eat" has higher SPS than "be" because eating is more semantically constrained. The association values show which classes the verb prefers most.

Lab 3: BIO Tagger for SRL

Implement sequence labeling with Begin-Inside-Outside (BIO) tags. This is the basis for neural SRL systems.

Python
def bio_tag_srl(tokens, predicate_idx, arg_spans):
    """
    Create BIO tags for semantic role labeling.

    Args:
        tokens: List of words
        predicate_idx: Index of the predicate (verb)
        arg_spans: Dict mapping role names to (start, end) indices

    Returns:
        List of BIO tags (one per token)
    """
    # Initialize all tokens as outside (O)
    tags = ['O'] * len(tokens)

    # Mark the predicate
    tags[predicate_idx] = 'B-V'

    # Mark arguments
    for role, (start, end) in arg_spans.items():
        # First token of argument gets B- prefix
        tags[start] = f'B-{role}'

        # Remaining tokens get I- prefix
        for i in range(start + 1, end):
            if i < len(tags):
                tags[i] = f'I-{role}'

    return tags

# Example sentence
sentence = "The chef served the customer pasta in the kitchen"
tokens = sentence.split()
predicate_idx = 2  # "served"

# Define argument spans (start_inclusive, end_exclusive)
arg_spans = {
    "ARG0": (0, 2),      # "The chef"
    "ARG2": (3, 5),      # "the customer"
    "ARG1": (5, 6),      # "pasta"
    "ARGM-LOC": (6, 9)   # "in the kitchen"
}

# Generate BIO tags
tags = bio_tag_srl(tokens, predicate_idx, arg_spans)

# Display results
print("BIO Tagging Results")
print("=" * 50)
print(f"{'Token':<15} {'Tag':<15}")
print("-" * 50)
for token, tag in zip(tokens, tags):
    print(f"{token:<15} {tag:<15}")

print("\n\nExtract Arguments from BIO Tags")
print("=" * 50)

def extract_args_from_bio(tokens, tags):
    """Convert BIO tags back to (role, span) pairs."""
    args = {}
    current_role = None
    start_idx = None

    for i, tag in enumerate(tags):
        if tag == 'O' or tag.startswith('B-'):
            # End current argument if exists
            if current_role is not None and start_idx is not None:
                span = ' '.join(tokens[start_idx:i])
                if current_role not in args:
                    args[current_role] = []
                args[current_role].append(span)

            # Start new argument
            if tag.startswith('B-'):
                current_role = tag[2:]  # Remove 'B-' prefix
                start_idx = i
            else:
                current_role = None
                start_idx = None
        elif tag.startswith('I-'):
            # Continue current argument (no action needed)
            pass

    # Handle last argument
    if current_role is not None and start_idx is not None:
        span = ' '.join(tokens[start_idx:])
        if current_role not in args:
            args[current_role] = []
        args[current_role].append(span)

    return args

# Extract and display
extracted = extract_args_from_bio(tokens, tags)
for role, spans in extracted.items():
    for span in spans:
        print(f"{role:12s}: {span}")

Key Concept: BIO tags encode argument boundaries and roles. B-(role) marks the first token; I-(role) marks continuation. Modern systems use neural encoders (BERT) to predict these tags and decode spans with global constraints.

D) Experiencer—an entity that experiences a state
Correct! The wind is a non-volitional causer—it has no intention or consciousness. An Agent requires volition (intentional action). Force is the role for natural, non-agentive causers like winds, earthquakes, or forces of nature.

C) Arg0 contains animate entities; Arg1 contains inanimate
D) Arg0 is transitive; Arg1 is intransitive
Correct! PropBank uses Dowty's proto-role framework. Arg0 corresponds to the proto-agent (entity with properties like volition, sentience, causation), while Arg1 corresponds to the proto-patient (entity that is affected or changes state). Q3: What does selectional preference strength (S_R(v)) measure?

B) How much information a verb provides about its arguments' semantic types ✓
C) The probability of a verb appearing in a passive construction
D) The frequency of a verb in a corpus
Correct! S_R(v) uses KL divergence to measure how much knowing a verb tells us about its argument's semantic class. High SPS (e.g., "eat") means the verb is very selective about argument types. Q4: In the primitive decomposition KILL(x,y) ⟺ CAUSE(x, BECOME(NOT(ALIVE(y)))), which primitive represents the change of state?
A) CAUSE—causes an event to happen
B) NOT—negates a predicate
C) BECOME—captures a change of state ✓
D) ALIVE—is the final state
Correct! BECOME is Schank's primitive for state transitions. The decomposition says: "X kills Y" means "X causes Y to become not-alive." CAUSE initiates the event; BECOME represents the change; NOT/ALIVE specify the new state. This decomposition highlights how complex concepts can be built from simpler semantic primitives.

Modern Era: Recent Advances

Neural SRL and Pre-trained Language Models

The 2018 release of BERT revolutionized SRL. Pre-trained transformers capture semantic relationships implicitly, enabling end-to-end SRL with minimal task-specific engineering.

  • He et al. (2017): First neural SRL systems using LSTMs and attention mechanisms
  • Shi et al. (2019): BERT-based SRL, achieving 85.5 F1 on CoNLL-2012
  • Larger Models: RoBERTa, ELECTRA, and T5 further improve performance

Multilingual and Cross-lingual SRL

Multilingual BERT enables SRL in 100+ languages with limited annotations. Zero-shot and few-shot learning approaches extend SRL to low-resource languages.

Implicit Argument Detection (iSRL)

Standard SRL only extracts explicit arguments. Implicit SRL recovers omitted arguments from context:

Example: "I met my friend yesterday. [He] was driving a new car." The implicit ARG0 of "was driving" is "he" (refers to the friend from the previous sentence).

Connotation Frames & Beyond

Connotation Frames extend SRL to capture pragmatic and connotative dimensions. Example: "The politician cut funding for education" carries a negative connotation; "The politician trimmed wasteful spending" is positive, despite similar surface semantics.

Applications in NLU Systems

  • Question Answering: SRL helps match questions to candidate passages by aligning semantic roles
  • Machine Translation: SRL preserves meaning during translation by maintaining role correspondence
  • Summarization: Extract key event participants to generate summaries
  • Fact Extraction: SRL output directly populates knowledge base facts

Historical Context: The Evolution of SRL

Ancient Foundations: Pāṇini (5th Century BCE)

The Sanskrit grammarian Pāṇini's Ashtadhyayi ("Eight Chapters") outlined the structure of Sanskrit grammar, including early insights into argument structure and verb classes. His case system prefigured modern thematic roles.

Case Grammar: Fillmore (1966–1968)

Charles Fillmore's Case Grammar proposed that deep semantic structure is built from a limited set of cases (Agent, Patient, Instrument, etc.) rather than traditional syntactic constituents. This theory directly inspired modern SRL.

Fillmore distinguished:

  • Deep case: Semantic role in event structure
  • Surface case: Grammatical marking (nominative, accusative, oblique)

Frame Semantics: Fillmore (1980s)

Fillmore extended his theory to Frame Semantics, proposing that word meanings are best understood in the context of conceptual frames (script-like structures). This directly led to the FrameNet project.

Generative Lexicon: Pustejovsky (1995)

James Pustejovsky's Generative Lexicon theory proposed rich lexical representations that encode how words compose and how their arguments coerce. Influenced later work on implicit semantics.

PropBank Project: Palmer et al. (2005)

Martha Palmer et al. created the first large-scale annotated corpus for semantic role labeling, PropBank, using the Penn Treebank. This enabled supervised learning of SRL models.

  • 100,000+ predicate-argument annotations
  • Frame files systematically describing verb senses
  • Enabled benchmark evaluation via CoNLL shared tasks

FrameNet Project: Fillmore et al. (1997–present)

An ongoing collaborative project to build a large lexical database based on frame semantics. Complements PropBank with richer, more abstract semantic descriptions.

Feature-Based SRL: Gildea & Jurafsky (2002)

Seminal work on automatic SRL using syntactic features from parse trees. Established SRL as a supervised learning problem and set the foundation for later systems.

CoNLL Shared Tasks (2004–2012)

Annual shared tasks that standardized SRL evaluation and drove progress:

  • CoNLL-2005: Semantic role labeling for English
  • CoNLL-2009: Multilingual semantic role labeling
  • CoNLL-2012: Coreference resolution and SRL combined

Neural Era (2011–present)

The deep learning revolution transformed SRL:

  • Collobert et al. (2011): First neural SRL using convolutional networks
  • RNN/LSTM approaches (2014–2017): Sequence models with attention
  • BERT & Transformers (2018+): Pre-trained models achieving near-human performance

Current state-of-the-art: 85%+ F-measure on CoNLL-2012

Key Insights from History

  • The move from rules to statistical learning enabled scaling SRL to real data
  • Pre-trained language models provide rich, task-agnostic representations that are ideal for SRL
  • The distinction between shallow syntactic roles and deep semantic roles remains fundamental
  • Multilingual and cross-lingual work is pushing boundaries for low-resource languages
