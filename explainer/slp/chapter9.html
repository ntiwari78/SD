Chapter 9: Masked Language Models

"Larvatus prodeo [Masked, I go forward]" — René Descartes
This chapter explores bidirectional transformer encoders and the paradigm shift introduced by BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers). Unlike autoregressive models that predict left-to-right, masked language models learn by predicting randomly masked tokens using context from both directions. We'll examine the training objectives (masked language modeling and next sentence prediction), the resulting contextual embeddings, and practical applications in classification and sequence labeling tasks like Named Entity Recognition. BERT and its variants (RoBERTa, XLM-RoBERTa) have become foundational models for understanding and leveraging bidirectional context in NLP.
Feynman

The Detective's Method: Understanding Masked Language Models

Imagine you're reading a mystery novel where someone has covered a few words with thick black ink. As a detective, you can't just read forward like a fortune teller predicting the future—instead, you look at all the surrounding clues. The words before AND after the covered word are equally important. You use context from both directions to deduce what might be hidden underneath.

This is fundamentally different from a "fortune teller" approach (autoregressive language models), who can only glance at what came before and must predict the next word with blinders on. They're missing half the clues!

BERT is the detective. It looks in both directions—backward and forward—at the entire sentence. During training, it's given millions of practice cases: sentences with randomly hidden words scattered throughout. The model becomes expert at deducing missing words from their complete context. This bidirectional reading is powerful because language is inherently bidirectional. In the sentence "The bank approved the application," you can't fully understand "bank" without reading ahead to "application."

Fine-tuning is like specialization. After years of general detective work (general pretraining on Wikipedia and books), the detective decides to specialize. They might focus exclusively on financial crimes (sentiment analysis), or become an expert at identifying suspects (named entity recognition). To do this, they add a small, specialized toolkit on top of their general detective skills—a tiny "classification head" that learns to apply their vast knowledge to a specific task.

The key insight: by forcing the model to predict masked words using full context, BERT learns rich, bidirectional representations that capture nuanced meaning. These representations then transfer beautifully to downstream tasks.

Technical

9.1 Bidirectional Transformer Encoders

Bidirectional encoders depart from the causal masking of autoregressive decoders. In standard transformer decoders, attention is masked so that position i can only attend to positions ≤ i (no "looking ahead"). Encoders remove this restriction entirely.

Attention Without Masking

Recall the attention mechanism:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$

In an encoder, there is no mask. The softmax operates over the entire key dimension. Each token can attend to every other token, including those that come later in the sequence. This allows attention heads to gather information from both directions.

BERT Architecture

The original BERT model (Devlin et al., 2018) uses these specifications:

  • Vocabulary: 30,522 tokens (WordPiece vocabulary)
  • Sequence length: N = 512 tokens
  • Hidden dimension: d = 768
  • Layers: L = 12 transformer blocks
  • Attention heads: A = 12 heads (d_k = 768/12 = 64)
  • Parameters: ~100 million (BERT-base); ~340 million (BERT-large)

Multilingual and Scaled Variants

XLM-RoBERTa (Conneau et al., 2019) scales up for 100+ languages:

  • Vocabulary: 250,000 tokens (SentencePiece)
  • Sequence length: N = 512
  • Hidden dimension: d = 1,024
  • Layers: L = 24
  • Attention heads: A = 16
  • Parameters: ~550 million

Encoder vs. Decoder Architecture

Encoders are fundamentally different from decoders:

Property Encoder Decoder
Attention Mask None (full bidirectional) Causal (lower triangular)
Generation Not designed for generation Generates left-to-right
Use Case Classification, NER, QA Machine translation, summarization
Context Type Bidirectional Unidirectional (autoregressive)

9.2 Training Bidirectional Encoders

Masked Language Modeling (MLM)

BERT's primary training objective is masked language modeling, which builds on the cloze task (Taylor, 1953). The cloze task asks: given a sentence with a word removed, fill it in. BERT applies this at scale.

Masking procedure:

  1. Randomly select 15% of tokens in a sequence.
  2. For each selected token:
    • 80% of the time: replace it with the special token [MASK]
    • 10% of the time: replace it with a random token from the vocabulary
    • 10% of the time: keep it unchanged

The 80/10/10 split (Devlin et al., 2018) encourages the model not to "rely on" the presence of [MASK] tokens at test time. A real-world sentence won't have masked tokens, so the model must learn to work without them.

MLM Loss

Let M be the set of masked positions and hiL be the hidden representation at layer L for position i:

$$u_i = h_i^L E^T \quad \text{and} \quad y_i = \text{softmax}(u_i)$$

where E is the embedding matrix (shared with input embeddings). The logits u_i score each vocabulary token. The loss for a single masked token is:

$$L_{\text{MLM}}(x_i) = -\log P(x_i | h_i^L)$$

The batch loss is:

$$L_{\text{MLM}} = -\frac{1}{|M|} \sum_{i \in M} \log P(x_i | h_i^L)$$

Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)

A secondary objective helps the model learn relationships between sentences:

$$y_{\text{NSP}} = \text{softmax}\left(h_{\text{[CLS]}}^L W_{\text{NSP}}\right)$$

BERT concatenates two sentences with special tokens: [CLS] sentence1 [SEP] sentence2 [SEP]. Half the time, sentence2 follows sentence1 in the original corpus (label: IsNext). Half the time, it's a random sentence (label: NotNext). The model predicts which case it is, using the [CLS] token's representation.

Special Tokens and Segment Embeddings

BERT uses special tokens and segment embeddings:

  • [CLS]: Classification token prepended to every sequence. Its final representation is used for sequence-level predictions.
  • [SEP]: Separator token placed between sentences (and at the end).
  • [UNK]: Unknown token for out-of-vocabulary words (rare with WordPiece).
  • [MASK]: Masking token during pretraining.
  • Segment embeddings: Token-level embeddings indicating whether a token belongs to the first or second sentence (e_A or e_B).

Training Regimes

BERT: Trained on English Wikipedia (2.5B tokens) and BookCorpus (0.8B tokens) for 1M steps with batch size 256. NSP included.

RoBERTa: Improved BERT by removing NSP, training longer (500K steps), and using more data (160GB). Found that larger batch sizes and more careful masking strategies helped.

Multilingual Masking Strategy

For multilingual models, vocabulary sampling is crucial. High-resource languages would dominate if sampled uniformly. Instead, use smoothed probability:

$$q_i = \frac{p_i^\alpha}{\sum_j p_j^\alpha}$$

where p_i is the fraction of training examples in language i, and α = 0.7 (hyperparameter). This reduces the exponent, giving low-resource languages more frequent masking and thus more training signal.

9.3 Contextual Embeddings

The hidden state hiL at layer L for token position i is a contextual embedding. It differs fundamentally from static embeddings (like word2vec):

  • Static embeddings: Each word type has one fixed vector (e.g., "bank" always has the same embedding).
  • Contextual embeddings: Each word instance has a different vector depending on context (e.g., "bank" in "river bank" vs. "bank account" have different h_i).

Word Sense Disambiguation with Contextual Embeddings

Contextual embeddings naturally support word sense disambiguation (WSD). For a polysemous word like "bank," collect its contextual embeddings across many sentences:

$$v_s = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} h_i^L$$

This computes an average embedding for sense s. For each new instance, compute cosine similarity:

$$\text{sense}(t) = \arg\max_s \cos(h_t^L, v_s)$$

The highest-scoring sense is selected. This simple approach works surprisingly well compared to more complex WSD systems.

The Anisotropy Problem

A technical issue: contextual embeddings from BERT tend to be anisotropic. The vectors concentrate in a narrow cone in high-dimensional space (Ethayarajh, 2019). This means they're less effective for simple distance-based comparisons (e.g., nearest neighbors).

Fix via standardization: Normalize embeddings layer-by-layer:

$$\mu = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} h_i^L$$
$$\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (h_i^L - \mu)^2$$
$$z_i = \frac{h_i^L - \mu}{\sigma}$$

Standardization improves downstream performance on similarity-based tasks.

9.4 Fine-Tuning for Classification

Sequence Classification

For sentiment analysis or text classification, fine-tune BERT by adding a simple classifier head:

$$\hat{y} = \text{softmax}(h_{\text{[CLS]}}^L W_c + b_c)$$

The [CLS] token's representation is projected through a learned weight matrix W_c to produce logits for each class. Cross-entropy loss is applied over the labels. Typically, fine-tuning runs for 3-5 epochs on the labeled dataset.

Sentence-Pair Classification

For tasks like Natural Language Inference (NLI), classify relationships between two sentences:

$$\hat{y} = \text{softmax}(h_{\text{[CLS]}}^L W_c + b_c)$$

Input format: [CLS] premise [SEP] hypothesis [SEP]. Classes: entailment, contradiction, neutral. Segment embeddings help distinguish the two sentences.

9.5 Sequence Labeling: Named Entity Recognition (NER)

NER assigns entity tags to each token. Common tags:

  • PER: Person (names)
  • ORG: Organization (company, agency)
  • LOC: Location (country, city)
  • GPE: Geopolitical entity (nation-state)
  • O: Other (non-entity)

BIO Tagging

BIO tagging (and variants) represents entity spans:

  • B-X: Beginning of entity type X
  • I-X: Inside (continuation) of entity type X
  • O: Outside (not part of any entity)

Example: "Jane Villanueva works at Ximena Corp" →
Jane (B-PER), Villanueva (I-PER), works (O), at (O), Ximena (B-ORG), Corp (I-ORG)

Variants include IO (no B tags) and BIOES (adds End and Single tags).

NER Fine-Tuning

For NER, add a classifier at each token position:

$$y_i = \text{softmax}(h_i^L W_K + b_K)$$

where W_K projects to |K| tag classes. Compute cross-entropy loss for each token and backpropagate:

$$t_i = \arg\max_k y_i$$

Tokenization Challenges

WordPiece tokenization can split words into subwords. For NER, you must align subword tokens back to the original tokens. Common approaches:

  • Assign to first subword: Only use the tag from the first subword token of a word.
  • Assign to all subwords: Apply the same tag to all subwords of a word.
  • Use a subword model: Train a separate CRF or tagging model on subword tokens.

Implementation: Training and Fine-Tuning BERT

1. MLM Data Preparation

Prepare training data for masked language modeling. Randomly select 15% of tokens and apply the 80/10/10 rule. 

import random
import numpy as np

def mask_sequence(tokens, vocab_size, mask_token_idx=103):
    """Apply MLM masking: 15% selection, 80/10/10 rule."""
    masked_tokens = tokens.copy()
    mask_indices = []

    # Select 15% of token indices
    num_mask = max(1, int(len(tokens) * 0.15))
    candidates = [i for i in range(len(tokens))
                  if tokens[i] not in [101, 102]]  # [CLS]=101, [SEP]=102
    mask_indices = random.sample(candidates, min(num_mask, len(candidates)))

    for idx in mask_indices:
        rand = random.random()
        if rand < 0.8:
            # 80%: Replace with [MASK]
            masked_tokens[idx] = mask_token_idx
        elif rand < 0.9:
            # 10%: Replace with random token
            masked_tokens[idx] = random.randint(999, vocab_size)
        # else: 10% keep unchanged

    return masked_tokens, mask_indices

# Example usage
tokens = [101, 2054, 2003, 2054, 102]  # [CLS] what is what [SEP]
masked, indices = mask_sequence(tokens, vocab_size=30522)
print(f"Masked indices: {indices}")

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModel

class SentimentClassifier(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, num_classes=2):
        super().__init__()
        self.bert = AutoModel.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(0.1)
        self.classifier = nn.Linear(768, num_classes)

    def forward(self, input_ids, attention_mask):
        outputs = self.bert(input_ids, attention_mask=attention_mask)
        cls_output = outputs.last_hidden_state[:, 0, :]  # [CLS] token
        cls_output = self.dropout(cls_output)
        logits = self.classifier(cls_output)
        return logits

3. Named Entity Recognition with BIO Tagging

Fine-tune BERT for NER by adding a classification layer at each token position. 

class NERModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, num_tags=9):  # O, B-PER, I-PER, ... (9 total)
        super().__init__()
        self.bert = AutoModel.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(0.1)
        self.classifier = nn.Linear(768, num_tags)

    def forward(self, input_ids, attention_mask):
        outputs = self.bert(input_ids, attention_mask=attention_mask)
        token_embeds = outputs.last_hidden_state  # [batch, seq_len, 768]
        token_embeds = self.dropout(token_embeds)
        logits = self.classifier(token_embeds)
        return logits

# Training for NER
model = NERModel(num_tags=9)
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=5e-5)
criterion = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()

for batch in train_loader:
    input_ids = batch['input_ids']
    attention_mask = batch['attention_mask']
    labels = batch['labels']  # shape: [batch, seq_len]

    logits = model(input_ids, attention_mask)  # [batch, seq_len, num_tags]
    logits_flat = logits.view(-1, 9)
    labels_flat = labels.view(-1)

    loss = criterion(logits_flat, labels_flat)
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

4. Extracting Contextual Embeddings for Word Sense Disambiguation

Use BERT to extract contextual embeddings and cluster them by word sense. 

def get_contextual_embedding(model, tokenizer, sentence, target_word):
    """Extract contextual embedding for a target word."""
    tokens = tokenizer.tokenize(sentence)
    input_ids = tokenizer.convert_tokens_to_ids(tokens)

    with torch.no_grad():
        outputs = model(torch.tensor([input_ids]))
        embeddings = outputs.last_hidden_state[0]  # [seq_len, 768]

    # Find target word position
    if target_word in tokens:
        idx = tokens.index(target_word)
        return embeddings[idx].numpy()
    else:
        return None

# Collect embeddings for word senses
senses = {
    "financial": [
        "I deposited money at the bank",
        "The bank approved my loan",
    ],
    "geographical": [
        "We sat on the river bank",
        "The sand bank was eroding",
    ]
}

embeddings_by_sense = {}
for sense, sentences in senses.items():
    embeddings_by_sense[sense] = []
    for sent in sentences:
        emb = get_contextual_embedding(model, tokenizer, sent, "bank")
        if emb is not None:
            embeddings_by_sense[sense].append(emb)

# Compute average embedding per sense
sense_centroids = {}
for sense, embs in embeddings_by_sense.items():
    sense_centroids[sense] = np.mean(embs, axis=0)
Modern

BERT's Legacy in 2026

Masked language modeling revolutionized NLP. BERT's 2018 release showed that bidirectional pretraining vastly outperforms left-to-right autoregressive models on downstream classification and understanding tasks. In 2026, we see how this paradigm has evolved and where encoder-based models stand.

Improvements and Variants

  • RoBERTa (2019): Removed NSP objective, trained longer on larger data. Outperformed BERT on most benchmarks.
  • ELECTRA (2020): Instead of masking (which creates an artificial training/test mismatch), ELECTRA uses a generator-discriminator approach. Generator corrupts tokens; discriminator identifies which are genuine vs. replaced.
  • DeBERTa (2020): Disentangled attention: separate query, key, value projections for content and position. Improved architecture efficiency.
  • Sentence Transformers: Fine-tuned BERT variants for semantic similarity and clustering. Crucial for semantic search in 2026.

Encoder vs. Decoder: The Ongoing Debate

By 2026, the field has largely split into two camps:

  • Encoder-only (BERT family): Fast inference, excellent for classification and understanding. Cannot naturally generate text. Best for: NER, sentiment, entailment, QA (when answer exists in context).
  • Decoder-only (GPT family): Can generate fluent text. Requires sequential decoding (slower). Best for: open-ended generation, summarization, machine translation.
  • Encoder-Decoder (T5, BART): Balance. Encoder extracts meaning, decoder generates response. Expensive but flexible for summarization, translation, question-answering with abstract answers.

Contextual Embeddings Transformed NLP

BERT showed that contextual embeddings (learned representations sensitive to surrounding context) are far more effective than static embeddings (word2vec, GloVe). This insight now underpins nearly all modern NLP:

  • Semantic search engines use contextual embeddings to find similar documents.
  • Few-shot learning leverages contextual embeddings' flexibility across domains.
  • Multi-lingual models (XLM-RoBERTa) show that contextual embeddings transfer across languages.

BERT in Production 2026

Most production NLP systems still use BERT variants:

  • Email spam filtering: BERT-based classifiers.
  • Customer support routing: NER to extract intent + classification to route tickets.
  • Content moderation: Fine-tuned BERT for toxic content detection.
  • Search ranking: Contextual embeddings for query-document relevance.

While larger language models dominate research headlines, BERT's efficiency, interpretability, and task-specific fine-tuning capability keep it relevant for resource-constrained deployments.

History

Historical Context: From Static to Contextual Embeddings

The journey to BERT involved decades of progress in representation learning. Here's the timeline:

Timeline of Key Developments (1953–2026) ▼
1953
Taylor's Cloze Task: Wilson Taylor introduces the cloze task, a foundational idea: predict a deleted word from context. This simple idea lay dormant for decades.
2013
Word2Vec (Mikolov et al.): Skip-gram and CBOW models learn static word embeddings from large text. Each word gets one vector (e.g., "bank" → fixed 300-d vector). Efficient and widely adopted.
2015
GloVe (Pennington et al.): Global Vectors for word representation. Combines distributional semantics with matrix factorization. Still static embeddings.
2016
FastText (Bojanowski et al.): Extends Word2Vec by learning subword representations. Handles OOV (out-of-vocabulary) words better.
2017
ELMo (Peters et al.): Embeddings from Language Models. Contextual embeddings from bidirectional LSTM. Major breakthrough: embeddings vary with context (e.g., "bank" gets different vectors in different sentences). Massive improvement on downstream tasks.
2018
BERT (Devlin et al.): Transformer-based bidirectional encoder. Masked language modeling + NSP. Outperforms ELMo. Becomes the standard for understanding tasks. Transformers prove superior to LSTMs for capturing long-range dependencies.
2018
GPT (Radford et al.): Decoder-only transformer. Left-to-right language modeling. Sets stage for GPT-2, GPT-3, and the rise of decoder-only models.
2019
RoBERTa (Liu et al.): Robustly Optimized BERT. Removes NSP, trains longer on 160GB data. Outperforms BERT. Shows that pretraining details matter.
2019
XLNet & ALBERT (Yang et al.): XLNet uses autoregressive language modeling (PLM) with two-stream attention. ALBERT parameter-shares across layers (lighter than BERT). Explorations of trade-offs.
2019
XLM-RoBERTa (Conneau et al.): Scales RoBERTa to 100+ languages on a massive multilingual corpus (2.5TB CommonCrawl). Shows contextual embeddings transfer cross-lingually.
2020
ELECTRA (Clark et al.): Replaces masked LM with a discriminative task. Generator-discriminator framework. More efficient training.
2020
DeBERTa (He et al.): Disentangled attention separates content and position information. Improves upon RoBERTa on SuperGLUE.
2020
T5 (Raffel et al.): Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer. Encoder-decoder. Frames all NLP as text-to-text. Unified framework.
2021+
Sentence Transformers & Embedding Models: Fine-tuned BERT for semantic similarity. Widely used for clustering, semantic search, recommendation systems.
2022+
Large Language Models (LLMs) Surge: GPT-3, PaLM, Claude, etc. decoder-only models dominate. Encoder-only models (BERT family) become more specialized (domain-specific: BioBERT, ClinicalBERT, SciBERT).
2026
Current Era: Hybrid architectures, multimodal pretraining, efficient transformers. BERT variants remain production-standard for classification, NER, tagging. LLMs used for generation. Contextual embeddings are universal.

Key Insights from History

  • Context Matters: ELMo's introduction of contextual embeddings (2017) was pivotal. Static embeddings couldn't capture polysemy or syntax-sensitive meanings.
  • Transformer Efficiency: Transformers (via self-attention) proved more effective than RNNs/LSTMs for capturing long-range dependencies, enabling scaling.
  • Bidirectional > Unidirectional (for understanding): BERT's bidirectional pretraining beats autoregressive left-to-right approaches on understanding tasks. But autoregressive models are necessary for generation.
  • Pretraining is Crucial: Task-specific architectures became obsolete. Today, all models begin with large-scale pretraining, then fine-tune.
  • Scale Trends: Early BERT was 100M–340M parameters. Modern LLMs reach billions. Trade-off: larger models are more capable but less efficient.

Socratic Knowledge Check: Diagnosing a Failing NER System

Scenario: Your team built a medical NER system to extract drug names, dosages, and conditions from clinical notes. You fine-tuned BERT on 5,000 labeled examples and achieved 92% F1 on your test set. But when deployed to real clinical workflows, doctors report that the system misses many drug names—especially newer medications and abbreviations. It also occasionally labels "Dr. Smith" as a drug name, causing confusion.

Below are five diagnostic questions. Click each to reveal expert explanations.

1. Why might the model miss newer drug names?
Answer: BERT's vocabulary (30,522 WordPiece tokens) was built on general English text from Wikipedia and books, collected in 2018. Novel drug names—especially brand names, abbreviations, or compounds developed after 2018—are not in this vocabulary. They get split into meaningless subword tokens (e.g., "Ozempic" → ['Oz', '##em', '##pic']). The model never encountered these drugs during pretraining or fine-tuning on your 5,000 examples.

Solution: Use domain-specific pretrained models like BioBERT or PubMedBERT, which were pretrained on biomedical text and have drug names in their vocabulary. Alternatively, augment your fine-tuning data with synonyms and abbreviations of newer drugs, or add a post-processing step that recognizes medical abbreviations.
2. Why would the model confuse "Dr. Smith" with a drug name?
Answer: "Dr." is an abbreviation that might appear in similar contexts to drug abbreviations in your training data. The tokenizer might split "Dr. Smith" awkwardly (e.g., 'Dr', '.', 'Smith'), and BIO tagging confusion arises: the model learned to tag "Dr" as B-DRUG and "Smith" as I-DRUG if it saw patterns like "Rx [drug abbreviation] [drug name]" in training data. The model is pattern-matching, not understanding—it sees "Dr [name]" and misfires.

Solution: Ensure your training data has diverse negative examples (titles like "Dr.", "Prof.", "Mr." paired with person names as O tags, not drug tags). Add more clinical context (e.g, "Dr. Smith prescribed...") explicitly labeled as person + action. Use a medical abbreviation dictionary to post-process and correct misfires.
3. Your test set achieves 92% F1, but real-world performance is poor. Is your test set representative?
Answer: Likely not. High test F1 but low real-world performance suggests a domain shift or data distribution mismatch. Possible causes:
  • Temporal shift: If your test set is from 2020 and deployment is 2026, new drugs exist that weren't in training.
  • Hospital/source shift: Training data from Hospital A, deployment at Hospital B with different clinical vocabulary or note formats.
  • Label leakage: Your train/test split might not be random. If you split by patient ID but not by time or location, temporal/source shift creeps in.
Solution: Evaluate with temporal splits (train on 2020–2022 data, test on 2024+ notes). Test on out-of-distribution data (different hospitals, different note types). Compute performance separately for seen vs. unseen drug names.
4. Should you scale up to BERT-large (340M parameters) for better accuracy?
Answer: Probably not. The issue is not model capacity but domain specificity and data quality. BERT-large would use more memory and compute without addressing the root cause: the model hasn't learned medical drug names because it wasn't exposed to them during pretraining.

Scaling a model trained on wrong data is wasteful. You'd be throwing more parameters at the problem when the real fix is better data and domain knowledge.

Better approach: Use BioBERT (BERT pretrained on PubMed + PMC biomedical literature) or ClinicalBERT (pretrained on clinical notes). These smaller models (~110M parameters) will outperform BERT-large on medical NER because they know drug names and medical terminology.
5. How should you handle abbreviations like "HTN" (hypertension) or "DM" (diabetes mellitus)?
Answer: Abbreviations are a major source of NER errors in medical text. The neural model alone often struggles because abbreviations are rare in general English.

Solutions:
  1. Create an abbreviation dictionary: Build a mapping of medical abbreviations to full forms (HTN → hypertension). Pre-process notes to expand abbreviations before BERT.
  2. Data augmentation: For every training example with a full condition name, create variants with its abbreviation. This teaches the model both forms.
  3. Two-stage pipeline: Run BERT for base NER, then post-process with a rule-based module that recognizes abbreviations and corrects tags.
  4. Domain-specific tokenization: Use a medical tokenizer (e.g., from scispacy) that preserves medical terms and abbreviations as single tokens, avoiding subword splits.
Best approach: Combine 1 + 3: expand abbreviations as preprocessing, run BERT, use rule-based post-processing to catch edge cases.
