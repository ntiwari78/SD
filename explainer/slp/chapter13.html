Recurrent Neural Networks
and Long Short-Term Memory

From simple recurrence to gated memory — the architecture that taught neural networks to understand sequences, and the attention mechanism that eventually replaced it.

Feynman Bridge

The Notebook Analogy

Imagine you're reading a mystery novel. As you turn each page, you don't start from scratch — you carry a mental summary of everything that's happened so far. You remember who the suspects are, what clues have been found, and the current state of the investigation. Each new page updates that summary: some details become more important, others fade away.

A Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) works exactly like this. It reads a sequence — word by word — and at each step combines the new word with its running summary (the hidden state) to produce an updated summary. It's like passing a note to your future self at each step, saying "here's what matters so far."

But there's a problem. Imagine your mystery novel is 800 pages long. By page 750, can you still remember the vital clue from page 12? Probably not — your mental notepad has been overwritten too many times. This is the vanishing gradient problem.

The LSTM solves this by giving you something better than a single notepad. It gives you a notepad plus a lockbox. The lockbox (called the context vector) can store important facts for hundreds of pages. Three special gatekeepers — the forget gate, add gate, and output gate — decide what goes into or out of the lockbox at each step. The forget gate shreds irrelevant notes. The add gate files new important clues. The output gate decides what to share with your current reading.

Technical Ascent

13.1 — Simple Recurrent Neural Networks

The Elman Network

A simple RNN (also called an Elman network) processes a sequence one element at a time. At each time step t, the hidden layer depends on both the current input and the hidden layer from the previous time step:

(13.1) $$\mathbf{h}_t = g(\mathbf{U}\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x}_t)$$
(13.2) $$\mathbf{y}_t = f(\mathbf{V}\mathbf{h}_t)$$

Three weight matrices are learned: W ∈ ℝdh×din (input → hidden), U ∈ ℝdh×dh (previous hidden → current hidden), and V ∈ ℝdout×dh (hidden → output). The softmax output is:

(13.3) $$\mathbf{y}_t = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{V}\mathbf{h}_t)$$

Training: Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT)

Training uses the same backpropagation algorithm, but applied to the unrolled network. The loss at time t depends on the hidden state, which depends on every previous hidden state. The gradient must flow backward through all time steps — this is called backpropagation through time. For very long sequences, gradients are subject to repeated multiplications that can drive them toward zero (vanishing gradients) or infinity (exploding gradients).

📜 Historical Context: Elman Networks & PDP

From Cognitive Modeling to NLP

Recurrent networks with hidden-layer recurrence were introduced by Jeffrey Elman (1990), building on the Parallel Distributed Processing (PDP) group at UC San Diego. Michael Jordan (1986) had earlier explored output-layer recurrence. These models were primarily studied for cognitive modeling before finding practical NLP applications. The key insight — that unrolling recurrence creates a standard feedforward graph — enabled training with ordinary backpropagation (Rumelhart & McClelland, 1986).

Technical Ascent

13.2 — RNNs as Language Models

An RNN language model predicts the next word given all previous words. Unlike n-gram models (fixed context) or feedforward LMs (fixed window), the RNN's hidden state can in principle represent unbounded context. At each step:

(13.4–13.6) $$\mathbf{e}_t = \mathbf{E}\mathbf{x}_t \qquad \mathbf{h}_t = g(\mathbf{U}\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \mathbf{W}\mathbf{e}_t) \qquad \hat{\mathbf{y}}_t = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{V}\mathbf{h}_t)$$

The probability of a word k being the next word is the k-th component of ŷt:

(13.7) $$P(w_{t+1} = k \mid w_1, \ldots, w_t) = \hat{\mathbf{y}}_t[k]$$

Self-Supervised Training

Training is self-supervised: the natural sequence of words provides its own labels. The cross-entropy loss at time t simplifies to the negative log probability assigned to the true next word:

(13.11) $$L_{CE}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_t, \mathbf{y}_t) = -\log \hat{\mathbf{y}}_t[w_{t+1}]$$

We always feed the correct word (not the model's prediction) as the next input — this is called teacher forcing.

Weight Tying

Since the input embedding matrix E and the output matrix V have transposed shapes, we can share weights by setting V = ET. This weight tying reduces parameters and often improves perplexity:

(13.14) $$\hat{\mathbf{y}}_t = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{E}^\top \mathbf{h}_t)$$

13.3 — RNNs for NLP Tasks

Sequence Labeling (e.g., POS Tagging)

Each input token receives a label. The RNN processes the sequence left-to-right, and at each time step a softmax over the tag set produces a distribution. Example: "Janet/NNP will/MD back/VB the/DT bill/NN".

The loss is the sum of cross-entropy losses at every position, enabling end-to-end training.

Sequence Classification (e.g., Sentiment)

The entire sequence maps to a single label. The RNN reads all tokens, and the final hidden state hn serves as a compressed representation of the whole input. This is fed to a feedforward classifier:

Alternatively, we can pool all hidden states (mean or max pooling):

(13.15) $$\mathbf{h}_{mean} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mathbf{h}_i$$

Autoregressive Generation

Autoregressive generation (or causal LM generation) uses a trained language model to produce text word-by-word. Starting from a special <s> token, each generated word is fed back as input for the next step. This continues until an end-of-sequence marker or length limit is reached.

The key: each generated word is conditioned on all previously generated words, via the hidden state that carries forward context.

Technical Ascent

13.4 — Stacked & Bidirectional Architectures

🏗️ Interactive: Architecture Explorer

Stacked RNNs feed the output of one RNN layer as input to the next. Multiple layers induce increasingly abstract representations — similar to how early visual cortex layers detect edges while deeper layers detect objects. Training costs rise with each added layer.

Bidirectional RNNs run two independent RNNs — one forward, one backward — and concatenate their hidden states. At each position t, the representation captures both left and right context:

(13.18) $$\mathbf{h}_t = \mathbf{h}_t^f \oplus \mathbf{h}_t^b$$

BiRNN for Classification: Instead of using only the final hidden state (which is biased toward the end of the sentence), we combine the final forward state hn→ and the final backward state h1←, giving balanced coverage of the entire input.

13.5 — The LSTM: Gated Memory

The LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) addresses the vanishing gradient problem by introducing an explicit context vector ct alongside the hidden state, controlled by three learned gates. Each gate uses sigmoid activation (producing values between 0 and 1) to act as a soft mask via element-wise (Hadamard) multiplication.

Putting it together

Forget Gate — Erasing Irrelevant Context

Computes a mask that decides which dimensions of the old context to keep or discard. A sigmoid squashes values near 0 (forget) or 1 (keep):

$$\mathbf{f}_t = \sigma(\mathbf{U}_f\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \mathbf{W}_f\mathbf{x}_t) \qquad \mathbf{k}_t = \mathbf{c}_{t-1} \odot \mathbf{f}_t$$

After applying the forget gate, kt is the "cleaned" version of the old context, with irrelevant entries suppressed.

Add Gate — Filing New Information

First, compute a candidate new information vector using tanh (the same basic recurrence). Then, an add (input) gate decides which parts to actually store:

$$\mathbf{g}_t = \tanh(\mathbf{U}_g\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \mathbf{W}_g\mathbf{x}_t) \qquad \mathbf{i}_t = \sigma(\mathbf{U}_i\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \mathbf{W}_i\mathbf{x}_t) \qquad \mathbf{j}_t = \mathbf{g}_t \odot \mathbf{i}_t$$

The filtered new information jt is added to the cleaned context to form the updated context: ct = jt + kt.

Output Gate — Revealing the Current State

The output gate decides which parts of the context are relevant for the current hidden state (as opposed to information preserved for future use):

$$\mathbf{o}_t = \sigma(\mathbf{U}_o\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \mathbf{W}_o\mathbf{x}_t) \qquad \mathbf{h}_t = \mathbf{o}_t \odot \tanh(\mathbf{c}_t)$$

The hidden state ht is what gets passed to the output layer and to the next time step. The context ct flows separately through the "highway" — this is the key to long-range memory.

Full Context Update Pipeline

Putting all three gates together in sequence:

$$\underbrace{\mathbf{c}_t = \underbrace{\mathbf{g}_t \odot \mathbf{i}_t}_{\text{new info}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{c}_{t-1} \odot \mathbf{f}_t}_{\text{retained old}}}_{\text{updated context}} \qquad \mathbf{h}_t = \mathbf{o}_t \odot \tanh(\mathbf{c}_t)$$

The context vector acts as a memory highway. Because it's updated via addition (not multiplication), gradients can flow through it without vanishing — solving the core problem of simple RNNs.

13.7 — The Encoder-Decoder Model

When input and output sequences differ in length and don't align word-for-word (e.g., machine translation, summarization), we use the encoder-decoder (sequence-to-sequence) architecture:

Encoder: Reads the input x1:n and produces contextualized hidden states h1:ne.
Context: c = hne — the final encoder state compresses the entire input.
Decoder: Generates output autoregressively, conditioned on context plus previous output:

(13.32) $$\mathbf{h}_t^d = g(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{t-1},\; \mathbf{h}_{t-1}^d,\; \mathbf{c})$$

The context vector c is provided at every decoding step (not just the first) to prevent its influence from fading. The decoder produces ŷt = softmax(htd) at each step, sampling or greedy-decoding the next token.

The Bottleneck Problem

A single fixed vector c must encode everything about the source — a severe bottleneck for long sentences. This motivates the attention mechanism (Section 13.8).

Technical Ascent

13.8 — Attention Mechanism

Instead of a single static context, attention provides a dynamic, per-step context that is a weighted average of all encoder hidden states, where the weights depend on the current decoder state:

(13.35) $$\text{score}(\mathbf{h}_{i-1}^d, \mathbf{h}_j^e) = \mathbf{h}_{i-1}^d \cdot \mathbf{h}_j^e \qquad \text{(dot-product attention)}$$
(13.36) $$\alpha_{ij} = \frac{\exp(\text{score}(\mathbf{h}_{i-1}^d, \mathbf{h}_j^e))}{\sum_k \exp(\text{score}(\mathbf{h}_{i-1}^d, \mathbf{h}_k^e))}$$
(13.37) $$\mathbf{c}_i = \sum_j \alpha_{ij}\, \mathbf{h}_j^e$$

The decoder hidden state is then computed with this dynamic context:

(13.34) $$\mathbf{h}_i^d = g(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{i-1},\; \mathbf{h}_{i-1}^d,\; \mathbf{c}_i)$$

A more powerful alternative uses bilinear attention: score = hdi-1 Ws hej, where Ws is a learned weight matrix. This evolves into the self-attention mechanism at the heart of Transformers (Chapter 8).

simple_rnn_forward.py — NumPy from scratch 
import numpy as np

# ── Simple RNN Forward Pass (Elman Network) ──
# Implements Eq. 13.1-13.3 from the textbook

def softmax(z):
    """Numerically stable softmax over last axis."""
    e = np.exp(z - np.max(z, axis=-1, keepdims=True))
    return e / e.sum(axis=-1, keepdims=True)

def rnn_forward(X, W, U, V, h0=None):
    """
    Forward pass for a simple RNN.

    Args:
        X:  input sequence, shape (T, d_in)       — T time steps
        W:  input-to-hidden weights (d_h, d_in)
        U:  hidden-to-hidden weights (d_h, d_h)    — the recurrence
        V:  hidden-to-output weights (d_out, d_h)
        h0: initial hidden state (d_h,)            — defaults to zeros

    Returns:
        Y: output sequence (T, d_out) — softmax probabilities
        H: hidden states   (T, d_h)  — for inspection / BPTT
    """
    T, d_in = X.shape
    d_h = W.shape[0]

    # Initialize hidden state to zeros if not provided
    h_prev = h0 if h0 is not None else np.zeros(d_h)

    H = []       # store all hidden states
    Y = []       # store all outputs

    for t in range(T):
        # Eq 13.1: h_t = g(U·h_{t-1} + W·x_t)
        # Using tanh as the activation function g
        h_t = np.tanh(U @ h_prev + W @ X[t])

        # Eq 13.3: y_t = softmax(V·h_t)
        y_t = softmax(V @ h_t)

        H.append(h_t)
        Y.append(y_t)
        h_prev = h_t    # pass hidden state to next step

    return np.array(Y), np.array(H)

# ── Demo: predict next character in "hello" ──
vocab = {'h': 0, 'e': 1, 'l': 2, 'o': 3}
d_in, d_h, d_out = 4, 8, 4

# Random weights (in practice, these are learned)
np.random.seed(42)
W = np.random.randn(d_h, d_in) * 0.1
U = np.random.randn(d_h, d_h) * 0.1
V = np.random.randn(d_out, d_h) * 0.1

# One-hot encode "hell" → predict "ello"
X = np.eye(d_in)[[vocab[c] for c in "hell"]]
Y, H = rnn_forward(X, W, U, V)

print("Hidden states shape:", H.shape)   # (4, 8)
print("Output probs shape:", Y.shape)     # (4, 4)
print("Predicted next chars:",
      [list(vocab.keys())[i] for i in np.argmax(Y, axis=1)])
lstm_language_model.py — PyTorch LSTM for Language Modeling 
import torch
import torch.nn as nn

# ── LSTM Language Model with Weight Tying (Eq 13.14) ──

class LSTMLanguageModel(nn.Module):
    """
    An LSTM-based language model implementing:
    - Embedding layer (E)
    - Multi-layer LSTM with dropout
    - Weight tying: V = E^T (Eq 13.14)
    """
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, d_model=256, n_layers=2, dropout=0.3):
        super().__init__()

        # Input embedding: x_t → e_t (Eq 13.4)
        self.embedding = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, d_model)

        # Stacked LSTM (Section 13.4.1)
        # Each layer: h_t, c_t = LSTM(e_t, h_{t-1}, c_{t-1})
        self.lstm = nn.LSTM(
            input_size=d_model,
            hidden_size=d_model,   # same as embedding for weight tying
            num_layers=n_layers,
            dropout=dropout if n_layers > 1 else 0,
            batch_first=True
        )

        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)

        # Output projection: h_t → logits over vocab
        self.output_proj = nn.Linear(d_model, vocab_size, bias=False)

        # Weight tying (Eq 13.14): V = E^T
        # Share weights between embedding and output projection
        self.output_proj.weight = self.embedding.weight

        self._init_weights()

    def _init_weights(self):
        """Initialize weights for stable training."""
        nn.init.uniform_(self.embedding.weight, -0.1, 0.1)
        for name, param in self.lstm.named_parameters():
            if 'weight' in name:
                nn.init.orthogonal_(param)  # helps gradient flow
            elif 'bias' in name:
                nn.init.zeros_(param)
                # Set forget gate bias to 1 (remember by default)
                n = param.size(0)
                param.data[n//4:n//2].fill_(1.0)

    def forward(self, x, hidden=None):
        """
        Args:
            x: token indices (batch, seq_len)
            hidden: tuple (h_0, c_0) or None
        Returns:
            logits: (batch, seq_len, vocab_size)
            hidden: updated (h_n, c_n)
        """
        # Eq 13.4: e_t = E·x_t
        emb = self.dropout(self.embedding(x))

        # Run through stacked LSTM
        # lstm_out contains h_t at every time step
        lstm_out, hidden = self.lstm(emb, hidden)
        lstm_out = self.dropout(lstm_out)

        # Eq 13.14: ŷ_t = softmax(E^T · h_t)
        # (softmax applied later in loss function)
        logits = self.output_proj(lstm_out)

        return logits, hidden

# ── Training loop sketch ──
vocab_size = 10000
model = LSTMLanguageModel(vocab_size, d_model=512, n_layers=2)
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3)
criterion = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()  # Eq 13.11: -log ŷ_t[w_{t+1}]

# Dummy training step (teacher forcing)
x = torch.randint(0, vocab_size, (32, 64))  # (batch=32, seq=64)
target = torch.randint(0, vocab_size, (32, 64))

logits, _ = model(x)                          # (32, 64, 10000)
loss = criterion(logits.view(-1, vocab_size), target.view(-1))
loss.backward()
torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 0.5)  # prevent exploding gradients
optimizer.step()

print(f"Parameters: {sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters()):,}")
print(f"Loss: {loss.item():.4f}")
seq2seq_attention.py — Encoder-Decoder with Dot-Product Attention 
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

# ── Encoder: Bidirectional LSTM ──
class Encoder(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, d_model=256, n_layers=2):
        super().__init__()
        self.embedding = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, d_model)
        self.lstm = nn.LSTM(
            d_model, d_model, n_layers,
            batch_first=True, bidirectional=True  # Eq 13.16-13.18
        )
        # Project bidir hidden states to decoder size
        self.proj = nn.Linear(d_model * 2, d_model)

    def forward(self, src):
        emb = self.embedding(src)
        enc_out, (h, c) = self.lstm(emb)
        # enc_out: (batch, src_len, 2*d_model) — all hidden states
        enc_out = self.proj(enc_out)  # → (batch, src_len, d_model)
        return enc_out, (h, c)

# ── Dot-Product Attention (Eq 13.35-13.37) ──
class DotProductAttention(nn.Module):
    def forward(self, decoder_state, encoder_states):
        # decoder_state: (batch, d_model)
        # encoder_states: (batch, src_len, d_model)

        # Eq 13.35: score = h^d · h^e (dot product)
        scores = torch.bmm(
            encoder_states,                        # (B, src, d)
            decoder_state.unsqueeze(2)             # (B, d, 1)
        ).squeeze(2)                               # (B, src)

        # Eq 13.36: α = softmax(scores)
        attn_weights = F.softmax(scores, dim=-1)  # (B, src)

        # Eq 13.37: c_i = Σ α_ij · h^e_j
        context = torch.bmm(
            attn_weights.unsqueeze(1),             # (B, 1, src)
            encoder_states                         # (B, src, d)
        ).squeeze(1)                               # (B, d)

        return context, attn_weights

# ── Decoder with Attention (Eq 13.34) ──
class AttentionDecoder(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, d_model=256):
        super().__init__()
        self.embedding = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, d_model)
        # Input = [embedding; context] → LSTM
        self.lstm_cell = nn.LSTMCell(d_model * 2, d_model)
        self.attention = DotProductAttention()
        self.output_proj = nn.Linear(d_model, vocab_size)

    def forward(self, tgt_token, h, c, enc_states):
        # Eq 13.34: h^d_i = g(ŷ_{i-1}, h^d_{i-1}, c_i)
        emb = self.embedding(tgt_token)          # (B, d)
        context, weights = self.attention(h, enc_states)
        lstm_input = torch.cat([emb, context], dim=-1)
        h, c = self.lstm_cell(lstm_input, (h, c))
        logits = self.output_proj(h)
        return logits, h, c, weights

print("✓ Encoder-Decoder with Attention defined")
Modern Bridge · 2026

From RNNs to the Transformer Era

The Attention Revolution

The attention mechanism introduced here (Bahdanau et al., 2015) was the critical stepping stone to Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). The key insight: if attention can look at all encoder states, why not let every position attend to every other position — even within the same sequence? This is self-attention, and it removes the sequential bottleneck of RNNs entirely.

Are RNNs Dead?

Not quite. While Transformers dominate language modeling and generation, RNN-like architectures have made a comeback in specific niches:

State Space Models (S4, Mamba): These architectures (Gu et al., 2022; Gu & Dao, 2023) reformulate recurrence as linear state equations, achieving O(n) inference while rivaling Transformer quality. Mamba and its successors are used in long-context applications where the quadratic cost of self-attention is prohibitive.

RWKV (Peng et al., 2023): A "Receptance Weighted Key Value" model that combines the parallelizable training of Transformers with RNN-like O(1) inference per token. Models up to 14B parameters have been trained.

Edge & Streaming: On-device NLP (keyword spotting, wake-word detection, real-time ASR) still relies heavily on LSTMs and GRUs due to their low memory footprint and constant-time per-step inference.

LSTMs in the Hallucination Conversation

An important linguistic note: RNN/LSTM language models exhibit the same tendency toward fluent but unfaithful generation that modern LLMs do. The autoregressive generation procedure (Section 13.3.3) conditions only on the model's own hidden state — there's no built-in mechanism for factual grounding. This is the same architectural root cause of hallucination that persists in Transformer-based systems. The attention mechanism helped by allowing direct access to source tokens (reducing hallucination in translation), foreshadowing RAG-style approaches.

📜 Historical Context: The LSTM Origin Story

Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997)

Sepp Hochreiter's 1991 diploma thesis first identified the vanishing gradient problem in detail. His collaboration with Jürgen Schmidhuber produced the LSTM in 1997. However, it took nearly 15 years for practical applications to emerge — Graves & Schmidhuber (2005) showed impressive results on phoneme recognition, and Graves et al. (2013) demonstrated breakthrough speech recognition performance. The combination of LSTMs with pretrained embeddings (word2vec, GloVe) in 2013-2014 launched the modern deep learning era in NLP, which was then rapidly superseded by Transformers after 2017.

Socratic Challenge

The Vanishing Agreement

Scenario: Subject-Verb Agreement Across a Relative Clause

You're evaluating a simple (non-LSTM) RNN language model on the sentence:

"The flights the airline was canceling were full."

Your model assigns high probability to "was" after "canceling" but very low probability to "were" — it predicts "was full" instead of "were full." The plural subject "flights" is 5 words back, and the intervening singular "airline" appears to confuse the model.

Question 1
Why does the simple RNN assign higher probability to "was" despite "flights" being the true subject? What property of hidden states causes this?
The hidden state is updated multiplicatively at each step via h_t = tanh(Uh_{t-1} + Wx_t). After 5 steps, the information about "flights" has been repeatedly transformed and blended with newer inputs. The hidden state is biased toward local/recent information — this is the essence of the vanishing gradient problem. The weights U are simultaneously trying to (a) represent current context and (b) carry forward distant information, and they can't do both well.
Question 2
How would an LSTM potentially solve this? Which specific gate would be most critical, and what would it need to learn?
The LSTM's context vector c_t provides an additive memory highway. The forget gate could learn to preserve the "plural subject" feature in the context across the relative clause. The add gate would store "subject is plural" when processing "flights", and the forget gate would NOT erase this while processing "the airline was canceling." Finally, the output gate would surface this information when the model reaches the position where a verb is predicted.
Question 3
Would a bidirectional RNN help with this specific task? Why or why not?
Yes, a bidirectional RNN would help significantly. The backward RNN would process "were full" before reaching "canceling", so at the position of "were", the backward hidden state already knows about the upcoming verb agreement. However, BiRNNs can only be used when the entire input is available (not for autoregressive generation). For language modeling (predicting the next word), we can only use unidirectional left-to-right models, so BiRNNs wouldn't apply there.
Question 4
Why is the gradient "vanishing" rather than "exploding" in this case? What mathematical property of the repeated matrix multiplication through time causes this?
During BPTT, the gradient of the loss with respect to h_1 involves multiplying the Jacobian ∂h_t/∂h_{t-1} repeatedly for each time step. Since tanh squashes values to [-1, 1] and its derivative is at most 1 (at tanh(0)), and the eigenvalues of U typically have magnitude < 1, the product of many such terms tends toward zero exponentially. If the largest eigenvalue of U were > 1, we'd get exploding gradients instead — which is why gradient clipping is used in practice.
Question 5
Modern Transformers handle this agreement task easily. What architectural feature — descended from the attention mechanism in this chapter — makes long-distance dependencies trivial for Transformers?
Self-attention allows every position to directly attend to every other position in O(1) hops, regardless of distance. The word "were" can directly attend to "flights" with a single attention computation, without information having to flow through 5 intermediate hidden states. The attention weights α explicitly learn that verb positions should attend to their grammatical subjects. This is a direct evolution from the encoder-decoder attention (Eq 13.35-13.37) described in this chapter — applied within a single sequence.
