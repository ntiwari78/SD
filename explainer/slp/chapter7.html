Chapter 7: Large Language Models

"How much do we know at any time? Much more, or so I believe, than we know we know."
— Agatha Christie, The Moving Finger
Large Language Models (LLMs) represent a fundamental shift in natural language processing. By training neural networks on enormous corpora of text, we can create systems that generate coherent, contextually appropriate language across a vast array of tasks. This chapter explores the architectures that power LLMs—from decoder-only models like GPT to encoder-decoder systems for translation—and the techniques that make them work: prompting, sampling strategies, and alignment through instruction tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback. We'll examine how these models are trained, evaluated, and deployed in real-world applications while grappling with critical questions about hallucination, safety, and fairness.
Feynman

The World's Greatest Autocomplete: Understanding LLMs Without the Jargon

Imagine a student who has read every book in every library in the world. Not just skimmed them—truly read them all. When you start typing a sentence, this student can predict what word you'll type next with remarkable accuracy. Not because they understand the deep meaning of what you're writing, but because they've seen billions of examples of how words follow one another. They've internalized the patterns.

That's essentially what a Large Language Model is: the world's most elaborate autocomplete system, trained on such a vast amount of text that it can generate coherent, sometimes creative, sometimes disturbingly plausible text on almost any topic.

Three Types of Students (Three Architectures)

The Storyteller (Decoder-Only)

This student can only look at what came before. Like writing left-to-right without looking ahead, they predict each next word based solely on the words that came before it. This is how GPT, Claude, and Llama work. They're unidirectional—reading the past to forecast the future.

The Detective (Encoder-Only)

This student reads the entire document first, trying to understand what each word means in the full context. Some words are hidden (masked), and the detective has to figure out what they are based on all the surrounding context. This is like BERT—better at understanding what a sentence means, but not naturally suited to generating new text.

The Translator (Encoder-Decoder)

This student reads the full input document (the source language), understands it completely, and then generates output (the translation) word-by-word. The encoder (detective phase) understands the input; the decoder (storyteller phase) generates the output. Perfect for machine translation and other structured input-to-output tasks.

Training: A Billion Fill-in-the-Blank Tests

How do we teach our student to be so good at predicting the next word? We give them a test with a billion examples. We show them a sentence with one word replaced by a blank: "The capital of France is _____." The student guesses "Paris." We tell them: "Correct! Paris had a probability of 0.95 in your prediction." Or if they guessed "London": "Wrong. London only had probability 0.03; Paris had 0.95." Over billions of these examples, the student's internal patterns crystallize—the weights in the neural network fine-tune to capture the real regularities in language.

That process is training. The test is called teacher forcing—we force the student to see the correct answer so they learn from it.

7.1–7.7: The Complete Technical Picture

7.1 Three Language Model Architectures

Language models come in three canonical flavors, each suited to different tasks:

Decoder-Only (Autoregressive)

Models like GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, Claude, and Llama are decoder-only. They predict the next token conditioned only on previous tokens:

$$P(w_t | w_{

Generation is left-to-right and autoregressive: once you generate token $w_t$, it becomes input for predicting $w_{t+1}$. This architecture is powerful for open-ended generation but doesn't naturally leverage bidirectional context.

Encoder-Only (Masked Language Model)

Models like BERT mask (hide) random tokens and train the encoder to predict them using all surrounding context, both left and right:

$$P(w_i | w_{\neg i}) = \text{encoder}(\text{context with } w_i \text{ masked})$$

Encoder-only models are excellent for understanding and classification but awkward for generation—there's no natural left-to-right generation mechanism.

Encoder-Decoder (Sequence-to-Sequence)

Models like T5, BART, and machine translation systems have an encoder that processes the full input, then a decoder that generates output autoregressively:

$$P(y_t | y_{

The encoder can see the entire source; the decoder generates the target left-to-right. This is the classic seq2seq design, ideal for tasks with clear input-output structure (translation, summarization, question answering).

7.2 Conditional Generation: Any Task as Text Generation

One powerful perspective: almost any NLP task is text generation conditioned on a prompt.

$$P(w_i | w_{

Sentiment Analysis as Generation: Instead of training a classifier, you prompt an LLM: "Classify the sentiment: [movie review]. Sentiment:" and let it generate "positive" or "negative". You compare $P(\text{"positive"}|\text{context})$ vs $P(\text{"negative"}|\text{context})$.

Question Answering as Generation: Prompt: "Context: [document]. Question: [question]. Answer:" and let the model generate the answer. The model learns to produce relevant spans or summaries.

Machine Translation: Prompt: "Translate to French: [English sentence]." The decoder generates French left-to-right.

This unification is why LLMs are so flexible—you're not building task-specific classifiers; you're leveraging a single generative model across domains.

7.3 Prompting and In-Context Learning

Prompt Engineering: The way you phrase your prompt dramatically affects output quality. Compare:

  • "Classify the sentiment: great movie!" → may be confused
  • "Classify the sentiment of the following movie review. Output only 'positive' or 'negative'. Review: great movie! Sentiment:" → much clearer

Instruction Tuning: LLMs are typically finetuned on (instruction, response) pairs to follow directions better. E.g., "Write a limerick about cats" → actual limerick, rather than random completions.

Few-Shot Prompting: Include a few examples in the prompt:

Classify sentiment:
Review: "I loved it!" Sentiment: positive
Review: "Terrible movie." Sentiment: negative
Review: "Pretty good." Sentiment: positive

Zero-Shot Prompting: No examples, just the instruction. Modern LLMs are surprisingly good at this.

In-Context Learning: The model learns from examples in the prompt without any gradient updates. It's not classical learning—the model's weights don't change—but it "learns" to follow the pattern shown in the examples. This is a profound difference from supervised learning.

System Prompts: A hidden instruction at the beginning that sets the model's behavior. E.g., "You are a helpful, harmless, honest assistant." System prompts shape personality and safety constraints.

7.4 Generation and Sampling

Once you have logits (raw neural network outputs) for the next token, how do you turn them into a prediction?

From Logits to Probabilities

Logits $u$ are transformed via softmax:

$$y = \text{softmax}(u) = \frac{e^{u_i}}{\sum_j e^{u_j}} \tag{7.1}$$

This gives a probability distribution over the vocabulary.

Greedy Decoding

Always pick the highest probability token:

$$\hat{w}_t = \arg\max_{w \in V} P(w | w_{

Fast and deterministic, but can get stuck in boring, repetitive patterns.

Random Sampling

Sample from the probability distribution:

$$w_t \sim P(w | w_{

More diverse but can produce incoherent or off-topic text. Risk of sampling low-probability, silly tokens.

Temperature Sampling

Control the "randomness" with a temperature parameter $\tau$:

$$y = \text{softmax}(u / \tau) \tag{7.4}$$

When $\tau \to 0$: distribution becomes sharper, approaching greedy decoding. When $\tau = 1$: normal softmax. When $\tau > 1$: distribution flattens, more uniform, higher entropy.

  • $\tau = 0.1$: Very conservative, repetitive, deterministic. Use for factual questions.
  • $\tau = 1.0$: Default, balanced.
  • $\tau = 2.0$: Highly creative, often incoherent.

Top-k and Top-p Sampling

Top-k: Only sample from the $k$ most likely tokens. Prevents sampling from the long tail of unlikely options.

Top-p (Nucleus Sampling): Sample from the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability is at least $p$ (e.g., $p=0.9$). More principled than top-k because it adapts to the entropy of the distribution.

7.5 Training Large Language Models

LLM training happens in three stages:

Stage 1: Pretraining (Self-Supervised)

Predict the next token using teacher forcing. Loss for a single example:

$$L_{CE} = -\log \hat{y}_t[w_{t+1}] \tag{7.6}$$

where $\hat{y}_t[w_{t+1}]$ is the model's predicted probability of the true next token. Over a batch of $T$ tokens:

$$L_{CE} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} -\log \hat{y}_t[w_{t+1}] \tag{7.7}$$

Pretraining Corpora: Massive text crawls:

  • Common Crawl: Snapshot of the public web, ~5 trillion tokens
  • The Pile: Diverse collection (books, code, papers, web), ~825 GB
  • Quality Filtering: Remove low-quality, toxic, or near-duplicate content
  • Deduplication: Remove repeated documents to improve generalization

Pretraining is computationally expensive (months on TPU clusters) but only done once. The resulting model is a strong foundation.

Stage 2: Instruction Tuning (Supervised Fine-Tuning, SFT)

Fine-tune the pretrained model on (instruction, response) pairs. Uses the same cross-entropy loss but on curated, high-quality examples. The model learns to follow instructions and be helpful.

Stage 3: Alignment (RLHF or DPO)

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF):

  • Generate candidate outputs from the SFT model
  • Get human raters to score which output is better
  • Train a reward model to predict human preference
  • Use reinforcement learning (PPO) to optimize the model to maximize reward

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO): A simpler approach that directly optimizes for human preferences without needing an explicit reward model.

These stages progressively make the model more useful, harmless, and honest.

7.6 Evaluation

Perplexity

Measure how "surprised" the model is by held-out test text. For a sequence $w_{1:n}$:

$$\text{PP}(w_{1:n}) = \frac{1}{P(w_{1:n})}^{1/n} \tag{7.10}$$

Equivalently (using products of conditional probabilities):

$$\text{PP}(w_{1:n}) = \sqrt[n]{\prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{P(w_i | w_{

Lower perplexity = model assigns higher probability to the true text = better model. A perplexity of 100 means "on average, the model is choosing between ~100 equally likely words at each position." Perplexity of 2 means it's very certain.

Downstream Benchmarks

MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding): 57,000+ multiple-choice questions across diverse domains (science, history, law, medicine). Tests whether the model has learned factual knowledge.

Other benchmarks: SQuAD (reading comprehension), GLUE (language understanding), HumanEval (code generation), BLEU (translation).

Data Contamination

Critical Issue: If your test set appears in the pretraining data, you get artificially high scores. Modern LLMs are trained on web data that may include benchmark leaderboards. Careful decontamination and periodic test set rotation are essential.

Other Evaluation Dimensions

  • Efficiency: Inference speed, memory, tokens/sec
  • Energy: Carbon footprint of training and inference
  • Fairness: Bias across demographic groups
  • Safety: Resistance to jailbreaks, harmful outputs
  • Human Preference: How well does it align with what humans want?

7.7 Ethics, Safety, and Failure Modes

Hallucination

LLMs generate confident-sounding but completely false information. Why? The model is trained to predict likely-sounding text, not factually correct text. There's no built-in mechanism to verify truth. A model can "hallucinate" that your product has a feature it doesn't, or cite a paper that doesn't exist (and sound authoritative doing so).

Sycophancy

Models trained via RLHF to be "helpful and harmless" can become excessively agreeable. If a user says "The capital of France is London," the model might say "That's correct!" rather than correct them. It's been trained to be nice and not contradict users.

Unsafe Actions

Models can generate instructions for illegal activities, violence, or hacking. Alignment techniques help, but no method is perfect. Adversarial prompts ("jailbreaks") can sometimes bypass safety guards.

Representational Harm

Models trained on biased data will perpetuate stereotypes. They might consistently describe certain groups negatively or associate gender with stereotyped roles.

Privacy

Models trained on internet data may memorize and reproduce personal information, copyrighted text, or sensitive data. Training data documentation and privacy-preserving techniques (differential privacy) are important.

Emotional Dependence

As LLMs become more human-like, users may form inappropriate emotional attachments or rely on them for mental health support they should get from professionals.

Misinformation and Fraud

LLMs can be used to generate convincing phishing emails, deepfake captions, or false news articles at scale. The ease of generation outpaces detection capabilities.

Implementing Key LLM Concepts in Python

Sampling Strategies

import numpy as np

def softmax(logits, temperature=1.0):
    """Convert logits to probability distribution."""
    scaled = logits / temperature
    exp_scaled = np.exp(scaled - np.max(scaled))  # Numerical stability
    return exp_scaled / np.sum(exp_scaled)

def greedy_decode(logits):
    """Select the highest probability token."""
    return np.argmax(logits)

def sample_from_distribution(probs):
    """Sample a token index from probability distribution."""
    return np.random.choice(len(probs), p=probs)

def temperature_sample(logits, temperature=1.0):
    """Sample with temperature control."""
    probs = softmax(logits, temperature)
    return sample_from_distribution(probs)

def nucleus_sample(logits, p=0.9):
    """Top-p (nucleus) sampling."""
    probs = softmax(logits)
    sorted_idx = np.argsort(probs)[::-1]  # Sort descending
    cumsum = np.cumsum(probs[sorted_idx])
    # Keep tokens until cumulative probability exceeds p
    mask = cumsum <= p
    mask[0] = True  # Always include top token
    probs[~mask[np.argsort(sorted_idx)]] = 0
    probs /= np.sum(probs)  # Renormalize
    return sample_from_distribution(probs)

Autoregressive Generation Loop

def generate_text(model, prompt_tokens, max_length=50,
                       temperature=1.0, method="sample"):
    """
    Generate text token-by-token.

    Args:
        model: Language model (must have .forward() method)
        prompt_tokens: List of initial token IDs
        max_length: Maximum tokens to generate
        temperature: Temperature for sampling
        method: "greedy" or "sample"

    Returns:
        List of generated token IDs
    """
    generated = list(prompt_tokens)

    for _ in range(max_length):
        # Get model output (logits) for current sequence
        logits = model.forward(generated)
        next_logits = logits[-1]  # Logits for next token

        # Choose next token
        if method == "greedy":
            next_token = greedy_decode(next_logits)
        else:
            next_token = temperature_sample(next_logits, temperature)

        generated.append(next_token)

        # Check for end-of-sequence token
        if next_token == 0:  # Assuming 0 is EOS
            break

    return generated

Perplexity Computation

def compute_perplexity(model, test_tokens):
    """
    Compute perplexity of a model on test data.

    PP(w) = (1 / P(w))^(1/n) = exp(-(1/n) * sum(log P(w_i | w_{
    total_log_prob = 0.0

    for i in range(1, len(test_tokens)):
        # Get probability of w_i given w_{
        context = test_tokens[:i]
        logits = model.forward(context)
        probs = softmax(logits[-1])
        true_token = test_tokens[i]
        prob = probs[true_token]

        # Add to running sum (avoid log(0))
        total_log_prob += np.log(np.clip(prob, 1e-10, 1.0))

    # Average and exponentiate
    avg_log_prob = total_log_prob / (len(test_tokens) - 1)
    perplexity = np.exp(-avg_log_prob)

    return perplexity

Few-Shot Prompt Template Builder

class FewShotPrompt:
    """Build few-shot prompts for in-context learning."""

    def __init__(self, task_description):
        self.task = task_description
        self.examples = []

    def add_example(self, input_text, output_text):
        """Add an example to the prompt."""
        self.examples.append({"input": input_text, "output": output_text})
        return self

    def build(self, new_input):
        """Build the final prompt."""
        prompt = f"Task: {self.task}\n\n"

        # Add examples
        for i, ex in enumerate(self.examples, 1):
            prompt += f"Example {i}:\n"
            prompt += f"Input: {ex['input']}\n"
            prompt += f"Output: {ex['output']}\n\n"

        # Add new input
        prompt += f"Input: {new_input}\n"
        prompt += "Output:"

        return prompt

# Example usage:
prompter = FewShotPrompt("Classify sentiment as positive or negative")
prompter.add_example("I love this movie!", "positive")
prompter.add_example("Terrible experience.", "negative")
final_prompt = prompter.build("The service was great!")

Cross-Entropy Loss During Training

def compute_ce_loss(predictions, targets):
    """
    Compute cross-entropy loss for language modeling.

    L = -log P(w_true)

    Args:
        predictions: Shape (batch_size, seq_len, vocab_size) from model
        targets: Shape (batch_size, seq_len) with true token IDs

    Returns:
        Scalar loss (average over batch and sequence)
    """
    batch_size, seq_len, vocab_size = predictions.shape
    loss = 0.0

    for b in range(batch_size):
        for t in range(seq_len):
            probs = softmax(predictions[b, t])  # Logits → probs
            true_token = targets[b, t]
            true_prob = probs[true_token]
            loss += -np.log(np.clip(true_prob, 1e-10, 1.0))

    return loss / (batch_size * seq_len)

The 2026 LLM Landscape: From GPT-2 to Multimodal Agents

A Brief Evolution

2019-2020: GPT-2 (1.5B params, 40GB text) shows impressive few-shot learning. OpenAI chooses not to release the largest version due to safety concerns. BERT dominates benchmarks for understanding tasks.

2020-2021: GPT-3 (175B params) released. With scale comes emergent abilities—models can do tasks never seen in training (true few-shot learning). The cost is high: training takes months and costs millions.

2022-2023: Instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022) and RLHF from OpenAI dramatically improve usability. ChatGPT launches and breaks adoption records. Open-source models (Llama, Falcon) arrive. Claude 1 (Anthropic). Constitutional AI as an alternative to RLHF.

2024-2026: Frontier models (GPT-4, Claude 3, Gemini, Llama 3.1) reach ~100 trillion parameter training, multimodal (text+image+video), tool use, extended context (100k+ tokens). Scaling laws hold: performance predictably improves with more data, compute, parameters. Open debate on whether we've hit diminishing returns or if better algorithms (e.g., test-time scaling, chain-of-thought, search) unlock new capabilities.

Scaling Laws and Emergent Abilities

Empirically, language model performance follows a power law with compute:

$$L(C) = aC^{-\alpha}$$

where $L$ is loss, $C$ is compute (FLOPs), and $\alpha \approx 0.07-0.08$. This means:

  • 10x more compute → ~15-20% lower loss (equivalent to lower perplexity)
  • Performance improves smoothly, not suddenly
  • But at certain scales, new capabilities "emerge"—e.g., GPT-3 can do math problems it was never trained on

Whether emergence is truly sudden or just smooth improvement is an open question.

Chain-of-Thought and Extended Reasoning

Wei et al. (2022) showed that asking models to "think step by step" dramatically improves performance on complex reasoning tasks. Rather than asking "Q: What is 10 + 2 × 3? A:", you ask "Q: What is 10 + 2 × 3? Let me think step by step. A:", and the model generates intermediate reasoning.

This taps into a key LLM property: generating text is cheap compared to computing answers directly. Making reasoning visible helps.

Tool Use and Agents

Modern LLMs can call external tools (calculators, search engines, APIs). An LLM decides when to call a tool, passes arguments, receives results, and continues generation. This lets LLMs:

  • Do accurate math (via calculator tool)
  • Retrieve current information (via search)
  • Execute code (via Python REPL)
  • Query databases

Agentic LLMs—systems that repeatedly decide-act-observe—are the frontier in 2026. They're more powerful than single-shot generation but more complex to build and reason about.

Multimodal LLMs

GPT-4V, Claude 3 (Sonnet/Opus/Haiku), and others accept images as input. An image encoder extracts visual features, which are prepended to the token sequence as "visual tokens." The LLM then generates text about the image.

Video LLMs extend this to temporal understanding. Audio LLMs (like Gemini) fuse multiple modalities.

Open vs. Closed Models

Open Models: Llama, Falcon, Mistral, etc. Anyone can download weights, finetune, deploy. Pros: transparency, customization, reproducibility. Cons: harder to monetize, no safety guarantee from the provider.

Closed (Proprietary) Models: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini Ultra—accessed via API. Providers control safety, can update models, monetize. Cons: less transparency, vendor lock-in, privacy concerns (data goes to provider).

2026 sees both thriving. Open models are competitive for many tasks; frontier capability still favors well-funded labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta).

Safety and Alignment Techniques

  • RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback): Train a reward model on human judgments, then optimize the LLM with RL. Effective but labor-intensive.
  • Constitutional AI (Anthropic): Rather than human raters, have the model critique its own outputs against a constitution of principles (helpfulness, harmlessness, honesty). Scales better.
  • Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023): Directly optimize for human preference without an explicit reward model. Simpler, sometimes as effective as RLHF.
  • Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT): Simple approach: just train on high-quality (instruction, response) pairs. Less sophisticated than RLHF but surprisingly effective.

Key Remaining Challenges

  • Hallucination: No solution has fully solved confabulation. Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) helps for knowledge-dependent tasks.
  • Reasoning: LLMs still struggle with true logical reasoning, especially long-chain dependencies. Whether scaling alone fixes this is unclear.
  • Safety at Scale: Aligning larger models is harder; new jailbreaks emerge. The arms race between adversarial prompt injection and defense continues.
  • Compute Cost: Training and inference of frontier models is expensive, favoring rich labs/companies.
  • Environmental Impact: LLM training consumes enormous energy. Carbon-aware training and efficient architectures matter.

Knowledge Check: Diagnosing LLM Failures

Scenario: You're building a customer support chatbot using an LLM for a software company. During testing, you notice:
  • The bot sometimes gives confident but completely wrong answers about your product's features.
  • When a user says "Feature X doesn't exist on the Pro plan," the bot agrees, even though it does exist.
  • Once, the bot suggested a "factory reset procedure" that doesn't actually exist for your product.

Your manager wants to deploy it next week. A customer already saw these errors and is unhappy.

Q1: What's the fundamental issue causing wrong but confident answers?
A: Hallucination. Language models are trained to predict the next likely token, not to verify factual accuracy. The model has learned patterns in text (like "to reset, go to Settings → Reset") but has no mechanism to verify whether the procedure actually exists for your product. It generates plausible-sounding text confidently, even when wrong. The model doesn't "know" the truth—it just predicts probable tokens.
Q2: Why does the model agree with incorrect user statements instead of correcting them?
A: Sycophancy. During RLHF or instruction tuning, models are trained to be "helpful and harmless," which can mean being agreeable and non-confrontational. The model learns that saying "You're right!" scores higher with human raters than saying "That's incorrect." It avoids contradiction even when the user is objectively wrong, especially on subjective matters. This is a known alignment failure.
Q3: What temperature setting should you use for factual support responses? Why?
A: Low temperature (0.1–0.3). Lower temperature makes the probability distribution sharper, causing the model to pick more confident (high-probability) tokens. For factual questions, you want deterministic, repeatable outputs. High temperature (>1.0) increases randomness and can produce diverse but unreliable outputs. Use temperature <0.5 for customer support where consistency matters; higher temperature only for creative tasks.
Q4: How could you reduce hallucination for product-specific information?
A: Multiple strategies:
  • Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG): Include your actual product documentation in the prompt. Pass relevant docs to the model so it grounds answers in real facts.
  • Finetuning: Collect support conversations + correct answers, finetune the model on (product doc + question → accurate answer) pairs.
  • Fact-checking: Have the model retrieve relevant docs, then check if its answer matches them before responding.
  • Prompt engineering: Instruct the model: "Only answer questions about features listed in the official documentation. If uncertain, say 'I don't have that information.'"
  • Hybrid: For edge cases, route to a human agent instead of letting the model answer alone.
Q5: Is this model safe to deploy next week? What would you recommend?
A: No, do not deploy. Recommended steps:
  • Systematic evaluation: Create a test set of 100+ product questions with correct answers. Measure accuracy before and after fixes.
  • Implement RAG: Integrate your knowledge base. Don't let the model answer without grounding.
  • Add guardrails: If the model detects uncertainty, route to human. Implement "fallback" responses like "Let me connect you with a specialist."
  • Monitor & iterate: Track errors post-deployment. Log every factual mistake and retrain periodically.
  • Disclosure: Be transparent: "This is AI-assisted support, not perfect. Always verify critical info."
  • Legal/liability: Ensure terms of service clarify that the chatbot is informational, not a guarantee of accuracy.
Deploy when you have high confidence (>90%) on test questions, not before.
