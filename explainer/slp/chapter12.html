The Feynman Bridge

🌎 Zero-Jargon Intuition

Imagine you're a simultaneous interpreter at the United Nations. A speaker says a full sentence in Chinese, and you need to produce the equivalent sentence in English. You can't translate word by word — Chinese puts the verb at a different position, doesn't mark plurals the same way, and sometimes omits pronouns entirely. You need to listen to the whole sentence first (encode it in your mind), then produce the English version (decode it) in the right order for English grammar.

That's exactly what an encoder-decoder model does. The encoder reads the entire source sentence and builds a rich internal representation — like a mental model of the meaning. The decoder then generates the target sentence one word at a time, consulting that mental model at each step through a mechanism called cross-attention (like glancing back at your notes of what the speaker said).

But how do you choose the best translation when there are millions of possible word sequences? That's where beam search comes in. Instead of committing to one word at a time (greedy), you keep the top few candidates alive at each step, like an interpreter mentally juggling multiple phrasings before committing to the best one.

🌐 Scale of the Problem

Google Translate alone processes hundreds of billions of words per day across 100+ languages. MT is probably the most widely deployed NLP technology in the world, helping bridge the digital divide in information access between high- and low-resource languages.

12.1

Language Divergences & Typology

📈 Technical Ascent

Languages differ systematically in ways that create translation divergences. Understanding these patterns — studied as linguistic typology — is essential for building MT systems that handle the structural reorderings between languages.

📜 Other Divergence Types

  • Lexical: English "wall" = German "Wand" (inside) OR "Mauer" (outside). One word maps to many.
  • Morphological: Isolating languages (Vietnamese: 1 morpheme/word) vs. polysynthetic (Yupik: whole sentence = 1 word).
  • Pro-drop: Japanese/Chinese omit pronouns ("cold" languages); English requires them ("hot" language).
  • Motion: English marks direction on particles ("float out"); Spanish marks it on verbs ("salió flotando").
12.3

The Encoder-Decoder Transformer

📈 Technical Ascent

The encoder-decoder architecture has two transformer stacks. The encoder processes source tokens \(x_1, \ldots, x_n\) with bidirectional self-attention (it can look at the full source). The decoder generates target tokens autoregressively with causal self-attention, plus a cross-attention layer that attends to the encoder output.

Encoder-Decoder Core Equations
$$\mathbf{H}_{\text{enc}} = \text{Encoder}(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \qquad y_{t+1} = \text{Decoder}(\mathbf{H}_{\text{enc}},\; y_1, \ldots, y_t)$$
Cross-Attention: Queries from decoder, Keys & Values from encoder
$$Q = H_{\text{dec}}^{[\ell-1]} W^Q \qquad K = H_{\text{enc}} W^K \qquad V = H_{\text{enc}} W^V$$
$$\text{CrossAttn}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$
Training Loss: Cross-Entropy with Teacher Forcing
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}(\hat{y}_t, y_t) = -\log \hat{y}_t[w_{t+1}]$$

⚡ Why Cross-Attention is the Key Innovation

In standard self-attention, Q, K, and V all come from the same sequence. In cross-attention, Q comes from the decoder (what am I looking for?) while K and V come from the encoder (what's available in the source sentence?). This lets every decoder position attend to every source position — the decoder "glances back at its notes" of the source at each generation step.

📜 Historical Context: From Phrase-Based SMT to Neural MT

▼

Before neural MT, the dominant paradigm was phrase-based statistical MT (2000s-2014), which translated by finding aligned phrase pairs in parallel corpora and scoring translations with a log-linear model. Neural MT (Sutskever et al., 2014; Bahdanau et al., 2015) replaced this with end-to-end sequence-to-sequence models, first using RNNs with attention, then transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). The transformer encoder-decoder became the standard MT architecture by 2018 and remains so today, powering Google Translate, DeepL, and Meta's NLLB (No Language Left Behind) for 200 languages.

12.4

Decoding: Beam Search

📈 Technical Ascent

Greedy decoding picks the highest-probability word at each step, but a locally optimal choice may lead to a globally suboptimal translation. Beam search keeps the top \(k\) hypotheses (the beam width) at each step, extending each by all vocabulary words, scoring the \(k \times |V|\) candidates, and pruning back to the best \(k\).

Beam Search Scoring (Log-Probability with Length Normalization)
$$\text{score}(y) = \frac{1}{t} \sum_{i=1}^{t} \log P(y_i \mid y_1, \ldots, y_{i-1}, x)$$

💡 Minimum Bayes Risk (MBR) Decoding

An alternative to beam search: sample many candidate translations, then choose the one most similar to all the others (using chrF or BERTScore). Instead of finding the most probable translation, MBR finds the one with the least expected error. Works with as few as 32-64 samples.

12.6

MT Evaluation: chrF, BLEU & BERTScore

📈 Technical Ascent

MT quality is judged on two axes: adequacy (is the meaning preserved?) and fluency (is the target grammatical and natural?). The simplest robust automatic metric is chrF — character n-gram F-score between the hypothesis and a human reference translation.

chrF: Character F-Score (β = 2 weighs recall 2x precision)
$$\text{chrF}_\beta = (1 + \beta^2) \frac{\text{chrP} \cdot \text{chrR}}{\beta^2 \cdot \text{chrP} + \text{chrR}} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \text{chrF}_2 = \frac{5 \cdot \text{chrP} \cdot \text{chrR}}{4 \cdot \text{chrP} + \text{chrR}}$$

Code Lab: Encoder-Decoder Translation

💻 Implementation
Python · HuggingFace Translation + chrF Eval
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# Machine Translation with Encoder-Decoder (Sec 12.2-3)
# + chrF Evaluation (Sec 12.6)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

from transformers import MarianMTModel, MarianTokenizer
import sacrebleu  # Standardized BLEU/chrF (Post, 2018)

# ── Load a pretrained encoder-decoder MT model ──
# MarianMT uses the transformer enc-dec architecture
# from Sec 12.3, with cross-attention in the decoder.
model_name = "Helsinki-NLP/opus-mt-en-es"  # English→Spanish
tokenizer = MarianTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_name)
model = MarianMTModel.from_pretrained(model_name)

# ── Translate: Encoder processes source, decoder generates target ──
# Internally this runs beam search (Sec 12.4) with k=4 by default
source = ["The green witch arrived"]
tokens = tokenizer(source, return_tensors="pt", padding=True)
translated = model.generate(
    **tokens,
    num_beams=5,          # Beam width k=5 (Sec 12.4)
    max_length=50,
)
output = tokenizer.batch_decode(translated, skip_special_tokens=True)
print(f"Translation: {output[0]}")
# → "Llegó la bruja verde"

# ── Evaluate with chrF (Sec 12.6) ──
# chrF = character n-gram F-score (β=2, weighs recall 2x)
# chrF_β = (1+β²) · chrP·chrR / (β²·chrP + chrR)
reference = ["Llegó la bruja verde"]
hypothesis = output[0]
chrf = sacrebleu.corpus_chrf([hypothesis], [reference])
print(f"chrF2 score: {chrf.score:.1f}")
# → chrF2 score: 100.0 (perfect match!)

# ── Also compute BLEU for comparison ──
bleu = sacrebleu.corpus_bleu([hypothesis], [reference])
print(f"BLEU score: {bleu.score:.1f}")

The 2026 Modern Bridge

🚀 2026 Perspective

🚀 LLMs as Universal Translators

Modern LLMs like GPT-4, Gemini, and Claude can translate between hundreds of languages via prompting alone — no dedicated encoder-decoder needed. However, dedicated MT systems still outperform LLMs on low-resource language pairs and domain-specific translation, because they're trained on curated parallel data rather than general web text.

🤖 NLLB: No Language Left Behind

Meta's NLLB project (2022) built a single encoder-decoder model supporting 200 languages, including many low-resource African and Asian languages. It uses the techniques from Section 12.5: backtranslation for data augmentation, multilingual training, and careful data curation with native speaker involvement. The cross-attention mechanism from Section 12.3 scales elegantly to this many-to-many setting.

📊 MBR Decoding Goes Mainstream

Minimum Bayes Risk decoding (Sec 12.4.1) has seen a resurgence. Instead of beam search, systems sample 64+ candidate translations, then select the one most similar to all others using chrF or BERTScore as the utility function. This consistently outperforms beam search across languages and is becoming the default for high-quality MT.

⚠ The Low-Resource Gap Remains

Despite progress, most of the world's ~7,000 languages still lack sufficient parallel data. The sociotechnical issues from Section 12.5.3 persist: native speakers are often excluded from curation and evaluation, corpora contain boilerplate and mistranslations, and most systems route through English as a pivot. Participatory design approaches (Nekoto et al., 2020) and direct non-English language pairs are active research areas.

❓

Linguistic Challenge: The Pro-Drop Problem

🧠 Socratic Knowledge Check

🔍 Failure Case: Lost in Translation from Japanese

Source (Japanese): 本を読んだ。 (hon-o yonda.) = "[Someone] read a book."

MT Output (English): "Read a book."

Correct Translation: "She read a book." (context: a story about a woman)

Problem: Japanese is a pro-drop language (Sec 12.1.4) — the subject pronoun is omitted. The MT system correctly translates the verb and object, but fails to recover the implied subject "she" that English grammar requires.
Question 1

What typological property of Japanese causes this failure? How does it relate to the "hot" vs. "cold" language distinction from Section 12.1.4?

Japanese is a pro-drop (or "cold") language — it routinely omits pronouns that are recoverable from context. English is a "hot" language that requires explicit pronouns. The MT system must perform pragmatic inference to recover the dropped pronoun, which requires understanding discourse context beyond the single sentence. The encoder-decoder processes one sentence at a time, so it can't see the broader narrative establishing that "she" is the topic.
Question 2

The cross-attention mechanism (Eq. 12.12) lets the decoder attend to source tokens. Why can't it solve the pro-drop problem in this case?

Cross-attention can only attend to what's present in the source sentence. If the subject pronoun is simply absent from the Japanese source (it's not a hidden token — it literally doesn't exist), there's nothing for the decoder to attend to. The information about "she" exists only in the discourse context (previous sentences), which the encoder never sees. Cross-attention is powerful but bounded by the encoder's input; it can't attend to information that was never encoded.
Question 3

Beam search (Sec 12.4) explores multiple hypotheses. Could increasing the beam width help recover the correct pronoun? Why or why not?

Increasing beam width would keep more candidate translations alive (e.g., "He read," "She read," "I read," "They read"). However, all candidates would be scored by the same model, which has no information about the correct pronoun since it's absent from the source. The model might assign the highest probability to the most frequent pronoun in training data (likely "he" due to gender bias in corpora), which might still be wrong. Beam search explores more paths but can't inject information the model doesn't have.
Question 4

How might translating at the document level (rather than sentence level) help with this problem? What are the trade-offs?

Document-level MT would feed multiple sentences (or the full document) to the encoder, giving cross-attention access to prior context where the subject was explicitly mentioned. This would let the model see "Yuki picked up a book" in a previous sentence and correctly infer "she" for the current one. Trade-offs: (1) Computational cost: encoding an entire document is much more expensive than a single sentence. (2) Training data: most parallel corpora are sentence-aligned, not document-aligned. (3) Attention dilution: with very long contexts, the model may struggle to focus on the right preceding sentence. Current research addresses this with hierarchical attention and sliding window approaches.
Question 5

The chrF metric (Sec 12.6) would give this translation a high score despite the missing pronoun. Why? What does this reveal about automatic evaluation?

chrF measures character n-gram overlap between hypothesis and reference. The hypothesis "Read a book" shares most characters with "She read a book" — the only difference is the missing "She " (4 characters out of ~18). chrF would compute high precision (all hypothesis characters appear in the reference) and moderate recall (most reference characters appear in the hypothesis), yielding a relatively high score despite a semantically critical error. This reveals that overlap metrics are insensitive to omissions of short but semantically crucial words. A single missing pronoun can completely change meaning ("he" vs. "she" vs. no one), but barely affects character overlap. This is why human evaluation and embedding-based metrics like BERTScore are needed for nuanced quality assessment.
