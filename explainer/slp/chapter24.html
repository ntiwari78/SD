Discourse Coherence

Chapter 24 · The Invisible Thread Connecting Sentences

Like how a well-edited film feels seamless — each scene connecting logically to the next through cause, contrast, or continuation — discourse coherence is what makes a sequence of sentences feel like a unified text rather than random fragments. Three invisible forces create this coherence: the rhetorical relations between sentences, the entities that persist across them, and the topical glue of shared vocabulary.

This chapter explores the computational models that capture these forces. We'll investigate how machines learn to parse the deep structure of discourse, track entities across sentences using centering theory, compute coherence scores for texts, and build systems that understand not just what words mean, but how ideas flow logically from one sentence to the next.

The Foundations of Coherence

24.1 Three Types of Coherence

Discourse coherence operates across three complementary dimensions:

1. Coherence Relations (Rhetorical)

Local, sentence-to-sentence connections driven by rhetorical relations. Relations like REASON, ELABORATION, and EVIDENCE bind adjacent discourse units into a meaningful structure.

2. Entity-Based Coherence

The persistence of entities (people, objects, concepts) across sentences. A coherent text mentions the same entities repeatedly and tracks them through roles like subject, object, or oblique.

3. Topical Coherence (Lexical Cohesion)

Shared vocabulary and semantic similarity between consecutive sentences. Words, synonyms, and related concepts create a semantic thread across the text.

24.1.1 Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST)

Mann and Thompson's Rhetorical Structure Theory (1987) models discourse as a hierarchical tree where sentences are grouped and related by rhetorical relations. Every relation has a nucleus — the central, essential unit — and a satellite — supporting information.

Key RST Relations (with examples)

  • Reason: Satellite explains why nucleus is true. "The engine failed. The fuel pump was broken."
  • Elaboration: Satellite provides detail about nucleus. "Alice won first place. She ran faster than all competitors."
  • Evidence: Satellite provides evidence for nucleus. "The climate is warming. Polar ice sheets are melting faster than predicted."
  • Attribution: Nucleus attributed to external source. "According to the report, productivity increased 12% last quarter."
  • List: Multiple equally important satellites. "Three factors matter: timing, cost, and risk."
  • Contrast: Satellite contradicts or opposes nucleus. "Some prefer coffee. Others choose tea."

In RST, discourse is built from Elementary Discourse Units (EDUs) — minimal discourse segments, typically clauses or phrases. These EDUs are recursively combined via rhetorical relations to form larger structures, creating a binary branching tree.

RST Discourse TreeBank

  • Size: 385 documents (Wall Street Journal, AP Newswire)
  • Relations: 78 relation types grouped into 16 major classes
  • Structure: Full hierarchical trees with EDU boundaries and relation labels
  • Use Cases: Parsing, generation, question answering, summarization

24.1.2 Penn Discourse TreeBank (PDTB)

The Penn Discourse TreeBank takes a different approach: instead of building trees, it annotates discourse connectives and their arguments. PDTB is lexically grounded, starting from discourse connectives like "because," "although," "when," and "as a result."

PDTB Architecture

For each connective, annotators mark:

  • Arg1 and Arg2: The two text spans connected by the relation
  • Explicit vs. Implicit: Some connectives appear in text (explicit); others are inferred (implicit)
  • Sense: The semantic relation type

PDTB corpus: ~18,000 explicit relations + ~16,000 implicit relations.

PDTB Sense Hierarchy (4 Top-Level Classes)
  • Temporal: Relations of time and sequence (Asynchronous, Synchrony)
  • Contingency: Cause, condition, pragmatic cause/condition (Reason, Result, Condition)
  • Comparison: Similarity and difference (Similarity, Contrast, Concession)
  • Expansion: Elaboration, instantiation, restatement (List, Disjunction, Instantiation, Restatement)

Total: 4 top-level sense classes, 16 types, 23 subtypes.

24.2 Discourse Structure Parsing

24.2.1 EDU Segmentation

The first step in discourse parsing is identifying EDUs. A neural sequence model solves this: tokens are encoded, and for each position, a softmax predicts whether an EDU boundary occurs.

Architecture: Bidirectional LSTM or Transformer encoder over word embeddings → linear layer → softmax over {0=no boundary, 1=boundary}. Each token is classified independently.

24.2.2 RST Parsing with Shift-Reduce

Yu et al. (2018) propose a shift-reduce parser for RST that builds discourse trees bottom-up. The parser maintains a stack (partially built subtrees) and a queue (remaining EDUs).

Shift-Reduce Actions

  • shift: Move next EDU from queue onto stack
  • reduce(l, d): Pop top two items from stack, create new subtree with relation type d (direction l ∈ {NN, NS, SN})
  • pop_root: Final action; mark top of stack as root

The model uses a hierarchical biLSTM encoder: word-level encoding feeds into EDU representation, which feeds into an EDU-level biLSTM for context.

Hierarchical BiLSTM Encoding

Word-level representations:

$$\mathbf{h}_1^w, \mathbf{h}_2^w, \ldots, \mathbf{h}_m^w = \text{biLSTM}(\mathbf{x}_1^w, \mathbf{x}_2^w, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_m^w) \tag{24.17}$$

EDU representation (average pooling from word positions $s$ to $t$):

$$\mathbf{x}^e = \frac{1}{t-s+1}\sum_{k=s}^{t}\mathbf{h}_k^w \tag{24.18}$$

EDU-level representations:

$$\mathbf{h}_1^e, \mathbf{h}_2^e, \ldots, \mathbf{h}_n^e = \text{biLSTM}(\mathbf{x}_1^e, \mathbf{x}_2^e, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_n^e) \tag{24.19}$$

At each parsing step, the decoder computes an output vector by concatenating representations of the top items on the stack and the first item in the queue:

Decoder output:

$$\mathbf{o} = \mathbf{W}[\mathbf{h}_{s_0}^t; \mathbf{h}_{s_1}^t; \mathbf{h}_{s_2}^t; \mathbf{h}_{q_0}^e] \tag{24.20}$$

where $\mathbf{h}_{s_i}^t$ is the representation of the $i$-th item on stack (with subtree representation):

$$\mathbf{h}_S^t = \frac{1}{j-i+1}\sum_{k=i}^{j}\mathbf{h}_k^e \tag{24.21}$$

Action probabilities (softmax):

$$p_a = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{o}_a)}{\sum_{a' \in A}\exp(\mathbf{o}_{a'})} \tag{24.22}$$

Training uses cross-entropy loss with L2 regularization on weights $\Theta$:

$$L_{CE} = -\log(p_a) + \frac{\lambda}{2}||\Theta||^2 \tag{24.23}$$

Evaluation (RST-Parseval): Compares predicted vs. gold trees on four metrics: Span (S), Nuclei (N), Relations (R), and Full match (F).

24.2.3 PDTB Discourse Parsing (Shallow)

PDTB parsing is simpler since it doesn't build trees, just identifies relation instances. The task breaks into four subtasks:

  1. Find connectives: Locate explicit discourse connectives in text
  2. Find spans: Identify Arg1 and Arg2 text spans for each connective
  3. Label relations: Classify the sense (Temporal, Contingency, Comparison, Expansion)
  4. Assign to pairs: Match relations to adjacent sentence pairs

Modern systems use BERT embeddings: concatenate representations of Arg1 and Arg2, pass through feedforward network, softmax for sense classification. Connective disambiguation (e.g., "and" as discourse vs. NP conjunction) is handled via fine-tuning on PDTB data.

24.3 Centering and Entity-Based Coherence

24.3.1 Centering Theory

Grosz, Joshi, and Weinstein's Centering Theory (1995) models how discourse maintains focus on specific entities. Each utterance $U_n$ is associated with:

  • Backward-looking center $C_b(U_n)$: The most salient entity in the previous utterance, typically realized by a pronoun or definite NP
  • Forward-looking centers $C_f(U_n)$: The set of entities mentioned in $U_n$, ranked by grammatical role: subject > object > others
  • Preferred center $C_p(U_n)$: The highest-ranked member of $C_f(U_n)$

Transition Types (Brennan, Friedberg & Pollard 1987): Coherent discourse prefers certain patterns of center change.

Centering Transitions
  • Continue: $C_b(U_{n+1}) = C_b(U_n)$ AND $C_b(U_{n+1}) = C_p(U_{n+1})$ — same center remains focus and is highest-ranked in next utterance
  • Retain: $C_b(U_{n+1}) = C_b(U_n)$ AND $C_b(U_{n+1}) \ne C_p(U_{n+1})$ — same center but not highest-ranked next
  • Smooth-Shift: $C_b(U_{n+1}) \ne C_b(U_n)$ AND $C_b(U_{n+1}) = C_p(U_{n+1})$ — shift to new center but it's highest-ranked
  • Rough-Shift: $C_b(U_{n+1}) \ne C_b(U_n)$ AND $C_b(U_{n+1}) \ne C_p(U_{n+1})$ — shift to new center that's not highest-ranked; incoherent

Two fundamental rules constrain coherent discourse:

Rule 1 (Pronoun Constraint): If any member of $C_f(U_n)$ is realized by a pronoun in $U_{n+1}$, then $C_b(U_{n+1})$ must be realized by a pronoun too.

Rule 2 (Preference): Transitions are ranked: Continue > Retain > Smooth-Shift > Rough-Shift.

24.3.2 Entity Grid Model

Barzilay and Lapata (2008) propose a simpler, data-driven model: the entity grid. A 2D array where rows are sentences and columns are entities. Each cell shows the grammatical role of that entity in that sentence.

Entity Grid Construction

Cell values:

  • S: Subject
  • O: Object
  • X: Neither (oblique, nested)
  • –: Absent from sentence

Features for ML: local entity transitions (pairs like [S –], [S S], [O –], [– X], etc.). Compute transition probability for each pattern type.

Training is self-supervised: discriminate original sentence orderings from permuted versions. A sentence-pair coherence scorer is trained to rank correct orderings higher than scrambled ones. This transfers to predicting coherence for new texts.

Entity Grid Evaluation Tasks

  • Sentence Ordering Discrimination: Rank original vs. permuted versions
  • Sentence Insertion: Find best position to insert sentence
  • Text Reconstruction: Reorder all sentences to maximize coherence

24.4 Representation Learning for Local Coherence

Beyond discrete models, neural approaches learn continuous representations of coherence. Two classical models laid groundwork:

LSA Coherence (Foltz et al. 1998)

Compute the semantic similarity between consecutive sentences using Latent Semantic Analysis:

$$\text{sim}(s,t) = \cos\left(\sum_{w \in s}\mathbf{w}, \sum_{w \in t}\mathbf{w}\right) \tag{24.31}$$

Overall text coherence: average similarity of adjacent sentences:

$$\text{coherence}(T) = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\cos(s_i, s_{i+1}) \tag{24.32}$$

Intuition: similar word spaces → semantic continuity → coherence.

Modern neural models use learned representations. The Local Coherence Discriminator (LCD) (Xu et al. 2019) scores a pair of sentences using rich feature interactions:

LCD Architecture

Given sentence embeddings $\mathbf{s}$ and $\mathbf{t}$, compute features:

$$[\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{t}, \mathbf{s}-\mathbf{t}, |\mathbf{s}-\mathbf{t}|, \mathbf{s} \odot \mathbf{t}]$$

where $\odot$ is element-wise multiplication. Feed through MLP:

$$f(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{t}) = \text{MLP}(\text{concat features}) \in [0, 1]$$

Training uses a margin loss: correct pairs score higher than incorrect ones:

$$\ell(f^+, f^-) = \max(0, \eta - f^+ + f^-) \tag{24.33}$$

where $\eta$ is a margin, $f^+$ is the score for a coherent pair, $f^-$ for an incoherent pair. The loss is 0 when the correct pair's score exceeds the incorrect pair's by at least $\eta$.

24.5 Global Coherence

Beyond local transitions, coherence operates at the global text level. Three major phenomena structure entire documents:

Narrative Structure

Propp's Morphology of the Folktale identified recurring patterns in narratives: characters (dramatis personae) and canonical story functions (quest, ordeal, return). Modern systems extract narrative chains from text or infer story structure.

Argumentation Mining

Extract claims and premises from argumentative text. Relations: SUPPORT (premise backs claim) and ATTACK (premise refutes claim). Persuasion features from Cialdini (1984): reciprocity, social proof, authority, scarcity, liking, commitment.

Scientific Discourse Structure (Argumentative Zoning)

Teufel et al. (1999, 2009) annotate scientific papers with 15 zone labels indicating rhetorical role:

  • AIM: Main research goal
  • OWN_METHOD: Authors' method
  • OWN_RESULTS: Authors' results
  • SUPPORT: Cites work supporting authors
  • ANTISUPPORT: Cites work opposing authors
  • GAP_WEAK: Identifies gap in prior work
  • USE: Future use or implication

This enables systems to understand how papers contribute to scientific discourse.

Code Lab: Building Coherence Models

Three hands-on implementations showing how to compute entity grids, coherence scores, and parse discourse structure.

Lab 1: Entity Grid Builder

Given sentences with (entity, role) annotations, construct an entity grid and compute local transition features.

Python: Entity Grid Construction 
import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict

class EntityGrid:
    def __init__(self, sentences):
        """
        sentences: list of lists of (entity, role) tuples
        role: 'S' (subject), 'O' (object), 'X' (other), None (absent)
        """
        self.sentences = sentences
        self.entities = self._extract_entities()
        self.grid = self._build_grid()

    def _extract_entities(self):
        """Collect all unique entities."""
        entities = set()
        for sent in self.sentences:
            for entity, role in sent:
                entities.add(entity)
        return sorted(list(entities))

    def _build_grid(self):
        """Create 2D grid: rows=sentences, cols=entities."""
        grid = []
        for sent_idx, sent in enumerate(self.sentences):
            entity_roles = {e: '-' for e in self.entities}
            for entity, role in sent:
                if role:
                    entity_roles[entity] = role
            row = [entity_roles[e] for e in self.entities]
            grid.append(row)
        return np.array(grid)

    def get_transitions(self, sent_idx):
        """Get entity transitions from sent_idx to sent_idx+1."""
        if sent_idx >= len(self.grid) - 1:
            return None

        row1 = self.grid[sent_idx]
        row2 = self.grid[sent_idx + 1]
        transitions = []

        for e_idx, entity in enumerate(self.entities):
            role1 = row1[e_idx]
            role2 = row2[e_idx]
            transitions.append((entity, role1, role2))

        return transitions

    def get_transition_features(self, sent_idx):
        """Extract transition patterns for ML features."""
        trans = self.get_transitions(sent_idx)
        if not trans:
            return []

        patterns = []
        for entity, r1, r2 in trans:
            pattern = (r1, r2)
            patterns.append(pattern)

        return patterns

    def display(self):
        """Print the grid nicely."""
        print("\\nEntity Grid:")
        print("Entity".ljust(15), end="")
        for i, sent in enumerate(self.sentences):
            print(f"S{i+1}".ljust(5), end="")
        print()

        for e_idx, entity in enumerate(self.entities):
            print(entity[:14].ljust(15), end="")
            for sent_idx in range(len(self.sentences)):
                print(str(self.grid[sent_idx][e_idx]).ljust(5), end="")
            print()

# Example usage
sentences = [
    [("Alice", "S"), ("prize", "O")],           # Alice won the prize
    [("Alice", "S"), ("medal", "O")],          # She also got a medal
    [("Bob", "S"), ("Alice", "O")],            # Bob congratulated her
    [("Bob", "S"), ("party", "O")]             # He organized a party
]

grid = EntityGrid(sentences)
grid.display()

for i in range(3):
    trans = grid.get_transition_features(i)
    print(f"\\nSentence {i} -> {i+1} transitions:")
    for pattern in trans:
        print(f"  {pattern}")

Lab 2: Coherence Scorer (LSA-style)

Compute text coherence by averaging cosine similarity of adjacent sentence embeddings.

Python: LSA Coherence Scoring 
import numpy as np
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity

class CoherenceScorer:
    def __init__(self, word_vectors=None):
        """
        word_vectors: dict mapping word -> embedding vector
        If None, uses random embeddings for demo
        """
        self.word_vectors = word_vectors or {}

    def sentence_to_vector(self, sentence):
        """
        Convert sentence to vector by averaging word embeddings.
        sentence: list of words
        """
        vectors = []
        for word in sentence:
            word_lower = word.lower()
            if word_lower in self.word_vectors:
                vectors.append(self.word_vectors[word_lower])

        if not vectors:
            # Fallback: return random vector
            return np.random.randn(100)

        return np.mean(vectors, axis=0)

    def compute_coherence(self, sentences):
        """
        Compute text coherence as average similarity of adjacent sentences.
        sentences: list of sentence word lists
        Returns: float in [0, 1]
        """
        if len(sentences) < 2:
            return 1.0

        # Convert sentences to vectors
        vectors = [self.sentence_to_vector(sent) for sent in sentences]

        # Compute pairwise similarities
        similarities = []
        for i in range(len(vectors) - 1):
            sim = cosine_similarity([vectors[i]], [vectors[i+1]])[0][0]
            similarities.append(sim)

        # Return average similarity (normalized to [0,1])
        avg_sim = np.mean(similarities)
        return (avg_sim + 1.0) / 2.0  # Map from [-1,1] to [0,1]

    def get_coherence_profile(self, sentences):
        """
        Return per-sentence coherence contribution.
        Useful for finding incoherent transitions.
        """
        if len(sentences) < 2:
            return [1.0]

        vectors = [self.sentence_to_vector(sent) for sent in sentences]
        profile = [1.0]  # First sentence has no predecessor

        for i in range(len(vectors) - 1):
            sim = cosine_similarity([vectors[i]], [vectors[i+1]])[0][0]
            profile.append((sim + 1.0) / 2.0)

        return profile

# Example: Create a toy word embedding space

Lab 3: RST Shift-Reduce Parser Simulator

Simulate the shift-reduce discourse parsing algorithm, showing stack and queue state at each step.

Python: RST Shift-Reduce Parser 
from collections import namedtuple

# Data structures
EDU = namedtuple('EDU', ['idx', 'text'])
Subtree = namedtuple('Subtree', ['nucleus', 'satellite', 'relation', 'direction'])

class RSTParser:
    def __init__(self, edus):
        """
        edus: list of EDU objects with idx and text
        """
        self.edus = edus
        self.history = []

    def parse(self, actions):
        """
        Execute a sequence of parsing actions.
        actions: list of ('shift' | ('reduce', relation, direction) | 'pop_root')

        Returns: parse tree and execution trace
        """
        stack = []
        queue = list(range(len(self.edus)))  # EDU indices
        tree = None

        self.history = []

        for action in actions:
            state = {
                'action': action,
                'stack': [self._describe_item(item) for item in stack],
                'queue': queue[:3]  # Show first 3
            }

            if action == 'shift':
                if queue:
                    item = queue.pop(0)
                    stack.append(self.edus[item])
                    state['result'] = f"Shifted EDU {item}"
                else:
                    state['result'] = "ERROR: Empty queue"

            elif action[0] == 'reduce':
                _, relation, direction = action
                if len(stack) >= 2:
                    right = stack.pop()
                    left = stack.pop()

                    # Build subtree
                    subtree = Subtree(
                        nucleus=left if direction[0]=='N' else right,
                        satellite=right if direction[0]=='S' else left,
                        relation=relation,
                        direction=direction
                    )
                    stack.append(subtree)
                    state['result'] = f"Reduced: {relation} ({direction})"
                else:
                    state['result'] = "ERROR: Not enough items on stack"

            elif action == 'pop_root':
                if stack:
                    tree = stack[0]
                    state['result'] = "Parse complete"
                else:
                    state['result'] = "ERROR: No root"

            self.history.append(state)

        return tree, self.history

    def _describe_item(self, item):
        """Describe a stack item for display."""
        if isinstance(item, EDU):
            return f"EDU{item.idx}: {item.text[:20]}"
        elif isinstance(item, Subtree):
            return f"Subtree[{item.relation}]"
        else:
            return str(item)

    def print_trace(self):
        """Print the execution trace."""
        print("\\nParsing Trace:")
        print("=" * 80)
        for step_num, state in enumerate(self.history, 1):
            print(f"\\nStep {step_num}: {state['action']}")
            print(f"  Stack: {state['stack']}")
            print(f"  Queue: {state['queue']} ...")
            print(f"  Result: {state['result']}")

# Example: Parse a small discourse
edus = [
    EDU(0, "Alice ran a marathon."),
    EDU(1, "She trained hard."),
    EDU(2, "She finished first."),
    EDU(3, "She was proud."),
]

parser = RSTParser(edus)

# Define parse actions for a simple tree
# We want: ((0 -- elaboration -- 1) nucleus -- reason -- 2) nucleus -- result -- 3
actions = [
    'shift',                                    # Stack: [EDU0]
    'shift',                                    # Stack: [EDU0, EDU1]
    ('reduce', 'elaboration', 'NS'),           # Stack: [Subtree(0-1)]
    'shift',                                    # Stack: [Subtree(0-1), EDU2]
    ('reduce', 'reason', 'NS'),                # Stack: [Subtree((0-1)-2)]
    'shift',                                    # Stack: [Subtree, EDU3]
    ('reduce', 'result', 'NS'),                # Stack: [FinalTree]
    'pop_root'                                  # Done
]

tree, history = parser.parse(actions)
parser.print_trace()

print(f"\\nFinal parse tree: {tree}")

The Modern Era: Discourse in the Age of LLMs

Transformer Attention as Implicit Discourse Modeling

Large language models don't explicitly compute coherence relations or entity grids, yet they implicitly capture discourse structure. Multi-head attention in transformers learns to attend to relevant prior context, effectively tracking entity chains and rhetorical relations through gradient-based learning rather than symbolic rules. Each head specializes: one may track subjects, another may focus on discourse connectives.

Prompt-Based Discourse Parsing

GPT-4 and Claude can perform discourse parsing via prompting. "Identify rhetorical relations between the following sentences" produces reasonable RST-style annotations without training on annotated data. However, these are typically less precise than task-specific models and sometimes hallucinate relations. Few-shot examples improve performance.

Discourse-Aware Text Generation

Modern summarization and paraphrase models benefit from discourse awareness. Systems that model source-target coherence produce more coherent summaries. Controllable generation can enforce specific rhetorical structures: "Generate a response with contrast first, then elaboration."

Neural Argumentation Mining

Pre-trained language models fine-tuned on argument annotations outperform feature-engineered systems. BERTology has shown that BERT learns argument structure implicitly. New datasets (IBM ArgMining, SemEval) push the field toward multimodal and cross-lingual argument extraction.

Remaining Challenges

  • Cross-document coherence: How do multiple documents on the same topic relate? Multi-document summarization is an open problem.
  • Multimodal discourse: Integrating text, images, video, audio. Discourse structure across modalities is not well understood.
  • Pragmatic coherence: Beyond linguistic coherence, does the text achieve its communicative intent? Still largely unsolved.
  • Long-range dependencies: Transformers struggle with very long documents (1000+ tokens). Efficient architectures needed.
  • Zero-shot transfer: Systems trained on news struggle on scientific papers or tweets. Domain adaptation remains crucial.

Historical Timeline: Discourse Coherence (1976–2019)

Key milestones in computational discourse understanding, from foundational linguistic theory to modern neural approaches.

1976
Halliday & Hasan: Cohesion in English
Introduces lexical cohesion as a mechanism for text coherence. Synonymy, hypernymy, and repetition of words create semantic unity. Foundational linguistic framework.
1979
Hobbs: Coherence Relations
Proposes a formal theory of coherence relations: Parallel, Elaboration, Cause, Occasion. Lays groundwork for computational models of discourse structure.
1987
Mann & Thompson: Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST)
Proposes RST, a hierarchical discourse model where texts are recursively decomposed into nuclei and satellites connected by rhetorical relations. Becomes the dominant symbolic discourse framework.
1987
Brennan, Friedberg & Pollard: Centering Theory Extensions
Extends Grosz & Sidner (1986) centering theory with transition rules (Continue, Retain, Shift). Provides formal coherence constraints on pronoun resolution and entity tracking.
1995
Grosz, Joshi & Weinstein: Centering Theory Formalized
Complete formalization of centering: backward-looking centers, forward-looking centers, preferred centers, and coherence-preferring transition rules. Becomes foundation for entity-based coherence modeling.
1997
Hearst: TextTiling
Proposes TextTiling algorithm using cosine similarity of text spans to identify topic boundaries. Bridges lexical cohesion and segmentation; shows that word similarity predicts discourse structure.
1998
Foltz, Kintsch & Landauer: LSA Coherence
Applies Latent Semantic Analysis to measure coherence. Shows sentence-pair semantic similarity predicts human coherence judgments. First large-scale neural coherence model.
1999
Teufel et al.: Argumentative Zoning
Introduces argumentative zoning for scientific discourse. 15 rhetorical labels (AIM, OWN, SUPPORT, etc.) structure research papers. Enables systems to extract scientific contributions.
2001
RST Discourse TreeBank Created
Large-scale annotation project produces 385 documents with full RST parses. Enables supervised discourse parsing research. 78 relation types organized into 16 major classes.
2005
Barzilay & Lapata: Self-Supervised Coherence
Proposes discriminative learning for coherence without gold annotations. Uses sentence order discrimination: original orderings are positive, permuted orderings negative. Shows self-supervised learning scales better than supervised approaches.
2008
Barzilay & Lapata: Entity Grid Model
Introduces entity grid: 2D array tracking entities and their grammatical roles across sentences. Computes transition probabilities as coherence features. Demonstrates entity-based coherence via self-supervision. Becomes influential benchmark.
2008
Penn Discourse TreeBank (PDTB) Published
Major discourse annotation resource: ~18,000 explicit + ~16,000 implicit relations. Lexically grounded in discourse connectives. Introduces 4-way sense hierarchy (Temporal, Contingency, Comparison, Expansion). Becomes standard for shallow discourse parsing.
2014
Ji & Eisenstein: Neural RST Parsing
First end-to-end neural model for RST parsing. Uses recursive neural networks to compose subtrees. Shows deep learning outperforms symbolic features on discourse structure prediction.
2018
Yu et al.: Shift-Reduce RST Parser
Proposes shift-reduce parsing for RST with hierarchical biLSTM encoder. Treats discourse parsing as sequence of parsing actions on stack. Achieves state-of-the-art performance with clearer interpretability than CKY-style parsers.
2019
Xu et al.: Local Coherence Discriminator (LCD)
Proposes LCD: neural model scoring sentence pairs for coherence using rich feature interactions. Uses margin loss to rank coherent orderings above incoherent ones. Shows that learned representations outperform hand-crafted features for coherence prediction.
