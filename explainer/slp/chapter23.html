Coreference Resolution & Entity Linking

Chapter 23 · Teaching Machines Who "They" Are

The Core Insight: When you read a novel, you effortlessly track that "the detective," "she," and "Holmes" all refer to the same character. Your brain maintains a discourse model—a mental set of entities in the story—and connects new mentions to existing characters. Coreference resolution gives machines this same ability. Entity linking goes further: it connects mentions in text not just to other mentions, but to real-world entities in knowledge bases like Wikipedia, creating a bridge between natural language and structured knowledge.

This chapter teaches us how to build systems that can:
  • Track entities across text, handling pronouns, synonyms, and varied mention forms
  • Cluster mentions into equivalence classes (coreference chains)
  • Link entities to knowledge bases for fact verification and knowledge integration
  • Navigate linguistic phenomena like cataphora, elision, and implicit references

Technical Ascent: The Core Concepts

23.1 Coreference Phenomena and Discourse Models

Before we can build systems to resolve coreference, we must understand the linguistic phenomena at play. Coreference is fundamentally about tracking who refers to whom in discourse.

Key Definitions

Mention: A span of text that refers to an entity (e.g., "the detective," "she," "Holmes," "it").

Referent: The real-world entity that a mention points to (e.g., the actual detective character).

Coreference: Two or more mentions that refer to the same entity form a coreference link. All coreferent mentions in a document form a coreference chain.

Discourse model: The set of entities (discourse referents) that have been introduced into the conversation. Each new entity evokes a new discourse referent; subsequent mentions access or modify existing referents.

Types of Referring Expressions:

  • Indefinites (e.g., "a dog", "an elephant") – introduce new discourse entities
  • Definites (e.g., "the dog", "the elephant") – refer to previously established entities
  • Pronouns (e.g., "it", "she", "they") – typically refer to recent antecedents
  • Demonstratives (e.g., "this", "that", "these") – spatially or temporally anchored references
  • Proper names (e.g., "Fido", "Maria") – directly reference entities
  • Zero anaphora (elided pronouns) – omitted pronouns, common in pro-drop languages like Chinese

Information Status (Prince 1981/1992)

Entities can be classified by their status in discourse:

Discourse Status: New vs. Old

  • Discourse-new: First mention in the discourse (indefinite NPs like "a dog")
  • Discourse-old: Previously mentioned (definite NPs, pronouns, names)

Hearer Status: New vs. Old

  • Hearer-new: Assumed not known to the listener (new information)
  • Hearer-old: Assumed known to the listener (shared knowledge)

Types of Entities (by information status):

  • Brand-new: Not previously mentioned or known ("I saw a dog")
  • Unused: Not mentioned but identifiable ("The president gave a speech")
  • Inferable: Inferrable from context ("The burglar broke into the house; the door was damaged")
  • Textually evoked: Previously mentioned in discourse ("I bought a dog; the dog is friendly")
  • Situationally evoked: Present in shared context ("Please pass the salt" at a dinner table)

Related Phenomena: Anaphora and Cataphora

Anaphora: A mention refers back to a previous antecedent. Example: "John arrived early. He was excited." (He = John, anaphoric)

Cataphora: A mention refers forward to a subsequent expression. Example: "When he arrived, John was excited." (he = John, cataphoric, less common)

Linguistic Constraints on Coreference

Binding Theory

Binding theory (from Chomsky) constrains pronoun and reflexive resolution:

  • Reflexives (himself, herself) must have antecedents in the same clause: "John saw himself" (reflexive = John)
  • Pronouns must have antecedents in different clauses: "John said he left" (he = someone other than John, typically)
  • Names and full NPs can corefer with nearly any other entity

Other Constraints:

  • Number agreement: Singular pronouns (he, she) match singular referents; plural (they) match plural referents
  • Gender agreement: he → masculine, she → feminine, it → neuter
  • Person agreement: I/we (first), you (second), he/she/it/they (third)
  • Grammatical role hierarchy: Subjects > Objects > Obliques (pronouns prefer subjects as antecedents)
  • Recency: Recent mentions are preferred over distant ones
  • Verb semantics: Some verbs prefer certain argument structures (e.g., "convince" prefers object control)

Non-Referring Expressions

Not all NPs refer to entities in the discourse model. Systems must identify and exclude:

  • Pleonastic "it": "It is raining" (it does not refer to an entity)
  • Appositives: "My friend, John" (John is an appositive, not a separate mention)
  • Predicative NPs: "He is a teacher" (a teacher is a property, not a coreference relation)
  • Generic NPs: "Dogs are animals" (no specific entity reference)

23.2 Coreference Tasks and Datasets

The primary task in coreference is mention clustering: partition all mentions in a document into equivalence classes such that mentions in the same class corefer.

Major Corpora
  • OntoNotes: Large corpus with coreference annotations across 7 genres (news, broadcast news, broadcast conversation, web data, pivotized English, telephone conversations, Chinese). ~3.6M words with detailed linguistic annotation.
  • CoNLL-2011/2012 Shared Tasks: Standardized evaluation on OntoNotes. CoNLL-2012 became the standard benchmark for English coreference.
  • SemEval tasks: Multilingual and cross-lingual coreference evaluation.

The Coreference Chain

Each document is represented as a set of coreference chains. A chain is a list of mentions that all refer to the same entity:

Chain 1 (President Obama):
  - "Barack Hussein Obama" (sentence 1)
  - "the President" (sentence 3)
  - "he" (sentence 5)

Chain 2 (White House):
  - "the White House" (sentence 2)
  - "the building" (sentence 4)

23.3 Mention Detection

The first step in coreference resolution is identifying all potential mentions. Mentions fall into three broad categories:

Three Types of Mentions

  • Pronouns: he, she, it, they, etc. — detected via POS tagging
  • Named entities: Persons, organizations, locations — detected via NER
  • Full noun phrases: "the dog", "a blue car", "my favorite book" — detected via constituency parsing

Mention Detection Strategy

Standard approach: Over-generate, then prune

  1. Extract mentions: From constituency/dependency parses and NER, extract all NP spans and pronouns
  2. Apply pruning heuristics: Remove non-referring expressions
    • Discard mentions that are numeric entities ("123", "50%")
    • Identify and remove pleonastic "it" (use statistical classifier or heuristics)
    • Remove mentions that are articles or determiners only
    • Discard mentions nested inside other mentions (prefer larger, complete NPs)

23.4 Coreference Resolution Architectures

Given a set of mentions, we need to partition them into coreference chains. Three main architectures have dominated the literature:

Architecture 1: Mention-Pair Classification

Mention-Pair Model

Idea: Train a binary classifier to decide if two mentions corefer.

Process:

  1. For each mention $m_i$, consider all earlier mentions as potential antecedents
  2. Score each pair $(m_i, m_j)$ with a classifier: $p(\text{corefer} | m_i, m_j)$
  3. Link each mention to a previous mention if the score exceeds a threshold, or use closest-first linking or best-first linking

Advantage: Simple, interpretable

Disadvantage: Does not enforce transitivity; makes local decisions without considering global structure

Architecture 2: Mention-Ranking

Mention-Ranking Model

Idea: For each mention, predict a probability distribution over candidate antecedents (including "new entity").

For mention $m_i$, compute softmax over all candidate antecedents $a_j$ and a special token $\epsilon$ (representing a new entity):

$$p(a_j | m_i) = \frac{e^{s(m_i, a_j)}}{\sum_{k=1}^{i-1} e^{s(m_i, a_k)} + e^{s(m_i, \epsilon)}}$$

where $s(m_i, a_j)$ is a scoring function (learned from features or neural representations)

Loss function: For each mention $m_i$, include all gold antecedents in the target set $\text{GOLD}(m_i)$:

$$L = -\sum_{i} \log \sum_{a_j \in \text{GOLD}(m_i)} p(a_j | m_i)$$

This marginalizes over all gold antecedents, allowing flexibility in linking decisions.

Advantages: Globally normalized (softmax); handles one-to-many mappings (multiple gold antecedents)

Disadvantage: Requires defining "all candidate antecedents" (window of recent mentions)

Architecture 3: Entity-Based Models

Cluster-Based Coreference

Idea: Maintain clusters of mentions. For each new mention, decide whether to create a new cluster or add to an existing one. Features consider entire clusters, not just pairwise mention interactions.

Example features:

  • Average distance between new mention and all mentions in cluster
  • Number of gender/number agreements within cluster
  • Semantic similarity between new mention and representative mention of cluster

Advantage: Leverages entity-level information

Disadvantage: Greedy decisions may not be recoverable

23.5 Classic Features for Coreference

Before neural methods dominated, systems relied on hand-crafted features. Many remain useful in hybrid approaches:

Feature Categories
  • Lexical: String match (exact or partial head match), synonym match using WordNet
  • Morphological: Gender agreement (he/she/it), number agreement (singular/plural), person (1st/2nd/3rd)
  • Syntactic: Binding theory constraints, grammatical role (subject > object), semantic role labels
  • Semantic: Named entity type match (PERSON, ORG, LOC), semantic class compatibility
  • Positional: Distance between mentions (recency bias), sentence distance, document position
  • Salience: Mention is a subject (high salience), pronoun vs. full NP, capitalization

23.6 Neural Coreference: End-to-End Models

Lee et al. (2017) introduced end-to-end neural coreference (e2e-coref), which eliminates the mention detection stage and jointly learns to identify mentions and resolve coreference.

Key Innovation: Span Representation

Instead of detecting mentions first, enumerate all possible spans up to length $L$ and score them. A span $i$ has representation:

$$g_i = [\mathbf{x}_{\text{START}(i)} ; \mathbf{x}_{\text{END}(i)} ; \hat{\mathbf{x}}_i ; \phi(i)]$$

where $\mathbf{x}_{\text{START}(i)}$ and $\mathbf{x}_{\text{END}(i)}$ are LSTM/transformer embeddings at span boundaries, $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_i$ is an attention-weighted head representation, and $\phi(i)$ are hand-crafted span features (length, position)

Scoring Functions

Mention score: Probability that span $i$ is a mention:

$$s_m(i) = \mathbf{w}_m \cdot \text{FFNN}_m(g_i)$$

where FFNN is a feed-forward neural network and $\mathbf{w}_m$ is a learned weight vector

Antecedent score: Compatibility of span $i$ with antecedent span $j$:

$$s_a(i,j) = \mathbf{w}_a \cdot \text{FFNN}_a([g_i ; g_j ; g_i \circ g_j ; \phi(i,j)])$$

where $\circ$ is element-wise multiplication and $\phi(i,j)$ includes span distance and other pair features

Final pairwise score (mention $i$, antecedent $j$):

$$s(i,j) = s_m(i) + s_m(j) + s_a(i,j)$$

Pruning Strategy

Since enumerating all spans is expensive, use a two-pass approach:

  1. First pass: Keep only top $\lambda T$ spans by mention score, where $T$ = document length, $\lambda \approx 0.4$
  2. Second pass: For remaining spans, score all antecedent pairs

Training loss: Marginal likelihood over all gold clusters:

$$L = -\log \sum_{(i,j) \in \text{GOLD}} p(j | i)$$

where gold pairs include all mentions linked via coreference chains

23.7 Entity Linking

Entity linking (EL) is the task of linking named entity mentions in text to entities in a knowledge base (Wikipedia, Wikidata, DBpedia, etc.). Unlike coreference, EL grounds mentions in structured knowledge.

Entity Linking Subtasks

EL Pipeline
  1. Entity recognition: Identify entity mention spans (often NER)
  2. Candidate generation: For each mention, generate likely knowledge base candidates
  3. Candidate ranking: Rank candidates and select best match

TAGME: Knowledge Base Entity Linking (Ferragina & Scairano 2010)

TAGME is a classical approach that builds on Wikipedia hyperlink statistics.

Anchor dictionary: Mine all Wikipedia hyperlinks to build a dictionary mapping mention strings to entities:

$$\text{prior}(e | m) = \frac{\text{count}(m \to e)}{\text{count}(m)}$$

This is the "link probability": given mention $m$, what is the probability it links to entity $e$?

Entity relatedness (Milne-Witten): Measure semantic similarity between candidate entities using Wikipedia link overlap:

$$\text{rel}(e_1, e_2) = \frac{\log(\max(|A|,|B|)) - \log(|A \cap B|)}{\log(|W|) - \log(\min(|A|,|B|))}$$

where $A$ and $B$ are sets of Wikipedia pages linking to entities $e_1$ and $e_2$, and $W$ is the set of all Wikipedia pages

TAGME scoring: For each mention and its candidates, combine prior probability and relatedness to other mentions' entities:

$$\text{score}(e | m) = \text{prior}(e|m) + \sum_{m' \text{ in context}} \text{rel}(e, e')$$

Modern Neural Approach: ELQ (Li et al. 2020)

Entity Linking with Bi-Encoders

Idea: Use BERT (or similar transformers) to encode mentions and entities into a shared embedding space, then link via dense similarity.

Architecture:

  • Mention encoder: BERT encodes mention and context → mention embedding
  • Entity encoder: BERT encodes entity title and description → entity embedding
  • Scoring: $\text{score}(e|m) = \text{sim}(\text{emb}_m, \text{emb}_e)$ (e.g., dot product, cosine similarity)

Training: Contrastive loss: push correct entity embedding closer, incorrect ones farther

23.8 Evaluation Metrics for Coreference

Evaluating coreference is complex because the task involves clustering. Three main metrics have been standardized:

MUC (Message Understanding Conference) Metric

Idea: Count minimum number of links needed to form predicted clusters from key (gold) clusters.

Recall:

$$\text{Recall}_{\text{MUC}} = \frac{\sum_i (|S_i| - |p(S_i)|)}{\sum_i (|S_i| - 1)}$$

where $S_i$ is a key cluster, $p(S_i)$ is its partition in the response

Precision: Swap roles of key and response.

Issue: MUC treats all mistakes equally; penalizes singleton (single-mention) errors less than large cluster errors.

B³ (Cubed) Metric

Idea: Compute precision and recall for each mention, then average.

For mention $m$ with gold cluster $K(m)$ and response cluster $R(m)$:

$$\text{Recall}(m) = \frac{|R(m) \cap K(m)|}{|K(m)|}, \quad \text{Precision}(m) = \frac{|R(m) \cap K(m)|}{|R(m)|}$$

Then average over all mentions.

Advantage: Gives equal weight to all mentions; handles singletons uniformly

CEAF (Entity-based F-Measure)

Idea: Find optimal one-to-one mapping between key and response clusters using entity-level similarity $\phi_4$:

$$\phi_4(K_i, R_j) = \frac{2 |K_i \cap R_j|}{|K_i| + |R_j|}$$

Then solve the assignment problem to maximize total similarity

CoNLL Metric (Standard Benchmark):

$$\text{F}_{\text{CoNLL}} = \frac{\text{F}_{\text{MUC}} + \text{F}_{\text{B}^3} + \text{F}_{\text{CEAF}_{\phi_4}}}{3}$$

Average of three F-scores

23.9 The Winograd Schema Challenge

The Winograd Schema Challenge (Levesque et al., 2012) highlights the limitation of shallow pattern matching in coreference resolution. It requires world knowledge and commonsense reasoning.

Classic Winograd Example

Sentence: "The city council refused the demonstrators a permit because they feared violence."

Question: Who is "they"?

Analysis: Without world knowledge, both "council" and "demonstrators" are plausible antecedents for "they" (plural). But human reasoning knows that councils, not demonstrators, have the authority to refuse permits and would fear violence.

Answer: they = council

Variant with opposite answer:

"The city council refused the demonstrators a permit because they advocated violence."

Now "they" more naturally refers to demonstrators (who advocate, not councils).

The Winograd Schema shows that pure coreference resolution (mention clustering) is insufficient; systems need world knowledge, event understanding, and inference capabilities.

23.10 Gender Bias in Coreference Systems

Like many NLP systems, coreference resolvers exhibit gender bias, learning stereotypical associations from training data. Several datasets have been introduced to measure and mitigate this:

WinoBias Dataset (Zhao et al. 2018)

Extends Winograd-style problems with gender stereotypes. Each example has two versions:

  • Stereotypical: Pronoun matches stereotyped occupation gender (e.g., nurse = female)
  • Anti-stereotypical: Pronoun contradicts stereotype

Example: "The nurse asked the doctor to help her patient" vs. "The nurse asked the doctor to help his patient."

Biased systems score stereotypical examples higher.

GAP Dataset (Webster et al. 2018)

Google AI's Gender-balanced Annotated Pronoun resolution dataset: ~4K examples with 50% pronoun-entity gender agreement, reducing gender stereotyping.

Mitigation strategies:

  • Use gender-balanced datasets (GAP)
  • Add gender fairness constraints during training
  • Data augmentation: create anti-stereotypical examples
  • Evaluate separately on stereotypical vs. anti-stereotypical subsets

Lab 1: Rule-Based Pronoun Resolution

A simple rule-based approach using recency and agreement constraints:

rule_based_pronoun_resolution.py 
import re
from collections import defaultdict

class PronounResolver:
    """Simple rule-based pronoun resolution using recency and agreement."""

    def __init__(self):
        self.pronouns_3rd = {
            'he': 'male', 'she': 'female', 'it': 'neuter',
            'him': 'male', 'her': 'female',
            'his': 'male', 'hers': 'female', 'its': 'neuter',
            'they': 'plural', 'them': 'plural', 'their': 'plural'
        }
        self.reflexives = {'himself', 'herself', 'itself', 'themselves'}

    def is_pronoun(self, word):
        return word.lower() in self.pronouns_3rd or word.lower() in self.reflexives

    def get_number(self, word):
        """Return singular or plural."""
        return 'plural' if word.lower() in ['they', 'them', 'their'] else 'singular'

    def get_gender(self, word):
        """Return gender of pronoun."""
        return self.pronouns_3rd.get(word.lower(), 'unknown')

    def extract_noun_phrases(self, sentence):
        """Simple NP extraction: sequences of DET+ADJ*+NOUN."""
        nps = []
        words = sentence.split()
        # Very simplified: just capture capitalized sequences or 'the X' patterns
        i = 0
        while i < len(words):
            if words[i].lower() in ['the', 'a', 'an']:
                start = i
                i += 1
                while i < len(words) and not words[i][0].isupper() and words[i].lower() not in ['.', ',']:
                    i += 1
                nps.append(' '.join(words[start:i]))
            elif words[i][0].isupper():
                nps.append(words[i])
                i += 1
            else:
                i += 1
        return nps

    def resolve(self, sentences):
        """Resolve pronouns across sentences."""
        chains = defaultdict(list)
        entities_seen = {}  # Map from entity name to chain ID
        chain_counter = 0

        for sent_idx, sent in enumerate(sentences):
            words = sent.split()
            nps = self.extract_noun_phrases(sent)

            # Add NPs to entity list
            for np in nps:
                head = np.split()[-1]  # Simple head: last word
                if np not in entities_seen:
                    entities_seen[np] = chain_counter
                    chains[chain_counter].append((sent_idx, np, 'NP'))
                    chain_counter += 1

            # Find and resolve pronouns
            for word_idx, word in enumerate(words):
                if self.is_pronoun(word):
                    # Find best antecedent from recent NPs
                    best_antecedent = None
                    best_distance = float('inf')

                    for entity, chain_id in entities_seen.items():
                        # Prefer recent mentions
                        if (sent_idx, entity, 'NP') in chains[chain_id]:
                            distance = sent_idx  # Recency
                            if distance < best_distance:
                                best_distance = distance
                                best_antecedent = entity

                    if best_antecedent:
                        chain_id = entities_seen[best_antecedent]
                        chains[chain_id].append((sent_idx, word, 'PRONOUN'))

        return chains

# Example usage
resolver = PronounResolver()
sentences = [
    "John arrived at the office yesterday.",
    "The manager greeted him warmly.",
    "He said the project was going well."
]

chains = resolver.resolve(sentences)
for chain_id, mentions in chains.items():
    print(f"Chain {chain_id}: {mentions}")

Lab 2: Feature-Based Mention-Ranking

A simplified neural-inspired mention-ranking model with feature vectors:

feature_based_mention_ranking.py 
import numpy as np
from itertools import combinations

class MentionRanker:
    """Simplified mention-ranking using feature vectors and dot-product scoring."""

    def __init__(self, feature_dim=10):
        self.feature_dim = feature_dim
        # Simulate learned weights
        self.weights = np.random.randn(feature_dim)

    def string_match(self, m1, m2):
        """1 if heads match, 0 otherwise."""
        h1, h2 = m1.split()[-1].lower(), m2.split()[-1].lower()
        return 1.0 if h1 == h2 else 0.0

    def distance_feature(self, idx1, idx2):
        """Negative distance (prefer recent antecedents)."""
        return -abs(idx2 - idx1)

    def length_feature(self, m1, m2):
        """Absolute difference in lengths."""
        return -abs(len(m1.split()) - len(m2.split()))

    def semantic_similarity(self, m1, m2):
        """Placeholder: word overlap similarity."""
        words1 = set(m1.lower().split())
        words2 = set(m2.lower().split())
        if not words1 or not words2:
            return 0.0
        return len(words1 & words2) / len(words1 | words2)

    def extract_features(self, mention_i, mention_j, idx_i, idx_j):
        """Extract feature vector for mention pair."""
        features = np.zeros(self.feature_dim)
        features[0] = self.string_match(mention_i, mention_j)
        features[1] = self.distance_feature(idx_i, idx_j) / 10.0  # normalize
        features[2] = self.length_feature(mention_i, mention_j) / 5.0
        features[3] = self.semantic_similarity(mention_i, mention_j)
        return features

    def score_pair(self, mention_i, mention_j, idx_i, idx_j):
        """Compute coreference score for mention pair."""
        features = self.extract_features(mention_i, mention_j, idx_i, idx_j)
        # Dot product with learned weights (simplified neural scoring)
        score = np.dot(self.weights[:len(features)], features)
        return score

    def cluster_mentions(self, mentions):
        """
        Cluster mentions using greedy best-first linking.
        Mentions: list of (mention_text, sentence_index) tuples
        """
        clusters = []  # List of lists, each inner list is a cluster

        for current_idx, (current_mention, current_sent) in enumerate(mentions):
            best_score = -float('inf')
            best_cluster = None

            # Score against existing clusters (use cluster representative)
            for cluster_idx, cluster in enumerate(clusters):
                representative = cluster[0]  # Use first mention as representative
                rep_mention, rep_sent = representative

                score = self.score_pair(current_mention, rep_mention, current_sent, rep_sent)

                if score > best_score:
                    best_score = score
                    best_cluster = cluster_idx

            # If score is negative, start new cluster
            if best_cluster is not None and best_score > 0:
                clusters[best_cluster].append((current_mention, current_sent))
            else:
                clusters.append([(current_mention, current_sent)])

        return clusters

# Example usage
ranker = MentionRanker()
mentions = [
    ("Barack Hussein Obama", 0),
    ("the President", 1),
    ("he", 2),
    ("the White House", 1),
    ("it", 3),
    ("the building", 4),
    ("he", 5),
]

clusters = ranker.cluster_mentions(mentions)
for i, cluster in enumerate(clusters):
    print(f"Cluster {i}: {cluster}")

Lab 3: Entity Linking with TAGME-Style Anchor Priors

A simplified entity linking system using anchor dictionary and entity relatedness:

entity_linking_tagme.py 
import math
from collections import defaultdict

class TAGMELinker:
    """Simplified TAGME-style entity linking using anchor priors and relatedness."""

    def __init__(self):
        # Anchor dictionary: mention -> {entity: count}
        self.anchor_dict = defaultdict(lambda: defaultdict(int))
        # Entity to Wikipedia pages linking to it (for relatedness)
        self.entity_links = defaultdict(set)
        # All entities
        self.all_entities = set()

    def add_anchor(self, mention, entity, count=1):
        """Add entry to anchor dictionary."""
        self.anchor_dict[mention.lower()][entity] += count
        self.all_entities.add(entity)

    def set_entity_links(self, entity, wikipedia_pages):
        """Set the Wikipedia pages linking to an entity."""
        self.entity_links[entity] = set(wikipedia_pages)

    def prior_probability(self, mention, entity):
        """P(entity | mention) from anchor dictionary."""
        mention_key = mention.lower()
        if mention_key not in self.anchor_dict:
            return 0.0

        entity_count = self.anchor_dict[mention_key].get(entity, 0)
        total_count = sum(self.anchor_dict[mention_key].values())

        return entity_count / total_count if total_count > 0 else 0.0

    def milne_witten_relatedness(self, e1, e2):
        """
        Milne-Witten semantic relatedness between two entities.
        Based on Wikipedia link overlap.
        """
        A = self.entity_links.get(e1, set())
        B = self.entity_links.get(e2, set())

        if not A or not B:
            return 0.0

        intersection = len(A & B)
        union = len(A | B)

        if intersection == 0:
            return 0.0

        max_ab = max(len(A), len(B))
        min_ab = min(len(A), len(B))
        all_pages = len(A | B) + 1000  # Approximate total Wikipedia pages

        numerator = math.log(max_ab + 1)
        denominator = math.log(all_pages)
        overlap_term = math.log(intersection + 1) / denominator

        relatedness = (numerator - overlap_term) / (math.log(all_pages) - math.log(min_ab + 1))
        return max(0, relatedness)  # Clamp to [0, 1]

    def link_entities(self, mentions, context_entities=None):
        """
        Link mentions to entities, considering context.

        mentions: list of mention strings
        context_entities: dict mapping previous mentions to their linked entities
        """
        if context_entities is None:
            context_entities = {}

        results = {}

        for mention in mentions:
            candidates = self.anchor_dict.get(mention.lower(), {})

            if not candidates:
                results[mention] = None
                continue

            best_entity = None
            best_score = -float('inf')

            for entity in candidates:
                # Start with prior probability
                score = math.log(self.prior_probability(mention, entity) + 1e-6)

                # Add context-based relatedness
                for prev_mention, prev_entity in context_entities.items():
                    if prev_entity:
                        relatedness = self.milne_witten_relatedness(entity, prev_entity)
                        score += relatedness

                if score > best_score:
                    best_score = score
                    best_entity = entity

            results[mention] = best_entity

        return results

# Example usage: build a small knowledge base
linker = TAGMELinker()

Socratic Challenge: Test Your Understanding

Answer these multiple-choice questions to deepen your understanding of coreference resolution and entity linking.

1. In the mention-ranking model, why do we sum the loss over all gold antecedents (rather than just one best antecedent)?
Correct Answer: B
In natural discourse, a pronoun can sometimes refer to multiple valid antecedents, creating genuine ambiguity. By summing over all gold antecedents, the loss $L = -\sum_{i} \log \sum_{a_j \in \text{GOLD}(m_i)} p(a_j | m_i)$ allows the model to distribute probability mass across all correct answers, rather than forcing it to pick a single "best" one. This is called marginalization and is key to handling ambiguity in structured prediction.
2. What key difference distinguishes the B³ metric from the MUC metric in coreference evaluation?
Correct Answer: B
MUC focuses on "edit distance"—how many links are needed to form predicted clusters from gold ones. This can underweight singleton mentions (entities mentioned once). B³, by contrast, computes precision and recall for each individual mention: how many predicted cluster members overlap with the gold cluster? This gives equal weight to all mentions, making B³ fairer for evaluating systems that must handle rare, singleton entities accurately.
3. Why is the Winograd Schema Challenge particularly challenging for coreference systems?
Correct Answer: B
Winograd Schema examples like "The city council refused the demonstrators a permit because they feared violence" require understanding that councils (not demonstrators) have the authority to refuse permits. This demands commonsense knowledge and event understanding, not just morphological agreement or recency. The schema highlights that pure clustering approaches are insufficient—we need reasoning about the world. Variants with opposite answers show that the task is not about grammatical patterns but semantic understanding.
4. In the end-to-end neural coreference model (Lee et al. 2017), what do the three components of the span score $s(i,j) = s_m(i) + s_m(j) + s_a(i,j)$ capture?
Correct Answer: B
The end-to-end model jointly learns mention detection and coreference linking. The mention scores $s_m(i)$ and $s_m(j)$ rate whether spans $i$ and $j$ are legitimate mentions (high scores for likely mentions, low for pleonastic "it" or determiners). The antecedent score $s_a(i,j)$ captures whether the two mentions are compatible—do they refer to the same entity? By summing all three, we get a holistic score: "both spans are mentions, and they're likely coreferent." During pruning, only high-scoring mentions are kept; during linking, we find the best antecedent.

Modern Bridge: LLMs and Beyond

How Large Language Models Handle Coreference

Modern large language models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) handle coreference implicitly through their transformer-based architectures. Rather than explicit clustering algorithms, they learn rich contextual representations where coreferent mentions converge to similar embeddings.

LLM Advantages
  • Implicit entity tracking: Transformer attention automatically tracks which tokens refer to the same entity
  • Commonsense knowledge: Pre-trained on vast text, LLMs understand world knowledge needed for Winograd-style examples
  • Zero-shot capability: Can resolve pronouns without task-specific training
  • Multilingual: Single model handles multiple languages, including pro-drop languages

Prompt-Based Coreference Resolution

Rather than training dedicated models, we can use in-context learning with LLMs:

LLM Prompt for Coreference 
Resolve coreference in the following text. For each pronoun, identify its antecedent.

Text: "John went to the store with his friend Maria. He bought a book for her. She was delighted."

Output:
- "He" (sentence 2) → "John" (sentence 1)
- "her" (sentence 2) → "Maria" (sentence 1)
- "She" (sentence 3) → "Maria" (sentence 2)

Entity Linking with Dense Retrieval and LLM Re-ranking

Modern entity linking combines dense vector retrieval with LLM-based re-ranking:

Hybrid Entity Linking Pipeline

  1. Mention encoding: Use BERT or bi-encoder to embed the mention and its context
  2. Candidate retrieval: Retrieve top-k entities from knowledge base via dense similarity search
  3. LLM re-ranking: Use a large language model to score candidates in context, considering:
    • Semantic fit with surrounding text
    • Entity type compatibility
    • Coherence with other linked entities
  4. Final linking: Select highest-scoring candidate

Coreference in Multilingual and Cross-Lingual Settings

Modern approaches extend coreference to multiple languages:

  • Multilingual transformers (mBERT, XLM-R): Single model handles 100+ languages with transfer learning
  • Cross-lingual coreference: Link coreferent mentions across different languages (useful for information extraction from multilingual documents)
  • Pro-drop handling: Languages like Chinese, Japanese, Spanish allow omitted pronouns (zero anaphora); models must infer these elided mentions

Remaining Challenges

Open Problems in Coreference
  • Implicit references: "I bought a book. The author was brilliant." (The author = the author of the book, inferred relation)
  • Bridging anaphora: "The car broke down. The engine was smoking." (The engine = part-of the car, not direct coreference)
  • Discourse deixis: "John left the party early. That surprised everyone." (That = the event of leaving, not an entity)
  • Rare entities: Systems fail on newly emerging entities (new organizations, people)
  • Long documents: Maintaining discourse model over 10k+ tokens remains challenging
  • Bias and fairness: Gender bias persists; need for inclusive training data
  • Joint tasks: Coreference often needs to coordinate with other tasks (named entity recognition, semantic role labeling, question answering)

Autoformalization and Coreference

Recent work on autoformalization (converting natural language to logical forms) highlights coreference as a bridge between informal language and formal reasoning:

Example: "If a student submits homework late, they are penalized."

Formal representation must track that "a student" introduces a variable, and "they" refers back to the same variable:

∀x. Student(x) ∧ SubmitsLate(x) → Penalized(x)

This shows that coreference resolution is foundational for tasks that require precise meaning extraction from natural language.

Historical Context: The Evolution of Coreference Research

Coreference resolution has evolved from simple heuristics to deep neural models over nearly 50 years. Here's a timeline of key milestones:

1976

Hobbs' Algorithm

Jerry Hobbs introduces the naive algorithm, a breadth-first search of the parse tree to find pronoun antecedents. Despite its simplicity, this algorithm achieves surprisingly good results (80%+) on limited domains, establishing that structure-based heuristics can be effective.

1981

Centering Theory

Aravind Joshi and colleagues propose centering theory, formalizing how discourse entities are tracked and preferred as antecedents. The "forward-looking center" (Cf) and "backward-looking center" (Cb) concepts become foundational for modeling discourse coherence.

1995

MUC Evaluation and Shared Tasks

The Message Understanding Conference introduces the first standardized MUC metric for coreference evaluation, enabling direct comparison of systems. The coreference shared task becomes a driver for research progress.

1998

B³ Metric Proposed

Amit Bagga and Breck Baldwin propose the B³ (cubed) metric, addressing limitations of MUC by evaluating precision and recall per mention. This enables fairer evaluation on documents with many singleton entities.

2005

CEAF Metric by Luo

Xiaoqiang Luo introduces CEAF (entity-based F-measure), which uses optimal cluster alignment. The combination of MUC, B³, and CEAF becomes the CoNLL metric, the gold standard in coreference evaluation.

2010

TAGME: Anchor-Based Entity Linking

Paolo Ferragina and Ugo Scairano propose TAGME, demonstrating that Wikipedia hyperlink anchor statistics can effectively link mentions to entities. This makes entity linking practical for large-scale knowledge bases.

2012

CoNLL Shared Task on OntoNotes

The CoNLL-2012 shared task on coreference resolution in OntoNotes becomes the standard benchmark for the next decade, driving adoption of neural methods and enabling systematic comparison of systems across multiple languages and genres.

2017

End-to-End Neural Coreference (Lee et al.)

Kenton Lee et al. introduce end-to-end neural coreference, eliminating the mention detection pipeline and jointly learning to identify mentions and resolve coreference. This approach achieves state-of-the-art on CoNLL-2012, establishing neural methods as dominant.

2018

Gender Bias Datasets: WinoBias and GAP

Jiaominag Zhao et al. introduce WinoBias and Google releases the GAP dataset, highlighting gender bias in coreference systems. These datasets drive research on fairness and mitigation strategies.

2020

ELQ: Neural Entity Linking with Dense Retrieval

Hovav Shwartz et al. and Xiang Li et al. propose ELQ and related dense retrieval approaches, replacing sparse, feature-based methods with neural bi-encoders. This enables scaling entity linking to millions of entities.

2023-2025

LLM-Based Coreference and Reasoning

Large language models (GPT-3/4, Claude, Gemini) demonstrate strong implicit coreference resolution and commonsense reasoning capabilities. Research shifts toward understanding how transformers handle discourse phenomena and leveraging LLMs for few-shot or zero-shot resolution.

Key Takeaway

Coreference resolution has evolved from heuristic-based algorithms to neural end-to-end models, and now to implicit resolution within large language models. Yet core challenges—ambiguity, world knowledge, rare entities—remain open. The field continues to drive innovation in discourse understanding, structured prediction, and fairness in NLP.
