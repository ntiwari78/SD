Chapter 17: Sequence Labeling for Parts of Speech and Named Entities

From Understanding Text to Labeling It

What You'll Learn

We've traveled through transformers and LLMs (Ch 7–9) and learned how RNNs process sequences (Ch 13). Now we zoom into a fundamental task at the heart of natural language understanding: assigning a label to every word in a sentence.

Imagine you're a postal worker sorting mail. Each letter (word) needs exactly one bin (label). Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging sorts words into grammatical bins—noun, verb, adjective, adverb, etc. Named Entity Recognition (NER) is like highlighting proper nouns with colored markers: yellow for persons, blue for locations, green for organizations.

These are sequence labeling tasks: the output has exactly the same length as the input. And while they seem simple, they're surprisingly tricky. Ambiguity is everywhere—the word "back" can be a noun, verb, adjective, adverb, or particle depending on context. Yet modern systems achieve ~97% accuracy, rivaling human annotators.

Why It Matters

POS tagging is a pipeline component for downstream tasks like syntactic parsing, chunking, and dependency parsing. Even at 97% accuracy, a 500-word document has ~15 errors—and these compound in later stages.

Named Entity Recognition powers information extraction, question answering, and knowledge base construction. Recognizing that "Apple" is a company (not a fruit) in "Apple announced Q4 earnings" is crucial.

Both tasks are now typically solved with Transformer models fine-tuned on task-specific data, but the probabilistic foundations—HMMs and Conditional Random Fields—remain essential for understanding why these models work and how to debug them.

Technical Ascent

17.1 English Word Classes

English words fall into two main categories:

Category Examples Defining Property
Open Classes nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs New words added regularly (e.g., "selfie", "blockchain")
Closed Classes determiners, pronouns, prepositions, conjunctions, auxiliaries, particles Fixed membership; rarely change

The Penn Treebank tagset has 36 POS tags. Examples:

  • NN (noun, singular), NNS (noun, plural), NNP (proper noun, singular), NNPS (proper noun, plural)
  • VB (verb, base), VBD (verb, past), VBG (verb, gerund), VBN (verb, past participle)
  • JJ (adjective), RB (adverb), PRP (pronoun, personal), DT (determiner)

The Universal Dependencies tagset harmonizes POS tags across 100+ languages with just 17 universal tags (NOUN, VERB, ADJ, ADV, DET, PRON, ADP, CCONJ, SCONJ, INTJ, PART, NUM, SYM, X).

Ambiguity in POS: While only 14–15% of word types (unique words) are ambiguous, roughly 55–67% of word tokens (actual occurrences) are ambiguous. The word "can" is both a modal verb (NN: tin can) and a verb (VB: they can run). Context is essential.

17.2 Part-of-Speech Tagging Problem

Input: A sequence of words $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$

Output: A sequence of tags $y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_n$ where each $y_i \in \mathcal{T}$ (the tag inventory)

Goal: Find the tag sequence that is most likely given the word sequence:

$$\hat{y}_{1:n} = \arg\max_{y_1 \ldots y_n} P(y_1 \ldots y_n | x_1 \ldots x_n)$$

State-of-the-art accuracy: ~97% across many algorithms (HMMs, MEMMs, CRFs, neural networks, Transformers).

Baseline: Most-frequent-class tagger (assign each word its most common tag) achieves ~92% accuracy—showing that a small 5% improvement requires careful modeling.

17.3 Named Entity Recognition

NER identifies and classifies named entities (persons, organizations, locations, etc.) within text.

Common entity types:

  • PER (person): John, Jane, Stalin
  • ORG (organization): Google, WHO, Democratic Party
  • LOC (location): France, New York, the Amazon
  • GPE (geopolitical entity): Germany (nation state)
  • TIME: Monday, 2024, yesterday
  • MONEY: $50, 100 euros

BIO Tagging Scheme: Since entities span multiple tokens, we use tags:

  • B-TYPE (Begin): the first token of an entity
  • I-TYPE (Inside): subsequent tokens of an entity
  • O (Outside): tokens that are not part of any entity

17.4 Hidden Markov Models for POS Tagging

HMMs model the problem probabilistically via two components:

1. Markov Assumption: The current tag depends only on the previous tag:

$$P(q_i = a | q_1, \ldots, q_{i-1}) = P(q_i = a | q_{i-1}) \quad \text{(Eq 17.3)}$$

2. HMM Structure: Hidden states (tags) generate observed outputs (words).

Transition Probabilities $P(t_i | t_{i-1})$: estimated by MLE from a tagged corpus:

$$P(t_i | t_{i-1}) = \frac{C(t_{i-1}, t_i)}{C(t_{i-1})} \quad \text{(Eq 17.8)}$$

where $C(t_{i-1}, t_i)$ is the count of bigrams $(t_{i-1}, t_i)$ in the training data.

Emission Probabilities $P(w_i | t_i)$: how likely a word is given a tag:

$$P(w_i | t_i) = \frac{C(t_i, w_i)}{C(t_i)} \quad \text{(Eq 17.10)}$$

HMM Decoding via Bayes' Rule: Find the tag sequence maximizing the joint probability:

$$\hat{t}_{1:n} = \arg\max_{t_1 \ldots t_n} \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(w_i|t_i) P(t_i|t_{i-1}) \quad \text{(Eq 17.17)}$$

The Viterbi Algorithm: This is a dynamic programming algorithm that finds the optimal path through a lattice (states × time steps) in $O(n \cdot |T|^2)$ time, where $|T|$ is the number of tags.

Recurrence relation:

$$v_t(j) = \max_{i=1}^{N} v_{t-1}(i) \cdot a_{ij} \cdot b_j(o_t) \quad \text{(Eq 17.19)}$$

where $v_t(j)$ is the best score to reach state $j$ at time $t$, $a_{ij}$ is the transition probability from state $i$ to state $j$, and $b_j(o_t)$ is the emission probability of observation $o_t$ in state $j$.

Key insight: Viterbi is to HMMs what beam search is to LSTMs. It trades exhaustive search for dynamic programming to find the optimal sequence efficiently.

17.5 Conditional Random Fields (CRFs)

CRFs are discriminative models that directly model $P(Y|X)$ rather than the joint $P(Y, X)$. They avoid making strong independence assumptions and allow arbitrary features.

Global CRF Probability:

$$p(Y|X) = \frac{1}{Z(X)} \exp\left(\sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k F_k(X,Y)\right) \quad \text{(Eq 17.24)}$$

where:

  • $Z(X)$ is the partition function: $\sum_{Y'} \exp(\sum_k w_k F_k(X,Y'))$
  • $w_k$ are learned weights
  • $F_k(X,Y)$ are global feature functions

Global Features Decompose into Local Features:

$$F_k(X,Y) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f_k(y_{i-1}, y_i, X, i) \quad \text{(Eq 17.26)}$$

Common Feature Templates:

  • Word identity: $w_i$, $w_{i-1}$, $w_{i+1}$
  • Morphological: suffixes (last 1–4 chars), prefixes, word shape (e.g., Xxxxx for "Janet")
  • POS context: previous tag $y_{i-1}$ (bigram features)
  • Lexical: capitalization, contains digit, contains hyphen

CRF Viterbi Decoding:

$$v_t(j) = \max_{i=1}^{N}\left[v_{t-1}(i) + \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k f_k(y_{t-1}, y_t, X, t)\right] \quad \text{(Eq 17.33)}$$

Training: CRFs are trained via SGD to maximize log-likelihood with L1 or L2 regularization. Unlike HMMs, CRFs can use overlapping, arbitrary features without worrying about independence.

17.6 Evaluation Metrics

POS Tagging: Simple accuracy (percentage of correct tags).

97%
State-of-the-art accuracy

Named Entity Recognition: Entity-level metrics (not token-level):

  • Precision: $\text{TP} / (\text{TP} + \text{FP})$ — fraction of predicted entities that are correct
  • Recall: $\text{TP} / (\text{TP} + \text{FN})$ — fraction of gold entities that were found
  • F1: $2 \cdot \text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall} / (\text{Precision} + \text{Recall})$

Significance Testing: Use paired bootstrap tests to determine if differences between systems are statistically significant.

Code Implementation

import numpy as np

class HMMTagger:
    """Bigram HMM POS Tagger with Viterbi decoding."""

    def __init__(self):
        self.transition = {}
        self.emission = {}
        self.tags = set()

    def train(self, tagged_sentences):
        """Estimate transition & emission probs."""
        tag_counts = {}
        bigram_counts = {}
        emission_counts = {}

        for sentence in tagged_sentences:
            prev_tag = '<s>'
            for word, tag in sentence:
                self.tags.add(tag)
                tag_counts[tag] = tag_counts.get(tag, 0) + 1
                tag_counts[prev_tag] = tag_counts.get(prev_tag, 0) + 1

                bigram_counts[(prev_tag, tag)] = bigram_counts.get((prev_tag, tag), 0) + 1
                emission_counts[(tag, word)] = emission_counts.get((tag, word), 0) + 1
                prev_tag = tag

        # MLE: Eq 17.8 and 17.10
        for (prev, curr), count in bigram_counts.items():
            self.transition[(prev, curr)] = count / tag_counts[prev]
        for (tag, word), count in emission_counts.items():
            self.emission[(tag, word)] = count / tag_counts[tag]

    def viterbi(self, words):
        """Find best tag sequence via Viterbi (Eq 17.19)."""
        tags = list(self.tags)
        n = len(words)

        viterbi = [{} for _ in range(n)]
        backptr = [{} for _ in range(n)]

        for tag in tags:
            trans_p = self.transition.get(('<s>', tag), 1e-10)
            emit_p = self.emission.get((tag, words[0]), 1e-10)
            viterbi[0][tag] = trans_p * emit_p
            backptr[0][tag] = '<s>'

        for t in range(1, n):
            for tag in tags:
                best_score, best_prev = 0, tags[0]
                for prev_tag in tags:
                    score = (viterbi[t-1].get(prev_tag, 0) *
                            self.transition.get((prev_tag, tag), 1e-10) *
                            self.emission.get((tag, words[t]), 1e-10))
                    if score > best_score:
                        best_score = score
                        best_prev = prev_tag
                viterbi[t][tag] = best_score
                backptr[t][tag] = best_prev

        # Backtrace
        best_tag = max(viterbi[-1], key=viterbi[-1].get)
        result = [best_tag]
        for t in range(n-1, 0, -1):
            best_tag = backptr[t][best_tag]
            result.insert(0, best_tag)
        return list(zip(words, result))
def bio_tag(entities, tokens):
    """Convert entity spans to BIO tags."""
    tags = ['O'] * len(tokens)
    for entity_type, start, end in entities:
        tags[start] = f'B-{entity_type}'
        for i in range(start + 1, end):
            tags[i] = f'I-{entity_type}'
    return list(zip(tokens, tags))

def evaluate_ner(gold_entities, pred_entities):
    """Entity-level precision, recall, F1."""
    gold_set = set(gold_entities)
    pred_set = set(pred_entities)

    tp = len(gold_set & pred_set)
    precision = tp / len(pred_set) if pred_set else 0
    recall = tp / len(gold_set) if gold_set else 0
    f1 = 2 * precision * recall / (precision + recall) \
        if (precision + recall) else 0

    return {'precision': precision, 'recall': recall, 'f1': f1}

# Example
tokens = "Janet Villanueva of United Airlines discussed Chicago".split()
entities = [('PER', 0, 2), ('ORG', 3, 5), ('LOC', 7, 8)]
tagged = bio_tag(entities, tokens)
print("BIO-tagged sentence:")
for word, tag in tagged:
    print(f"  {word:15s} {tag}")
def extract_crf_features(sentence, i, prev_tag):
    """Extract CRF features for position i (Eq 17.26)."""
    word = sentence[i]
    features = {}

    # Word identity
    features[f'word={word.lower()}'] = 1
    features[f'prev_tag={prev_tag}'] = 1

    # Context window
    if i > 0:
        features[f'prev_word={sentence[i-1].lower()}'] = 1
    if i < len(sentence) - 1:
        features[f'next_word={sentence[i+1].lower()}'] = 1

    # Suffix/prefix features (up to 4 chars)
    for k in range(1, min(5, len(word) + 1)):
        features[f'suffix_{k}={word[-k:]}'] = 1
        features[f'prefix_{k}={word[:k]}'] = 1

    # Word shape (Xxxxx, xxxxx, Xxxxx, ddddd, etc.)
    shape = ''.join('X' if c.isupper() else 'x' if c.islower()
                     else 'd' if c.isdigit() else c for c in word)
    features[f'shape={shape}'] = 1

    # Short shape (collapse consecutive chars)
    short = shape[0]
    for c in shape[1:]:
        if c != short[-1]:
            short += c
    features[f'short_shape={short}'] = 1

    # Lexical
    features['is_upper'] = int(word[0].isupper())
    features['is_all_caps'] = int(word.isupper())
    features['has_hyphen'] = int('-' in word)
    features['has_digit'] = int(any(c.isdigit() for c in word))

    return features

# Example
sent = "Jane Villanueva of United Airlines Holding discussed Chicago".split()
feats = extract_crf_features(sent, 0, '<s>')
print("CRF features for 'Jane':")
for k, v in sorted(feats.items()):
    if v:
        print(f"  {k}")

Why 97% Accuracy Still Matters

97% might sound near-perfect, but in practice it's not:

  • Downstream cascading errors: POS tagging is a pipeline component. A 3% error rate compounds with parsing (97%) and semantic role labeling (95%), yielding ~91% end-to-end accuracy.
  • Real document sizes: A 500-word document has ~15 errors at 97%. A 10,000-word book has 300 errors.
  • Rare phenomena are harder: Ambiguous words, unfamiliar domains, and new entities are tagged worse than 97%. Real user data is messier than test sets.

Lesson: The final 3% requires careful feature engineering, domain adaptation, and ensemble methods. This is where the art of NLP lives.

Modern Bridge: From HMMs to Transformers

Beyond Classical Sequence Labeling

While HMMs and CRFs remain useful teaching tools and strong baselines, modern systems leverage pretrained Transformer models:

BERT and Fine-tuning

Take a pretrained BERT (or RoBERTa, DistilBERT, etc.) and add a single classification layer per token. Fine-tune on task-specific data. Typical workflow:

  1. Load pretrained model (e.g., bert-base-uncased)
  2. Add token classification head: BERT → [hidden_size] → [num_tags]
  3. Fine-tune on annotated corpus (Penn Treebank for POS, CoNLL for NER)
  4. Achieve ~97–98% POS accuracy and ~91–92% NER F1 with minimal effort

The key advantage: Transformers capture long-range dependencies and rare-word semantics via subword tokenization.

Cross-Lingual Transfer

Multilingual models (mBERT, XLM-R, mT5) enable zero-shot transfer: train on English, apply to 100+ languages. Useful when target-language data is scarce.

Few-Shot NER with LLMs

Recent work (2022+) shows that prompting GPT-4 or Claude directly achieves competitive NER without fine-tuning:

Example prompt: "Extract all PERSON, ORG, LOC, and GPE entities from: [text]"

Advantages: No labeled data needed, adapts to new domains/entity types instantly.
Tradeoff: Slower inference, less controllable, may hallucinate entities.

Nested NER

Real-world NER often involves overlapping entities: "Bank of [LOC America]" is both [ORG Bank of America] (containing LOC America). BIO tags can't represent this. Solutions:

  • Multi-level tagging: Multiple independent BIO sequences for different entity types
  • Span-based methods: Score all spans, select non-overlapping set with highest total score
  • Graph-based: Formulate as structured prediction on entity graphs

Universal Dependencies Across Languages

The Universal Dependencies project (2014+) provides harmonized POS and dependency annotations for 100+ languages and treebanks. This enables:

  • Multilingual model training (single model for all languages)
  • Systematic comparison across typologically diverse languages
  • Cross-lingual transfer learning for low-resource languages

Historical Context

From Hand-Written Rules to Neural Networks

1958–1959
Zellig Harris & TDAP: The Text Data Automation Program — earliest POS tagger using 14 hand-written, deterministic rules.
1963
Klein & Simmons' CGC (Computational Grammar Coder): Combined lexicon, morphological analyzer, and context-sensitive rules. First modular approach.
1971
TAGGIT tagger (Greene & Rubin): 87 tags on the Brown Corpus. Probabilistic lexicon combined with deterministic rules. Introduced the Brown Corpus, still used today.
1976
Bahl & Mercer (IBM): First probabilistic tagger using Viterbi decoding. Demonstrated that probability-based methods outperform rule-based.
1980s
CLAWS (Constitutent Likelihood Automatic Word-tagging System): Approximate HMM for the LOB Corpus. Semi-probabilistic: lexicon probabilities + context rules.
1988–1989
Church's PARTS tagger: First practical implementation of HMM tagging with smoothing and rare-word handling. ~96% accuracy on Brown Corpus.
1992–1994
Explicit HMM tagging (Kupiec, Weischedel, Merialdo): Detailed algorithms for training and decoding HMMs. Clear presentation of Viterbi and smoothing techniques.
1996
Ratnaparkhi's MXPOST: Maximum Entropy Markov Model (MEMM) — discriminative alternative to generative HMMs. Richer features, better accuracy. First neural-adjacent tagger.
1997–2000
NER systems: Bikel et al. (1997) use HMMs for NER. McCallum et al. (1998) introduce MEMMs for NER. Bikel & Rizzo (1998) on MUC tasks.
2001
Conditional Random Fields (Lafferty, McCallum, Pereira): Solves label bias problem of MEMMs. Discriminative, globally normalized. Becomes gold standard for 15+ years.
2003–2007
CRF Applications: McCallum & Li (2003) on NER. Settles & Craven (2005) on information extraction. CRFs dominate academic papers and industry systems.
2011
Collobert et al. (2011): Neural NER with ConvNet embeddings + CRF output layer. Shows neural features can compete with hand-crafted features.
2015–2016
BiLSTM-CRF Models (Huang, Ma & Hovy; Lample et al.): Bidirectional LSTM encodes context, CRF layer decodes optimal tags. Becomes new standard. ~97% POS accuracy, 90%+ NER F1.
2018+
Transformer-Based Tagging (BERT, ELMo, etc.): Fine-tuned pretrained language models achieve 97–98% POS and 91–92% NER on standard benchmarks. Reduces need for extensive feature engineering.
