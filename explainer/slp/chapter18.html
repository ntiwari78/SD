From Labeling Words to Understanding Structure

In Chapter 17, we learned to assign labels to individual words: a word is a noun, a verb, an adjective. That's useful—but it's only half the story. A sentence is more than a sequence of labeled tokens. It's a symphony of organization, where words group into meaningful units.

The Family Tree Analogy

Imagine parsing a sentence like building a family tree:

  • Words are individual family members.
  • Phrases are families (e.g., "the tall man" groups three words into a noun phrase).
  • Clauses are clans (combining noun phrases and verb phrases).
  • Sentences are whole dynasties—the complete inheritance structure.

Context-Free Grammars (CFGs) define the "rules of kinship" that tell us which groupings are valid. They answer: Given these words and these labels, how can they legally fit together?

Consider the sentence: "I prefer a morning flight."

  • "I" is a pronoun (Pro) — a complete noun phrase (NP).
  • "a morning flight" groups three words: determiner + adjective + noun → another noun phrase (NP).
  • "prefer a morning flight" groups a verb + its object → a verb phrase (VP).
  • Together: pronoun + verb phrase = a complete sentence (S).

The parse tree for this sentence visualizes these relationships—each branch is a valid grouping, each non-terminal label is a "family type." CFGs are the formal machinery that generates all valid trees and allows computers to find them automatically.

In this chapter, we'll learn:

  • Constituency: What it means for words to group into phrases.
  • CFG Formalism: The mathematical notation for defining these rules.
  • Parsing Algorithms: The CKY algorithm—a dynamic programming method to efficiently find parse trees.
  • Ambiguity: Why one sentence can have multiple valid interpretations.
  • Modern Neural Methods: How deep learning has transformed parsing in the era of BERT and LLMs.

Technical Foundations

18.1 Constituents and Constituency Tests

A constituent is a group of words that functions as a single grammatical unit. The phrase "the morning flight" is a constituent; "morning flight" is a constituent; but "morning a" is not.

How do we know what's a constituent? Linguists use several empirical tests:

Test 1: Movement

Can you move the group as a unit?

  • ✓ "A morning flight, I prefer." (moved "a morning flight")
  • ✗ "A flight morning, I prefer." (cannot move "flight morning" as a unit)

Test 2: Pronoun Substitution

Can you replace it with a pronoun?

  • ✓ "I prefer it." (it = a morning flight)
  • ✗ Can't easily replace "flight morning" with a single pronoun

Test 3: Coordination

Can you coordinate it with another similar unit?

  • ✓ "a morning flight and an evening connection" (both are NPs)
  • ✗ "morning flight and evening connection" sounds awkward without articles

18.2 Context-Free Grammars

A context-free grammar (CFG) is a formal system for generating and parsing structured language. It consists of four components:

$$G = (N, \Sigma, R, S)$$
  • $N$: Non-terminals (phrase types) — e.g., $S$ (sentence), $NP$ (noun phrase), $VP$ (verb phrase), $Pro$ (pronoun).
  • $\Sigma$: Terminals (words) — the actual words in the vocabulary.
  • $R$: Production rules — rewrite rules of the form $A \to \beta$ where $A \in N$ and $\beta \in (N \cup \Sigma)^*$ (a sequence of non-terminals and/or terminals).
  • $S$: Start symbol — usually $S$ (sentence), the root of every parse tree.

Example Grammar $L_0$

Non-terminals: $S, NP, VP, Pro, Verb, Nom, Det, Adj, N, Prep, PP$

Rules:

$$\begin{align} S &\to NP \, VP \\ NP &\to Pro \mid Det \, Nom \mid Det \, Adj \, Nom \\ Nom &\to N \mid Adj \, Nom \\ VP &\to Verb \mid Verb \, NP \mid Verb \, NP \, PP \\ PP &\to Prep \, NP \\ Pro &\to \text{I} \mid \text{you} \mid \text{she} \mid \ldots \\ Verb &\to \text{prefer} \mid \text{book} \mid \text{include} \mid \ldots \\ Det &\to \text{a} \mid \text{the} \\ Adj &\to \text{morning} \mid \text{direct} \mid \text{cheap} \mid \ldots \\ N &\to \text{flight} \mid \text{book} \mid \text{breakfast} \mid \ldots \\ Prep &\to \text{through} \mid \text{in} \mid \text{from} \mid \ldots \end{align}$$

Derivations and Parse Trees

A derivation is a sequence of rewriting steps, starting from $S$ and ending with a sequence of terminals (words). A parse tree is the graphical representation of a derivation.

For "I prefer a morning flight":

$$S \Rightarrow NP \, VP \Rightarrow Pro \, VP \Rightarrow \text{I} \, VP \Rightarrow \text{I} \, Verb \, NP \Rightarrow \ldots$$

In bracketed notation (useful for programming):

[S [NP [Pro I]] [VP [Verb prefer] [NP [Det a] [Nom [Adj morning] [N flight]]]]]

18.3 Treebanks

A treebank is a large corpus of sentences with annotated parse trees. Major treebanks include:

Penn Treebank

The most famous English treebank, with ~40,000 sentences from the Wall Street Journal, labeled with constituency trees using a detailed tag set.

Universal Dependencies

A cross-lingual standard for dependency structures (different from constituency), covering 100+ languages.

Chinese Treebank, Arabic Treebank

Constituency treebanks for other major languages.

Extracting grammars: From a treebank, we can automatically extract all production rules. For instance, if we see [NP [Det a] [N book]] many times, we extract the rule $NP \to Det \, N$. Probabilistic CFGs (PCFGs) count how often each rule appears and use those frequencies as probabilities.

18.4 Grammar Equivalence and Chomsky Normal Form

Two grammars are strongly equivalent if they generate the same set of parse trees. They are weakly equivalent if they generate the same language (strings), but possibly with different trees.

Chomsky Normal Form (CNF)

Chomsky Normal Form restricts rules to exactly two forms:

$$A \to B \, C \quad \text{(binary branching)} \quad \text{or} \quad A \to a \quad \text{(lexical)}$$

where $A, B, C$ are non-terminals and $a$ is a terminal (word).

Why CNF? CNF ensures binary branching trees

Conversion Steps

Step 1: Eliminate unit productions (rules like $A \to B$).

Step 2: Break long RHS into binary rules. For $VP \to V \, NP \, PP$, introduce a dummy non-terminal:

$$\begin{align} VP &\to V \, X \\ X &\to NP \, PP \end{align}$$

18.5 Structural Ambiguity

Structural ambiguity occurs when one sentence has multiple valid parse trees. This is one of the fundamental challenges in NLP.

The Classic Groucho Marx Example

"I shot an elephant in my pajamas."

Two interpretations:

  1. PP attached to VP: [I [shot [an elephant] [in my pajamas]]] — I did the shooting while in pajamas.
  2. PP attached to NP: [I [shot [an elephant [in my pajamas]]]] — The elephant was in pajamas.

Other Common Ambiguities

Coordination Ambiguity

"old men and women"

  • Reading 1: (old men) and (women) — any women, but only old men
  • Reading 2: (old (men and women)) — both old

Garden-Path Sentences

"The horse raced past the barn fell." (Hard to parse, but grammatical: [The horse [raced past the barn]] fell.)

Ambiguity Explosion

The number of parse trees for a sentence grows exponentially with length. For a sentence with $n$ binary-branching constituents, the number of distinct trees is the $n$-th Catalan number:

$$C_n = \frac{1}{n+1}\binom{2n}{n}$$

For $n=10$: 16,796 possible trees. For $n=20$: over 6 billion. This is why we need efficient algorithms and statistical disambiguation.

18.6 The CKY Parsing Algorithm

CKY (Cocke-Kasami-Younger) is a classic dynamic programming algorithm for parsing with CFGs in Chomsky Normal Form. It fills an upper-triangular table in $O(n^3 |G|)$ time.

The Idea

For a sentence of $n$ words, we create an $n \times n$ table $T$. Cell $T[i,j]$ contains all non-terminals that can derive the substring from position $i$ to position $j$.

$$\text{If } B \in T[i,k] \text{ and } C \in T[k,j] \text{ and } (A \to B \, C) \in \text{rules},$$ $$\text{then } A \in T[i,j]$$

Algorithm Pseudocode

procedure CKY(words, grammar) n ← length(words) for j = 1 to n do T[j-1, j] ← {A : A → words[j-1] ∈ grammar} for span = 2 to n do for i = 0 to n - span do j ← i + span for k = i+1 to j-1 do for (A → B C) ∈ grammar do if B ∈ T[i,k] and C ∈ T[k,j] then T[i,j].add(A) return T

Complexity

  • Time: $O(n^3 |G|)$ — three nested loops (span, start, split point) × grammar size
  • Space: $O(n^2)$ — the table

18.7 Span-Based Neural Constituency Parsing

Modern parsers replace hand-crafted grammars with neural networks. The idea: score each possible span and label, then use dynamic programming to find the highest-scoring tree.

The Span-Based Approach

For every contiguous span $(i, j)$ and every non-terminal label $A$, a neural network computes a score:

$$s(i, j, A) = \mathbf{w}^T \cdot \text{repr}(i, j, A)$$

where $\text{repr}(i, j, A)$ is a learned representation of the span, typically from a Transformer encoder (e.g., BERT).

The score of a tree is the sum of span scores:

$$s(T) = \sum_{(i,j,A) \in T} s(i, j, A)$$

A modified CKY algorithm finds the highest-scoring tree:

$$\text{best}[i,j] = \max_A \left( s(i,j,A) + \max_{i \lt k \lt j} (\text{best}[i,k] + \text{best}[k,j]) \right)$$

This approach works without a hand-crafted grammar and can be trained end-to-end on treebank data. State-of-the-art parsers (using BERT) achieve $>95\%$ F1 on the Penn Treebank.

18.8 Evaluating Parsers: PARSEVAL Metrics

Parser quality is measured using PARSEVAL metrics: Precision, Recall, and F1-score over labeled brackets.

A labeled bracket is a tuple $(i, j, A)$: the start position, end position, and non-terminal label. For example, a noun phrase spanning words 2–5 with label NP would be the bracket $(2, 5, \text{NP})$.

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{\text{# correct brackets in parser output}}{\text{# total brackets in parser output}}$$ $$\text{Recall} = \frac{\text{# correct brackets in parser output}}{\text{# total brackets in gold tree}}$$ $$F_1 = 2 \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}$$

A cross bracket occurs when a predicted bracket partially overlaps with a gold bracket—a common source of errors in ambiguous sentences.

18.9 Heads and Head-Finding Rules

Each phrase has a head word — the most important word in that constituent. For example:

  • The head of an NP is (usually) the rightmost noun.
  • The head of a VP is the main verb.
  • The head of a PP is the preposition.

Head-finding rules automatically propagate heads up the tree. This is crucial for dependency parsing and semantic interpretation (in later chapters).

Code Implementation: Building a CKY Parser

def cky_parse(words, grammar):
    """
    CKY parsing algorithm for CNF grammars.

    Args:
        words: list of word strings
        grammar: dict {lhs: [rhs1, rhs2, ...]} where rhs is tuple

    Returns:
        table: 2D list where table[i][j] = set of non-terminals
        back: backpointers for extracting trees
    """
    n = len(words)

    # Initialize table
    table = [[set() for _ in range(n+1)] for _ in range(n+1)]
    back = [[{} for _ in range(n+1)] for _ in range(n+1)]

    # Fill diagonal: lexical rules A -> word
    for j in range(1, n+1):
        word = words[j-1]
        for lhs, rhs_list in grammar.items():
            for rhs in rhs_list:
                if rhs == (word,):
                    table[j-1][j].add(lhs)

    # Fill upper triangle: binary rules
    for span in range(2, n+1):
        for i in range(0, n-span+1):
            j = i + span
            for k in range(i+1, j):
                for lhs, rhs_list in grammar.items():
                    for rhs in rhs_list:
                        if len(rhs) == 2:
                            B, C = rhs
                            if B in table[i][k] and C in table[k][j]:
                                table[i][j].add(lhs)
                                back[i][j][lhs] = (B, C, k)

    return table, back

def build_tree(back, words, symbol, i, j):
    """Recursively construct parse tree from backpointers."""
    if j - i == 1:
        return f"({symbol} {words[i]})"
    if symbol in back[i][j]:
        B, C, k = back[i][j][symbol]
        left = build_tree(back, words, B, i, k)
        right = build_tree(back, words, C, k, j)
        return f"({symbol} {left} {right})"
    return f"({symbol} ?)"

def to_cnf(grammar):
    """
    Convert a CFG to Chomsky Normal Form.

    Rules become either:
      A -> B C (binary non-terminal)
      A -> a   (single terminal)
    """
    cnf = {}
    dummy_count = 0

    for lhs, rhs_list in grammar.items():
        cnf.setdefault(lhs, [])
        for rhs in rhs_list:
            if len(rhs) == 1:
                # Terminal or unit production
                cnf[lhs].append(rhs)
            elif len(rhs) == 2:
                # Already in CNF
                cnf[lhs].append(rhs)
            else:
                # Break into binary rules using dummy symbols
                symbols = list(rhs)
                current_lhs = lhs
                while len(symbols) > 2:
                    first = symbols.pop(0)
                    dummy = f"X{dummy_count}"
                    dummy_count += 1
                    cnf.setdefault(current_lhs, [])
                    cnf[current_lhs].append((first, dummy))
                    current_lhs = dummy
                cnf.setdefault(current_lhs, [])
                cnf[current_lhs].append(tuple(symbols))

    return cnf

def format_cnf(cnf):
    """Pretty-print CNF rules."""
    for lhs, rhs_list in sorted(cnf.items()):
        for rhs in rhs_list:
            rhs_str = ' '.join(rhs)
            print(f"{lhs} -> {rhs_str}")

}

def print_tree(tree_str, indent=0):
    """
    Pretty-print a bracketed parse tree with indentation.

    Input: "(S (NP (Pro I)) (VP (V prefer) (NP (Det a) (N flight))))"
    Output: Indented tree with proper formatting
    """
    i = 0
    depth = 0
    result = []

    while i < len(tree_str):
        if tree_str[i] == '(':
            if depth > 0:
                result.append('\n' + '  ' * depth)
            depth += 1

            j = i + 1
            while j < len(tree_str) and tree_str[j] not in '( )':
                j += 1
            label = tree_str[i+1:j]

            result.append(f'({label}')
            i = j

        elif tree_str[i] == ')':
            depth -= 1
            result.append(')')
            i += 1

        elif tree_str[i] == ' ':
            result.append(' ')
            i += 1

        else:
            j = i
            while j < len(tree_str) and tree_str[j] not in '()':
                j += 1
            result.append(tree_str[i:j])
            i = j

    print(''.join(result))

def tree_depth(tree_str):
    """Calculate maximum depth of tree."""
    depth = 0
    max_depth = 0
    for char in tree_str:
        if char == '(':
            depth += 1
            max_depth = max(max_depth, depth)
        elif char == ')':
            depth -= 1
    return max_depth

# Example
tree = "(S (NP (Pro I)) (VP (V prefer) (NP (Det a) (N flight))))"
print_tree(tree)
print(f"Tree depth: {tree_depth(tree)}")

The Parsing Explosion: A Socratic Challenge

The Problem

Imagine you're building an automatic flight-booking system. You hear the user say: "I'd like a flight from Boston to New York in the evening with a connection through Philadelphia."

Your parser produces thousands of valid parse trees. How does it know which one is correct?

The Catalan Explosion

The number of binary-branching parse trees for an $n$-word sentence is the $n$-th Catalan number. This grows catastrophically:

Sentence Length Catalan Number Interpretation
5 words 42 Manageable
10 words 16,796 Many
15 words ~1.3 billion Overwhelming
20 words $>$ 6 trillion Impossible

This is why we can't enumerate all parses. We must use scores to rank them. Three main approaches:

1. Probabilistic CFGs (PCFGs)

Assign a probability to each grammar rule, learned from a treebank. The score of a tree is the product of its rule probabilities. Highest probability wins.

Drawback: Probability independence assumption often violated; doesn't capture lexical preferences ("prefer" vs. "shot").

2. Lexicalized PCFGs

Enhance PCFG by conditioning rule probabilities on the head word. "VP → V NP" has different probabilities if V is "prefer" vs. "put".

Drawback: More parameters to learn; sparse data problem.

3. Neural Scoring (Modern)

Use a neural network to assign continuous scores to spans and complete trees. Train end-to-end on annotated treebanks.

Advantage: Implicitly learns complex dependencies; achieves state-of-the-art performance.

Reflection Questions

  • Why does length matter so much for ambiguity? (Catalan growth is super-exponential.)
  • What linguistic phenomena are hard to capture with PCFGs? (Garden-path effects, semantic plausibility.)
  • If LLMs like GPT-4 can parse fluently, do they use explicit parse trees internally? (Research question: probing studies suggest yes, implicitly.)

Neural Parsing in the LLM Era

The Shift: From Symbolic to Neural

For decades, parsing relied on hand-crafted grammars or PCFGs learned from treebanks. Today, neural models—especially Transformers—have transformed the field.

Span-Based Neural Parsers

Modern constituency parsers score every possible span $(i, j)$ with every non-terminal label. A Transformer (like BERT) encodes the sentence, and a small neural network predicts span scores:

$$s(i, j, A) = W_A \cdot \text{SPAN}(i, j)$$

where $\text{SPAN}(i, j)$ is a representation combining the inside span and boundary words.

A variant of CKY finds the highest-scoring valid tree, balancing span scores against structural constraints.

Key Advantages

  • No grammar engineering: The model learns rules implicitly.
  • Transfer learning: Use BERT or other pre-trained models as the encoder.
  • State-of-the-art: Modern parsers achieve $>95\%$ F1 on Penn Treebank.
  • Cross-lingual: Multilingual BERT enables zero-shot transfer to new languages.

Implicit Syntax in LLMs

Recent research (probing studies) shows that large language models like BERT, GPT-2, and GPT-3 implicitly compute hierarchical syntactic structures even when not trained explicitly on parsing.

Evidence

  • Layer-wise hierarchy: Lower BERT layers capture POS tags; middle layers capture phrase structure; upper layers capture higher-level semantics.
  • Attention patterns: Attention heads learn to focus on constituent boundaries and head-dependent relationships.
  • Syntactic sensitivity: Predictions degrade when syntax is violated (scrambled word order, garden-path sentences).

Do We Still Need Explicit Parse Trees?

A provocative question: If LLMs perform well on downstream tasks (machine translation, question answering, summarization) without explicit parses, are parse trees still necessary?

Arguments for Explicit Parses

  • Interpretability: A parse tree explains the model's structural analysis.
  • Compositionality: Semantic interpretation rules can be applied recursively over tree structure.
  • Bootstrapping: Parse trees from a parser can improve downstream tasks (e.g., information extraction, semantic role labeling).
  • Linguistic insight: Grammars encode linguistic knowledge; trees are testable hypotheses about language.

Arguments for Implicit Structure

  • End-to-end learning: Neural models learn directly from task supervision, no pipeline.
  • Ambiguity modeling: LLMs capture multiple interpretations implicitly (soft distributions over states).
  • Efficiency: No need for a separate parsing stage; parse structure emerges during encoding.
  • Empircal success: LLMs achieve SOTA on many benchmarks despite no explicit parse tree.

Current consensus: Explicit parsing is still valuable for interpretability, semantic composition, and specialized domains (code, formal languages). But end-to-end neural approaches dominate practical NLP.

Historical Context: The Long Arc of Parsing

1956–1957: Chomsky's Revolution

Noam Chomsky introduces formal grammars and the Chomsky hierarchy. CFGs are defined as generative systems for language. This is a paradigm shift: language is not just data, but structure governed by rules.

1960s: Syntax Analysis Emerges

Augmented Transition Networks (ATN) developed by Kaplan. These extend simple state machines with stack-based computation, allowing recursive parsing. Early computational linguistics begins.

1970s: Dynamic Programming & Chart Parsing

Kay (1973) introduces chart parsing, a graph-based method that generalizes CKY. This allows efficient parsing of any CFG without conversion to CNF, via the Earley algorithm (Earley, 1970).

1960–1973: CKY Discovered Independently

The CKY algorithm is rediscovered by Cocke (1969), Kasami (1965), and Younger (1967)—independently! It becomes the canonical $O(n^3)$ parsing algorithm.

1990–1991: The Penn Treebank Era

Mitchell Marcus, Beatrice Santorini, Mary Ann Marcinkiewicz create the Penn Treebank—a massive corpus of parse-annotated English sentences. This enables statistical parsing: extract rules and probabilities from data, not by hand.

1995–1997: Probabilistic and Lexicalized Parsing

Collins (1997) introduces lexicalized PCFGs, conditioning rule probabilities on head words. Achieves ~90% accuracy—major progress.

2003: Unlexicalized Parsing

Klein & Manning (2003) show that simple, carefully designed unlexicalized PCFGs can match lexicalized parsers on Penn Treebank. Emphasizes the importance of grammar design over lexicalization.

2015–2016: Neural Constituency Parsing

Vinyals, Lek, Bengio (2015) apply LSTMs to constituency parsing. Stern, Andreas, Klein (2017) propose span-based neural parsing. Speed and accuracy both improve.

2018–2020: BERT-Based Parsing

Kitaev & Klein (2018) combine BERT representations with span-based parsing, achieving 95.1% F1—near human agreement. The field reaches a plateau on Penn Treebank; focus shifts to:

  • Cross-lingual parsing (Universal Dependencies)
  • Domain adaptation (social media, speech)
  • Implicit syntax in LLMs

2020–2026: The LLM Era

Foundation models (GPT-2, BERT, ELECTRA, DeBERTa, ...) implicitly learn syntax. Parsing becomes less of an isolated task and more a byproduct of language understanding. Parsing research shifts toward:

  • Probing: What syntax do LLMs encode?
  • Few-shot parsing: Can LLMs parse unseen languages with minimal examples?
  • Semantic-syntactic integration: How do syntax and semantics interact in deep models?

Key Takeaways

1. Constituents Group Words into Phrases

Words don't exist in isolation; they form phrases (NPs, VPs, PPs). Empirical tests (movement, substitution, coordination) reveal constituency.

2. Context-Free Grammars Formalize Structure

A CFG is a set of rules $A \to \beta$ that generate valid parse trees. Derivations expand the start symbol until all terminals (words) appear.

3. CKY Parsing Solves Ambiguity Efficiently

The CKY algorithm uses dynamic programming to find parse trees in $O(n^3 |G|)$ time. It requires CNF, but any CFG converts to CNF.

4. Ambiguity Grows Exponentially

The number of parses is governed by Catalan numbers—growing super-exponentially with sentence length. We must score trees (probabilistically or neurally) to find the best one.

5. Modern Parsing Is Neural

Span-based neural parsers using BERT achieve 95%+ F1. They learn implicit grammars from data, with no hand-crafted rules.

6. Implicit Syntax in LLMs

Language models learn hierarchical structure implicitly. The question of whether explicit parse trees are needed remains open—likely "yes" for interpretability, "no" for end-to-end performance.
