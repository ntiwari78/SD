✦ Historical Context

From Markov to Transformers: The journey of language modeling spans over a century. Andrei Markov first analyzed letter sequences in Pushkin's Eugene Onegin (1913). Shannon applied information theory to language (1948). N-gram models dominated for decades until Bengio's neural LMs (2003). The attention mechanism (Bahdanau 2014) and Transformer architecture (Vaswani 2017) revolutionized the field, leading to today's LLMs.

The Chomsky Hierarchy meets Deep Learning: Formal language theory classified grammars into a hierarchy (regular, context-free, context-sensitive, unrestricted). Modern neural models blur these boundaries — Transformers can implicitly learn context-sensitive patterns without explicit grammar rules, yet constituency and dependency parsing remain vital for evaluation and interpretability.

Why Classical NLP Still Matters: Techniques like POS tagging, dependency parsing, and coreference resolution are not relics. They serve as evaluation frameworks for LLMs, features in hybrid systems, and essential tools for linguistic analysis. Every chapter bridges classical and modern approaches.
