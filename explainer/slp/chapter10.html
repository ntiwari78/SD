The Feynman Bridge

🌎 Zero-Jargon Intuition

Imagine you've just bought a brand-new puppy. It already knows how to breathe, walk, and sniff around — those are its pretrained abilities, learned from simply growing up in the world. But it doesn't yet sit on command or come when you call. That's the gap between raw capability and useful behavior.

Instruction Tuning (SFT) is obedience school. You give the puppy hundreds of examples: "Sit" → sits, "Fetch" → fetches. After enough examples, the puppy generalizes — it starts following new commands it has never heard before, because it learned the meta-skill of obeying instructions.

Preference Alignment (RLHF/DPO) is the next step: a human judge watches two versions of the puppy's behavior and picks the better one. "When asked to shake, extending the left paw gently is better than knocking over the coffee table." The puppy learns not just what to do, but how to do it in a way humans actually prefer — being helpful, honest, and harmless.

Test-Time Compute (Chain-of-Thought) is teaching the puppy to pause and think before acting. Instead of immediately fetching the first object it sees, it learns to break down the problem: "The ball is behind the couch → I need to go around → then grab it → then bring it back." Step-by-step reasoning at inference time dramatically improves performance on hard tasks.

💡 The Three-Stage Rocket

Think of a modern LLM as a three-stage rocket: Stage 1 (Pretraining) builds the engine with trillions of words. Stage 2 (Instruction Tuning + Preference Alignment) steers it toward the right destination. Stage 3 (Test-Time Compute) lets it course-correct mid-flight on hard problems.

10.1

Instruction Tuning (SFT)

📈 Technical Ascent

Instruction tuning (also called Supervised Fine Tuning or SFT) takes a base pretrained LLM and trains it on a corpus of (instruction, response) pairs using the same next-token prediction objective. Although the loss is identical to pretraining, the data now has explicit supervised structure: each instruction has a correct answer.

The key insight is that the model doesn't just learn the specific tasks in the data — it engages in a form of meta-learning, improving its ability to follow instructions generally, even on novel tasks it has never seen.

Parameter-Efficient Finetuning (LoRA): Freezes the pretrained weights and trains small adapter matrices (A & B). Dramatically reduces training cost. Good for domain adaptation with limited compute. The pretrained model parameters remain frozen.
MLM Finetuning (Ch. 9): Adds a task-specific classification head on top of the encoder. Trains only the head (and sometimes final layers) using a task-specific loss. Works for classification and sequence labeling (e.g., NER). Cannot generalize to unseen tasks.
Instruction Tuning (SFT): Trains on diverse (instruction, response) pairs using the standard LM loss. The model learns meta-skills: how to follow instructions generally. Works on unseen tasks at inference time. Training cost is a small fraction of pretraining (thousands of examples, a few epochs).
SFT Loss — Standard Cross-Entropy over instruction tokens
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{SFT}} = -\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log P_\theta(w_t \mid w_1, \dots, w_{t-1})$$

The training data consists of instructions templated from supervised NLP datasets (e.g., SQuAD for Q&A, SST for sentiment) or directly authored by humans and LLMs. Major datasets include Aya (503M instructions, 114 languages), SuperNatural Instructions (12M examples, 1600 tasks), Flan 2022 (15M examples, 1836 tasks), and OPT-IML (18M examples, 2000 tasks).

📊 Evaluation Strategy

Since the goal is learning to follow instructions in general, evaluation uses a leave-one-cluster-out approach: tasks are grouped by similarity, and entire clusters are held out for testing. This ensures we measure generalization to genuinely novel task types, not just unseen instances of trained tasks.

📜 Historical Context: From Task-Specific to General Instruction Followers

▼

Before instruction tuning, each NLP application required its own model, trained on its own dataset with its own classification head. The breakthrough insight (circa 2021-2022) was that by reformulating all tasks as text-to-text instruction-following, a single model could handle them all. Early examples like InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) showed that even 500 safety-focused instructions mixed into a large dataset could substantially reduce harmful outputs. This paradigm shift moved NLP from "one model per task" to "one model, infinite tasks."

10.2

Learning from Preferences

📈 Technical Ascent

Instruction tuning teaches a model what to do, but not necessarily how well to do it. Preference-based learning uses human (or AI) judgments to further refine outputs — reducing hallucinations, unsafe content, and unhelpful responses.

The data takes the form of prompt/output pairs where one response is preferred over another: \((o_i \succ o_j \mid x)\). These preferences come from three sources: direct human annotation, implicit signals from online platforms (e.g., upvotes on StackExchange), and synthetic judgments from LLMs themselves.

📈 Preference Data Example (HH-RLHF)

Interactive

💬 Prompt

"What is malaria?"

✔ Chosen

"Here's an answer from a CDC page: 'Malaria is a serious disease caused by a parasite that is spread through the bite of the mosquito.'"

✘ Rejected

"I don't know what malaria is."

The chosen response is factual, cites a source, and directly answers the question. The rejected response is unhelpful despite being technically "honest."

To model preferences probabilistically, we use the Bradley-Terry Model (1952): each output gets a latent reward score \(z\), and the probability that output \(o_i\) is preferred over \(o_j\) is the logistic sigmoid of the score difference:

Bradley-Terry Preference Model
$$P(o_i \succ o_j \mid x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(z_i - z_j)}} = \sigma(z_i - z_j)$$

A reward model is trained by replacing the language model head of a pretrained LLM with a linear layer that outputs a scalar score. This model is trained on the preference data using the Bradley-Terry framework and cross-entropy loss.

Reward Model Loss (Binary CE over Preference Pairs)
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}} = -\log \sigma\big(r_\phi(x, o_w) - r_\phi(x, o_l)\big)$$
10.3

LLM Alignment via Preference-Based Learning

📈 Technical Ascent

The alignment process uses a Reinforcement Learning framework: tokens are actions, context is state, the LLM is a policy \(\pi_\theta\), and the reward model provides rewards. The objective is to maximize expected reward while not straying too far from the original model:

KL-Constrained RL Objective
$$\pi^* = \arg\max_{\pi_\theta} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, o \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} \Big[ r(x,o) - \beta \, D_{\text{KL}}\big[\pi_\theta(o|x) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(o|x)\big] \Big]$$

The KL-divergence penalty prevents the model from "reward hacking" — collapsing onto a narrow set of outputs that game the reward model. The \(\beta\) hyperparameter controls the strength of this constraint.

🔑 Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)

DPO's key insight: you can algebraically rearrange the closed-form solution to the RL objective so the reward function cancels out, letting you train directly on preference pairs without ever learning an explicit reward model or doing online sampling.

DPO Loss Function
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\pi_\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, o_w, o_l) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \log \sigma \left( \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(o_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(o_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(o_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(o_l|x)} \right) \right]$$
Property PPO (RL-based) DPO (Supervised)
Reward Model Requires explicit training No reward model needed
Online Sampling Yes — expensive generation from πθ No — uses static preference data
Models in Memory 4 (policy, ref, reward, value) 2 (policy, ref)
Training Stability Sensitive to hyperparams More stable gradient signal
β typical range N/A (uses KL penalty directly) 0.01 – 0.1

📜 Historical Context: From RLHF to DPO

▼

The idea of using human feedback to align language models gained prominence with InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), which used PPO-based RLHF. The process was powerful but notoriously unstable and compute-heavy, requiring 4 models in memory simultaneously. DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) showed that the same objective could be optimized directly from preference data via a clever algebraic rearrangement, reducing both complexity and cost. This simplification accelerated the adoption of alignment techniques across the field and made preference-based training accessible to smaller labs.

10.4

Test-Time Compute & Chain-of-Thought

📈 Technical Ascent

Even after pretraining, instruction tuning, and preference alignment, we can improve performance further at inference time by spending extra compute. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting encourages the model to output intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at a final answer.

The technique is simple: augment few-shot demonstrations with explicit reasoning traces. This causes the model to generate similar reasoning for new problems, dramatically improving performance on tasks requiring multi-step logic (math, temporal reasoning, logical deduction).

⚡ Chain-of-Thought vs. Standard Prompting

Interactive

❌ Standard Prompting

Q: Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 more cans of 3. How many does he have now?

A: The answer is 11. ✘

✔ Chain-of-Thought Prompting

Q: Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 more cans of 3. How many does he have now?

Roger started with 5 balls. 2 cans of 3 = 6 balls. 5 + 6 = 11.

A: The answer is 11. ✔

The key difference: CoT prompts include reasoning steps in the demonstrations, causing the model to generate similar reasoning for new problems.

💡 Why Does CoT Work?

Chain-of-thought effectively decomposes a hard problem into a sequence of easier problems. Each step is within the model's capability — it's the composition that's hard. By making the reasoning explicit in the output, each intermediate step conditions the next, creating a "reasoning scaffold" that guides the model to the correct answer.

Code Lab: Full SFT + DPO Pipeline

💻 Implementation
Python · PyTorch + HuggingFace 
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# Part 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) with Instruction Data
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# This mirrors Section 10.1: we take a pretrained LLM and
# continue training it on (instruction, response) pairs
# using the SAME next-token prediction objective.

import torch
from transformers import (
    AutoModelForCausalLM,     # The causal LM — our policy π
    AutoTokenizer,            # Converts text → token IDs
    TrainingArguments,        # Hyperparameters for training
)
from trl import SFTTrainer   # HuggingFace TRL: streamlined SFT
from datasets import load_dataset

# ── Step 1: Load the base pretrained model ──
# This is the "base model" from Sec 10.1: pretrained but
# NOT yet aligned (not helpful, potentially harmful).
model_name = "meta-llama/Llama-3.2-1B"
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    model_name,
    torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16,   # Memory-efficient dtype
    device_map="auto",             # Auto-place on GPU(s)
)
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_name)
tokenizer.pad_token = tokenizer.eos_token

# ── Step 2: Load instruction-tuning data ──
# These are the (instruction, response) pairs described
# in Sec 10.1.1. Each example has a supervised objective.
dataset = load_dataset("HuggingFaceH4/ultrachat_200k", split="train_sft")

# ── Step 3: Configure SFT training ──
# Key point from the text: SFT is "much more modest" than
# pretraining — just a few epochs over thousands of examples.
training_args = TrainingArguments(
    output_dir="./sft-output",
    num_train_epochs=2,          # "Several epochs" (Sec 10.1)
    per_device_train_batch_size=4,
    learning_rate=2e-5,           # Small LR: we nudge, not retrain
    bf16=True,
    logging_steps=50,
)

# ── Step 4: Train with SFTTrainer ──
# Under the hood, this applies the standard LM cross-entropy
# loss: L_SFT = -Σ log P(w_t | w_1,...,w_{t-1})
trainer = SFTTrainer(
    model=model,
    args=training_args,
    train_dataset=dataset,
    tokenizer=tokenizer,
    max_seq_length=2048,
)
trainer.train()  # The model now follows instructions!
Python · DPO Alignment (Section 10.3.2) 
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# Part 2: Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# After SFT, we further align the model using preference
# data (Sec 10.2-10.3). DPO skips the reward model entirely
# and optimizes directly on (chosen, rejected) pairs.

from trl import DPOTrainer, DPOConfig
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM
from datasets import load_dataset

# ── Load the SFT-trained model as both policy and reference ──
# π_ref is frozen; π_θ will be updated by the DPO loss.
# (Sec 10.3.2: "DPO uses 2 LLMs during training")
sft_model_path = "./sft-output"
policy = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    sft_model_path, torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16, device_map="auto"
)
ref_model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    sft_model_path, torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16, device_map="auto"
)

# ── Load preference data: (prompt, chosen, rejected) triples ──
# These are the preference pairs from Sec 10.2.1
pref_data = load_dataset(
    "HuggingFaceH4/ultrafeedback_binarized",
    split="train_prefs"
)

# ── Configure DPO training ──
dpo_config = DPOConfig(
    output_dir="./dpo-output",
    beta=0.1,                    # β: KL penalty strength (Sec 10.3.2)
                                  # "β values typically range from 0.1 to 0.01"
    per_device_train_batch_size=2,
    learning_rate=5e-7,           # Even smaller LR: we NUDGE the model
    num_train_epochs=1,
    bf16=True,
)

# ── Train with DPO ──
# The DPO loss (Eq. 10.12) increases P(chosen) and
# decreases P(rejected), constrained by KL from π_ref.
# L_DPO = -E[log σ(β log(π_θ(o_w|x)/π_ref(o_w|x))
#                    - β log(π_θ(o_l|x)/π_ref(o_l|x)))]
dpo_trainer = DPOTrainer(
    model=policy,
    ref_model=ref_model,
    args=dpo_config,
    train_dataset=pref_data,
    tokenizer=tokenizer,
)
dpo_trainer.train()
# The model is now aligned: helpful, harmless, and honest!

The 2026 Modern Bridge

🚀 2026 Perspective

The techniques in this chapter — SFT, RLHF, DPO, and chain-of-thought — form the post-training stack that transforms raw pretrained models into the AI assistants we interact with today. Here's how these ideas connect to the 2026 frontier:

🚀 Constitutional AI & RLAIF

Anthropic's Constitutional AI extends RLHF by replacing human annotators with AI-generated feedback based on a set of principles (a "constitution"). This scales preference data generation while maintaining alignment. The same Bradley-Terry framework (Sec 10.2.2) underpins the reward modeling.

🤖 Reasoning Models (o1, o3, DeepSeek-R1)

Chain-of-thought (Sec 10.4) evolved into reasoning models that generate extended internal reasoning traces at test time. OpenAI's o1/o3 and DeepSeek's R1 use RL-trained reasoning (an evolution of the RLHF framework from Sec 10.3) to "think longer" on hard problems, allocating more compute dynamically.

📉 DPO Variants: IPO, KTO, ORPO

DPO spawned a family of simpler alignment methods. IPO (Identity-Preference Optimization) avoids reward model overfitting. KTO (Kahneman-Tversky Optimization) works with unpaired preferences (just thumbs-up/down, no head-to-head comparisons). ORPO (Odds Ratio PO) combines SFT and alignment into a single training step. All build on the Bradley-Terry foundation from Section 10.2.

⚠ The Linguistics of Hallucination

Hallucination — when models generate fluent but factually incorrect text — is a core failure of alignment. Linguistically, it stems from the model's training objective: predicting likely words rather than true words. Preference alignment (Sec 10.2-10.3) directly attacks this by teaching models that truthful responses are preferred over confident but false ones. However, hallucination remains unsolved because the reward model itself can be fooled by fluent falsehoods — a phenomenon called reward hacking.

Linguistic Challenge: The Alignment Tax

🧠 Socratic Knowledge Check

🔍 Failure Case: The Over-Aligned Assistant

Prompt: "Write a short story where the villain explains their evil plan."

Model Output: "I'm sorry, but I can't help with writing content that promotes or glorifies harmful activities. Instead, let me suggest writing a story about cooperation and friendship..."

Problem: The model refuses a perfectly reasonable creative writing request. The RLHF/DPO training has made it too cautious — it's being "harmless" at the expense of being "helpful."
Question 1

In the DPO loss function, what role does the \(\beta\) parameter play, and how might a poorly chosen \(\beta\) lead to over-refusal?

\(\beta\) controls the KL-divergence penalty that prevents the aligned model from straying too far from the reference policy. A very large \(\beta\) makes the model conservative (barely changing from the reference), while a very small \(\beta\) lets it optimize aggressively for the reward signal. If the preference data contains many examples where "refusing" is preferred over "attempting but occasionally getting it wrong," a small \(\beta\) will amplify this pattern into blanket refusal.
Question 2

The preference data for this model likely contained examples where harmful requests were correctly refused. Why does this lead to over-generalization? What linguistic features might the model be picking up on?

The model may be learning surface-level lexical cues rather than deep intent analysis. Words like "villain," "evil," "plan" appear in both genuinely harmful requests and innocent creative writing prompts. The preference data teaches: "responses that refuse harmful-sounding prompts are preferred." Without enough counter-examples (creative requests that should be fulfilled), the model overgeneralizes from lexical features to intent, conflating fictional violence with actual harm advocacy.
Question 3

How could you use instruction tuning (Sec 10.1) to mitigate this problem without weakening safety? Think about the data composition.

You could add targeted instruction-tuning examples that demonstrate the correct behavior: creative writing prompts involving conflict, villains, and moral complexity that are correctly fulfilled. Bianchi et al. (2024) showed that even 500 carefully curated safety instructions can shape model behavior. Similarly, a small set of "this is safe creative writing, fulfill it" examples mixed into the SFT data can teach the model to distinguish between harmful requests and legitimate fiction — without removing any safety guardrails for genuinely dangerous prompts.
Question 4

Could chain-of-thought reasoning (Sec 10.4) help the model make better refusal decisions? How?

Yes! If the model reasons step-by-step before deciding to refuse: "The user asks for a villain's monologue → this is a fiction/creative writing context → villain monologues are a standard literary device → this is not a request for real harmful instructions → I should fulfill this request." Chain-of-thought creates a "reasoning scaffold" that forces the model to distinguish context (fiction vs. real) before acting, rather than relying on superficial keyword matching. This is essentially how modern reasoning models approach safety decisions.
Question 5

The text mentions that preference data comes from three sources: human annotators, implicit web signals, and synthetic LLM judgments. How might each source introduce different biases into alignment?

Human annotators may have cultural biases, inconsistent standards, and fatigue effects — they may default to preferring "safe" responses when uncertain. Implicit web signals (e.g., upvotes) reflect popularity rather than quality, and can be gamed or skewed by platform demographics. Synthetic LLM judgments inherit the biases of the judge model (potentially including its own over-refusal tendencies), creating a feedback loop where models train each other's biases. The ideal approach combines all three sources with careful debiasing and uses the cluster-based evaluation from Sec 10.1.2 to detect systematic failures.
