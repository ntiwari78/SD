The Power of Action Words

Verbs are incalculably useful. Every sentence contains an implied or expressed verb — it carries the action, the movement, the force of your ideas. The richer and more extensive your vocabulary of verbs, the more accurately and expressively you can communicate understanding of actions, reactions, attitudes, and emotions.

Chapter 9 teaches you the vocabulary for precise expression through vivid scenarios. Each situation shows you exactly why one verb is perfect where others would fail.

The Key Insight: Each verb captures a unique shade of action. The difference between "disparage" and "malign," between "equivocate" and "obviate," is the difference between expressing your meaning precisely or leaving your reader confused.

The 10 Verbs of Action

1. Playing It Down
Disparage
Republican apologists for Dewey's 1948 election loss claimed Truman's victory was pure accident, luck, or due to other factors. They were disparaging his achievement.
The verb: DISPARAGE
dis-PAIR'-əj
to belittle; to speak of in a way that lowers in esteem

2. Playing It Safe
Equivocate
Dewey on the Taft-Hartley Act: was he for it? Yes—the good parts. Against it? Yes—the bad parts. He was equivocating, speaking with equal voice on both sides.
The verb: EQUIVOCATE
ee-KWIV'-ə-kayt'
to purposely talk in a vague, misleading way; to say yes and no simultaneously

3. Enjoying the Little Things
Titillate
A delightful novel, a charming play, a delicious dessert—not overwhelming emotion, but pleasant, gentle stimulation. These pleasures titillate you without excess.
The verb: TITILLATE
TIT'-ə-layt'
to tickle pleasurably; to stimulate agreeably but not too sharply

4. Playing It Way Up
Adulate
Teenagers idolizing rock stars, fans fawning over celebrities with ingratiating, almost servile attention. They adulate such celebrities with lavish flattery.
The verb: ADULATE
AJ'-ə-layt'
to flatter lavishly; to worship and flatter fulsomely

5. Accentuating the Negative
Proscribe
Doctor to a patient with low blood sugar: "No candy, no pastries, no chocolate, no ice cream!" The doctor is proscribing harmful items—forbidding them.
The verb: PROSCRIBE
prō-SKRĪB'
to prohibit; to forbid; to interdict

6. Accentuating the Affirmative
Obviate
A warm, friendly, outgoing person who is easy to please, honest, and passionate about relating to people. Need they fear making friends? Obviously not. Their characteristics obviate such fears.
The verb: OBVIATE
OB'-vee-ayt'
to make unnecessary; to eliminate the need for; to prevent by anticipating

7. Playing It Wrong
Militate
Neurotic people who unconsciously wish to fail do exactly the wrong things in interviews, say exactly the wrong words. Their neurotic tendency militates against success.
The verb: MILITATE
MIL'-ə-tayt
to work against; to have force or effect in opposition (always with "against")

8. Playing It Dirty
Malign
A gossip revealing closet alcoholics, infidelity, embezzlement, molestation about everyone they know. They're maligning everyone, speaking evil and spreading malicious rumors.
The verb: MALIGN
mə-LĪN'
to speak evil of; to slander; to spread malicious rumors; to blacken someone's reputation

9. Giving the Benefit of Any Doubt
Condone
"It's wrong to cheat on taxes, but doesn't everyone?" "Men are essentially polygamous by nature." Such reactions condone the transgressions, overlooking them tacitly.
The verb: CONDONE
kən-DŌN'
to give implicit forgiveness for a wrongdoing; to overlook; to pardon tacitly

10. Changing Hostility
Placate
You've accidentally offended your best friend. His friendship is valuable; you wish to restore the relationship. You try to placate him, changing his hostility to friendliness.
The verb: PLACATE
PLAY'-kayt'
to change hostility or resentment to friendliness; to appease; to pacify

Why This Matters: These 10 verbs represent the deepest and richest expressions of human action. They describe not just what we do, but how we do it—with precision, clarity, and the power to persuade.

Root Families and Etymology

These 10 action verbs come from different root families, but each reveals its meaning through etymology. Understanding the origins helps you remember and use them precisely.

Etymology Reference Table

Verb Etymology Key Memory Note
disparage DIS- + PAR (equal) To lower someone's equality/standing
equivocate AEQUUS (equal) + VOX (voice) To speak with equal voice on both sides; deliberate vagueness
titillate Latin: titillare (to tickle) Pleasant, gentle stimulation—not overwhelming
adulate Latin: adulari (to flatter) Excessive, lavish flattery and worship
proscribe PRO- + SCRIBERE (write) Written as an order to FORBID (vs. prescribe = to recommend)
obviate OB- + VIA (way) To get out of the way; make unnecessary
militate MILITARE (to serve as a soldier) To work against; always used with "against"
malign MALUS (bad, evil) From same root as malice, malevolent, malefactor
condone CON- + DONARE (to give) To give tacit forgiveness; overlook a transgression
placate PLACARE (to soothe, appease) From same root as placid, placebo (something that soothes)

Key Distinctions

Proscribe vs. Prescribe: These words look almost identical but mean opposite things. Proscribe = FORBID. Prescribe = ORDER/RECOMMEND. In Roman law, proscribe meant to post someone's name publicly as an outlaw. Remember: "proscribe" starts with "pro-" which means "publicly" or "before"—you're posting a prohibition.

Disparage vs. Malign: Both negative, but distinct. Disparage = to belittle, to downplay. Malign = to speak evil, to slander with intent to harm. If you disparage someone's achievement, you're saying it's not that impressive. If you malign someone, you're spreading rumors to destroy their reputation.

Militate vs. Obligate: Militate is always "militate against"—it's the force working against you. You don't "militate for" something. It's always opposition.

Explore the Depths of Word Meaning

Pre-populated question: "What is the difference between proscribe and prescribe? They look almost identical but mean opposite things."

The Answer

Etymology: Proscribe = PRO- (before/publicly) + SCRIBERE (to write) → "written as an order to AVOID." Prescribe = PRE- (before) + SCRIBERE (to write) → "written as an order to DO/TAKE." In Roman law, proscribe meant to post someone's name publicly as an outlaw.
Meaning: Doctors prescribe medicine (order you to take it). Doctors proscribe harmful activities (order you to avoid them). The difference is prohibition vs. recommendation.
Suggested Explorations
Can someone both condone AND disparage the same action — or are they mutually exclusive? Does condoning mean you approve, or just that you overlook?
Why is it significant that "militate" is always used with "against"? What does this tell you about the word's meaning?
What is the relationship between "equivocate" and "unequivocal"? If someone speaks unequivocally, are they the opposite of equivocating?
Can you titillate someone with something serious (like a profound idea), or does titillation require lightness? What does this tell you about the word?
If you adulate someone, are they likely to appreciate it or be disturbed by such fulsomeness? What does this imply about the word's connotation?

Extend Your Thinking

These questions have no single "correct" answer. Instead, they invite you to apply what you've learned to explore nuances of meaning. This is the power of deep vocabulary study: you don't just learn what words mean, you learn how they relate to one another, how they imply different attitudes, and how they reveal the speaker's perspective.

The Deeper Lesson: Action verbs are not interchangeable. The verb you choose reveals not just what happened, but your judgment about it. Does he "disparage" the achievement (belittle it) or "malign" the achiever (attack his character)? The choice of verb tells the reader everything about your perspective.
