Session 44

Origins and Related Words — simulate, alleviate, commiserate, vacillate & their families

← Session 43 Word Power Made Easy Session 45 →

Session 43 traced the first five verbs of Chapter 15. Here we follow the etymological trail of the remaining five — simulate, intimate, alleviate, commiserate, and vacillate — and discover the rich families of related words that grew from the same Latin roots.

1. Not the Real McCoy

The Latin verb simulo means to copy or to imitate — and from it comes simulate, to produce a copy or imitation. But Latin similis also underlies many English words meaning like or similar: simile, similar, similarity, resemble.

simulate ← Latin simulo (to copy, to imitate) → to produce a false or misleading appearance of something

A simulation is a pretense, a copy — it looks like the real thing but isn't. Notice that dis- before a word acts as a pejorative prefix, giving it a negative or sinister twist.

dissimulate (də-SIM′-yə-layt′) ← dis- (pejorative) + simulo → to hide one's true feelings or intentions by pretending the opposite

2. Hints and Helps

Latin intimus means innermost — and from it we get intimate, which as a verb (stress on the last syllable: IN′-tə-mayt′) means to hint or suggest indirectly, as if sharing something innermost.

intimate (verb) ← Latin intimus (innermost) → to suggest indirectly; to hint

Intimation (in′-tə-MAY′-shən) is the noun: a hint, an indirect suggestion.

The word alleviate lightens a burden — etymologically, it makes something lighter.

alleviate ← Latin ad- (al-), to, toward + levis, light → to lighten; to make less severe

The root levis (light) is wonderfully productive. Elevate (← e- + levis) literally lifts something up — makes it lighter in position. Levitate (LEV′-ə-tayt′) is to rise in the air as if by illusion, defying gravity's weight. Levitation (lev′-ə-TAY′-shən) is the magical act of floating. Levity (LEV′-ə-tee) is lightness of mood — flippancy or frivolity when seriousness is called for. And relieve is also from levis: to take away the heaviness.

3. Sharing Someone's Misery

To commiserate is to share another person's grief — the com- (together) prefix signals that you join them in their wretchedness.

commiserate ← Latin con- (com-), together + miser, wretched → to feel pity or sorrow with another; to share grief

Latin miser (wretched) is richly generative. A miser is someone wretched about parting with money. Miserable means in a state of wretchedness. Miserly describes the stinginess of a miser. And misery is the state of utter wretchedness itself.

4. Swing and Sway

To vacillate is to swing back and forth in indecision — derived from the Latin vacillo, to sway or totter.

vacillate ← Latin vacillo (to sway, to totter) → to swing back and forth between opposing positions; to be indecisive

Vacillation (vas′-ə-LAY′-shən) is the noun; vacillatory (VAS′-ə-lə-tawr′-ee) is the adjective describing someone tending to waver.

The word ambivalent (am-BIV′-ə-lənt) comes from Latin ambi-, meaning both — as in ambidextrous (able to use both hands). To be ambivalent is to feel both ways at once — to hold conflicting emotions simultaneously.

ambivalent ← Latin ambi- (both) + valere (to be strong) → experiencing simultaneous and contradictory feelings

And oscillate (OS′-ə-layt′) — from Latin oscillum, a swing — is to swing back and forth like a pendulum, in the purely physical sense (though it can be used metaphorically too).

oscillate ← Latin oscillum (a swing) → to swing back and forth like a pendulum

The Socratic Sandbox invites you to think with these words — not just recognize them. Use the prompts below to reflect, compose, or debate. There are no wrong answers, only deeper engagement.

Introspection

Have you ever dissembled — pretended to feel one way while feeling another entirely? What social pressure made it necessary? Was it a form of self-protection or a small betrayal of authenticity?

Distinction

What is the difference between levity and genuine humor? Can levity be harmful? Think of a situation where someone's levity struck you as inappropriate — and one where it was exactly what the moment needed.

Etymology in Action

The root miser (wretched) gave us both miser (a stingy person) and misery (great suffering). Why might ancient Romans have associated wretchedness so closely with money? Does that association hold today?

Debate

Ambivalence is sometimes seen as weakness — the inability to commit. But might ambivalence also be a sign of intellectual honesty, of seeing genuine complexity where others see false certainty? Argue both sides.

Word in the Wild

Write two sentences using oscillate — one describing a physical phenomenon (pendulum, waves, sound), and one using it as a metaphor for human behavior or decision-making.
