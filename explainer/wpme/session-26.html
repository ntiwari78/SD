Word Families & Pronunciations

Laconic Family

laconic

ləˈKÄN-ik
From Laconia, ancient Sparta
Adj. Pithy, concise, economical in words; opposite of verbose.
"His laconic response—'If'—impressed the gathered senators more than any lengthy oration."

laconicity

lakəˈNISətē
laconic + -ity (noun suffix)
Noun. The quality or state of being laconic; conciseness.
"The laconicity of her remarks belied the depth of her knowledge."

laconism

ˈLAKənizəm
laconic + -ism (noun suffix)
Noun. A pithy expression; economy in speech.
"His laconisms were memorable: 'Less is more,' 'Do or do not.'"

laconicism

ləˈKÄNəsizəm
laconic + -icism (variant noun suffix)
Noun. Conciseness or brevity in speech or style.
"The poet's laconicism stripped away all superfluous language."

Eloquent Family — Speech & Expression

eloquent

ˈELəkwənt
From e- (out) + loquor (to speak)
Adj. Vividly expressive, fluent, forceful, persuasive; artistic expression with emotional appeal.
"Her eloquent defense of the cause moved the entire assembly to tears."

eloquence

ˈELəkwəns
eloquent + -ence (noun suffix)
Noun. Fluent, forceful, and artistically expressive speech or writing.
"The senator's eloquence was legendary; few could resist his persuasive voice."

magniloquent

magˈNILəkwənt
magnus (large) + loquor (to speak)
Adj. High-flown, pompous, grandiose language; bombastic.
"His magniloquent speeches were impressive but often lacked substance."

magniloquence

magˈNILəkwəns
magniloquent + -ence (noun suffix)
Noun. Grandiose, pompous, or flowery speech; eloquence marked by inflated language.
"The reviewer criticized the author's magniloquence as overwrought and pretentious."

grandiloquent

granˈDILəkwənt
grandis (grand) + loquor (to speak)
Adj. Virtually identical to magniloquent; high-sounding, bombastic language.
"The governor's grandiloquent announcement promised reforms but delivered little action."

grandiloquence

granˈDILəkwəns
grandiloquent + -ence (noun suffix)
Noun. Lofty, pompous, or bombastic language or style.
"His grandiloquence could not hide the emptiness of his actual achievements."

Speech Quality Distinctions

verbose

vərˈBŌS
From verbum (word)
Adj. Using or containing more words than necessary; wordy.
"His verbose response to a simple yes-or-no question frustrated the audience."

verbosity

vərˈBÄSətē
verbose + -ity (noun suffix)
Noun. The quality of using too many words; wordiness that obscures meaning.
"The teacher marked down the essay for its verbosity, asking for more concision."

voluble

ˈVÄlyəbəl
From volvo (to roll)
Adj. Speaking fluently, easily, and rapidly; talkative but not necessarily shallow.
"The voluble tour guide entertained us with rapid-fire facts and colorful anecdotes."

volubility

välˈyəˈBILətē
voluble + -ity (noun suffix)
Noun. Fluency and ease in speaking; readiness to talk rapidly.
"Her volubility was infectious; within minutes, the shy newcomer was chatting freely."

garrulous

ˈGAR(ə)ləs
From garrio (to chatter)
Adj. Given to excessive talking, especially about trivial matters; loquacious and tiresome.
"The garrulous old woman could talk for hours about the weather and her neighbors."

garrulity

gəˈROōlətē
garrulous + -ity (noun suffix)
Noun. Excessive talkativeness, especially about trivial matters; chattiness.
"The dinner companion's garrulity prevented anyone else from speaking."

Cogent Family — Logic & Persuasion

cogent

ˈKŌjənt
From cogo (to drive together, compel)
Adj. Compelling through logical force, reason, or persuasiveness; convincing.
"The prosecutor presented cogent evidence that swayed the entire jury."

cogency

ˈKŌjənsē
cogent + -cy (noun suffix)
Noun. Quality of being cogent; keen, logical persuasiveness; compelling force.
"The cogency of her argument left no room for disagreement."

Magnus Family — Great & Large

magnanimous

magˈNANəməs
magnus (big) + animus (mind)
Adj. Big-hearted, generous, forgiving; showing nobility of mind.
"The magnanimous king pardoned his enemies and gained their respect."

magnate

ˈMAGnāt
From magnus (big)
Noun. A person of great power or influence, especially in business; a big wheel.
"The railroad magnate accumulated vast wealth and political influence."

magnum opus

ˈMAGnəm ˈÖpəs
magnus (big) + opus (work)
Noun phrase. A great work, especially an artist's masterpiece or greatest achievement.
"Historians consider her five-volume biography her magnum opus."

magnify

ˈMAGnəˌfī
magnus (big) + -fy (to make)
Verb. To make larger or more imposing; to exaggerate.
"The microscope magnified the specimen a thousand times."

magnificent

magˈNIFəsənt
magnus (big) + -fic (make)
Adj. Grand, splendid, impressive in appearance or effect.
"The magnificent cathedral took two centuries to complete."

Verbum Family — Words & Speech

verbatim

vərˈBATəm
From verbum (word)
Adv./Adj. Word-for-word; in exactly the same words.
"The journalist quoted the senator verbatim from the transcript."

verbal

ˈVərbəl
From verbum (word)
Adj. Pertaining to words or language; oral rather than written; given to talking.
"A verbal agreement is less binding than a written contract."

verbalize

ˈVərbəˌlīz
verbal + -ize (verb suffix)
Verb. To express in words; to put thoughts or feelings into language.
"The therapist helped him verbalize his fears and anxieties."

verbiage

ˈVərˌbāZH
From verbum (word)
Noun. Excess of words; wordiness; or the particular style of using words.
"The legal verbiage in the contract obscured its true meaning."

Volvo Family — Rolling & Turning

revolve

rəˈVÄLV
re- (again) + volvo (to roll)
Verb. To turn around a center or axis; to rotate repeatedly.
"The Earth revolves around the sun once per year."

revolution

ˌrevəˈLOōSHən
revolve + -tion (noun suffix)
Noun. A complete turn; radical change; political upheaval or overthrow.
"The Industrial Revolution transformed society in the 18th century."

revolutionary

ˌrevəˈLOōSHəˌnerē
revolution + -ary (adjective suffix)
Adj./Noun. Involving or causing radical change; a person who advocates or engages in revolution.
"The revolutionary technology changed the industry overnight."

involve

inˈVÄLV
in- (in) + volvo (to roll)
Verb. To include or engage as a necessary part; to entangle or engage deeply.
"The scandal involved several prominent politicians."

evolve

əˈVÄLV
e- (out) + volvo (to roll)
Verb. To unfold gradually; to develop or change gradually over time.
"Species evolve over millions of years through natural selection."

evolution

ˌevəˈLOōSHən
evolve + -tion (noun suffix)
Noun. Gradual development or change; the biological process of species development.
"Darwin's theory of evolution explains the diversity of life on Earth."

evolutionary

ˌevəˈLOōSHəˌnerē
evolution + -ary (adjective suffix)
Adj. Relating to or resulting from evolution; developing gradually.
"Evolutionary biology studies how organisms change and adapt."
