The Family DNA Analogy

Understanding how two roots — EGO and ALTER — generate an entire vocabulary family.

Feynman Mental Model · The Root as Family DNA

Think of a Latin or Greek root as genetic code. It contains a core meaning that every word descended from it will carry. Two people who share DNA look different on the surface — one is tall, one is short — but their underlying structure is the same. The same is true of words.

The root EGO is Latin for I or self. This single syllable spawns an entire family. The egoist lives by the I-principle. The egotist loudly announces the I-experience. The egocentric person places I at the center (Latin: centrum) of everything. The egomaniac has I-obsession escalated to mania — a madness, a disease of the I.

Then there is the opposite family: ALTER, Latin for other. The altruist — with a slightly softened spelling — lives for the other. Altruism is the philosophy of the other. Altruistic is its adjective. Alter ego is literally your other I — a person so like you they feel like a second self.

And then ALTER gives us a rich vein of everyday words. To alter something is to make it other than it was. Alternative is the other option available. Alternate means taking one and skipping to the other in turn. And altercation — a heated argument — comes from having other opinions, strongly held.

Two three-letter roots. Thirteen words. Once you see the DNA, every word in both families becomes instantly decipherable — including words you've never encountered before.

Critical Distinction — Egoist vs. Egotist
egoistacts selfishly — puts I first in behavior and decisions. A philosophy of self-advancement.
egotisttalks about I excessively — boasts, brags, advertises themselves. A behavior of self-promotion.
Memory hook: the extra t in egotist — think of the t in talk. The egotist talks about himself.
