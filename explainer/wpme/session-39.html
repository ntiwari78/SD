Ideas — Ten Everyday Concepts Made Precise

Every word in this session names something you have experienced. The joy is discovering the exact word that fits the feeling or situation perfectly.

1. "For Want of the Green Stuff"
Extreme, abject lack of money — not just being broke, but genuine dire want — is: penury (PEN'-yə-ree). From Latin penuria, need, neediness.
2. "At Least I Can Watch It"
When your child scores the winning goal and you feel the thrill too; when you cry watching a film because the character's grief feels like your own — you are experiencing emotion vicariously (vī-KAIR'-ee-əs). Living or feeling through another's experience rather than directly.
3. "Time Is Fleeting"
The dayfly (Greek: ephemera) lives only a single day — it is gone before you can appreciate it. Anything equally short-lived and leaving no trace is: ephemeral (ə-FEM'-ə-rəl).
4. "How Not to Call a Spade…"
Words are only symbols of things. Many people avoid direct words for death, sex, or bodily functions — substituting politer roundabouts. These circumlocutions are: euphemisms (YOO'-fə-miz-əm). Greek eu- (good) + pheme (voice) = "saying it in a good way."
5. "Small Talk"
"I took a twip yesterday." "A twip?" "Yes, I took a twip on a twain!" — light, half-teasing, non-malicious wit intended to amuse is: badinage (BAD'-ə-nəj). A French borrowing.
6. "Everything but Give Milk"
A cow contentedly chewing cud — calm, patient, placid, utterly unexcitable. Some people share this quality. They are: bovine (BŌ'-vīn'). Latin bos, bovis (ox, cow) + -ine (like, characteristic of).
7. "Good Old Days"
A keen, almost physical longing for places or times of the past — the strange ache when you pass the neighborhood of your childhood. This is: nostalgia (nə-STAL'-jə). Greek nostos (a return) + algos (pain).
8. "Sounds That Grate"
Traffic noise, chalk squeaking on a blackboard, electronic feedback — blaring, ear-splitting, discordant sound is: cacophony (kə-KOF'-ə-nee). Greek kakos (bad, harsh) + phone (sound).
9. "Eating Habits"
Lions, tigers, wolves — subsisting entirely on flesh, refusing all vegetables. These animals are: carnivorous (kahr-NIV'-ər-əs). Latin carnis (flesh) + voro (to devour).
10. "Private and Public"
Some activities are not merely private — they are shrouded in secrecy because they are illegal, unethical, or dangerous. Such meetings or activities are: clandestine (klan-DES'-tin). Latin clam (secretly).

Key Roots in This Session

Greek: eu- (good) + pheme (voice) + -ism → euphemism
The same eu- prefix gives: euphony (good sound), euphoria (good feeling), eulogy (good speech), euthanasia (good death)
Greek: kakos (bad, harsh, ugly) + phone (sound) → cacophony
The opposite of euphony. Phone also gives: telephone, microphone, phonetics, saxophone
Latin: carnis (flesh) + voro (to devour) → carnivorous
Voro also gives: herbivorous (herba = herb), omnivorous (omnis = all), voracious (greedy, gluttonous)
Greek: nostos (return) + algos (pain) → nostalgia
Algos (pain) also appears in: neuralgia, cardialgia
Latin: bos/bovis (cow) + -ine (like) → bovine
The -ine suffix turns animal names into adjectives: canine (dog), feline (cat), equine (horse), porcine (pig)

Word Families — Chapter 14 Introduction

Poverty Spectrum

penury
PEN'-yə-ree
Dire, abject poverty; utter lack of financial resources. Latin penuria.
penurious
pə-NYOOR'-ee-əs
Stingy; tight-fisted. (Note: penurious more commonly means stingy than poverty-stricken!)

Sound

cacophony
kə-KOF'-ə-nee
Harsh, discordant, unpleasant sound. From Greek kakos (bad) + phone (sound).
cacophonous
kə-KOF'-ə-nəs
Harshly discordant; unpleasantly noisy (adj.).
euphony
YOO'-fə-nee
Pleasant, melodic sound. The opposite of cacophony.
euphonious
yoo-FŌ'-nee-əs
Pleasing to the ear (adj.).

Time & Impermanence

ephemeral
ə-FEM'-ə-rəl
Lasting only a very short time; fleeting. Greek ephemera = dayfly.
evanescent
ev'-ə-NES'-ənt
Vanishing quickly; fleeting; barely perceptible. From e- + vanesco (to vanish).

Eating

carnivorous
kahr-NIV'-ər-əs
Flesh-eating; subsisting on meat. carnis + voro (to devour).
herbivorous
hur-BIV'-ər-əs
Plant-eating; subsisting on herbs, grasses, vegetation. herba + voro.
omnivorous
om-NIV'-ər-əs
Eating everything; consuming all kinds of food. omnis (all) + voro.
voracious
vaw-RAY'-shəs
Devouring; greedy or gluttonous; may apply to food, reading, any appetite.

Banter & Talk

badinage
BAD'-ə-nəj
Light, half-teasing, non-malicious banter; playful wit. French origin.
persiflage
PUR'-sə-flahzh'
Slightly more derisive than badinage; teasing with a hint of mockery — but still non-malicious.

Memory & Feeling

nostalgia
nə-STAL'-jə
Acute longing for the past or for home. Greek nostos (return) + algos (pain).
nostalgic
nos-TAL'-jik
Feeling or characterized by nostalgia (adj.).
vicarious
vī-KAIR'-ee-əs
Experienced through another person's actions or feelings rather than directly.

Secrecy & Concealment

clandestine
klan-DES'-tin
Kept secret, especially for sinister or illegal purposes. Latin clam (secretly).
surreptitious
sur'-əp-TISH'-əs
Stealthy, sneaky, furtive — generally because of fear of detection. Close synonym of clandestine.

Socratic Sandbox

Engage with these prompts before revealing the model answers.

A person who grew up in grinding poverty wins the lottery and becomes excessively stingy, refusing to spend even on necessities. Which two different vocabulary words from this session could describe their situation (one for their past, one for their current behaviour)?
Penury describes their impoverished past — dire, abject poverty. Penurious describes their current behaviour — ironically, the adjective derived from penury most commonly means stingy or tight-fisted, not merely poor. They escaped poverty but developed penuriousness.
Why does the word nostalgia literally mean "pain of return"? What does this reveal about the nature of nostalgia?
From Greek nostos (a return) + algos (pain). The etymology captures something psychologically profound: nostalgia is not simply pleasant reminiscence but a yearning that hurts — the pain of wanting to return to a time or place you cannot reach. The poet Thomas Wolfe captured it: "You can't go home again." Memory idealises the past; the real past, if revisited, would likely disappoint.
What is the difference between clandestine and surreptitious? Can you construct a sentence that uses each correctly?
Clandestine implies secrecy in the planning or conducting of something — typically illegal or unethical activities: "clandestine plans," "a clandestine meeting." Surreptitious implies stealthy movement or action, often small-scale furtive behaviour: "a surreptitious glance," "he surreptitiously pocketed the coin." You can have a clandestine meeting, but you might enter it via a surreptitious side entrance.
Is it possible to live entirely vicariously? What might be lost or gained by doing so?
One who lives entirely vicariously — through others' experiences, fiction, or spectating — avoids the risks of direct participation but also never develops authentic skills, relationships, or self-knowledge. The pleasure is real (neuroscience confirms vicarious experiences activate similar brain regions to direct ones) but remains secondhand. The word itself suggests a substitute — vicarius in Latin means a substitute or deputy.
