Mental Model

Fixing Bones, Reading Hearts & Healing Minds

The Straightener Analogy
ORTHOS: the root that means "right"

Imagine you're a master carpenter. Your job is to make crooked things straight and wrong things correct. That's ORTHOS — the Greek root meaning straight or correct. When ORTHOS teams up with PAIDOS (child), you get orthopedics — the specialty that literally "straightens children." When ORTHOS teams up with ODONTOS (tooth), you get orthodontia — the straightening of teeth. Same root, two different partners, two different specialties.

Think of ORTHOS as the quality-control inspector of the body: wherever something is crooked — bones or teeth — ORTHOS fixes it.

The Recording Studio Analogy
KARDIA: the heart writes its own song

Your heart broadcasts its own electrical signal with every beat. A cardiograph (KARDIA + GRAPHEIN, to write) is the instrument that records this signal. A cardiogram (KARDIA + GRAMMA, a record) is the paper printout it produces. Think of the cardiograph as the recording machine and the cardiogram as the finished record.

This -graph / -gram distinction appears throughout English: a phonograph plays, a phonogram is what it plays. A telegraph sends, a telegram is what it sends. Master this pattern and dozens of word pairs unlock instantly.

The Spectrum of Mind Analogy
Neurosis vs. Psychosis: a crucial distinction

Imagine mental health as a spectrum. At one end, everyone has occasional worries, quirks, inefficiencies — this is the territory of neurosis (NEURON + -OSIS, abnormal condition): an emotional disturbance that doesn't break contact with reality. At the far other end is psychosis (PSYCHE + -OSIS): a complete break from reality, full mental unbalance.

A neurotic person worries too much; a psychotic person may believe they are Napoleon. The psychiatrist (PSYCHE + IATREIA, medical healing) treats both. One root — PSYCHE — generates the entire vocabulary of mind treatment. The key insight: neurosis is about energy misspent; psychosis is about reality lost.

The Life Cycle Analogy
GERAS + IATREIA: medicine for the end chapters

Just as PAIDOS gives us the doctor for the beginning of life (pediatrician), GERAS (Greek: old age) gives us the doctor for the final chapters: the geriatrician. The specialty is geriatrics. The suffix -IATREIA means medical healing — the same root that gives us pediatrics and psychiatry. Three specialties, one healing suffix, different life stages: childhood (paidos), mind (psyche), old age (geras).

Etymological Ascent

Root Families and Formulas

⊳ ORTHOS Family (Greek: straight, correct)
ORTHOS+PAIDOS → orthopedics
awr-thə-PEE'-diks. Etymologically: the "straightening of children." Today: the medical specialty dealing with deformities, injuries, and diseases of the bones and joints. Coined in 1741. Adjective: orthopedic (awr-thə-PEE'-dik).
ORTHOS+ODONTOS → orthodontia
awr-thə-DON'-shə. The straightening of teeth — correcting (orthos) tooth (odontos) alignment. The specialist is an orthodontist (awr-thə-DON'-tist). Adjective: orthodontic (awr-thə-DON'-tik).
⊳ KARDIA Family (Greek: heart)
KARDIA+LOGOS → cardiology
kahr-dee-OL'-ə-jee. The science (logos) of the heart (kardia). Adjective: cardiological (kahr'-dee-ə-LOJ'-ə-kəl). Specialist: cardiologist.
KARDIA+-AC → cardiac
KAHR'-dee-ak. Pertaining to the heart. A cardiac condition = a heart condition. The suffix -AC means "pertaining to."
KARDIA+GRAMMA → cardiogram
KAHR'-dee-ə-gram'. The electrically produced record (gramma = something written) of the heartbeat. This is the printed output — the piece of paper.
KARDIA+GRAPHEIN → cardiograph
KAHR'-dee-ə-graf'. The instrument (graphein = to write/record) that produces the cardiogram. Cardiograph = the machine; cardiogram = what the machine produces.
⊳ NEURON Family (Greek: nerve)
NEURON+LOGOS → neurology
noor-OL'-ə-jee. The science of the nervous system. Adjective: neurological (noor-ə-LOJ'-ə-kəl). Specialist: neurologist.
NEURON+ALGOS → neuralgia
noor-AL'-jə. Acute pain (algos = pain) along the nerves and their branches. Note: ALGOS is the pain root — it also gives us nostalgia (nostos = home + algos = pain → homesickness).
NEURON+-ITIS → neuritis
noor-Ī'-tis. Inflammation (-itis = inflammation) of the nerves. Compare: dermatitis (skin inflammation), arthritis (joint inflammation).
NEURON+-OSIS → neurosis
noor-Ō'-sis. Despite containing NEURON, this is not a nerve disorder but an emotional/personality disturbance — excessive energy spent on unproductive worry without loss of reality. Adjective/noun: neurotic (noor-OT'-ik).
⊳ PSYCHE Family (Greek: spirit, soul, mind)
PSYCHE+-OSIS → psychosis
sī-KŌ'-sis. A full-blown mental disorder — loss of contact with reality. More severe than neurosis. Adjective/noun: psychotic (sī-KOT'-ik).
PSYCHE+IATREIA → psychiatry
sī- or sə-KĪ'-ə-tree. The medical specialty (iatreia = healing) of the mind (psyche). Deals with mental, emotional, and personality disturbances. Adjective: psychiatric (sī-kee-AT'-rik). Specialist: psychiatrist.
⊳ GERAS Family (Greek: old age)
GERAS+IATREIA → geriatrics
jair'-ee-AT'-riks. The medical specialty dealing with the particular needs of the elderly (geras = old age + iatreia = medical healing). Specialist: geriatrician (jair'-ee-ə-TRISH'-ən). Adjective: geriatric (jair'-ee-AT'-rik).

Socratic Sandbox

The Challenge Word

iatrogenic
eye-AT'-rō-jen'-ik
Caused by a physician or medical treatment — said of a disease, condition, or symptom inadvertently induced by medical intervention.
Root formula: IATREIA (medical healing) + GENESIS (origin, production) + -IC → "produced by healing." The bitter irony: the healer's own treatment becomes the cause of harm.

Expand each question to reveal the Socratic hint. Work through the logic before peeking.

1. If iatreia means "medical healing" and genesis means "origin," what does iatrogenic literally mean — and why is it ironic? ▼
Hint: The word literally means "produced by the healer." The irony is built into the etymology itself — the very person whose job is to restore health has instead created a new problem. Iatrogenic infections acquired during surgery are a major concern in modern hospitals. The root reminds us that medicine, like all human endeavors, carries risk.
2. Can you construct the word for "the science of the causes of disease" using the roots for "disease" (pathos) and "science" (logos)? ▼
Hint: PATHOS (disease, suffering) + LOGOS (science) = pathology. By extension, iatrogenic pathology = disease caused by medical treatment. The root PATHOS gives us: pathological, psychopath (psyche + pathos), empathy (em- = into + pathos = feeling into another's suffering), and sympathy (syn- = with + pathos = feeling with).
3. If iatreia gives us pediatrics (child-healing), psychiatry (mind-healing), and geriatrics (old-age-healing), what would "podiatry" mean — and what Greek root is pod-? ▼
Hint: POUS/PODOS is Greek for "foot." PODOS + IATREIA = podiatry = treatment of foot disorders. This is related to the Latin PEDIS (foot) that gives us pedestrian, pedal, and pedestal. Be careful: PAIDOS (child) and PODOS (foot) look similar but are entirely different roots — one of English's classic false-friend traps.
4. The suffix -OSIS appears in neurosis, psychosis, and also in "osmosis" and "thrombosis." What does -OSIS signal in medical vocabulary? ▼
Hint: -OSIS is a Greek suffix meaning "abnormal condition" or "process." In medical vocabulary it consistently signals: (1) a diseased or abnormal state (neurosis, psychosis, thrombosis, cirrhosis), or (2) a natural process (osmosis, mitosis). The distinction helps you navigate medical terminology — if you see -OSIS, expect either a condition or a biological process at play.
5. The word "nostalgia" — which means homesickness — shares the ALGOS root with neuralgia. What does NOSTOS mean, and can you now construct its etymology? ▼
Hint: NOSTOS is the Greek word for "a return home." NOSTOS + ALGOS = nostalgia = the pain (algos) of longing to return home (nostos). The Odyssey is the great NOSTOS epic — Odysseus's entire journey is a nostos. The suffix -ALGIA appears in neuralgia (nerve pain), cardialgia (heart pain), odontalgia (toothache), and myalgia (muscle pain). Once you own ALGOS, you unlock a whole pain vocabulary.
