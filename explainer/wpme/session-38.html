Socratic Sandbox — Reflecting on Chapters 1–12

Use these prompts to consolidate your learning across the entire first half of the book.

Which root family from Chapters 1–12 gave you the most new vocabulary? Name three words from it and explain how the root meaning connects them all.
A strong candidate is logos / loquor / vox — the "speech and word" cluster. Loquacious (from loquor), soliloquy (from solus + loquor), and eloquent (from e- + loquor) all involve speaking. The root meaning "to speak" makes clear why someone who is loquacious never stops speaking, and why a soliloquy is literally speaking alone.
Explain the difference between cogent and specious. Can an argument be both at the same time?
Cogent means genuinely convincing — the logic holds up and the argument is forceful and valid. Specious means seemingly persuasive but actually false. An argument cannot be both genuinely cogent and specious simultaneously: if it's specious it may feel cogent but fails under scrutiny. The key distinction is whether the underlying logic is sound.
The comprehensive test asks whether satyriasis and nymphomania are the same or opposite. What is the book's answer and why might this be debated?
The book marks them as OPPOSITE — satyriasis applies to men and nymphomania to women, making them gender-opposites of the same condition. One could reasonably argue they are the same in describing uncontrollable sexual desire, differing only in which sex is affected. The "opposite" answer reflects the gendered contrast, not a contrast in the nature of the condition.
If a piece of writing contains many clichés, bromides, and platitudes, what does that reveal about the writer? Use at least two vocabulary words from Sessions 1–38 in your answer.
A writer who relies on clichés, bromides, and platitudes reveals a lack of perspicacity — the sharp mental keenness needed to see beyond the obvious. Such writing is by definition banal, offering nothing fresh or insightful. Truly scintillating prose requires the courage to abandon the worn path and forge new expressions.
