I. The Wealth Spectrum

The scale from dire poverty to obscene wealth has its own precise vocabulary. Learn to place each word correctly on the spectrum.

Latin: penuria → penury, penurious, penuriousness
Penury = dire, abject poverty. Penurious = stingy (the adjective shifted in meaning!). Penuriousness = stinginess.
Latin: parsimonia → parsimonious, parsimony
A synonym of penurious (= stingy), but weaker: parsimonious = mildly stingy; penurious = twice as stingy. Parsimony = the noun (stinginess, excessive frugality).
Latin: indigeo (to be in want) → indigent, indigence
Not absolutely penniless — simply living in reduced circumstances, lacking creature comforts but not starving.
Latin: de- + statuere (to deprive of position) → destitute, destitution
The strongest poverty word alongside penury. Destitute people lack even basic subsistence — on the verge of starvation.
Latin: ad- (af-) + fluo (to flow) → affluent, affluence
Money keeps "flowing in." Wealthy and growing more so. Other fluo words: fluid, influence, confluence, fluent.
Latin: opulentus → opulent, opulence
More than wealthy — ostentatiously luxurious. Rolls-Royces, estates, yacht clubs. No other English words derive from this root.

II. Vanishing — Evanescent

Latin: e- (ex-, out) + vanesco (to vanish) + -ent (adjective suffix)
Evanescent = vanishing quickly; barely perceptible before it is gone. Noun: evanescence. Verb: evanesce (to begin to vanish).

The suffix -esce means "begin to." It gives: evanesce (begin to vanish), convalesce (begin to get well), putrescent (beginning to rot), obsolescent (becoming obsolete), adolescent (beginning to grow up).

III. The eu- Prefix — Good Things

Greek eu- means good. It combines with different roots to give an entire cluster of "good" words.

eu- (good) + pheme (voice) + -ism → euphemism
"Saying something in a good voice" — substituting an inoffensive term for a direct one. Adjective: euphemistic.
euphemism — inoffensive substitute euphemistic — using euphemisms (adj.)
eu- (good) + phone (sound) → euphony
Pleasant, musical sound. Adjectives: euphonic, euphonious.
euphony — pleasant sound euphonic / euphonious — pleasant-sounding
eu- (good) + logos (word/speech) → eulogy
"A good speech" — formal, laudatory tribute, usually at a funeral. Adjective: eulogistic. Verb: eulogize. Person: eulogist.
eulogy — formal praise eulogistic — highly praising (adj.) eulogize — to deliver praise (v.)
eu- (good) + phoreo (to carry/bear) → euphoria
A sense of mental buoyancy and physical well-being; a "good feeling." Adjective: euphoric.
euphoria — sense of well-being euphoric — feeling tiptop (adj.)
eu- (good) + thanatos (death) → euthanasia
"Good death" — painless death inflicted on those with incurable diseases. The Greek root thanatos = death.
euthanasia — mercy killing

IV. Modes of Expression — Badinage & Its Relatives

badinage (French) — light, half-teasing banter
Non-malicious, frivolous wit intended to amuse rather than wound.
persiflage — slightly derisive banter
Closer synonym of badinage but a trifle more contemptuous — still totally non-malicious.
cliché — a worn-out phrase
A pattern of words once fresh, now so old and threadbare that only unimaginative speakers use them. "Fast and furious." "Green with envy." "By leaps and bounds."
bromide — a trite, dull remark
Any trite, probably fallacious remark showing no original thinking. Named after the drug (sedative) that dulls the senses. Adjective: bromidic.
Greek: platys (broad, flat) → platitude
A dull, trite, hackneyed statement — used by the speaker with an air of novelty, as if he just made it up. Flat + tude (state). Adjective: platitudinous.
platitude — flat remark platitudinous — full of clichés (adj.) plateau — flat land platypus — flat-footed
Greek: an- (negative) + odyne (pain) → anodyne
Medically: a drug that relieves pain (aspirin). Figuratively: a statement made to allay someone's fears or anxieties — usually not believed by the speaker, but intended to be believed by the listener.
anodyne — pain-relieving; anxiety-allaying

Socratic Sandbox

Deepen your understanding through reflection.

What is the practical difference between a cliché, a bromide, and a platitude? Are they interchangeable in all contexts?
All three describe worn-out, unoriginal expressions. The distinctions: Cliché — a phrase once vivid, now dead from overuse ("the elephant in the room"). Bromide — a trite, dull, probably false remark showing no original thinking; implies the speaker is a bore ("Better safe than sorry!"). Platitude — a flat remark delivered with an air of novelty, as if the speaker thinks they're being original. A platitude adds insult to injury by presenting banality as insight.
The eu- prefix means "good." Given this, what do euthanasia, eulogy, and euphemism have in common — and how does each express a different kind of "goodness"?
Euthanasia = good death (from thanatos = death) — death made easy/painless. Eulogy = good speech (from logos) — speaking well of someone. Euphemism = good voice (from pheme = voice) — saying something in an inoffensive way. All three are about making something harsh or difficult more gentle or pleasant through language or action.
Distinguish affluent from opulent. Write a sentence for each that could NOT be used for the other.
Affluent = comfortably and increasingly wealthy; implies money flowing in. Opulent = lavishly and conspicuously wealthy; implies ostentatious display of luxury.

Sentence using affluent only: "The town's affluent residents drove sensible cars and lived in well-kept but modest homes." (Affluent people can be understated.)
Sentence using opulent only: "The ballroom's opulent décor — gold chandeliers, marble floors, and silk draperies — left visitors breathless." (Opulence demands visible extravagance.)
An anodyne is a statement meant to relieve anxiety, usually not believed by the speaker. Is this ethical? When is it justified?
The ethics of anodynes depend on context. "Prosperity is just around the corner" (said during the Great Depression) was a classic anodyne — comforting but not believed by those who said it. In medicine, a physician might offer an anodyne to reduce a patient's fear before treatment. The tension is between respecting autonomy (people deserve truth) and benevolence (sometimes truth causes unnecessary suffering). Most ethicists today favour transparency over comforting falsehoods — but the word captures a real human tendency.
