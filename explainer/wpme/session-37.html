Root Review — Chapter 12 at a Glance

This session revisits every root and word family from Chapter 12 — sharpening your recall of minds, courage, cities, and clarity. Use this tab to cement the etymologies before tackling the drills in Practice.

I. Mind & Soul — animus / anima

Latin: animus / anima
meaning: mind, spirit, soul, feeling

Animus is the driving mental force — the attitude or disposition behind an action. Anima is the soul or life-principle. Together they yield a rich cluster of English words dealing with mental states, emotions, and collective will.

animosity — hostility animus — hostile feeling magnanimous — great-souled pusillanimous — petty-souled unanimous — of one mind equanimity — mental balance

II. The Compounds — magnus, pusillus, unus, aequus + animus

magnus (great) + animus → magnanimous
Magnanimity: generosity of spirit; rising above petty resentment
pusillus (tiny, weak) + animus → pusillanimous
Pusillanimity: pettiness, cowardice, small-mindedness
unus (one) + animus → unanimous
Unanimity: complete agreement — all of one mind
aequus (equal) + animus → equanimous
Equanimity: calmness, emotional balance under pressure

III. Courage & Its Opposite — intrepid / trepidation

Latin: trepido — to tremble, to be afraid
Gives us trepidation (fear, alarm) and intrepid (in- = not + trepidus = fearful → utterly fearless)
intrepid — fearless intrepidity — fearlessness trepidation — fear, alarm

IV. Stoicism — stoic / stoicism

Greek: Stoa Poikile (the Painted Porch)
Zeno taught philosophy on the porch (stoa) of Athens. His followers — the Stoics — held that virtue and self-control are paramount and that pain, grief, and pleasure should not disturb the rational mind.
stoic — one who endures without complaint stoical — uncomplaining (adj.) stoicism — philosophy of calm endurance

V. A Spark — scintilla / scintillate

Latin: scintilla — a spark
From the literal spark of a fire to the figurative spark of wit or intelligence
scintilla — a tiny trace or spark scintillate — to sparkle, to be brilliantly witty scintillating — dazzling, brilliantly clever scintillation — a flash of brilliance

VI. City & Country — urbs / rus

Latin: urbs (city) / rus, ruris (country, open land)
The great Roman contrast: the cultivated city vs. the rustic countryside
urban — of the city urbanity — city polish, sophistication suburban — near the city suburbia — the suburbs collectively exurbs — regions beyond the suburbs interurban — between cities intraurban — within a city rural — of the country rustic — countrified rusticate — to live in the country

VII. Looking — specto / specio

Latin: specio / specto / spectus — to look at, to see
A vast family: retrospect (looking back), introspect (looking inward), circumspect (looking all around = cautious), perspicacious (seeing through keenly), perspicuous (able to be seen through = clear)
retrospect — a looking back introspection — looking inward circumspect — looking all around; wary perspicacious — keen-sighted, shrewd perspicacity — mental keenness perspicuous — clear, easy to understand perspicuity — clarity of expression specious — plausible but false

VIII. Acumen & Acuity — acuo

Latin: acuo — to sharpen; acus — a needle
Sharpness applied to the mind: acumen is practical business keenness; acuity is the sharpness of sight, hearing, or mind; perspicacity is the same but emphasizes seeing through complexity.
acumen — keen practical insight acuity — sharpness of sense or mind acute — sharp, keen acupuncture — puncturing with a needle

IX. Versatility — verto / versus

Latin: verto / versus — to turn
Versatile literally means "able to turn easily" — one who can turn in many directions, mastering many skills.
versatile — skilled in many areas versatility — the quality of turning to many skills vertex — turning point; top invert — to turn inward diverse — turned in different directions

Socratic Sandbox

Reflect on these prompts. Write your thoughts — then reveal the model answer.

A defeated politician congratulates his opponent warmly and pledges full support. Which word from this session best describes his spirit?
Magnanimous (magnanimity). He rises above personal defeat with generosity of spirit — the opposite of pusillanimity.
A surgeon keeps perfectly calm during a crisis while all others panic. What quality does she display, and what is the related noun?
Equanimity — mental and emotional balance under pressure. The adjective is equanimous (though uncommon); the verb phrase "keep one's equanimity" is standard usage.
What is the key difference between perspicacious and perspicuous? Construct a sentence using each correctly.
Perspicacious describes a person (or mind) — keenly perceptive, sharp. Perspicuous describes language or writing — clear and easy to understand.

Example: "The perspicacious editor immediately spotted the flaw." "She rewrote the contract in perspicuous prose that anyone could follow."
Someone argues "We should legalize this practice — after all, it is natural." Is this reasoning necessarily valid? Which vocabulary word applies to seemingly compelling but actually flawed arguments?
Specious. The "appeal to nature" fallacy sounds persuasive but is logically hollow — many natural things are harmful, and many good things are artificial. A specious argument is plausible on the surface but doesn't hold up under scrutiny.
Describe in one or two sentences the difference between urban, suburban, and exurban as a concentric geography.
The urban core is the dense city center; suburban areas ring it — residential, car-dependent, near enough for daily commuting; the exurbs lie beyond the suburbs — semi-rural, farther out, occupied by those who want space but still link to the city economy.
