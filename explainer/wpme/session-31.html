I. No Reverence: Iconoclasm

Greek eikon (religious image) + klaein (to break) → iconoclast: literally, one who breaks images. Originally applied to those who destroyed religious icons; now broadly applied to anyone who attacks cherished beliefs or institutions.

iconoclast
One who attacks or destroys cherished beliefs, customs, or institutions; a breaker of images.
iconoclasm
i-KON'-ə-klaz'-əm
The doctrine or practice of attacking or overthrowing traditional beliefs or institutions.
iconoclastic
i-kon'-ə-KLAST'-ik
Of or relating to iconoclasm; aggressively challenging established beliefs.
II. Is There a God?

Greek a- (not/without) + theos (God) → atheist. Greek gnostos (known) + a- → agnostic: one for whom God's existence is not knowable.

atheism / atheist / atheistic
AY'-thee-iz-əm
The belief that no God exists; one who holds this belief; adj. form.
agnosticism / agnostic
ag-NOS'-tə-siz-əm / ag-NOS'-tik
The belief that God's existence is unknowable; one who holds this view.
III. How to Know: Diagnosis & Prognosis

Greek gnosis (knowledge) + dia- (through, across) → diagnosis. Greek pro- (before) + gnosis → prognosis: foreknowledge of outcome.

diagnosis
dī-əg-NŌ'-sis
Identification of a disease or condition from its symptoms; any careful analytical determination.
diagnose / diagnostic / diagnostician
DĪ'-əg-nōs' / dī'-əg-NOS'-tik / dī'-əg-nos-TISH'-ən
To identify; of/relating to diagnosis; an expert in making diagnoses.
prognosis
prog-NŌ'-sis
A forecast or prediction of the probable course and outcome of a condition.
prognostic / prognosticate / prognostication
prog-NOS'-tik / prog-NOS'-tə-kayt' / prog-nos'-tə-KAY'-shən
Predictive; to predict or forecast; the act or art of forecasting.
IV. Getting Back to God: Theism Families

Greek theos (God) + logos (study) → theology. The prefix determines how many gods: mono- (one), poly- (many), pan- (all).

monotheism / monotheist / monotheistic
MON'-ə-thee-iz-əm
Belief in one God; one who holds this; adj. form.
polytheism / polytheist / polytheistic
POL'-ee-thee-iz-əm
Belief in many gods; one who holds this; adj. form.
pantheism / pantheist / pantheistic
PAN'-thee-iz-əm
The doctrine that God is identical with the universe; one who holds this; adj. form.
theology / theologian / theological
thee-OL'-ə-jee / thee'-ə-LŌ'-jən / thee'-ə-LOJ'-ə-kəl
The study of God and religious doctrine; an expert in theology; adj. form.

VII. Under & Over: Hypo- & Hyper-

Greek hypo- means under, below, deficient. Greek hyper- means over, above, excessive. These prefixes generate medical and common vocabulary in pairs of opposites.

HYPO- (under/below/deficient):
hypochondria / hypochondriac / hypochondriacal
hī-pə-KON'-dree-ə / hī'-pə-kən-DRĪ'-ə-kəl
Excessive worry about one's health, imagining illness. (From Greek hypo- + chondros = cartilage — the area under the ribs where illness was thought to reside.)
hypodermic
Under the skin (as in a hypodermic needle).
hypotension / hypotensive
hī'-pō-TEN'-shən / hī'-pō-TEN'-siv
Abnormally low blood pressure; adj. form.
hypothyroid
Having insufficient thyroid activity.
HYPER- (over/above/excessive):
hypercritical
Excessively or unnecessarily critical.
hyperthyroidism
Overactivity of the thyroid gland.
hypertension / hypertensive
hī'-pər-TEN'-shən / hī'-pər-TEN'-siv
Abnormally high blood pressure; adj. form.

Brief Intermission Seven — Some Interesting Derivations

From People's Names

bloomers — loose trousers worn by women; named after Amelia Bloomer, 19th-c. women's rights advocate
boycott — to refuse to deal with; named after Capt. Charles Boycott, an Irish land agent ostracized by tenants
marcel — a type of wave in hair; named after François Marcel, French hairdresser
silhouette — a shadow outline; named after Étienne de Silhouette, French finance minister known for cheapness
derrick — a hoisting apparatus; named after Thomas Derrick, a hangman who devised a crane-like gallows
sadism — pleasure from inflicting pain; named after the Marquis de Sade
galvanism — electricity produced by chemical action; named after Luigi Galvani, Italian physiologist
guppies — tropical fish; named after R.J.L. Guppy, who sent specimens to the British Museum
nicotine — the alkaloid in tobacco; named after Jean Nicot, who introduced tobacco to France

From Place Names

bayonet — from Bayonne, France, where this weapon was first made
cantaloupe — from Cantalupo, an estate near Rome where this melon was first grown
calico — from Calicut (now Kozhikode), India, where this cloth originated
tuxedo — from Tuxedo Park, New York, where this formal jacket was first worn at a ball
Gypsies — from Egypt, as they were mistakenly thought to come from there
damask — from Damascus, Syria, famous for its woven fabrics
satin — from Tzu-t'ing (Zaytun), a Chinese port city
frankfurters — from Frankfurt, Germany, where this sausage originated
Socratic Sandbox
On Iconoclasm

An iconoclast literally breaks images. Figuratively, iconoclasts break cherished ideas. Is all iconoclasm good — a clearing of superstition — or can it be destructive? Think of a time when an iconoclast changed something for the better, and one where the destruction of tradition caused harm.

On Atheism vs. Agnosticism

The atheist says "God does not exist." The agnostic says "God's existence cannot be known." Which position requires more intellectual humility? Is there a meaningful difference in practice? Can one act ethically without theistic belief?

On Prognosis

A doctor's prognosis can shape a patient's will to live. If a prognosis is grim, should it always be disclosed in full? Consider the ethics of hope vs. honesty in medicine. When does a forecast become self-fulfilling?

On Hypo- and Hyper-

Language reflects our conceptual structures. We use hypo- and hyper- constantly in science and medicine. What other paired prefixes capture the idea of "too little / too much"? (Think: under/over-, de-/super-, micro-/macro-.) How does naming a concept help us perceive it?

On Word Origins from Names

Many words come from people's names — boycott, silhouette, sadism. What does it mean to have your name become a common word? Is it a form of immortality, or a form of reduction? Which of the derivations in the Intermission surprises you most, and why?
