Teaser Questions for the Amateur Etymologist

These questions invite you to think creatively about etymology. Use your knowledge of roots and their meanings to explore further.

1. The word "notables" — define in context of "known"
Answer: People who are known; people of prominence or note. From Latin "notus" (known).
2. "Notify" and "notice" — define both in context of "known"; what does the suffix -fy mean?
Answer: Notify = to make known. Notice = that which is made known. The suffix -fy (also -ify) means "to make" or "to cause to be." So "notify" literally means "to make known."
3. Knowing CHRONOS (time) and GRAPHEIN (to write), what might "chronograph" mean?
Answer: A chronograph is an instrument that records or writes time; a precise timepiece. The root "chronograph" combines chronos (time) + graphein (write) = a device that records time.
4. Recognizing GENESIS in "generate" — define both; also "regenerate"; what does re- mean?
Answer: Generate = to produce or bring into being. Genesis = origin, beginning. Regenerate = to generate again, to be born anew. The prefix re- means "again" or "back."
5. Knowing OMNIS (all): define "omnipotent" and "omnipresent"; what are their noun forms?
Answer: Omnipotent = all-powerful (potent = powerful). Omnipresent = present everywhere (present = being in all places). Noun forms: omnipotence and omnipresence.
6. Using the ANA- negative prefix and "aphrodisiac" — what word means "that which reduces sexual desire"?
Answer: An-aphrodisiac. The prefix ana- (or an- before a vowel) means "not" or "against." So an-aphrodisiac literally means "anti-aphrodisiac" or "that which is against sexual desire."
