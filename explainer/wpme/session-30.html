I. Brothers & Sisters, Wives & Husbands

Latin frater (brother) and soror (sister) give us many English words relating to kinship and fellowship. The Latin words for wife (uxor) and husband (maritus) yield a cluster of marital terms.

II. Of Cabbages & Kings

Latin rex, regis means king. Words deriving from it carry connotations of royalty, stateliness, and splendor.

III. Of Madness & Obsession

Greek mania means madness, frenzy, or obsession. Combined with other roots, it names specific forms of irrational passion or compulsion. Each word generates a trio: noun (-mania), person (-maniac), and adjective (-maniacal).

IV. Of Fears & Phobias

Greek phobos means morbid dread or fear. Combined with other roots, it names specific irrational fears. Each yields a noun (-phobia), person (-phobe), and adjective (-phobic).

Socratic Sandbox

Explore the words from this session through guided reflection. Think about each question before reading on.

On Brotherhood & Sisterhood

The words fraternal and sororal both mean "of a sibling," yet society gave us fraternity for men's groups and sorority for women's. Why might the masculine form have taken on the broader sense of "fellowship"? Consider how language reflects cultural assumptions about who forms bonds in public life.

On Devotion and Excess

The word uxorious is almost always used critically — a man who is too devoted to his wife. Is there an equivalent word for a wife who is excessively devoted to her husband? Why does English lack such a term? What does this asymmetry tell us about historical attitudes toward marriage?

On Manias & Labels

Kleptomania frames compulsive stealing as a medical condition. Megalomania frames grandiose delusion as one. How does giving something a Greek/Latin name change how we think about it — as illness vs. crime vs. personality flaw? Can naming something pathological be both a mercy and an excuse?

On Phobias & Their Opposites

Claustrophobia is fear of enclosed spaces; agoraphobia is fear of open ones. These seem like opposites, yet both are debilitating. What might this tell us about anxiety — that it is not about what triggers it, but about loss of control? Can you think of a phobia you find understandable, and one that seems paradoxical?

On Regalia

Regalia originally meant the emblems of kingship — crown, scepter, orb. Today we use it for any ceremonial trappings (graduation regalia, Masonic regalia). What happens to a word when it moves from the sacred to the secular? Is something lost, or gained, in the democratization of royal language?
