BONUS/BENE Family — "The Good News"

While MALUS gives us evil, BONUS/BENE gives us good. Every mal- word has a ben- counterpart!

BONUS / BENE (Latin) = "good, well"

Contrast Table: MALUS vs. BONUS/BENE

MALUS (bad) BONUS/BENE (good)
malediction (curse) benediction (blessing)
maleficent (doing harm) beneficent (doing good)
malefactor (evildoer) benefactor (one who does good)
malevolent (wishing evil) benevolent (wishing good)

BENE Tree: Full Branches

  • benign (bə-NĪN') → benignant → benignity, benignancy
  • benediction (ben'-ə-DIK'-shən): blessing; "saying good" → benedictory
  • beneficent (bə-NEF'-ə-sənt): doing good → beneficence
  • benefactor (BEN'-ə-fak'-tər): one who does good → benefactress, benefaction, beneficiary
  • benefit, beneficial
  • benevolent (bə-NEV'-ə-lənt): wishing good → benevolence

BONUS Family

  • bonus: extra payment for good performance
  • bonbon: French for "good-good" = candy
  • bona fide (BŌ'-nə FĪD'): "in good faith" = sincere, valid, without pretense
  • FIDES → fidelity (fə-DEL'-ə-tee): faithfulness; infidel (IN'-fə-dəl): non-believer; infidelity (in'-fə-DEL'-ə-tee): unfaithfulness

DICO/DICTUS Family

DICO, DICTUS (Latin) = "to say, tell"
  • benediction: bene + dico = "saying good" = blessing
  • malediction: malus + dico = "saying bad" = curse
  • dictate, dictator, dictation, dictatorial (dik'-tə-TAWR'-ee-əl): telling others what to do
  • predict: pre- + dico = to say beforehand
  • Dictaphone: dico + phone (sound)
  • contradict: contra- + dico = to say against
  • addiction: ad- + dico = "a saying toward" = compulsion

FACIO/FACTUS Family

FACIO, FACTUS / fec-, fic-, -fy (Latin) = "to do, make"
  • factory: -ory (place where) = place where things are made
  • fact: something done = something that exists
  • fiction: something made up or invented
  • manufacture: manus (hand) + factus = make by hand
  • artificial: made by human art
  • benefactor, malefactor (bene/malus + factor)
  • clarify, simplify, liquefy, magnify: -fy verbs

VOLO Family

VOLO (Latin) = "to wish, to will, to be willing"
  • voluntary, involuntary, volunteer
  • volition (vō-LISH'-ən): the act or power of willing; free will

PLAC- Family

PLAC- (Latin) = "to please, appease, soothe, pacify"
  • placation (play-KAY'-shən): act of placating
  • placative (PLAK'-ə-tiv) or placatory (PLAK'-ə-taw-ree): adjective = tending to pacify
  • placable (PLAK'-ə-bəl): able to be soothed
  • implacable (im-PLAK'-ə-bəl): NOT able to be soothed; unyielding; relentless; harsh
  • implacability: noun
  • placid (PLAS'-id): calm, easygoing, serene, undisturbed → placidity (plə-SID'-ə-tee)
  • complacent (kəm-PLAY'-sənt): pleased with oneself (com- = with/together); self-satisfied; smug
  • complacence (kəm-PLAY'-səns) or complacency (kəm-PLAY'-sən-see)

DONO Family

DONO (Latin) = "to give"
  • condone: con- + dono = to give together; to forgive; to overlook an offense
  • condonation (kon'-dō-NAY'-shən): act of overlooking or forgiving an offense
  • donor: one who gives
  • donate, donation

benign
(bə-NĪN')
[adjective]
Definition: kindly; good-natured; not harmful; not cancerous (medical)
From: BENE (good) + -ign
Example: "The benign judge showed mercy to the first-time offender."
benignant
(bə-NIG'-nənt)
[adjective]
Definition: kindly; gracious; favorable; good-natured (slightly more formal than benign)
Example: "She had a benignant attitude toward even the most difficult students."
benediction
(ben'-ə-DIK'-shən)
[noun]
Definition: a blessing; a "saying of good"; a prayer asking for divine blessing
From: BENE (good) + DICO/DICTUS (to say)
Contrast: malediction = curse; benediction = blessing
Example: "The priest gave a benediction at the end of the service."
beneficent
(bə-NEF'-ə-sənt)
[adjective]
Definition: doing good; performing acts of kindness or charity
From: BENE (good) + FACIO/FACTUS (to do/make)
Example: "The beneficent philanthropist funded schools throughout the region."
benefactor
(BEN'-ə-fak'-tər)
[noun]
Definition: one who does good things for another; a patron; one who provides financial or other help
From: BENE (good) + FACTOR (one who does)
Example: "An anonymous benefactor donated the entire library collection."
Word family: benefactress (female), benefaction (the deed), beneficiary (recipient)
beneficiary
(ben'-ə-FISH'-ər-ee)
[noun]
Definition: the recipient of money, kindness, gifts, help, etc.
Example: "She was named as the beneficiary of the life insurance policy."
benevolent
(bə-NEV'-ə-lənt)
[adjective]
Definition: wishing good things for others; well disposed; kindly
From: BENE (good) + VOLO (to wish)
Example: "The benevolent monarch was loved by all her subjects."
Noun: benevolence (bə-NEV'-ə-ləns)
bona fide
(BŌ'-nə FĪD')
[adjective/adverb]
Definition: in good faith; sincere; genuine; valid; without pretense or deception
From: Latin BONUS (good) + FIDES (faith) = "in good faith"
Example: "This is a bona fide offer — we have the funds available."
fidelity
(fə-DEL'-ə-tee)
[noun]
Definition: faithfulness; loyalty; accuracy of reproduction
From: FIDES (faith) + -ity
Example: "High-fidelity audio reproduces sound with great accuracy."
infidel
(IN'-fə-dəl)
[noun]
Definition: one who does not have the "right" faith or religion; a non-believer
From: in- (not) + FIDES (faith)
Example: "Throughout history, religious wars were fought against 'infidels.'"
infidelity
(in'-fə-DEL'-ə-tee)
[noun]
Definition: unfaithfulness; the violation of a trust or marriage vow
From: in- (not) + FIDELITY (faithfulness)
Example: "His infidelity broke his marriage apart."
dictatorial
(dik'-tə-TAWR'-ee-əl)
[adjective]
Definition: domineering; giving orders in a manner permitting no refusal; tyrannical
From: DICO/DICTUS (to say/tell) + -orial
Example: "His dictatorial management style drove away talented employees."
volition
(vō-LISH'-ən)
[noun]
Definition: the act or power of willing; free will; an act performed by one's own choice
From: VOLO (to wish, to will)
Example: "She left of her own volition — no one forced her."
placatory
(PLAK'-ə-taw-ree)
[adjective]
Definition: tending, or intended, to pacify; to soothe; to change hostility into friendliness
Also: placative (PLAK'-ə-tiv)
Example: "He made a placatory gesture to end the argument."
implacable
(im-PLAK'-ə-bəl)
[adjective]
Definition: not to be soothed or pacified; unyielding to pity or entreaty; relentless; harsh
From: im- (not) + plac- (to please) + -able
Example: "The implacable prosecutor refused to accept any plea bargain."
placid
(PLAS'-id)
[adjective]
Definition: calm; easygoing; serene; undisturbed; unruffled
From: plac- (to please) → etymologically, pleased with things as they are
Example: "The placid lake reflected the mountains like a mirror."
Noun: placidity (plə-SID'-ə-tee)
complacent
(kəm-PLAY'-sənt)
[adjective]
Definition: pleased with oneself; smug; self-satisfied; too easily satisfied with one's own position
From: com- (with/together) + plac- (to please) = pleased with oneself
Example: "The complacent team assumed they would win and didn't prepare."
Noun: complacence or complacency
condone
(kən-DŌN')
[verb]
Definition: to overlook or forgive an offense; to excuse; to implicitly accept or allow
From: con- (together/with) + DONO (to give)
Example: "I cannot condone his behavior, even if others do."
condonation
(kon'-dō-NAY'-shən)
[noun]
Definition: the act of overlooking, forgiving, or pardoning an offense or transgression
From: con- + DONO (to give) = to give forgiveness; to give over
Example: "His condonation of the fraud made him legally complicit."

Deepen Your Understanding

Reflect on these philosophical and linguistic questions. Use the text areas to record your thoughts.

1. The Good News (BENE vs. MALUS)

Session 22 explored MALUS (evil). This session brings the counterpart: BENE (good). Why does English seem to have more vivid, specific words for evil than for goodness? Is malevolence more memorable than benevolence? What does this asymmetry say about human psychology?

2. Bona Fide vs. Bad Faith

Bona fide means 'in good faith.' Acting in good faith has legal and moral implications. Can someone act in good faith and still cause harm? Is intention sufficient to determine moral worth?

3. Implacable Conviction

Implacable means unyielding, relentless, impossible to soothe. We sometimes admire implacable fighters for justice (e.g., abolitionists). But when does principled implacability become fanaticism? What distinguishes the two?

4. Complacency as Danger

Complacent means self-satisfied, smug — pleased with oneself. Complacency has been blamed for corporate failures, military defeats, and personal stagnation. Can you think of a time when complacency — your own or others' — led to a bad outcome?

5. Dictatorial Leadership

Dictatorial means giving orders that cannot be refused. Some management theorists argue that certain crises require dictatorial leadership. Is this true? When, if ever, is dictatorial authority justified?

6. Condone vs. Forgive

To condone is to overlook or excuse an offense without necessarily approving it. To forgive is to release resentment toward someone who wronged you. Are these the same? Can you condone something without forgiving it? Can you forgive without condoning?
