The Ten Adjectives for Liars

Chapter 7 introduces a nuanced vocabulary for describing different types of liars and lying. Each of these ten words has a precise connotation—some emphasizing the notoriety of the lie, others the skill involved, others the compulsive nature of the behavior. All are expressive and versatile, usable far beyond the context of deception.

The key insight: each word captures a different nuance of falsehood. Understanding these distinctions allows you to pinpoint not just that someone lies, but how and why they lie, and what makes their deception distinctive.

The Challenge: All ten words describe liars, but each emphasizes a different quality: notoriety, skill, incorrigibility, habit, congenital nature, frequency, compulsion, lack of conscience, smoothness, or viciousness.

The Ten Liars

Notorious

nə-TAWR'-ee-əs

A notorious liar: your lying is so well-established that everyone knows about it. You are famous—or rather infamous—for your falsifications.

Why This Matters: These ten adjectives are not mere synonyms. Each carries its own history, its own precise connotation. Master these distinctions, and you gain the ability to describe human behavior with surgical precision. You become not someone who uses words, but someone who wields them.

The Etymology of Deception

Each adjective has a story rooted in ancient languages. Understanding the etymology illuminates not just the word itself, but the precise nuance it conveys. These roots are the DNA of meaning.

Word Etymology Special Nuance
notorious NOTUS (known) Unfavorably known; famous for bad acts
consummate SUMMUS (highest) Perfect, highly skilled, brought to completion
incorrigible IN + CORRIGO (correct) Beyond reform; not correctable
inveterate VETUS (old) Deep-rooted habit; grown old in the practice
congenital CON + GENESIS (birth) From birth; present from the beginning
chronic KHRONOS (time) Continuing or recurring over long period
pathological PATHOS + LOGOS (disease/study) Diseased compulsion; irresistible impulse
unconscionable UN + CONSCIENTIA (conscience) Without conscience; lacking moral sense
glib Unknown origin Suspiciously smooth; superficially fluent
egregious EX + GREX (out of flock) Oustandingly bad; standing out from the norm

Etymology Breakdown

Notorious
NOTUS (known) → notorious, notoriety (noun: the state of being unfavorably known)
Consummate
SUMMUS (highest) → consummate (adj. at the highest point), summit, sum, consummation (noun)
Incorrigible
IN- (not) + CORRIGO (to correct) → incorrigible, incorrigibility (noun)
Inveterate
VETUS (old) → inveterate, veteran (one who has grown old in a profession)
Congenital
CON- (with) + GENESIS (birth) → congenital, genetics, genealogy, genesis
Chronic
KHRONOS (time) → chronic, chronological, chronicle, chronometer
Pathological
PATHOS (disease) + LOGOS (study) → pathological, pathology, sympathy
Unconscionable
UN- (not) + CON- (with) + SCIENTIA (knowledge/conscience) → unconscionable, conscience
Glib
Origin uncertain. Possibly from Old Norse GLYPR (mockery) or Low German.
Egregious
EX- (out of) + GREX (flock) → egregious, gregarious (living in flocks), congregate

Which Liars Can Be Good?

Here's a thought-provoking question: Which of these ten adjectives can actually be used positively—and which are almost always derogatory?

The Challenge Question

Which of these ten adjectives can be used positively—and which are always negative?
Sample answer: Consummate and congenital can be neutral or even positive when applied to qualities other than lying. "She has a consummate artistry at the keyboard." "He has a congenital cheerfulness." But words like notorious, incorrigible, pathological, unconscionable, and egregious are almost always derogatory, regardless of context.

The Deeper Insight

These ten adjectives are not mere synonyms for "liar." Each reveals a different dimension of deception: the public nature of it, the skill involved, the resistance to reform, the long habituation, the early origins, the frequency, the compulsive nature, the absence of conscience, the smoothness of delivery, and the viciousness of intent.

By mastering these distinctions, you gain the ability to describe not just that someone lies, but how and why they lie, with precision and nuance. This is the power of a rich vocabulary: it allows you to see the world more clearly.

The Final Lesson: Language is not just a tool for communication. It is a tool for thought. The more precisely you can name something, the more clearly you can think about it. Master these words, and you master a new way of understanding human nature.
