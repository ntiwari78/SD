Family 1: SCIO / SCIENS (Latin: to know / knowing)

From unconscionable (Session 14 — without conscience), we trace back to Latin SCIO (to know) and SCIENS (knowing). Both words use the prefix CON- (with, together). Your conscience is literally your knowledge with a moral sense of right and wrong. If you are unconscionable, your conscience is not working.

The Pattern: CON- (with, together) + SCIENS (knowing) = "knowing together with" a moral sense

Family 2: GLIB (Old English: slippery)

When someone is glib, their words are smooth and slippery. They lack sincerity and conviction. Glib liars fool only the nescient — those too ignorant to recognize the smoothness as a warning sign.

Family 3: GREX / GREGIS (Latin: herd, flock)

From egregious — standing out (E-/EX-) from the herd (GREX) — a whole family of words about groups, gathering, and separation. The connections between these words reveal how a single root, combined with different prefixes, creates words with opposite meanings.

The GREX Family at a Glance:
  • CON + GREX → congregate (gather together)
  • SE + GREX → segregate (set apart)
  • AD/AG + GREX → aggregate (bring toward)
  • EX + GREX → egregious (stand out from)

Why This Matters: The GREX family shows how a single Latin root combines with different prefixes to create words with opposite or complementary meanings. This is one of the most powerful patterns in English vocabulary.

Root Families: SCIO/SCIENS, GLIB, and GREX/GREGIS

The words in this session come from three different sources: Latin SCIO (to know), Old English GLIB (slippery), and Latin GREX (herd). Understanding how these roots combine with prefixes reveals the logic of English word formation.

Root Decoder: What Does It Mean?

SCIO / SCIENS (Latin)
Meaning: to know / knowing

Examples: conscious, science, omniscient, prescient

CON- (Latin prefix)
Meaning: with, together

When combined with SCIO: "knowing together with" → conscience, conscious

OMNIS (Latin)
Meaning: all

Omniscient = all-knowing

PRE- (Latin prefix)
Meaning: before

Prescient = knowing before → foreknowledge

NE- (Latin negative prefix)
Meaning: not, no

Nescient = not knowing → ignorant

UN- (English negative prefix)
Meaning: not

Unconscionable = not having conscience

GLIB (Old English)
Meaning: slippery, smooth

Glibness = the quality of being suspiciously smooth and fluent

GREX / GREGIS (Latin)
Meaning: herd, flock

Forms the root of gregarious, congregate, segregate, aggregate, egregious

EX- / E- (Latin prefix)
Meaning: out, out of

Egregious = standing out from the herd (in a bad way)

SE- (Latin prefix)
Meaning: apart, aside

Segregate = to separate from the herd

AD- / AG- (Latin prefix)
Meaning: to, toward

Aggregate = to bring toward the herd; gather into a mass

-NESS (English suffix)
Meaning: the state or quality of

Glibness, egregiousness, gregariousness = the state of being glib, egregious, gregarious

-ION (Latin suffix)
Meaning: acts as a noun suffix

Congregation, segregation, aggregation

The Challenge Question

How does egregious relate to gregarious? They share the same root GREX, yet mean opposite things in practice. What's the connection?

Answer

Both words derive from GREX (herd/flock). Gregarious = WITH the herd (sociable, enjoying groups). Egregious = standing OUT OF the herd — standing out, but in a bad way. The two words demonstrate how a single root, combined with different prefixes, creates near-opposite meanings.
