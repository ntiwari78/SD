I. No Fatigue

Indefatigable is derived from fatigue — in- is a negative prefix, the suffix -able means able to be; hence literally, indefatigable means unable to be fatigued.

indefatigable
in'-də-FAT'-ə-gə-bəl
Incapable of being fatigued; untiring; not subject to exhaustion.
indefatigability
in'-do-fat'-ə-gə-BIL'-ə-tee
The quality or state of being indefatigable; tirelessness.
II. How Simple Can One Be?

Ingenuous is a complimentary term, implying frankness and openness. Do not confuse it with ingenious (in-JEEN'-yəs), which means shrewd, clever, inventive — a different word entirely, despite near-identical spelling.

ingenuous / ingenuousness
in-JEN'-yōō-əs
Frank, open, artless; without guile or hidden motive. The noun is ingenuousness.
ingenious / ingenuity / ingeniousness
in-JEEN'-yəs / in'-jə-NŌŌ'-ə-tee
Shrewd, clever, inventive. The nouns are ingenuity and ingeniousness.
naïve / naïveté
nah-EEV' / nah-eev-TAY'
Inexperienced and trusting; unsophisticated. Implies idealism from ignorance or inexperience.
credulous / credulity
KREJ'-ə-ləs / krə-JŌŌ'-lə-tee
Willing to believe almost anything, no matter how fantastic. From Latin credo, to believe.
gullible / gullibility
GUL'-ə-bəl / gul'-ə-BIL'-ə-tee
Easily tricked or deceived; a stronger and more derogatory word than credulous. Gullibility results more from stupidity than ignorance.

Four Synonyms — Subtle Differences:
1. ingenuous — frank; not given to concealment
2. naïve — inexperienced, unsophisticated, trusting
3. credulous — willing to believe; not suspicious or skeptical
4. gullible — easily tricked

III. Belief and Disbelief: The CREDO Family

Latin credo means to believe. The suffix -ous usually means full of → credulous = full of belief. The suffix -ible means can be → credible = can be believed.

IV. What People Believe In

From credo (to believe) come four more useful English words about personal conviction and trust.

credo
KREE'-dō
A personal belief; a code of ethics; the principles by which people guide their actions.
creed
KREED
A close synonym of credo; also specifically a religious belief (e.g., Catholicism, Judaism, Islam).
credence
KREE'-dəns
Belief; trust; as in, "I place no credence in his stories."
credentials
krə-DEN'-shəlz
Documents proving a person's right to a title, privilege, or identity — a right to be believed.
V. Heads and Tails: Disingenuous

If ingenuous means frank, open, then disingenuous (dis-in-JEN'-yōō-əs) should mean simply not frank or open. But in practice it means far more: crafty, cunning, dishonest, artful, insincere, untrustworthy — all while making a pretense of being simple and honest. The wolf in sheep's clothing.

disingenuous / disingenuousness
dis'-in-JEN'-yōō-əs / dis'-in-JEN'-yōō-əs-nəs
Not candid or sincere; crafty and calculating while pretending to be frank; intentionally deceptive.

Socratic Sandbox
On Ingenuous vs. Ingenious

Two words, nearly identical in spelling, utterly different in meaning. One describes a quality of honesty; the other, a quality of cleverness. Can a person be both simultaneously — ingeniously ingenuous? Or does cunning intelligence tend to erode frankness over time?

On Credulity and Society

A credulous society is easily manipulated by propaganda. Yet total incredulity — refusing to believe anything — is a form of paralysis. What is the right degree of skepticism? How do we distinguish healthy doubt from cynicism? Where should credence be given, and where withheld?

On Disingenuousness

The disingenuous person is more dangerous than the simply dishonest one — because they have adopted the appearance of honesty as a weapon. Consider a politician who says "I just want to be transparent" before concealing something important. What cues help us detect disingenuousness? Is it detectable at all, or only in retrospect?

On Credentials

Credentials are documents proving one's right to be believed. But can credentials become a substitute for actual competence? Consider: how much should a degree, a title, or a certificate substitute for direct evidence of skill? When should credence rest on credentials, and when on demonstrated performance?
