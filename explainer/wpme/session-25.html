Latin Root
TACEO
"to be silent"

About keeping one's mouth shut

Taciturn comes from Latin TACEO, to be silent. It has many synonyms — silent, uncommunicative, reticent, reserved, secretive, close-lipped — but no other word indicates the permanent, habitual, and temperamental disinclination to talk that taciturn implies.

Important Distinction

taciturn: a permanent temperamental disinclination to talk (describes a PERSON)
tacit: unspoken, understood without being said (describes an AGREEMENT, ARRANGEMENT, etc.) — a person is NEVER called tacit
reticent: preferring silence, especially from shyness or fear of revealing something

Examples

tacit
"They had a tacit agreement to never discuss the subject."
taciturn
"Coolidge was famously taciturn."
reticent
"She was reticent about her health problems."
Latin Root
LOQUOR
"to speak"

Talk, talk, talk!

Loquacious derives from LOQUOR. The noun is loquacity (lō-KWAS'-ə-tee) or loquaciousness.

Word-by-word Explanations

soliloquy

Speaking to oneself. In theater, characters speak their thoughts aloud for the audience. Eugene O'Neill made novel use of soliloquies in Mourning Becomes Electra.

soliloquist: one who speaks to themselves
soliloquize: to speak to oneself; to deliver a soliloquy
ventriloquism

The art of "speaking from the belly." The listener thinks the sound comes from a different source (a dummy, an object, etc.).

VENTER, VENTRIS (belly) + LOQUOR (speak) = ventriloquism
ventriloquist: one who practices ventriloquism
ventriloquistic: adjective
ventriloquize: to throw one's voice
colloquial

CON- + LOQUOR = speaking together; informal conversational language

  • Colloquial patterns are perfectly correct — they are simply informal
  • colloquialism: a conversational-style expression (e.g., "He hasn't got any" vs. formal "He has none")
  • Note: CON- becomes COL- before L, COR- before R, COM- before M/P/B
circumlocution

CIRCUM- (around) + LOQUOR (speak) = "talking around"

Any roundabout, indirect way of expressing an idea
circumlocutory: adjective

Pronunciations & Definitions

From TACEO (to be silent)

taciturnity
tas-ə-TURN'-ə-tee
noun
Definition: habitual disinclination to conversation; the quality of being taciturn
From: TACEO (to be silent) + -urn + -ity
"His taciturnity at social events made him seem aloof, though he was merely shy."
tacit
TAS'-it
adjective
Definition: unspoken; understood without being said; not verbalized; implied by actions or circumstances
From: TACEO (to be silent)
"There was a tacit agreement between them never to discuss that night."
Important: NEVER used to describe a person — only agreements, arrangements, understandings, etc.
Noun: tacitness
reticent
RET'-ə-sənt
adjective
Definition: inclined to be silent or secretive; reluctant to speak, especially about personal matters
From: re- (again) + TACEO (to be silent)
"She was reticent about her past, revealing little even to close friends."
Noun: reticence (RET'-ə-səns) or reticency (RET'-ə-sən-see)

Three Words from TACEO — Three Different Meanings

taciturn: a person's permanent tendency not to talk (describes personality)
tacit: unspoken but understood (describes agreements, understanding)
reticent: reluctant to speak or reveal things (describes behavior from shyness/fear)

From LOQUOR (to speak)

loquacity
lō-KWAS'-ə-tee
noun
Definition: talkativeness; the quality of being loquacious
From: LOQUOR (to speak) + -acity
Also: loquaciousness (lō-KWAY'-shəs-nəs)
"His loquacity made him a great conversationalist but a poor listener."
soliloquy
sə-LIL'-ə-kwee
noun
Definition: a speech to oneself; in theater, a speech in which a character speaks thoughts aloud, often alone on stage
From: SOLUS (alone) + LOQUOR (to speak) = "speaking alone"
"Hamlet's 'To be or not to be' is the most famous soliloquy in English."
Verb: soliloquize / Noun for performer: soliloquist
ventriloquism
ven-TRIL'-ə-kwiz-əm
noun
Definition: the art of throwing one's voice; making sounds seem to come from another source
From: VENTER, VENTRIS (belly) + LOQUOR (speak) = "speaking from the belly"
"His skill at ventriloquism made the dummy seem truly alive."
Practitioner: ventriloquist / Adjective: ventriloquistic / Verb: ventriloquize
colloquial
kə-LŌ'-kwee-əl
adjective
Definition: informal; suitable for everyday conversation; characteristic of ordinary speech
From: CON-/COL- (together) + LOQUOR (speak) = speaking together informally
"The essay was criticized for its colloquial tone — too casual for academic writing."
Note: colloquial is NOT the same as vulgar or illiterate — it is simply informal
colloquialism
kə-LŌ'-kwee-ə-liz-əm
noun
Definition: an informal expression used in everyday conversation
Examples: "He hasn't got any" (colloquial) vs. "He has none" (formal)
"The speech was full of Americanisms and regional colloquialisms."
circumlocution
sur'-kəm-lō-KYOO'-shən
noun
Definition: a roundabout, indirect way of expressing an idea; "talking around" the subject
From: CIRCUM- (around) + LOQUOR (speak) = "talking around"
"Instead of saying 'no,' he used endless circumlocution to avoid giving a direct answer."
circumlocutory
sur'-kəm-LOK'-yə-tawr'-ee
adjective
Definition: roundabout; indirect; using more words than necessary to express something
"The politician's circumlocutory style made it impossible to know his real position."
