Session 46

Related Words — the etymological families branching from Chapter 16's ten -ous adjectives

← Session 45 Word Power Made Easy Session 47 →

Session 46 extends Chapter 16 by tracing the related words that radiate from the same Latin and French roots behind our ten -ous adjectives. Each root family reveals how a single ancient word branched into modern English vocabulary across wildly different domains — from funeral rites to cavalry charges, from corporate finance to poetic grief.

From sequor — to follow

The verb sequor ("to follow") underlies both obsequious (following slavishly) and a cluster of everyday words about things that come after or in order:

obsequies
OB′-sə-kweez
Funeral rites; the ceremonies that "follow after" a death. Always plural.
subsequent
SUB′-sə-kwənt
Coming after or later; following in time or order. Sub- = under/after.
sequel
SEE′-kwəl
A following event; a literary or film work that continues another.
sequence
SEE′-kwəns
Things following in proper order; a series of related events.
Non sequitur — Latin for "it does not follow." A conclusion with no logical connection to the premise; by extension, any irrelevant statement.

From pecus — cattle (wealth)

Because ancient wealth was measured in livestock, pecus spawned words about money:

pecuniary
pə-KYOO′-nee-air′-ee
Pertaining to money; involving financial matters. Pecuniary considerations, pecuniary affairs.

From noceo — to injure

Innocuous means harmless (in- + noceo). Its sibling points the other way:

noxious
NOK′-shəs
Harmful, poisonous, unwholesome. The direct opposite of innocuous.

From bibo — to drink

Bibulous means addicted to drink. Two relatives complete the family:

imbibe
im-BĪB′
To drink in; to absorb; to take in. Can be used metaphorically: "to imbibe learning." (Im- = in)
bib
Upper part of an apron, or a napkin tied around a child's neck — it prevents what is drunk (or eaten) from dribbling on clothing.

From doleo — to suffer or grieve

Dolorous describes mournful sadness. The root family includes poetic and social words:

dolor
DŌ′-ler
A poetic synonym of grief; pain or sorrow. The name Dolores means "sorrows" etymologically.
doleful
DŌL′-fəl
Exaggeratedly sorrowful; mournfully dismal — with a slightly humorous edge.
condole
kən-DŌL′
To suffer or grieve with another (con- = with/together); a less common synonym of commiserate.
condolence
kən-DŌ′-ləns
An expression of sympathy, especially on a death. Much more frequent than the verb condole.

From cheval / caballus — horse

French cheval (from Latin caballus, an inferior horse) gave us the chivalrous knight tradition and the cavalry military division. Latin equus (a noble horse) gave us the equestrian arts:

cavalcade
KAV′-əl-kayd′
A procession of persons on horseback; a parade of mounted riders.
cavalier (adj.)
kav-ə-LEER′
Offhand; haughty; unmindful of others' feelings — as a mounted soldier might look down on foot soldiers.
cavalry
KAV′-əl-ree
The mounted, "horsed" division of an army; soldiers on horseback.
chivalry
SHIV′-əl-ree
The gallant system of knightly conduct, especially courtesy toward women.
chivalric
shə-VAL′-rik
Of or pertaining to chivalry; less common adjective form identical in meaning to chivalrous.
equestrian
ə-KWES′-tree-ən
A horseman; of or relating to horseback riding. An equestrian statue shows the subject on a horse.
equestrienne
ə-KWES′-tree-en′
A horsewoman; feminine form of equestrian.
equine
EE′-kwīn′
Horselike; of, relating to, or characteristic of a horse.

From cado — to fall

One's "final fall" is death — hence the corpse and its moral cousin decadence (de- = down + cado = fall):

cadaver
kə-DAV′-ər or kə-DAY′-vər
A corpse, especially one used for surgical dissection.
decadent
DEK′-ə-dənt or də-KAY′-dənt
Morally deteriorating; in a state of decay. Used figuratively of moral or spiritual decline, not physical rot.
decadence
DEK′-ə-dəns or də-KAY′-dəns
A state of moral or cultural decline; spiritual deterioration. The noun form of decadent.

Etymology

The words cavalier (adj.) and chivalrous both descend from horse-related roots, yet mean nearly opposite things in tone. How did "mounted horseman" lead to both "haughty" and "gallant"? What does this reveal about the complex social role of knights?

Usage

Write a short paragraph about a funeral, a political scandal, or a financial matter — using at least four words from this session: perhaps obsequies, decadence, pecuniary, condolence, subsequent, or cavalier.

Distinction

What is the difference between doleful and dolorous? Between cadaverous and a cadaver? Between decadent and decadence? Try to use each word in a sentence that illustrates the distinction.

Non Sequitur

The phrase non sequitur — "it does not follow" — comes from our root sequor. Write a brief dialogue in which one speaker keeps making non sequiturs. Can the other speaker use condolence, decadence, or cavalier in response?
