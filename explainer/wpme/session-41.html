1. People Are the Craziest Animals

bovis (ox/cow) + -ine = bovine

Bovine — placid like a cow, stolid, patient, unexcitable. To call someone bovine implies mild contempt; considerably stronger than phlegmatic. A bovine person eats, grows, and lives but seems to lack strong feelings.


The suffix -ine = like, similar to, characteristic of forms all these animal adjectives from Latin:

leonine (LEE′-ə-nīn′)
leo → lion-like
canine (KAY′-nīn′)
canis → dog-like
feline (FEE′-līn′)
felis → cat-like
porcine (PAWR′-sīn′)
porcus → pig-like
vulpine (VUL′-pīn′)
vulpus → fox-like
ursine (UR′-sīn′)
ursus → bear-like
lupine (LOO′-pīn)
lupus → wolf-like
equine (EE′-kwīn′)
equus → horse-like
piscine (PIS′-ĭn′)
piscis → fish-like

Fun facts: pork derives from porcus · Ursa Major/Minor are "the bears" · lupus (disease) eats flesh like a wolf · Canary Islands were named for canis (wild dogs lived there!) · piscis → Pisces (zodiac)

2. You Can't Go Home Again

nostos (a return) + algos (pain) = nostalgia

Nostalgia — the emotional pain and longing that arise when some external stimulus floods your mind with scenes from an earlier, pleasanter day. Your conscious memory stores the pleasant experiences; unpleasant ones sink into the unconscious. When you're lonely or unhappy you begin to relive those stored pleasures — that longing is nostalgia.

Adjective: nostalgic (nos-TAL′-jik) — "motion pictures that are nostalgic of the fifties."

algos also appears in: neuralgia (nerve-pain) · cardialgia (heart-pain)

3. Soundings

kakos (bad, harsh, ugly) + phone (sound) = cacophony

Cacophony — the only word that exactly describes unmusical, grating, ear-offending noise. Adjective: cacophonous (kə-KOF′-ə-nəs).

phone (sound) appears in:

  • telephone — etymologically "sound from afar"
  • euphony — pleasant sound (eu- = good)
  • phonograph — etymologically "writer of sound"
  • saxophone — a musical instrument (the sound) invented by Adolphe Sax
  • xylophone — "sounds through wood" (Greek xylon = wood)
  • phonetics (fə-NET′-iks) — science of the sounds of language; adj. phonetic (fə-NET′-ik); expert: phonetician (fō′-nə-TISH′-ən)
  • phonics — science of sound; also the method of teaching reading by drilling letter sounds
4. The Flesh and All

carnis (flesh) + voro (to devour)

Carnivorous — an animal whose main diet is meat; carnivore (KAHR′-nə-vawr′).

voro (to devour) family:

  • herbivorous (hur-BIV′-ər-əs) — subsisting on plants; Latin herba = herb. Animal: herbivore (HUR′-bə-vawr′)
  • omnivorous (om-NIV′-ər-əs) — eating everything. Humans and rats are omnivores; an omnivorous reader devours all kinds of reading matter
  • voracious (vaw-RAY′-shəs) — devouring; greedy, gluttonous. Noun forms: voracity / voraciousness
5. "Allness"

omnis (all)

  • omnipotent (om-NIP′-ə-tənt) — all-powerful. omnis + potens (powerful). Noun: omnipotence. Related: impotent (powerless) · potent · potentate · potential
  • omniscient (om-NISH′-ənt) — all-knowing; infinitely wise. omnis + sciens (knowing). Noun: omniscience
  • omnipresent (om′-nə-PREZ′-ənt) — present everywhere at once. Synonym: ubiquitous (yoo-BIK′-wə-təs) from Latin ubique (everywhere). Nouns: ubiquity / ubiquitousness
  • omnibus (OM′-nə-bəs) — etymologically "for all." Shortened to bus; a Galsworthy omnibus = all his works; an omnibus bill = all remaining provisions
6. More Flesh

carnis (flesh) — extended family

  • carnelian (kahr-NEEL′-yən) — a reddish color, the color of red flesh
  • carnival (KAHR′-nə-vəl) — originally pre-Lent merrymaking; Carne vale! = "Oh flesh, farewell!" (Latin vale = farewell, goodbye). Today: any riotous outdoor festivities
  • carnal (KAHR′-nəl) — pleasures or appetites of the flesh; sensual, lecherous. Noun: carnality (kahr-NAL′-ə-tee)
  • carnage (KAHR′-nəj) — great destruction of life (human flesh), as in war
  • reincarnation (ree′-in-kahr-NAY′-shən) — rebirth; soul persists and reappears in a new body. Verb: reincarnate (ree′-in-KAHR′-nayt)
  • incarnate (in-KAHR′-nət) — in the flesh; "the devil incarnate." Verb incarnate (in-KAHR′-nayt) = to embody, give bodily form to
7. Dark Secrets

clam (secretly) → clandestine

Clandestine comes from Latin clam (secretly) and implies secrecy or concealment in working out a dangerous or illegal plan.

Close synonym: surreptitious (sur′-əp-TISH′-əs) — stealthy, sneaky, furtive, generally because of fear of detection. Noun: surreptitiousness.

Distinction: both words apply to meetings or arrangements; but usually only clandestine plans and only surreptitious movements or actions.
