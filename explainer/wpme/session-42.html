Session 42

Ten verbs ending in -ate — from exhaustion to indecision

← Session 41 Word Power Made Easy Session 43 →

Words are symbols of ideas. In this session each of ten vivid scenarios reveals a precise verb — all ending in -ate — that names exactly what is going on. Read each situation, then discover the word.

Idea 1
complete exhaustion

You stayed up all night — not playing poker, not writing, not in stimulating conversation, but in helpless, anguished vigil beside someone gravely ill. By dawn you are utterly drained: mentally, emotionally, physically. Every nerve feels ripped out.

enervate EN′-ər-vayt′
Idea 2
tongue-lashing

Pulled over for speeding at 70 mph, you feel guilty and irritated. The officer is polite — which makes it worse. You explode: lashing out verbally, questioning his honesty, his ancestry, his entire existence. The ticket arrives anyway.

castigate KAS′-tə-gayt′
Idea 3
altruistic

Phyllis always puts others first. Her children eat well while she goes without. She wears a seven-year-old coat so her daughters look smart. She perpetually says "No!" to her own desires, her own pleasures, her own needs.

self-abnegate self-AB′-nə-gayt′
Idea 4
repetition

You've delivered a long, complicated lecture. To lock the key ideas in your students' minds, you now restate the main points briefly — step by step, a concise summary, omitting all extraneous detail. You go through the headings again.

recapitulate ree′-kə-PICH′-ə-layt′
Idea 5
no joie de vivre

Gloomy Monday morning: same debts, same hundred dollars in the bank, same tuna-fish sandwich, same dreary ride home. Intellectually no progress — scarcely half a dozen books read in five years, no new exciting friends, no startling thoughts. No change. Nothing.

vegetate VEJ′-ə-tayt′
Idea 6
pretense

Your neighbor pops in uninvited to share her endless troubles with therapist, hairdresser, husband, children. You have a desk piled high with work — yet you dare not offend her, so you nod wisely, feigning rapt attention you do not remotely feel.

simulate SIM′-yə-layt′
Idea 7
slight hint, no more

Your editor makes statements that might — or might not — mean the company will promote your book heavily: "If we put some real money behind this, we might sell a few copies…" No clear promises. Only the slight and oblique mention of possibilities.

intimate IN′-tə-mayt′
Idea 8
helpful

Aspirin doesn't cure diseases — but this popular and inexpensive drug is universally used to lighten and relieve various unpleasant symptoms: aches and pains, fever, inflammations. It makes things easier to bear, if only temporarily.

alleviate ə-LEE′-vee-ayt′
Idea 9
when the bell tolls

A friend has suffered a bereavement. You cannot undo the loss — but you sit with them, sharing their grief as your own. "No man is an island… any man's death diminishes me." Your sympathy is itself alleviation enough.

commiserate kə-MIZ′-ə-rayt
Idea 10
when two men propose

John or George? You decide on John — then switch to George — then back to John. The pendulum swings. You cannot make up your mind and stick to it. Which is it, which is it? First one, then the other, then the first again…

vacillate VAS′-ə-layt

These ten verbs are tools for precise thought. The prompts below invite you to explore them through reflection, analogy, and real-life application. Write freely — no wrong answers, only deeper understanding.

Distinction

A doctor says a treatment will alleviate but not cure a patient's condition. What exactly does this mean? How is alleviating different from healing? Can you think of three situations in daily life where you alleviate — but cannot fix — a problem?

Etymology

Enervate means to rip the nerves out — yet energy (from en- + ergon) means to put work in. They look similar but are nearly opposite. Can you think of other word pairs that look like antonyms but are actually unrelated in origin? What does this tell you about the danger of judging words by appearance?

Real-World

Castigate is stronger than scold, criticize, rebuke, or reprimand — closer to "tongue-lash" or "rail at." In your experience, when does criticism cross into castigation? What makes the difference — tone, content, intent, audience?

Ethics

Phyllis is the archetype of self-abnegation. The book describes her admiringly — but is self-abnegation always a virtue? Can it become self-harm? Where is the line between admirable sacrifice and unhealthy self-erasure?

Logic

Recapitulate comes from capitulum (little head, chapter heading). To recapitulate is to "go through the headings again." But capitulate — from the same root — means to surrender. How did arranging headings come to mean giving up? Trace the logic of this semantic evolution.

Nuance

Vacillate describes a pendulum-like indecision. But is all indecision vacillation? What distinguishes wise deliberation from pathological vacillation? Name a famous historical figure who vacillated disastrously, and one who deliberated wisely.

Scenario

You are a politician who must intimate (without stating directly) that your party may raise taxes. Write two or three sentences that intimate this without committing to it — the kind of oblique language the book describes.

Synergism

The book warns that alcohol and barbiturates work synergistically — each multiplies the other's effect dangerously. Can you think of two positive things (habits, ideas, skills, people) that also work synergistically — where the combined effect is far greater than either alone?
