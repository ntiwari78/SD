Origins and Related Words — the roots behind the ten -ate verbs

← Session 42 Word Power Made Easy Session 44 →

Where do the ten verbs of Session 42 come from? Here we trace their Latin and Greek roots, discover the related words that spring from the same sources, and deepen understanding through etymology.

1. More Than Fatigue

When you are enervated, you feel as if your nerves have been ripped out — or so the etymology of the word indicates.

enervate ← e- (ex-), out + Latin nervus, nerve → to draw the nerves out of

Enervation (en′-ər-VAY′-shən) is not just fatigue, but complete devitalization — physical, emotional, mental — as if every ounce of the life force has been sapped out, as if the last particle of energy has been drained away.

Despite its similar appearance to the word energy, enervation is almost a direct antonym. Energy is derived from the Greek prefix en-, in, plus the root ergon, work. Erg is the term used in physics for a unit of work or energy.

⚠ Alcohol and barbiturates work synergistically — the effect of each is increased by the other if the two are taken together. Don't mix them.

2. Verbal Punishment

Castigate is derived from a Latin verb meaning to punish. In present-day usage, the verb generally refers to verbal punishment — usually harsh and severe.

castigate ← Latin castigare, to punish → to subject to severe verbal chastisement

It is somewhat synonymous with scold, criticize, rebuke, censure, reprimand, or berate — but much stronger than any of these. Closer synonyms are rail at, rant at, slash at, lash out at, or tongue-lash.

scoldrebukecensurereprimandberatecastigatetongue-lash

When candidates for office castigate their opponents, they do not mince words.

castigation

3. Saying "No!" to Oneself

Abnegate is derived from Latin ab-, away (as in absent), plus nego, to deny.

self-abnegate ← ab- (away) + nego (to deny) → to deny oneself; to say "No!" to what you want

Self-abnegation (ab′-nə-GAY′-shən) is self-denial. Nego itself is a contraction of Latin neg-, not, no, and aio, I say. To be self-abnegating is to say "No!" to what you want, as if some inner censor were at work whispering, "No, you can't have that, you can't do that, you don't deserve that, you're not good enough for that…"

4. Heads and Headings

Latin caput, capitis means head.

caput, capitis = head → captain (head of a group), capital (head city), decapitate (remove the head)

The captain is the head of any group; the capital is the "head city" of a state or nation; and to decapitate (dee-KAP′-ə-tayt′) is to chop off someone's head — a popular activity during the French Revolution after the guillotine was invented.

Latin capitulum is a little head, or, by extension, the heading or title of a chapter. So when you recapitulate, you go through the chapter headings again (re-), etymologically speaking — or you summarize and review the main points.

5. Mere Vegetables

Vegetable is from Latin vegeto, to live and grow — which is what vegetables do. But that is all they do. So to vegetate is, by implication, to do no more than stay alive: stuck in a rut, leading an inactive, unstimulating, emotionally and intellectually stagnant existence.

vegetate ← Latin vegeto, to live and grow → to do nothing but live and grow; to stagnate
vegetation
vej′-ə-TAY′-shən
any dull, passive, stagnant existence; also any plant life, as the thick vegetation of a jungle

The origins of words illuminate their meanings with a precision no definition alone can provide. Engage with the etymologies below to deepen your understanding of what it truly means to enervate, to capitulate, to negate.

Antonyms by Appearance

Enervate and energy look as if they should be related — and they nearly are, but in opposite directions. Enervation rips the nerves out; energy puts work in. Can you think of three other pairs of words that look like they should be related but are actually opposites or unconnected? What does this teach about judging words by their surface appearance?

Synergism in Life

The book warns of the dangerous synergism of alcohol and barbiturates — each amplifies the other's danger. But synergism can also be wonderful. Think of two things in your own life — habits, skills, relationships, ideas — that work synergistically. How does the combined effect exceed the sum of the parts?

Semantic Evolution

Capitulate literally means "to arrange headings" — to set out the terms or chapters of an agreement. But it evolved to mean "to surrender." Trace the logic: how does organizing conditions of negotiation become synonymous with giving in? Is there a moment in real negotiations where this transition happens?

The Power of "No"

The root nego (to deny) gives us negate, negation, negative, and self-abnegate. These words carry a heavy psychological weight — denial, self-erasure, destruction. Yet "No" can also be enormously powerful and liberating. When is saying "No!" an act of strength rather than self-abnegation?

Etymology as Memory

The book shows that knowing caput (head) gives you captain, capital, decapitate, capitulate, recapitulate, and more. Using this method, how many English words can you derive just from knowing the Latin root manus (hand)? Try to list at least six.

Vegetation as Metaphor

The verb vegetate comes from vegeto (to live and grow) — but its modern meaning is entirely pejorative: to stagnate, to do nothing more than merely exist. What does it say about human values that "merely living and growing" became a term of reproach? Are vegetables being unfairly maligned?
