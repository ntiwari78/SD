The PAR Family (from disparage)

PAR
Latin: equal
"Disparage" comes from Old French desparager — to marry below one's class — which came from Latin PAR (equal). To disparage someone is to put them on an unequal level, to treat them as inferior. When you disparage someone's work, you're suggesting it's not on equal footing with better work.

The AEQUUS+VOX Family (from equivocate)

AEQUUS + VOX
Latin: equal + voice/word
"Equivocate" combines AEQUUS (equal) and VOX/VOCIS (voice, word). To equivocate means to use equal (i.e., two-sided) voices — to speak with deliberate ambiguity, giving yourself room to be understood in more than one way. It's purposeful vagueness designed to avoid commitment to a single clear position.

The Words

parity
PAIR'-ə-tee
[noun]
Equality; the state of being equal; equivalent value or status.
From: Latin PAR (equal) + -ity (noun suffix)
"The union demanded wage parity with workers in other divisions."
Related words: disparity, disparate, disparage, peer
disparity
dis-PAIR'-ə-tee
[noun]
Inequality; great difference; a notable lack of similarity or equality.
From: dis- (apart, away) + PAR (equal) + -ity (noun suffix)
"The disparity in their incomes made their friendship awkward at times."
disparate
DIS'-pə-rət
[adjective]
Essentially or completely different in kind; not comparable; widely dissimilar in character or quality.
From: dis- + PAR (equal) + -ate (adjective suffix)
"Their disparate personalities made collaboration surprisingly difficult."
disparagement
dis-PAIR'-əj-mənt
[noun]
The act of disparaging; belittlement; the act of speaking slightingly of or treating as inferior.
From: disparage + -ment (noun suffix)
"His constant disparagement of her work slowly destroyed her confidence."
disparaging
dis-PAIR'-əj-ing
[adjective]
Belittling; showing or expressing a low opinion of something or someone; slighting.
From: disparage + -ing (present participle)
"Her disparaging remarks about his cooking hurt his feelings more than she realized."
peer
PEER
[noun]
One's equal in age, rank, position, or ability; a member of the same group, class, or standing; a British nobleman.
From: Latin PAR (equal) → Old French per
"She was judged by a jury of her peers."
The phrase "peer pressure" derives from this root — pressure from equals to conform. Note: To peer (verb) means to look closely; this is a different origin (from Old English).
equivocate
ee-KWIV'-ə-kayt'
[verb]
To use deliberately vague or ambiguous language; to hedge; to avoid committing to a clear, direct statement or position.
From: AEQUUS (equal) + VOX (voice) + -ate (verb suffix) — to use "equal voices" or two-sided meanings
"The politician equivocated when asked for a direct answer on new taxes."
equivocation
ee-kwiv'-ə-KAY'-shən
[noun]
The act of equivocating; a statement that is deliberately vague or open to contrary interpretations; intentional ambiguity.
From: equivocate + -ion (noun suffix)
"His equivocation on the issue made voters deeply suspicious."
equivocal
ee-KWIV'-ə-kəl
[adjective]
Deliberately vague or uncertain; open to more than one interpretation; purposely ambiguous; having two possible meanings.
From: AEQUUS (equal) + VOX (voice) + -al (adjective suffix)
"Her equivocal response to the marriage proposal left him uncertain of her true feelings."
unequivocal
un'-ee-KWIV'-ə-kəl
[adjective]
Clear and direct; admitting of only one interpretation; unmistakable; leaving no doubt; absolute and unambiguous.
From: un- (not) + equivocal
"The general gave an unequivocal order to advance immediately."
ambiguous
am-BIG'-yoo-əs
[adjective]
Open to more than one interpretation; unclear in meaning; uncertain; having a double meaning (often accidentally).
From: AMBI- (both) + AGERE (to drive) + -ous (adjective suffix) — driven in both directions
"The contract contained ambiguous language that later led to disputes."
Key distinction: Ambiguous means accidentally vague or unclear. Equivocal means purposely, deliberately vague to mislead.
ambiguity
am'-bə-GYOO'-ə-tee
[noun]
The quality of being open to misinterpretation or having multiple meanings; a statement or phrase with unclear meaning.
From: ambiguous + -ity (noun suffix)
"Legal documents are carefully written to avoid ambiguity."
double entendre
DOOB'-lə-ahn-TAHN'-drə
[noun phrase]
A statement or word with two meanings, one of which is risqué, indelicate, or of possible sexual connotation; a phrase with a double meaning.
From: French "double hearing" or "double understanding"
"The comedian's act was full of clever double entendres that made the audience laugh."
The deliberate use of double entendres can shock puritanical or prudish people. These are often used in comedy and wit.
Important Distinction: Ambiguous vs. Equivocal
Ambiguous
Accidentally or unintentionally vague, unclear, or open to misinterpretation. The vagueness is unplanned and unclear. Example: "The contract's language was ambiguous, leading to lawsuits."
Equivocal
Purposely, deliberately, and intentionally vague — designed to confuse, mislead, or allow room to interpret in multiple ways. The speaker chooses ambiguous language on purpose. Example: "The politician's equivocal response showed he was avoiding the question."

The Equivocator's Art

Parity and Fairness

Disparate Worlds

Peer Pressure

The word "peer" comes from PAR (equal). How does peer pressure derive its power from equality? Are we more influenced by our peers or by those above us in status?

Ambiguous vs. Equivocal

Ambiguous means accidentally unclear; equivocal means purposely unclear. Can you think of real examples of each from everyday life — in politics, art, or conversation?

The Double Entendre

Humor often relies on double entendres. Why do humans find double meanings funny? What does this say about language and the mind?
