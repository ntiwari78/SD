The AEQUUS Extended Family

AEQUUS

Latin: "equal"

⚠️ Don't Confuse These Roots!

AEQUUS = equal

Gives us: equity, equinox, equivocate, equanimity, equilibrium, equilateral

EQUUS = horse

Gives us: equestrian, equestrienne, equine

equestrian

(ə-KWES'-tree-ən)

Definition: a person on horseback; pertaining to horseback riding or horseback riders

From: Latin EQUUS (horse), NOT from AEQUUS (equal)!

"The equestrian statue of General Grant stands in the park."

equestrienne

(ə-kwes'-tree-EN')

Definition: a woman on horseback; a female equestrian

From: Latin EQUUS (horse), feminine form

"The equestrienne took first place in the jumping competition."

equine

(EE'-kwīn)

Definition: of, pertaining to, or like a horse

From: EQUUS (horse) + -ine (like, descriptive of)

"The veterinarian specialized in equine medicine."

The VOX/VOCIS Family

VOX, VOCIS

⚠️ CRITICAL DISTINCTION: inequity ≠ iniquity

inequity (in-EK'-wə-tee) = unfairness, injustice
iniquity (in-IK'-wə-tee) = sin, wickedness, immorality

These look similar but mean very different things. Inequity is about fairness; iniquity is about morality.

⚠️ CRITICAL DISTINCTION: AEQUUS ≠ EQUUS

AEQUUS = equal → equity, equilibrium, equinox, equanimity
EQUUS = horse → equestrian, equestrienne, equine

Don't let the similar spelling fool you—these roots are completely different!

The AEQUUS Family

equity

(EK'-wə-tee)

noun

Definition: justice; fairness; equal and fair treatment; also, the financial value of property beyond what is owed on it
From: AEQUUS → equity
"Equity in the justice system means all people are treated fairly regardless of status."
Word family: equitable, inequity, inequitable

equitable

(EK'-wə-tə-bəl)

adjective

Definition: fair; just; characterized by equity
"The mediator proposed an equitable division of the estate."

inequity

(in-EK'-wə-tee)

noun

Definition: unfairness; injustice; lack of equity
From: in- (not) + equity
"The inequity of the tax system angered many citizens."

inequitable

(in-EK'-wə-tə-bəl)

adjective

Definition: unfair; unjust
"The inequitable distribution of resources led to protests."

iniquity

(in-IK'-wə-tee)

noun

Definition: sin; vice; wickedness; an egregiously immoral act
From: AEQUUS compressed to -iqu- + in- (not)
"The preacher railed against the iniquities of the modern world."
Note: INEQUITY = unfairness; INIQUITY = wickedness/sin — don't confuse them!

iniquitous

(in-IK'-wə-təs)

adjective

Definition: sinful; wicked; grossly immoral
"The iniquitous scheme defrauded thousands of innocent people."

equinox

(EE'-kwə-noks')

noun

Definition: either of two times of year (around March 21 and September 23) when day and night are of equal length
From: AEQUUS (equal) + NOX, NOCTIS (night)
"The vernal equinox marks the official beginning of spring."
Word family: equinoctial (adjective)

equinoctial

(ee'-kwə-NOK'-shəl)

adjective

Definition: of or pertaining to an equinox; occurring at the equinox
"Equinoctial storms often occur near the time of the vernal equinox."

nocturnal

(nok-TURN'-əl)

adjective

Definition: of or pertaining to night; active or flourishing at night
From: NOX, NOCTIS (night)
"Owls are nocturnal hunters."

nocturne

(NOK'-turn)

noun

Definition: a piece of music inspired by or evocative of night; a painting of a nighttime scene
"Chopin's nocturnes are among the most beautiful piano pieces ever written."

equanimity

(ee'-kwə-NIM'-ə-tee)

noun

Definition: evenness of temper; composure; calmness, especially under stress or trial
From: AEQUUS (equal) + ANIMUS (mind)
"She faced the diagnosis with remarkable equanimity."

equability

(ee'-kwə-BIL'-ə-tee)

noun

Definition: evenness of temperament; close synonym of equanimity
Adjective: equable (EE'-kwə-bəl) — calm; serene; unruffled; even-tempered
"His equability made him a calming presence in any crisis."

equilibrium

(ee'-kwə-LIB'-ree-əm)

noun

Definition: physical balance; a state of balance between opposing forces or influences
From: AEQUUS (equal) + LIBRA (balance, weight, pound)
"After the accident, she struggled to regain her equilibrium."
Note: LIBRA also gives us: Libra (the scales in the zodiac), pound sterling (£), the unit of weight

equilibrist

(ə-KWIL'-ə-brist)

noun

Definition: a tightrope walker; one who performs feats of balance
From: equilibrium + -ist (person who)
"The equilibrist crossed the chasm on a wire, seemingly defying gravity."

equilateral

(ee'-kwə-LAT'-ər-əl)

adjective

Definition: having all sides equal (said of a geometric figure)
From: AEQUUS (equal) + LATUS, LATERIS (side)
"An equilateral triangle has three equal sides and three equal angles."

equestrian

(ə-KWES'-tree-ən)

noun/adjective

Definition: a person on horseback (noun); of or pertaining to horseback riding (adjective)
From: Latin EQUUS (horse) — NOT from AEQUUS (equal)!
"The equestrian statue of General Grant stands in the park."

equestrienne

(ə-kwes'-tree-EN')

noun

Definition: a woman who rides horseback; a female equestrian
From: EQUUS (horse) — feminine form
"The equestrienne took first place in the jumping competition."

equine

(EE'-kwīn)

adjective

Definition: of, pertaining to, or like a horse
From: EQUUS (horse) + -ine (like, descriptive of)
"The veterinarian specialized in equine medicine."
The VOX/VOCIS Family

vociferous

(vō-SIF'-ər-əs)

adjective

Definition: loud; noisy; clamorous; making a great outcry
From: VOX, VOCIS (voice) + FERO (to bear/carry) + -ous → carrying one's voice forcefully
"The crowd was vociferous in its demands for justice."

vocal

(VŌ'-kəl)

adjective

Definition: of or pertaining to the voice; freely or loudly expressing by voice
From: VOX, VOCIS (voice) + -al
"She was vocal in her opposition to the new policy."

vocalize

(VŌ'-kə-līz')

verb

Definition: to give voice to; to express aloud; to sing
From: vocal + -ize
"The singer vocalized scales every morning to warm up."

vocalist

(VŌ'-kə-list)

noun

Definition: a singer
From: vocal + -ist (person who)
"She was the lead vocalist in a jazz quartet."

Magnavox

(MAG'-nə-voks)

noun (brand name)

Definition: a trade name for electronic audio equipment; literally "great voice"
From: MAGNUS (large, great) + VOX (voice)
"Magnavox was a pioneer in the television and electronics industry."

Deepen Your Understanding

Open-ended questions for reflection. No right or wrong answers—just think deeply.

1. Equity vs. Equality

"Equity means fairness, while equality means sameness. Are they always the same thing? Can you think of a situation where equal treatment would actually be inequitable?"

2. The Nature of Iniquity

"Iniquity is derived from the same root as equity — both come from AEQUUS (equal). Why might the ancients have defined wickedness as a kind of inequality? What does this say about their moral worldview?"

3. Equanimity Under Pressure

"Stoic philosophers prized equanimity above almost all other virtues. Is emotional calmness always a virtue? Could suppressing excitement or grief ever be harmful?"

4. The Equinox and Time

"The equinox — from AEQUUS (equal) + NOX (night) — marks the moment of perfect balance between day and night. Many cultures celebrate this moment. Why might balance in nature feel spiritually significant to humans?"

5. Vociferous vs. Vocal

"Being vocal means freely expressing yourself; being vociferous means being loud and clamorous. When does healthy self-expression become problematic noise? Is there a civic duty to be vocal?"

6. EQUUS vs. AEQUUS — Etymology Traps

"Equestrian (horse-riding) and equilibrium (balance) look like they share a root, but they don't — EQUUS (horse) vs. AEQUUS (equal). How does knowing etymology help avoid these confusions? Can you think of other English words that look related but aren't?"

7. The Equilibrist

"An equilibrist walks a tightrope — maintaining perfect physical balance. Life often requires psychological equilibrium too. What are the 'opposing forces' in your life that you must balance? How do you maintain your equilibrium?"

Your reflections are for personal growth. They are not graded or submitted.
