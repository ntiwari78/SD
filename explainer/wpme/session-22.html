SCRIBO, SCRIPTUS (Latin) = "to write"

PRO- (before, publicly) + SCRIPTUS → proscription: to write someone's name on a public list marking them for punishment; to forbid

SCRIBO / SCRIPTUS
├─ proscribe → proscription → proscriptive
├─ prescribe → prescription → prescriptive (pre- = before)
├─ describe → description → descriptive (de- = down)
├─ subscribe → subscription → subscriptive (sub- = under)
├─ inscribe → inscription → inscriptive (in- = in/into)
├─ postscript (post = after)
├─ manuscript (manus = hand + scriptus)
├─ the Scriptures (holy writings)
└─ scribe, scribble, script, typescript
Verb Noun Adjective
prescribe prescription prescriptive
subscribe subscription subscriptive
describe description descriptive
inscribe inscription inscriptive
proscribe proscription proscriptive

VIA (Latin) = "road"

OB- (in the way of) + VIA → obvious: "right in the middle of the road, impossible to miss"

OB- + VIA + -ATE → obviate: to remove obstacles from the road; to make unnecessary

VIA (road)
├─ obvious (ob- + via = in the road) → easily seen; not hidden
├─ obviate (ob- + via + -ate) → to prevent; to make unnecessary → obviation
├─ trivial (tri- = three + via) → where three roads meet = commonplace
└─ via = the road itself (Via Appia)

MILITIS (Latin) = "soldier / fighting man"

militate: always used with "against" — works to one's disadvantage (fights against you)

IMPORTANT: militate takes NO personal subject — habits, tendencies, actions militate, not people

MILITIS
├─ militate (against)
├─ militant (MIL'-ə-tənt)
├─ militancy (MIL'-ə-tən-see)
├─ military
└─ militia

MALUS (Latin) = "bad, evil"

The most prolific source of "evil" words in English

MALUS (bad, evil)
├─ malign (verb: to slander; adj: bad/harmful)
│ ├─ malignant
│ └─ malignancy, malignity
├─ malus + DICO/DICTUS (say) → malediction (a curse)
│ └─ maledictory
├─ malus + VOLO (wish) → malevolent (wishing evil)
│ └─ malevolence
├─ malus + FACIO (do/make) → maleficent (doing harm)
│ └─ maleficence
├─ malus + FACIO → malefactor (evildoer)
│ └─ malefaction (evil deed)
├─ French mal + adroit → maladroit (clumsy)
│ └─ maladroitness
├─ French mal + aise (ease) → malaise (bad ease)
└─ malicious, malice, malady, maladjusted, malcontent, malpractice, malnutrition

proscription

(prō-SKRIP'-shən) [noun]
Definition: a formal prohibition; formal forbidding of something injurious
From: pro- (publicly/before) + scribo/scriptus (to write) + -ion
Example: "The church issued a proscription against the banned texts."

proscriptive

(prō-SKRIP'-tiv) [adjective]
Definition: relating or pertaining to prohibitions
Example: "The proscriptive regulations limited what could be published."

obviation

(ob'-vee-AY'-shən) [noun]
Definition: the fact or act of making unnecessary; prevention by taking effective measures
From: ob- (in the way) + via (road) + -ate + -ion
Example: "Regular maintenance allows for the obviation of costly repairs."

trivial

(TRIV'-ee-əl) [adjective]
Definition: of no great consequence; unimportant; insignificant
From: tri- (three) + via (road) → where three roads meet = commonplace public place
Example: "Don't let trivial disagreements damage important friendships."

trivialities

(triv'-ee-AL'-ə-teez) [noun, plural]
Definition: unimportant things; trifles
Example: "She spent the morning on trivialities instead of important work."

trivia

(TRIV'-ee-ə) [noun, plural]
Definition: insignificant trifles; things of little importance
Example: "The quiz show focused on pop culture trivia."

militant

(MIL'-ə-tənt) [adjective/noun]
Definition: aggressive; combative; fighting for a cause with great determination
From: militis (soldier) + -ant
Example: "The militant reformer fought tirelessly for social change."

militancy

(MIL'-ə-tən-see) [noun]
Definition: aggressiveness; the quality of being combative or fighting for a cause
Example: "The union's militancy led to significant wage increases."

malign

(mə-LĪN') [verb/adjective]
Definition (verb): to speak evil about; to defame; to slander
Definition (adj): bad; harmful; evil; hateful
From: Latin malus (bad, evil)
Example (verb): "She felt it was wrong to malign someone who couldn't defend himself."
Example (adj): "The malign influence of his addiction destroyed his career."

malignant

(mə-LIG'-nənt) [adjective]
Definition: showing deep hatred or ill-will; cancerous (medical); evil; harmful
Example: "The biopsy revealed a malignant tumor."
Word family: malignancy (noun), malignity (noun)

malediction

(mal'-ə-DIK'-shən) [noun]
Definition: a curse; an evil saying
From: malus (bad) + dico/dictus (to say) = "evil saying"
Example: "The witch hurled a malediction at her enemies."
Adjective: maledictory

malevolent

(mə-LEV'-ə-lənt) [adjective]
Definition: wishing evil or harm to others; bearing ill-will
From: malus (bad) + volo (to wish) = "wishing bad/evil"
Example: "His malevolent glance made everyone in the room uncomfortable."
Noun: malevolence (mə-LEV'-ə-ləns)

maleficent

(mə-LEF'-ə-sənt) [adjective]
Definition: doing harm or evil; causing hurt
From: malus (bad) + facio (to do/make) = "doing bad/evil"
Example: "The villain's maleficent schemes threatened the entire kingdom."
Noun: maleficence (mə-LEF'-ə-səns)

malefactor

(MAL'-ə-fak'-tər) [noun]
Definition: a wrongdoer; an evildoer; a criminal
From: malus (bad) + factor (one who does/makes)
Example: "The malefactor was caught and brought to justice."
Noun: malefaction (mal'-ə-FAK'-shən) = an evil deed; a crime

maladroit

(mal'-ə-DROYT') [adjective]
Definition: clumsy; bungling; awkward; unskillful
From: French mal (bad) + adroit (skillful) = having a "bad right hand"
Example: "His maladroit handling of the situation made things worse."
Noun: maladroitness (mal'-ə-DROYT'-nəs)

malaise

(mə-LAYZ') [noun]
Definition: a vague feeling of bodily discomfort; an indefinite sense of something being wrong
From: French mal (bad) + aise (ease) = "bad ease"
Example: "A general malaise settled over the workforce after the layoffs."

Deepen Your Understanding

Reflect on these questions to develop deeper insight into the words and concepts.

1

The Written Prohibition

"Proscription in ancient Rome meant publicly writing someone's name on a list marking them for punishment. Modern 'cancel culture' has been compared to this practice. Is public shaming through social media a form of proscription? What are the similarities and differences?"

2

The Road Metaphor

"Via, meaning road, gave us both obvious ('in the middle of the road') and trivial ('where three roads meet = commonplace'). What other road metaphors shape our thinking about ideas? Why do we use journey and path metaphors so frequently in English?"

3

Militant vs. Aggressive

"Militant implies fighting for a cause with conviction. When does legitimate advocacy become dangerous militancy? Where is the line between passionate reform and destructive extremism?"

4

The MALUS Family

"The Latin root malus (bad, evil) has given English dozens of words. Consider: malice, malignant, malevolent, malefactor, malaise. Why are there so many more 'bad' words derived from malus than 'good' words from bonus? What does this say about human language and psychology?"

5

Maladroit in Leadership

"Maladroit literally means 'bad right hand' — unskillful. What forms of maladroitness are most damaging in leadership? Can someone be intellectually brilliant but socially or politically maladroit?"

6

The Vague Malaise

"Malaise describes a vague, indefinite feeling of discomfort — not quite illness, not quite wellness. Sociologists speak of a 'cultural malaise' when societies lose direction. Can you think of a time when you personally experienced malaise? What caused it and what resolved it?"
