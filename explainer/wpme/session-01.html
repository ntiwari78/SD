The Flashlight Analogy

Understanding all ten personality types without any jargon — just a single physical image.

Feynman Mental Model · Zero Jargon

Imagine everyone carries an invisible flashlight. It emits the energy of their attention, care, and concern. The most fundamental question about any person is: where do they point it?

Some people point their beam inward — at the cave of their own mind. It is richly lit inside: thoughts, reflections, inner worlds. They are quiet, self-contained, recharged by solitude. These are introverts.

Others swing the beam outward — onto parties, stages, conversations, crowds. External life is their electricity. These are extroverts. Most of us can swivel the beam in both directions as circumstances demand — we are ambiverts.

Now ask: what are they lighting up with that beam? An egoist lights only their own interests — self-advancement is the one true north. An egotist points the flashlight at their own accomplishments and narrates everything aloud, loudly. The altruist, remarkably, inverts the beam entirely — lighting up other people's lives, often at personal cost.

Then there are those who refuse to share their light with whole categories of the world. The misanthrope has switched off the beam for people in general — humanity is unworthy. The misogynist has turned it off for women specifically. The misogamist has sealed it away from the institution of marriage — the ties that bind are too binding.

Finally, the ascetic runs their flashlight on the barest minimum of fuel — by deliberate, principled choice. Not hatred, not selfishness, but discipline: self-denial, austerity, contemplation. The monk's path.

Ten distinct people. Ten very different answers to: where do I point my light, and who do I share it with?
