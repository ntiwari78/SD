Ten Types of Talkers

Adjectives That Describe How People Speak

What kinds of people might you find yourself in conversation with? In this chapter we examine ten types, discovering the adjective that aptly describes each one.
What adjective describes people who...
  • are disinclined to conversation?
  • are brief and to the point in their speech?
  • are blocked or incoherent in their speech?
  • show by their speech that they are trite and unimaginative?
  • use more words than necessary?
  • are forcefully compelling and logical in their speech?
  • talk rapidly and fluently?
  • are noisy and clamorous?
  • are talkative?
1. SAYING LITTLE
There are some people who just don't like to talk. It's not that they prefer to listen. Good listeners hold up their end of the conversation delightfully—with empathetic smiles, giggles, and encouraging nods. These people like neither to talk nor to listen—they act as if conversation is a bore, even a painful waste of time. Try to engage them, and the best you may expect is a vacant stare, a noncommittal grunt, or an impatient silence. Finally, in frustration, you give up, thinking: "Are they self-conscious? Do they hate people? Do they hate me?"
TACITURN
(TAS'-ə-turn)
Disinclined to conversation; habitually silent; uncommunicative
2. SAYING LITTLE—MEANING MUCH
There is a well-known anecdote about Calvin Coolidge, who, when he was President, was often called "Silent Cal." A young newspaperwoman was sitting next to him at a banquet, and turned to him mischievously: "Mr. Coolidge," she said, "I have a bet with my editor that I can get you to say more than two words to me this evening." "You lose," Coolidge rejoined simply.
LACONIC
(lə-KON'-ik)
Using few words packed with meaning; brief and pithy; economical in expression
3. WHEN THE WORDS WON'T COME
Under the pressure of some strong emotion—fear, rage, anger—people may find it difficult, or even impossible, to utter words, to get their feelings unjumbled and untangled enough to form understandable sentences. They undoubtedly have a lot they want to say, but the best they can do is sputter!
INARTICULATE
(in'-ahr-TIK'-yə-lət)
Unable to speak clearly or expressively; blocked or incoherent in speech
4. MUCH TALK, LITTLE SENSE
Miss Bates, a character in Emma, a novel by Jane Austen: "So obliging of you! No, we should not have heard, if it had not been for this particular circumstance, of her being able to come here so soon. My mother is so delighted! For she is to be three months with us at least. Three months, she says so, positively... The Campbells are going to Ireland. Mrs. Dixon has persuaded her father and mother to come over and see her directly... I was going to say, but, however, different countries, and so she wrote a very urgent letter to her mother, or her father, I declare I do not know which it was..."
GARRULOUS
(GAIR'-ə-ləs)
Excessively talkative about trivial matters; rambling; chattering
5. UNORIGINAL
Some people are completely lacking in originality and imagination—and their talk shows it. Everything they say is trite, hackneyed, commonplace, humorless—their speech patterns are full of clichés and stereotypes, their phraseology is without sparkle.
BANAL
(BAY'-nəl)
Hackneyed; trite; unoriginal; lacking freshness or originality; commonplace
6. WORDS, WORDS, WORDS!
They talk and talk and talk—it's not so much the quantity you object to as the repetitiousness. They phrase, rephrase, and re-rephrase their thoughts—using far more words than necessary, overwhelming you with words, drowning you with them, until your only thought is how to escape, or maybe how to die.
VERBOSE
(vər-BŌS')
Using more words than necessary; wordy; repetitious
7. WORDS IN QUICK SUCCESSION
They are rapid, fluent talkers, the words seeming to roll off their tongues with such ease and lack of effort, and sometimes with such copiousness, that you listen with amazement.
VOLUBLE
(VOL'-yə-bəl)
Talking rapidly and fluently; glib; flowing readily in speech
8. WORDS THAT CONVINCE
They express their ideas persuasively, forcefully, brilliantly, and in a way that calls for wholehearted assent and agreement from an intelligent listener.
COGENT
(KŌ'-jənt)
Forcefully convincing; compelling; logically persuasive
9. THE SOUND AND THE FURY
Their talk is loud, noisy, clamorous, vehement. What may be lacking in content is compensated for in force and loudness. (Note: Already learned — VOX + FERO = carrying one's voice loudly)
VOCIFEROUS
(vō-SIF'-ər-əs)
Loud; noisy; clamorous; making a great outcry
10. QUANTITY
They talk a lot—a whole lot. They may be voluble, vociferous, garrulous, verbose, but never inarticulate, taciturn, or laconic. No matter. It's the quantity and continuity that are most conspicuous. "Were you vaccinated with a phonograph needle?" is the question you are tempted to ask as you listen.
LOQUACIOUS
(lō-KWAY'-shəs)
Talkative; given to much talking

Taciturn
(TAS'-ə-turn)
[adjective]
Definition
Habitually silent; disinclined to conversation; uncommunicative by temperament. Implies a permanent disposition, not just temporary quiet.
Synonyms
Silent, reticent, reserved, uncommunicative — but taciturn adds a temperamental quality
Example
"The taciturn detective gave little away in his manner."
Related
Noun: taciturnity
Laconic
(lə-KON'-ik)
[adjective]
Definition
Using few words; brief and pithy; expressing much in little; economical in expression
Etymology
From Laconia (Sparta) — the Spartans were famous for terse, direct speech. When threatened: "If we enter your city, we will burn it to the ground." Sparta's reply: "If."
Example
"His laconic reply — 'No' — ended the discussion."
Inarticulate
(in'-ahr-TIK'-yə-lət)
[adjective]
Definition
Unable to speak clearly or coherently; unable to express oneself fluently; blocked in speech
Etymology
From in- (not) + articulate (to join/form words clearly)
Example
"Overcome with emotion, she became completely inarticulate."
Garrulous
(GAIR'-ə-ləs)
[adjective]
Definition
Excessively talkative, especially about trivial matters; rambling; chattering ceaselessly
Example
"The garrulous shopkeeper delayed us for twenty minutes with irrelevant stories."
Related
Noun: garrulity
Banal
(BAY'-nəl)
[adjective]
Definition
Hackneyed; trite; unoriginal; lacking freshness or vitality; commonplace; clichéd
Example
"His conversation was so banal that I struggled to stay awake."
Related
Noun: banality
Verbose
(vər-BŌS')
[adjective]
Definition
Using more words than necessary; wordy; repetitious in expression
Etymology
From verbum (Latin) = word + -ose (full of) → full of words
Example
"The verbose professor spent forty minutes saying what could have been said in five."
Related
Noun: verbosity (vər-BOS'-ə-tee)
Voluble
(VOL'-yə-bəl)
[adjective]
Definition
Talking rapidly and fluently; glib; having a ready flow of words
Etymology
From volvere (Latin) = to roll → words rolling off the tongue
Example
"The voluble salesman pitched enthusiastically for an hour without pausing."
Related
Noun: volubility (vol'-yə-BIL'-ə-tee)
Cogent
(KŌ'-jənt)
[adjective]
Definition
Powerfully convincing; logically compelling; forceful and clear
Etymology
From co- + agere (Latin) = to drive together → driving thoughts together logically
Example
"Her cogent argument dismantled every counterpoint he raised."
Related
Noun: cogency (KŌ'-jən-see)
Vociferous
(vō-SIF'-ər-əs)
[adjective]
Definition
Loud; noisy; clamorous; making a great outcry
Etymology
From VOX, VOCIS (voice) + FERO (to carry) + -ous
Example
"Vociferous protesters filled the streets outside the courthouse."
Note
Already studied in Session 21!
Loquacious
(lō-KWAY'-shəs)
[adjective]
Definition
Talkative; given to much talking; fond of conversation
Etymology
From loquor (Latin) = to speak + -ious
Example
"The loquacious tour guide narrated every detail of every building."
Related
Noun: loquacity (lō-KWAS'-ə-tee)

Deepen Your Understanding

These reflection questions invite you to think more deeply about the words and the concepts they represent. There are no "right" answers—only your own thoughtful engagement with language and human nature.

1. THE TACITURN AND THE LACONIC
Two Types of Quiet
Taciturn people dislike conversation; laconic people speak briefly but meaningfully. Both say little — but they are very different. Can you think of historical figures or people you know who exemplify each? Is either quality a virtue or a flaw?
2. INARTICULATE EMOTION
When Words Fail
Rage, grief, and awe can make even articulate people inarticulate. There is something that exceeds language. Does this mean emotion is more real than words? Or does inarticulateness represent a failure of self-mastery?
3. THE GARRULOUS AND THE VERBOSE
Two Types of Excess
Garrulous people ramble about trivial things; verbose people use too many words for any subject. Both are exhausting in different ways. Which do you find more tolerable? What drives each type of talker — insecurity, enthusiasm, habit?
4. BANAL SPEECH AS CULTURAL SYMPTOM
The Cliché Question
Banal speech — full of clichés and hackneyed phrases — may reflect a lack of original thought. Or it may be social lubricant: we use familiar phrases to signal belonging. When is a cliché useful, and when is it a sign of intellectual laziness?
5. THE COGENT ARGUMENT
Logic vs. Persuasion
A cogent argument is logically compelling. But humans are not purely rational — we are often convinced by emotion, authority, or familiarity rather than logic. Can an argument be both cogent and ineffective? What makes persuasion truly work?
6. LOQUACITY AS A GIFT OR A BURDEN
The Talker's Dilemma
Some of the world's greatest storytellers, comedians, and teachers are loquacious. Yet excessive talkativeness can dominate conversations and exclude others. Is there a difference between loquacity that enriches and loquacity that merely exhausts?
