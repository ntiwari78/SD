Chapter 16 · How to Talk About a Variety of Personal Characteristics (Sessions 45–46)

Session 45

Ideas — ten adjectives ending in -ous that paint portraits of character

← Session 44 Word Power Made Easy Session 46 →

The suffix -ous means "full of." In this session we meet ten adjectives that capture human personalities, appearances, and behaviors — words derived from Latin and French roots that once described armies, cattle, eyebrows, and the dead. Beneath each lies a vivid etymological story.

1. Compliance

Some people are so eager to please, so slavishly attentive, that they seem to follow you like a shadow — fawning, flattering, agreeing with everything you say. The Latin verb sequor means to follow, and someone who follows in this subservient way is called—

obsequious
obsequious (ob-SEEK′-wee-əs) ← Latin ob- (toward) + sequor (to follow) → excessively eager to follow or please; fawningly compliant
obsequies
OB′-sə-kweez
funeral rites — the ceremonies that "follow" after death

SUB′-sə-kwənt
coming after or later; following in time or order
sequel
SEE′-kwəl
a following event; a literary work that follows another
sequence
SEE′-kwəns
a following in order; a series of events in proper order

2. Complaints

Some people are chronic complainers — forever whining, nagging, dissatisfied with everything, finding fault at every turn. The Latin verb queror means to complain, and such a person is called—

querulous
querulous (KWAIR′-ə-ləs) ← Latin queror (to complain) → habitually complaining; whining; fretful

A querulous voice, a querulous old man, a querulous complaint — the word always implies chronic, habitual, petulant dissatisfaction.

3. Snobbery

Latin cilium means eyelid — and super means above. A person who is contemptuous of others literally raises an eyebrow above (super-cilium) in that classic gesture of disdain and arrogance. Such a person is called—

supercilious
supercilious (soo′-pər-SIL′-ee-əs) ← Latin super (above) + cilium (eyelid) → haughtily contemptuous; looking down on others with an upraised eyebrow of scorn

The supercilious person communicates disdain through posture, tone, and that subtle raising of the brow — as if the world below is barely worth their attention.

4. Noise

The Latin root strepo means to make a noise. A child (or adult) who is noisy, unruly, boisterous — difficult to manage or restrain — is called—

obstreperous
obstreperous (əb-STREP′-ər-əs) ← Latin ob- (against) + strepo (to make noise) → noisily and stubbornly resistant; boisterously unmanageable

An obstreperous crowd, an obstreperous child, an obstreperous opponent at a town hall meeting — always noisy, always difficult.

5. Moneyless

The Latin root pecus means cattle — and at one time in human history, a person's wealth was measured not by stocks and bonds but by stocks of domestic animals. Someone who had lots of pecus was rich; someone without pecus was indigent, destitute, "broke." And so today we call someone who is habitually without funds—

impecunious
impecunious (im′-pə-KYOO′-nee-əs) ← Latin im- (not) + pecus (cattle, wealth) → habitually without money; chronically short of cash

This word does not necessarily imply living in reduced circumstances or want, but quite simply being short of cash — habitually.

pecuniary
pə-KYOO′-nee-air′-ee
pertaining to money; of or involving financial matters

6. Horses

The French word cheval means horse; and in medieval times only gentlemen and knights rode on horses — common people walked. Traditionally, knights were courteous to women, attentive to female desires, and self-sacrificing when their own interests conflicted with those of the fair sex. Hence, we call a modern man who has a knightly attitude toward women—

chivalrous
chivalrous (SHIV′-əl-rəs) ← French cheval (horse) ← Latin caballus (inferior horse) → courteous and attentive to women; gallant in the manner of a medieval knight

7. No Harm Done

The Latin root noceo means to injure. Someone who need cause you no fear — so harmless as to be unable to interfere with you or get you into trouble — is called—

innocuous
innocuous (ə-NOK′-yoo-əs) ← Latin in- (not) + noceo (to injure) → causing no harm; harmless; unlikely to offend or disturb
innocent
not guilty of crime or injury; from the same root
noxious
NOK′-shəs
harmful, poisonous, unwholesome — the opposite of innocuous

8. Alcoholic

The Latin root bibo means to drink; and one who likes to tipple beyond the point of sobriety — who has an overfondness for drinks with a pronounced alcoholic content — is called, usually humorously—

bibulous
bibulous (BIB′-yə-ləs) ← Latin bibo (to drink) → addicted to drink; overly fond of alcohol
imbibe
im-BĪB′
to drink in, soak up, absorb. Often used metaphorically: "to imbibe learning"
bib
upper part of an apron, or an apronlike napkin — it prevents what is drunk (or eaten) from dribbling on clothing

9. Like Death Itself

The Latin root cado means to fall — one's final fall is of course always in death. Someone who looks like a corpse — pale, gaunt, thin, haggard, eyes deep-sunk, limbs wasted — the extreme opposite of glowing health — is called—

cadaverous
cadaverous (kə-DAV′-ər-əs) ← Latin cado (to fall) → looking like a corpse; haggard, pale, gaunt, deathlike
cadaver
kə-DAV′-ər

decadent
DEK′-ə-dənt

decadence
DEK′-ə-dəns
a state of decay; moral or spiritual decline

10. Pain and Misery

The Latin root doleo means to suffer or to grieve. One who is mournful and sad, whose melancholy comes from physical pain or mental distress, who seems to be suffering or grieving, is called—

dolorous
dolorous (DOL′-ər-əs or DŌ′-lər-əs) ← Latin doleo (to suffer, to grieve) → sorrowful; mournful; expressing pain or grief

DŌ′-ler

kən-DŌL′

condolence
kən-DŌ′-ləns

The Socratic Sandbox invites you to think with these words — not just recognize them. Engage with the prompts below for reflection, composition, or debate. There are no wrong answers, only deeper thinking.

Character Study

Think of someone (real or fictional) who could be described as obsequious. What drives that behavior — genuine admiration, fear, ambition, insecurity? Is obsequiousness ever admirable? Or does it always diminish the person who practices it?

Etymology in the Flesh

The etymology of supercilious involves raising an eyebrow. The etymology of impecunious involves cattle. The etymology of cadaverous involves falling. Choose one of these words and describe a scene where the origin story becomes literally visible in the word's use.

Distinction

What is the difference between being querulous and being legitimately aggrieved? Is there a point at which a complaint stops being querulousness and becomes justified? What determines that line?

Word in the Wild

Write a short paragraph (3–5 sentences) using at least four of today's ten adjectives. Each word should feel natural and contextually apt — not forced.
