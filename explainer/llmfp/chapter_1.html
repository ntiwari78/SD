Chapter 01

The Engine: Transformer Architecture

How we stopped treating text like a string and started treating it like a graph.

💡 The Core Intuition

In classical NLP, models (like RNNs) read text sequentially, like a person walking down a narrow corridor looking at one word at a time. The Transformer is like a bird's eye view; it sees the entire landscape (the sentence) at once.

To keep track of where everything is, it uses Positional Encoding. Think of this as adding a unique GPS coordinate to every word's semantic vector so the model knows that "dog" is at the start and "man" is at the end.

Part A: The Attention Equation

The mechanism that allows a word to "attend" to others is the Scaled Dot-Product Attention:

$$ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V $$

Part B: Evolution of Positional Encoding (PE)

Architecture choice significantly impacts how well a model handles long scrolls of ancient text. Let's break down the primary methods used in production today.

1. Absolute Positional Encoding (APE) GPT-1 / BERT

The model adds a fixed sinusoidal signal to the input embedding. It uses waves of different frequencies to ensure uniqueness across thousands of positions.

$$ PE_{(pos, 2i)} = \sin\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{model}}}\right) $$ $$ PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} = \cos\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{model}}}\right) $$

Limitation: It doesn't scale well. If the model sees 512 tokens during training, it won't know what to do with token 513.

2. Relative Positional Encoding (RPE) T5 / DeBERTa

Instead of asking "Where am I?", the model asks "How far away is my neighbor?". It adds a learned bias term to the attention score based on the distance $k = i - j$.

$$ e_{ij} = \frac{Q_i K_j^T + w_{i-j}}{\sqrt{d_k}} $$

3. Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) LLaMA 1-3 / PaLM

The current state-of-the-art. It encodes relative position by rotating the Query and Key vectors in a complex plane. The rotation preserves the magnitude but changes the phase.

$$ \text{RoPE}(x, pos) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(pos \theta) & -\sin(pos \theta) \\ \sin(pos \theta) & \cos(pos \theta) \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Feature: It naturally decays the attention score as distance increases, which mimics how humans read.

4. ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases) MPT / Bloom

ALiBi doesn't add anything to embeddings. Instead, it subtracts a value from the attention score that increases linearly with distance.

$$ \text{score}(i, j) = q_i \cdot k_j - m \cdot |i - j| $$

Strength: Incredible extrapolation. A model trained on 2k tokens can generalize to 32k or more instantly.

The Ancient Language Challenge

When translating fragmented languages like **Sumerian**, sentence structures are often non-linear. **RoPE** is particularly useful here because it treats the relationship between words as a rotation. This allows the model to "feel" the grammatical tension between a subject and a verb even if the tablet is broken and hundreds of characters are missing in between.

Knowledge Check

1. Why do we divide the argument by $\sqrt{d_k}$ in the attention score?

When the dimension $d_k$ is large, the dot product $Q K^T$ can grow extremely large in magnitude. This pushes the softmax function into regions where the gradient is almost zero.

Scaling by $\frac{1}{\sqrt{d_k}}$ ensures the variance of the dot product remains roughly $1$, preventing gradients from vanishing during backpropagation.

2. Which encoding method is best for translating a book that is 10x longer than the training data?

ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases) is specifically designed for this. Unlike APE, which is fixed to a certain length, ALiBi's linear penalty generalizes gracefully to sequences much longer than those seen during training.
